## Materials Methods
CO2 (99.9%; SON, Naples, Italy), polyvinyl alcohol [PVA, molecular weight (MW): 30,000 -55,000 Da; Aldrich Chemical Company, Milan, Italy], AC (purity 99.9%; Aldrich Chemical Company), GLY (purity 99%, Aldrich Chemical Company), RA (Aldrich Chemical Company), and PLGA (75:25 MW: 20,000 Da, Resomer RG 752S; Boehringer, Firence, Italy) were used as received.
Emulsions Formulation
Emulsions with different oil-in-water (o-w) ratio of 20:80 and 10:90 were prepared. Oily phase was prepared dissolving fixed amount of the polymer [45% or 55% (w/w) of the oily phase] and drug [3%, 6%, or 10% (w/w) of the polymer] into AC. Water phase was prepared dissolving PVA [0.6% (w/w) of the water phase] into an aqueous GLY solution (80:20 GLY/water). The oily phase was added into the water phase using a high-speed stirrer (model L4RT; Silverson Machines Ltd., Waterside, Chesham Bucks, UK) at 4000 rpm for 3 min.
Liquid Emulsion Extraction Procedure
Emulsion (20 g) was charged in a beaker and the solvent was extracted adding dropwise 60 g of aqueous GLY (ratio 50:50 water/GLY), the resulting solution was stirred for 80 min at 400 rpm; subsequently, 60 g of pure water was added dropwise and the extraction was performed at the same stirring conditions for other 100 min.
SEE: Apparatuses and Procedures
Schematic representations of the two SEE process layouts (batch and continuous operating mode) are reported in Figures 1a and 1b, respectively. SEE-batch operation layout (SEE-B) apparatus consisted of a 0.25 dm3 cylindrical stainless steel extractor in which 40 g of emulsion was placed for each experiment. SC-CO2 was homogeneously dispersed in the emulsion through a cylindrical stainless steel porous dispenser, using a high-pressure diaphragm pump (model Milroyal B; Milton Roy, Pont Saint Pierre, France) at a constant flow rate (0.4 kg/h). The vessel was located in an air-heated thermostated oven. The extracted solvent of the oily phase was recovered in a separator downstream the extractor in which the pressure was regulated by a backpressure valve. A rotameter and a dry test meter were used to measure the CO2 flow rate and the total quantity of CO2 delivered, respectively. When the extraction process was complete, the microspheres suspension was removed from the bottom of the extraction vessel for further processing.
Supercritical emulsion extraction-continuous operation layout (SEE-C) apparatus consisted of a 107 cm long column with an internal diameter of 1.3 cm. The three stages are formed by stainless steel cylindrical elements of 30 cm height connected by four way (cross) unions and packed with stainless steel packings (1889 m-1 specific surface; 0.94 of voidage; Pro-Pak, Scientific Development Company, State College, Pennsylvania). Cross-unions were also used to insert temperature controls at different heights of the column; the apparatus was thermally insulated by ceramic cloths and its temperature profile was controlled by six temperature controllers. SC-CO2 was fed at the bottom of the column by a high-pressure diaphragm pump (model Milroyal B; Milton Roy) at a constant flow rate of 1.4 kg/h. The emulsion was taken from a reservoir and fed to the column by a high pressure piston pump (model 305; Gilson, France, Villiers le Bel) at the top of the column at a constant flow rate of 0.15 kg/h (2.5 mL/min). A separator, located downstream the top of the column, was used to recover the extracted oily phase. Microspheres suspension was continuously collected at the bottom of the column by decompression using a needle valve. At the end of each run, microspheres suspension was washed several times by centrifugation with distilled water, recovered by membrane filtration, and dried at air. SEE-B and SEE-C apparatuses were also described in detail in previous works.29,30
Droplets and Microspheres Morphology and Size Distributions
The droplets formed in the emulsion were observed using an optical microscope (model BX 50; Olympus, Tokyo, Japan) equipped with a phase contrast condenser. A field-emission scanning electron microscope (FE-SEM model LEO 1525; Carl Zeiss SMT AG, Oberkochen, Germany) was used to study the morphology of the produced microspheres. PLGA/RA microspheres were recovered for FE-SEM analysis by membrane filtration and then dried at air. Samples were fixed over an adhesive carbon tab, previously stuck to an aluminium stub and covered with gold (layer thickness 250 A) using a sputter coater (model 108 A; Agar Scientific, Stansted, UK). Droplet size distribution (DSD) and particles size distribution (PSD) were measured by dynamic light scattering (DLS, model; Mastersizer S, Malvern Instruments Ltd., Worcherstershire, UK). Analyses were performed immediately after emulsion/suspension preparation.
Solid-State Characterization
Thermograms of powder samples were obtained using a differential scanning calorimeter (DSC model TC11; Mettler Toledo, Inc., Columbus). Fusion temperature and enthalpy were calibrated with an indium standard (melting point 156.6degC). The samples (+-5 mg) were accurately weighed, crimped in an aluminum pan, and heated from 25degC to 300degC at 10degC/min under nitrogen purge of 50 mL/min. Diffraction patterns (XRD) were obtained using an X-ray diffractometer (model D8 Discover; Bruker AXS, Inc., Madison, Wisconsin) with a Cu-sealed tube source. Samples were placed in the holder and flattened with a glass slide to assure a good surface texture. The measuring conditions were as follows: Ni-filtered CuK radiation, l = 1.54 A, 2th angle ranging between 5deg and 70deg with a scan rate of 3 s/step and a step size of 0.2deg. DSC and X-ray analyses were performed in three replicates for each batch of material.
Solvent Residue Analysis
Acetone content in the suspensions was measured to check the efficiency of solvent removal from the emulsion. The solvent residue was measured using a headspace sampler (model 50 Scan; Hewlett-Packard, Palo Alto, California) coupled to a gas chromatograph interfaced with a flame ionization detector (GC-FID, model 6890 Agilent Series; Agilent Technologies Inc., Wilmington, Delaware). AC was separated using a fused silica capillary column 30 m length, 0.25 mm internal diameter, 0.25 um film thickness (model DB-1; J&W, Folsom, California). The oven temperature in the GC was set to 40degC for 8 min. The injector was maintained at 180degC (split mode, ratio 1:1) and helium was used as the carrier gas (7 mL/min). Headspace conditions were: equilibration time 60 min at 100degC, pressurization time 2 min, and loop fill time 1 min. Headspace samples were prepared in 10 mL vials filled with 3 mL of suspension.
Drug Loading and Release
Drug loading was measured by dissolving a known mass (5 mg) of microspheres in 2 mL of AC. Samples were stirred for 1 h at 100 rpm to ensure the complete dissolution of the polymer. Pure RA was also tested to verify its sftability in AC and no drug degradation was observed. The concentration of drug in the resulting solution was determined by HPLC (model 1200 series; Agilent Technologies Inc.) using a C18 column (LiChrosphere, particle size 10 um, 250 x 3.9 mm) at room temperature. Elution was performed with methanol/water (98:2, v/v) at a flow rate of 1 mL/min; 20 uL was injected and the absorbance was monitored at 324 nm. The amount of measured drug was then converted in the effective drug loaded into microspheres as given in the following formula: 100x (weight of monitored drug/weight of sample taken for analysis). The encapsulation efficiency was calculated as 100x (effective loading/theoretical loading).
As the solubility of RA in n-propanol/water solution (30:70, v/v) is about 0.03 mg/mL, for each release test, 100 mg of microspheres was suspended in 500 mL of this solution, continuously stirred at 100 srpm in a 35degC incubator to maintain adequate sink conditions. The concentration of the released drug in the external medium was continuously detected by a UV probe at 324 nm (model Cary 50; Varian, Palo Alto, California).

## Results
Selection of SEE Operating Conditions
When AC, water, and GLY are mixed together, the phase diagram of the ternary system shows a two-phase region due to phase separation.21 To obtain a stable emulsion, the ternary mixture composition should lie in this region. To verify the influence of the position of the operating points on the emulsion stability, two different oil-water ratios were prepared of 20:80 and 10:90, with a given composition of the oily phase [AC with 45% (w/w) of PLGA plus 3% of RA] and the water phase [GLY/water 80/20 with 0.6% (w/w) of PVA]. Figure 2 reports the ternary AC-water-GLY diagram in which two red dots indicate the position of the operating points selected for emulsions formulation. One point is located in the Phase I region (i.e., in the area of complete miscibility between the three components) and the second near the border of Phase II (i.e., in the two phase region). Solid PLGA/RA products obtained after SEE-B treatment of these emulsions are reported in the SEM images in 3a and 3b, respectively. The emulsion with the o-w ratio of 20:80 was stable and led to well-shaped PLGA microspheres after the oily phase removal, whereas the emulsion with the o-w ratio of 10:90 produced only large polymer aggregates. These results mean that the emulsion with the o-w ratio of 10:90 is not stable and in the following part of the study only the o-w ratio of 20:80 is used.
All SEE experiments were performed operating at 80 bar, 36degC because at this process conditions, AC is completely miscible in SC-CO2,31 whereas the solubility of the GLY-water external phase in SC-CO2 is extremely small32,33; therefore, a selective extraction of the oily phase from emulsions is expected. The difference between the two SEE processes (batch and continuous operating layouts) consists of the contacting mode between liquid and supercritical phase; this contact is very important because the mixing between the two fluids promotes the migration of SC-CO2 inside the emulsion and the subsequent solubilization/extraction of the oily phase. Using the SEE-B process layout, the mixing is favored by SC-CO2 bubbling in the extraction vessel. However, the recovery of the microparticles in some cases can be difficult (especially if they range in submicron size) due to their adhesion on the walls of the vessel. The SEE-C layout is based on the countercurrent contact between two films (liquid and SC-CO2) flowing on the extremely high surface of the tower internal packings. As a consequence, the mixing is more efficient and allows reduced residence times in the apparatus. Moreover, the operation is performed at steady-state conditions that can be maintained as long as required depending on the amount of emulsion to be treated, that is, batch-to-batch differences are avoided and higher product repeatability can be achieved.30
Conventional Liquid-Liquid Extraction Versus Supercritical Extraction
In this study, the comparison of the extraction processes (liquid and supercritical) is proposed, based on the characteristics of the microspheres obtained by SEE-B and by conventional liquid extraction (LEE). The same emulsions (o-w ratio 20:80) have been processed using the two technologies and the morphology of microspheres produced by SEE-B was in all cases spherical and no coalescing, whereas collapsed particles were often produced by LEE. An example of the different results obtained is illustrated in the SEM images reported in Figures 4a and 4b, which are related to microparticles obtained from emulsions prepared with PLGA concentrations of 45% and RA content of 6%. The water phase was formed by GLY-water (80:20) with 0.6% (w/w) of PVA. The SEM image in Figure 4b shows large aggregates of microparticles. This phenomenon can be due to an inadequate amount of the extraction agent or an insufficient absorbing rate of the solvent leached from the solidifying microspheres that may generate intraparticle adhesion.18 On the contrary, the enhanced mass transfer characteristic of SC-CO2 generates a faster extraction avoiding particles adhesion, as confirmed by SEM image reported in Figure 4a. Distribution data, encapsulation efficiency, and solvent residues of the microspheres illustrated in Figures 4a and 4b are summarized in Table 1. For example, starting from an emulsion with a droplet mean size of 4.1 um (SD +-2.2), microspheres with a mean size of 3.6 um (SD +-1.9) were obtained by SEE-B and with a mean size of 4.3 um (SD +-2.3) were produced by conventional LEE. The encapsulation efficiency into PLGA microspheres was 80% when operating with SEE-B and 73% when the conventional extraction process was used. Both encapsulation values can be considered excellent and indicate that the emulsion proposed is stable.
DSD and produced PSDs are reported for comparison purposes.
Solvent residue monitored after SEE-B processing was 3591 ppm and was significantly lower than the value found after the conventional LEE that produces suspensions with an AC residue of the order of 50,000 ppm. As a consequence, more complex downstream processes are required for particle recovery such as prolonged washing with pure water as well as AC-GLY-water mixture separation to recover the pure solvents. Also the processing time is different because SEE-B process requires only 60 min against the 160 min of the LEE to process the same quantity of emulsion.
SEE Process Layouts: Batch Versus Continuous Operation
Emulsions with o-w ratio of 20:80 were formulated fixing the PLGA concentration in AC at 45% (w/w) and varying the RA theoretical loading from 3%, 6%, and 10% (w/w). They were treated using the two different SEE layouts: batch (SEE-B) and continuous (SEE-C). The PSDs data of the microspheres recovered using the two process layouts and other significant process information are reported in Table 2. The DSDs data of the emulsions treated are also reported for comparison purposes.
Microsphere size and distribution are the first parameters considered for the comparison. Looking at the PSDs data of the microspheres produced by SEE-B and SEE-C, it is evident that they are similar and the process layout does not have a significant effect on the PSD. Indeed, starting from an emulsion with a droplet mean size of 3.5 um (SD +-1.9), microspheres with a mean size of 3.2 um (SD +-1.4) were obtained by SEE-B and with a mean size of 3.3 um (SD +-1.9) were produced by SEE-C. The drug encapsulation efficiency measured for all the microdevices produced ranged between 80% and 90%.
As previously indicated, the two process layouts can show different recovery efficiency, phase mixing and, therefore, processing time. Indeed, the batch process allowed the recovery of maximum 56% of the charged material because part of the microsphere suspension was lost in the gas stream released during the final depressurization step. On the contrary, the continuous process allowed the recovery of about 85%-90% of the charged material. The solvent residue measured after the continuous process was about 1000 ppm, that is, a value considerably lower than the ones measured during the batch SEE operations that ranged between 2000 and 4000 ppm. This result confirms that the continuous process produces a better contact between the two phases (emulsion/SC-CO2), enhancing mass transfer. The final remarkable result is the processing time; indeed, considering as a reference time, the residence time of the liquid phase in the packed column, in the continuous processing the extraction takes place in about 10 min, the residence time of the emulsion/suspension in the column, whereas about 60 min is the process time necessary when the emulsion is treated by SEE-B and 160 min are necessary to perform the conventional LEE.
Microspheres Produced by SEE-C: Size Tailoring and Solid-State Characterization
Different oily phase compositions were processed by SEE-C to explore the possibility of microsphere size tailoring. Particularly, PLGA concentration in the AC oily phase was varied between 45% and 55% (w/w) at a fixed drug concentration (3%, w/w) and the influence of polymer concentration in the internal phase was evaluated on droplets/microspheres size and size distribution. The emulsion o-w ratio and the composition of the water external phase was maintained always the same, as reported above. Looking at the values reported in Table 3, it can be noted that an increase of PLGA content in the oily phase generate a slightly increase of droplet mean size and an enlargement of their standard deviation. This result is in agreement with those observed in some previous works, in which the increase in the oily phase viscosity (due to higher PLGA content) caused an increase of droplet size and, consequently, particle size.30 All the emulsions showed noncoalescing droplets, as illustrated in the optical microscope image reported in Figures 5a and 5b and the derived microspheres were almost all well-defined spheres, as shown in the SEM images illustrated in Figures 5c and 5d); their PSDs are reported in Figure 6. Microspheres with a mean size of 3.2 um (SD +-1.4) were obtained at the PLGA percentage of 45% (w/w) and with a mean size of 4.0 um (SD +-2.1) at the PLGA percentage of 55% (w/w) charged in the oily phase, respectively. The PSDs data of the microspheres obtained are reported in Table 3, together with the DSDs data of the corresponding emulsions, for comparison purposes.
Thermal analysis (DSC) was performed on the produced microspheres and on raw PLGA and RA for comparison purposes. The corresponding thermograms are reported in Figure 7. PLGA (75:25) shows a glass transition that remains relatively constant (onset 39.98degC, endset 45.32degC) in all the observed samples and the polymer decomposition occurs above 300degC. Raw RA shows an endothermic peak at about 58degC due to its melting. The PLGA/RA microspheres show the same degradation event of the unprocessed polymer, confirming that the supercritical process does not modify the polymer structure, whereas the characteristic peak of RA is no longer visible in the microspheres, even when the drug loading was 8.8% (w/w). This fact suggests that the drug is dissolved into the PLGA matrix.
X-ray analyses were performed to obtain information about the solid state of the produced materials. The diffraction patterns of microparticles are reported in Figure 8. The XRD of raw polymer and RA are also reported for comparison. XRD analyses show that the particles obtained are amorphous due to the fast polymer precipitation inside the droplets during the oily phase extraction.
Microspheres Produced by SEE-C: Drug Loading and Release Study
As previously mentioned, microspheres with different drug loading were also produced by SEE-C with excellent encapsulation efficiencies (between 87% and 90%). PLGA microspheres produced by SEE-C and with RA effective loadings of 2.7%, 5.2% and 8.8% (w/w) were tested for drug release studies in a modified media34 of water/n-propanol (70:30) at 35degC (microspheres distribution data are reported in Table 2). The release profiles are illustrated in Figure 9; in the same figure, the dissolution rate of pure RA is also reported. Drug release was monitored only for 6 days because RA degradation in the external medium was observed for longer times. Release profiles obtained at different loadings show that, when the RA content is increased, a slower drug release is obtained, that is, 90% of RA is released in 6 days when the drug loading is 2.7% (w/w), whereas in the same time, only 40% and 10% of the drug is released when the charge is 5.2% and 8.8% (w/w), respectively.
It is known from the literature that drug release from PLGA can be described in two steps; the first one reflects the diffusion-controlled mass transfer of the drug accessible at the solid/dissolution medium interface and the second step reflects the release of drug entrapped in the polymer during its bulk degradation.35., 36., 37. The release behavior at different drug loadings observed in this study can be explained considering the low solubility of RA in the release medium (see measured solubility data in the Methods section). Indeed, as suggested by Lee,38 when the concentration of the drug in the polymer microsphere is higher than its equilibrium solubility in the external medium, two moving fronts can be established (diffusion followed by bulk erosion) and they are synchronized (the second motion of the front is influenced by the first one). In our case, the diffusion front is very slow and can move toward the internal of the particles only when the concentration in the solid phase decreases to values lower than the equilibrium value; as a consequence, the higher is the drug concentration in the polymer, the slower is the movement of the diffusion front.39

Looking at the kinetic parameters reported in Table 4, it can be observed that Kd values decrease by increasing the drug loading (from 0.0085 to 0.0011 day-1 with a drug loading of 2.7% and 8.8%, respectively); this behavior could mean that increasing the loading of a low solubility drug, the apparent diffusivity of the drug will decrease and, therefore, the release kinetic constant is slower. Tmax values were directly evaluated from the experimental curves and fixed to 1.5, 3.2, and 3.5 days. The timing of the maximum drug release rate should be explained taking into account that bulk erosion front follows the diffusion front, as stated before; therefore, when the diffusion front is delayed (by higher drug loading of the low soluble drug), also the Tmax value increases. Finally, the bulk erosion kinetic constant K was calculated at 0.68 day-1 and was maintained constant in all data fitting because it should be independent from the drug loading, being only related to the depolymerization reaction.

## Conclusion
Supercritical fluids emulsion extraction technology offers a new strategy in the biopolymer microsphere production with a better particle size control and excellent encapsulation efficiency, especially when labile compounds, such as RA, are processed. Taking advantage of the enhanced mass transfer of the supercritical solvent, spherical PLGA microspheres, with less residual solvent respect to the conventional process, can be successfully produced with an excellent drug encapsulation.
The successful upgrade of the SEE technology into the continuous process layout (SEE-C) allows a higher throughput with smaller plant volumes, eliminating all the batch-to-batch repeatability problems. Improved recovery efficiencies and shorter processing time are other advantages of the SEE continuous operation. This technology could be proposed as a process innovation that may allow the fast production of tailored PLGA/drug microspheres with an excellent control of drug release.
