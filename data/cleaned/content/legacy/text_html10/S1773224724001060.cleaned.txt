## Materials Methods
Lina was provided by (EIPICO), 10th of Ramadan City, Egypt. PLGA of different viscosity grades and different ratios of lactide and glycolide (65:35, 75:25, and 85:15) with molecular weights of 60,000-70,000 Da and intrinsic viscosity (1.05 dl/gm) were purchased from LACTEL International Absorbable Polymers, Birmingham, USA. Dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) was provided by Merck, Germany. Methanol, methylene chloride, and acetonitrile (HPLC gradient grade) were acquired from Fisher Scientific (New Jersey, USA). Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) (molecular weight of 70000-100000 Dalton), dibasic potassium hydrogen phosphate, and monobasic sodium hydrogen phosphate were also provided from Spectrum Chemical MFG. Corp, New Jersey, USA.

A pre-screening study was conducted prior to constructing the current experimental design. Five variables were investigated such as the concentration of PLGA, concentration of lactic acid in PLGA, stirring rate, organic phase ratio. Based on the findings, three formulation parameters were chosen to further investigate their impact on the characteristics of Lina nanoparticles. In this regard, a 33 Box-Behnken Experimental Design was developed using Design-Expert(r) software (version 11, Stat-Ease Inc., Minneapolis, MN, USA). The following were the independent variables (formulation factors) chosen: Lactide concentration in PLGA (A), PLGA concentration (B), and Organic phase ratio (C). Each independent variable in the design was investigated at three different levels (-1, 0, +1). Entrapment efficiency (Y1), particle size (Y2), and release percent after seven days (Y3) were the dependent variables (responses) (Table 1).
Table 2 shows the design matrix constructed by the software which comprised of 15 experiments and three of which were replicated runs. To reduce the effect of inexplicable variability in the observed response, all trials were performed in a random order. The design role was to estimate the primary impacts and the interaction effects on the researched responses as well as to optimize the chosen formulation elements. The computer program generates a single equation for each response that illustrates how the independent variables affected that response.
A- Lactide conc. in PLGA; B- PLGA concentration and C- solvent ratio.
In order to fit the experimental data to the desirable model and to check the validity of the experimental design, analysis of variance (ANOVA) was performed and R2 was calculated. The fitted model is considered adequate if the model is significant (p < 0.05) and the lack of fit is not significant (p > 0.05).
The current study's goal was to develop injectable Lina nanoparticles with desired high entrapment efficiency, small particle size, as well as sustained release over seven days utilizing biodegradable PLGA polymer at varied grades with different organic solvents ratios and polymer concentration. The selected formulation, which demonstrated excellent entrapment effectiveness, tiny particle size, and sustained in-vitro drug release over seven days, was injected subcutaneously into rats to evaluate the drug's pharmacokinetic profile in vivo. Reduced drug concentrations released from the NPs systems, as well as the drug's extended circulation effect in vivo, were sought to improve Lina's effectiveness and bioavailability. Because of its effectiveness in managing blood glucose levels over time, such formulation has become an option for the patient to lower drug dosing frequency, reduce side effects, and improve patient compliance. A three-factor and three-level  Box-Behnken design was used to create all formulations using a single emulsion evaporation method to look at how closely the dependent and independent variables correlate.

2.4. preparation of lina nanoparticle formulations
Utilizing the single emulsion evaporation approach, Lina-loaded NPs were created. PLGA and Lina powder were dissolved using a magnetic stirrer through a solution of 2 ml of methylene chloride and 1:1 DMSO. To create the emulsion, a tiny glass beaker containing 4 ml of 2.5 % PVA was placed in a bath containing ice and the drug-PLGA solution was added portion-wise. The mixture was then combined via a probe sonicator at 4 degC and 50 W. Using a rotating vacuum evaporator at reduced pressure, 25 degC, and 100 rpm for 15 min, the organic solvents were eliminated. To create formulations containing 2.5 mg of Lina per ml, the produced polymeric NPs are diluted with 6 ml of lower concentration of PVA (0.3%) and then kept in a refrigerator for further work .
2.5. characterization of nanoparticles formulations
2.5.1. Determination of particle size, polydispersity index (PDI), and zeta potential of Lina loaded NPs formulations
The dynamic light scattering technique (DLS) was used to measure the particle size, PDI, and zeta potential of the Lina polymeric NP formulations using the Zeta sizer apparatus (version 6.32, Malvern instrument Ltd). To avoid the multi-scattering phenomenon, distilled water were used to dilute samples for 2 min at 25 degC before testing .
2.5.2. Evaluation of entrapment efficiency & drug loading of NPs formulations
To separate the free drug, the centrifugation technique was used to measure the efficiency of entrapment of Lina NPs . At 12000 rpm and 4 degC, collected samples were centrifuged for 10 min. To make sure that no free drug was present, the free Lina was measured by UV at a wavelength at 294 nm. The supernatant was diluted using PVA solution (0.3% w/v) and the same diluted medium was used as a blank. The equation below is used to determine the entrapment efficiency:Entrapment efficiency (%) = (Qt - Qs / Qt) 100Where Qt: estimated quantity of Lina and Qs: actual quantity of Lina that was found in the supernatant .
Drug loading (%) = Amount of drug found in the NPs/Amount of total NPs x 100 .
2.5.3. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) study
The shape of the dispersed particles was examined using JEM-2100 transmission electron microscope (JEOL, Japan). Two drops of the diluted preparation from the optimized Lina NPs mixture was exposed to a carbon-coated grid after being diluted with distilled water. This was allowed to sit for 2 min to enhance absorption on the carbon. The samples were stained for 3 min via 1% uranyl acetate solution prior examination by TEM.
2.5.4. Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC)
The calorimetric properties of Lina raw material, PLGA polymer, polyvinyl alcohol, a binary mixture of PVA and PLGA Lina-free NPs formulation (placebo), and optimized Lina loaded NPs formulation were investigated. The samples were exposed to heating in closed aluminum pans using a nitrogen environment at a constant heating rate of 10 degC/min. Using a Model (DSC 4000, PerkinElmer, Waltham, MA, USA) with equipment and a PC control unit at a heating rate of 10 degC/min and a nitrogen atmosphere, DSC thermograms were produced at temperatures ranging from 30 to 400 degC. Pyris(tm) Manager Software was used for data analysis .
2.5.5. Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy
Using potassium bromide disc technique, the FTIR spectrum of pure Lina, PLGA, polyvinyl alcohol, a binary mixture of PVA and PLGA Lina-free NPs formulation (placebo), and optimized Lina loaded NPs formulation were scanned. The resolution was 1 cm-1 and the scanning range was 4000-500 cm-1. The FTIR spectra were achieved by FTIR spectrometer (Nicolet iS10, Thermo Scientific, USA) covered the range 400-4000 cm-1 .
2.5.6. In-vitro release study
An in-vitro release study of Lina from freshly made PLGA nanoparticle formulae were carried out via using dialysis bags of cellophanes membrane (molecular weight cut-off 12-14 KDa). After centrifugation, 2 ml of the polymeric NPs were put into the bags which were then suspended in closed containers having 100 ml of phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) and shaken at 60 rpm, 37 degC, in a shaking water bath. At 1, 2, 5, 8, 24, 48, 96, 124, and 168 h, 3 ml samples were taken out. As previously mentioned, a spectrophotometric analysis of Lina concentration was performed at 294 nm. The sink condition was attained via using 100 ml volume of release. Substituting the withdrawn release medium with fresh buffer to maintain volume is constant ..
2.6. In vivo evaluation of the optimized lina nanoparticles
2.6.1. Animals and ethical approval
We used mature male albino rats that weighed 200-250 gm. The animals was bought from the institute for animal breeding at Zagazig University in Egypt. Rats were housed for 7 day at room temperature in a 12 h light, 12 h dark cycle with free access to eat and drink before the studies began. The Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC; number of approval: ZU-IACUC/3/F/157/2021) of Faculty of Pharmacy provided recommendation. All procedures of experimental design and animal handling were carried out according to the approved guidelines of the Animal Ethics Committee of Zagazig University, Egypt that comply with the ARRIVE guidelines and UK animals Act.
2.6.2. Diabetes induction in rats
The rats were given a high-fructose/high-fat/high-salt diet (10% w/v fructose in drinking water, 25% vegetable fat, and 3% sodium chloride in food) for four weeks to induce insulin resistance. After that, the rats received a single intraperitoneal injection of streptozotocin (40 mg/kg) to induce moderate hyperglycemia, which mimics the pathophysiology of type 2 diabetes in humans. The rats exhibiting prolonged elevated levels of fed glucose (ranging from 200 to 300 mg/dl) were classified as diabetic for experimental reasons [, , ].
2.6.3. Animal groups
The rats were divided into four groups (n = 10) as follows.
- Group 1: The control animals in this group were given regular doses of purified water and standard meals for a period of six continuous weeks.
- Group 2: The diabetes-induced animals in this group were given a placebo for a further two weeks.
- Group 3: The diabetes-induced rats in this group were given daily oral Lina solution (4 mg/kg) for 10 days.
- Group 4: The diabetes-induced rats in this group were given one injectable subcutaneous dose of Lina NPs (10 mg/kg) which contained 150 mg PLGA 65:35 and a 10% solvent ratio/10 ml NPs formulation.
The Lina NPs formulae were injected subcutaneously with 16 gauge needles at doses of (10 mg/kg), and at 1, 2, 4, 24, 72, and 168 h (1 week), blood samples (0.5 ml) were taken from the orbital vein into heparinized tubes. The drawn blood samples were then centrifuged for 10 min at 4000 rpm to get plasma .
2.6.4. Pharmacokinetic study
Eight diabetic rats were given to one of two experimental group: the standard Lina group (Lina Oral), which administered one dose of 4 mg/kg of lina as a suspension orally, and the nano formula group (Lina. Nano), which received one dose of 10 mg/kg of lina as a pharmaceutical formulation in the form of nanoparticles. Then, 1, 2, 4, 24, 72, and 168 h (7 days) following treatment, 0.5 mL blood was drawn from the retro-orbital plexus vein in heparinized tubes while being lightly sedated with ether. This blood was used to determine the presence of Lina by HPLC. To separate plasma, blood samples centrifugation for 10 min at 4 degC at 10,000 rpm. Until analysis, storage of samples at -80 degC.
Determination of pharmacokinetic parameters, plasma concentration against time data were used. Half-life (t1/2), Mean Residence Time (How long drug molecules stay in the body, MRT), and apparent volume of distribution at steady state (Vss) were the pharmacokinetic parameters that were calculated. The results were shown as mean +- standard error of the mean (SEM). Unpaired Student t-test was used to statistically compare Lina oral with Lina Nano for t1/2, MRT and Vss,. Analysis done by PK Solver (r) software.
Plasma concentrations of Lina oral and Lina Nano was performed using two-way ANOVA followed by a Tukey's post hoc test.
2.6.5. Quantification of Linagliptin in plasma by HPLC analysis
A stock solution of Lina was dissolved in methanol (1000 mg/ml). Drug solutions were produced in serial concentrations of 5, 10, 30, 50, 100, 200, 300, and 500 mg/ml. The calibration solutions were made by combining 10 mL of working solutions with 90 mL of rat blank plasma, with concentration ranges of 0.5-50 mg/ml 900 ml of methanol was then added and vortexed for 5 min. Prior to being injected into the HPLC system, total mixture was centrifuged at 12000 rpm for 15 min at 25 degC. After that, it was filtered using a 0.45 mm syringe filter. At 294 nm, Lina concentrations were observed. The drug analysis was conducted using an HPLC device using a Venusil XBP, 5 m C18 column (25 cm x 4.6 mm) from Thermo Electron Corporation in Bellefonte, Pennsylvania, USA. Elution pumps operated an isocratic modified mobile phase containing methanol (40:60% v/v) and potassium dihydrogen phosphate buffer (pH 4.5). The auto sampler used methanol as a rinse fluid. The injection volume was 20 ml, and the flow rate was 1 ml/min. The HPLC test was run at 294 nm using a photodiode array detector. Sonication was used to degas the mobile component .
2.6.6. Pharmacodynamics study
Assessment of the antidiabetic activity by rats' post-meal blood sugar was measured which were randomly divided into four groups (n = 10). In all experimental groups, the blood glucose level was checked at 1, 3, and 7 days after the oral glucose load. After an 8 h of fasting, the rats received a continuous glucose load (0.5 gm). A glucose meter was used to measure the blood glucose from the tail droplet after an hour. PK Solver (r) tool was used for analysis of the experiment parameters via a two-way ANOVA followed by a Tukey's post hoc test.
2.6.7. Statistical analysis
Through analysis of variance two way (ANOVA), the pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamics parameters collected from the three groups were analyzed using PKSolver (r) software. An add-in program for pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamics data analysis in Microsoft Excel.

## Discussion
The goal of this work was to develop an effective lina nanoparticle formulation by testing PLGA as a biodegradable polymer at different grades with different organic ratios and polymer concentrations. This was based on the hypothesis that this would provide a more sustained treatment effect, increasing the drug half-life and decreasing the frequency of administration .This would result in a consistent, predictable, or desired drug release profile with improved pharmacokinetic properties, based on the low bioavailability of oral lina. The influences of several independent factors, such as lactide concentration in the PLGA polymer, on the entrapment efficiency were examined by adjusting the various lactide and glycolide concentration distributions in the PLGA skeleton, which altered the hydrophobic nature of the NP system, thus controlling how much water is taken in and how quickly the system degraded .
So the model was valid, reliable & applicable to describe the chosen variables on the observed responses. The difference between experimental values of optimized formula and the predicted value were showed from % prediction error which less than 5% .
An increase in the organic solvent ratio was expected to potentially minimize the Y1 response, consistent with the idea that the high lipophilicity characteristics of lactide, which are greater than those of glycolide, will lead to lower water uptake, accelerated NPs solidification, and enhanced entrapment of linagliptin in NP systems with higher concentrations of lactide in the PLGA polymer & higher PLGA amount . Furthermore, increasing the viscosity of the prepared mixture by decreasing the solvent amount during the NP synthesis was also expected to improve the entrapment efficiency of the lina .
The influence of various independent factors on particle size distribution was maximized by increasing the PLGA concentration and minimized by increasing in the organic solvent ratio as required for testing. In theory, high viscosity can be best achieved by reducing the solvent quantity during the preparation of NP, and this could increase the entrapment efficiency of linagliptin with respect to the outside buffer. The resulting increase in particle size was indeed illustrated by the fact that the thickness of the polymeric skin increased, as observed in TEM imaging .
Increased PLGA concentrations thus increase the viscosity of the polymeric solution, leading to the development of coarser emulsions and an increased particle size during diffusion & EE% .
The zeta potential values of the nanoparticle formulae thus ranged from -10.98 +- 0.82 to -16.64 +- 0.62 mV, highly charged. So demonstrating regular dispersal across the preparations & stable preparation .
The influence of independent factors on percentage release can be summarized as follows: the increase of PLGA concentration highly minimized the Y3 response (release %), while the increase in organic solvent ratio also minimized the Y3 response up to the 20% level, after which release increased. This was in agreement with the theory that an increase in particle size causes an increase in the thickness of the polymeric nanoparticles, supporting the hypothesis that using lower quantities of solvents during NP formulation increases the encapsulation effect of high volumes of PLGA, resulting in increased entrapment efficiency in the drug with respect to the external buffer, hence improving the release% .
Fig. S5 demonstrates that the release of lina. NPs was reduced by increased lactide concentrations of up to 85% at an organic solvent ratio of 20% for 100 mg of polymer. As previously indicated, an increase in lactide concentration could also potentially reduce the Y3 response, consistent with the results of Park et al. .

By increasing the PLGA concentrations and lactide ratios, the zeta potential values were also raised. The effect of the carboxylic moiety on the polymer that forms NPs may be the main cause of the resulting negative charge; regardless, these groups repel one another, resulting in a stable suspension that is evenly dispersed and less likely to clump . Uniformity of particle size and shape was also demonstrated by TEM as well as being evidenced by the low PDI results .
The compatibility study utilized DSC and FTIR. In terms of the DSC results, no change in the thermal behavior of the optimized NPs formula was seen as compared with the placebo, and no lina peak was observed. This suggests that complete incorporation and encapsulation of the lina. into the NP system was achieved . With respect to the FTIR, there was a clear lack of linagliptin's distinctive peaks, and no new peaks appeared in the FTIR spectrum in the optimized NP formulation. This indicated that the drug was found in its amorphous form in the formulation of the generated NPs, with no obvious chemical reactions between the drug and any other excipients. Fig. 8 (a) displays the plasma linagliptin calibration curve, with an R2 of 0.9993 demonstrating excellent linearity over the measured concentration range (0.5-50 g/ml).

This offers a clear advantage in the injectable lina nano formula as compared to the standard oral lina oral formula .
As illustrated in Fig. 9, the pharmacodynamics results represent the influence of the nano preparation of lina on the blood glucose levels on diabetic rats, including a significant promising decrease. These results support the hypothesis that the antidiabetic activity of the injectable lina PLGA loaded nanoparticle formulation is enhanced as compared to that provided by the conventional oral tablet, with the in-vivo study results offering agreement with work done by Gomaa et al. using same biodegradable polymer PLGA . .
Finally, Formulation of lina NPs injection with higher half life time, better pharmacokinetic properties with prolonged release profile & accepted patient compliance with lower side effects in comparison with other combination.

## Conclusion
To achieve our aim, a seven-day sustained release profile for lina NPs were prepared by single emulsion evaporation, an optimized NPs formula was selected (65:35, 150 mg of PLGA in 10% organic solvent). The results indicates that, the half-life of the selected formulation was significantly increased when tested in vivo (t1/2 = 231.6 +- 43.9 h vs. 11.8 +- 0.3 h for Lina NPs and lina Oral, respectively). The AUC0- was also significantly higher for the NP formulation of lina, launching from 33.73 to 60.53 ng/ml.h. Subcutaneous administration of 4 mg per kilogram of lina. nano preparation improved glycemic control in diabetic rats for 7 days. Therefore, it may be helpful in the management of diabetes mellitus to develop subcutaneous NPs formulations containing lina that provide prolonged release profiles especially in the form of injectable pre-filled syringe. It is probable that this intervention will enhance patient adherence and mitigate the adverse effects associated with repetitive administration of the same doses.
