## Abstract
Purpose Ursolic acid (UA), a pentacyclic triterpenoid extracted from plants, shows promising inhibitory effect in different
tumor bearing cell lines. In the present study we fabricated
UA loaded PLGA nanoparticles (UA-NPs) as the drug carrier
and thoroughly evaluated in vitro and in vivo the differential
tumor targeting effects of UA and UA-NPs in B16F10 melanoma cells.
Methods Ursolic acid loaded PLGA nanoparticles were prepared by emulsion solvent evaporation technique and evaluated for particle size, polydispersity, zeta potential and drug
release potency. MTT assay as well as flow cytometric and
confocal microscopic analyses were done in B16F10 mouse
melanoma cell lines. Formulations were labeled with
technetium-99m to evaluate the biodistribution and perform
scintigraphic imaging studies following intravenous administration in tumor bearing mice model.

Preparation of UA-NPs

UA-NPs were prepared by a single emulsion solvent evaporation technique as described previously with minor modification . In brief, UA (5 mg) was dissolved in acetone (1 mL)
with gentle heating, then cooled and treated with PLGA
(50 mg). The solution was added to an aqueous solution
(12 mL) of PVA (2%) and immediately sonicated (for 2 min)
under cold condition using a microtip probe sonicator set at
55 W of energy output (Labsonic Sonicator, B. Brown,
Germany). The oil-in water emulsion was allowed to stir at
room temperature for 4-5 h for the removal of acetone. The
prepared UA-NPs were recovered by ultracentrifugation
(30,000 rpm, 4degC) for 30 min (Sorvall Ultraspeed
Centrifuge, Kendro, USA) and washed thrice with double
distilled water to remove PVA and unencapsulated free UA.
The pellet obtained was lyophilized for 6 h to obtain a free
flowing powder.
Nanoparticles were also prepared by the multiple emulsification method . Briefly, UA (10 mg) was dissolved in 2 mL
of DCM-acetone mixture (1:1) by gentle heating; the solution
was cooled and treated with PLGA (100 mg). To this solution
0.5 mL of 3% PVA solution in water (w/v) was added
dropwise, and the mixture was homogenized at room temperature at a speed of 16,000 rpm for 4 min to form the primary
emulsion. This was added dropwise during 6 min to 75 mL of
1.5% (w/v) PVA solution in water with continuous homogenization using a homogenizer. The solution was allowed to stir
at room temperature for 3-4 h to remove organic solvents.
The emulsion was sonicated for 10 min in a bath sonicator
and filtered through filter paper (11 mm) to remove aggregates. Then the particles were separated by centrifugation at
16,000 rpm for 20 min at 4degC (Sorvall Ultraspeed Centrifuge,
Kendro, USA), washed twice with double distilled water to
remove the excess of PVA and free drug, and lyophilized for
further use.
Blank nanoparticles were produced in a similar manner
without adding drugs. FITC was used as a fluorescent marker;
0.1 mL of its stock solution (0.4% w/v in ethanol) was added
to the PVA solution during emulsification to obtain FITC
labeled UA-NPs. The prepared nanoparticles were lyophilized for further use.

Hemo-compatibility

The hemolytic potentials of UA-NPs were assessed after incubation with rat RBCs in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS).
About 3 mL of fresh blood was collected from rat (Sprague
Dawley) in an EDTA-tube and was centrifuged for 5 min
(1000 g) at 4degC. Red blood cells (RBCs) were separated from
the plasma and washed three times with PBS (pH 7.4) to
remove serum proteins if present. Then 100 mL of the suspension (1%) of RBC in PBS was plated into each well of the 96well plate and treated with the nanoparticle suspension with
increasing concentration of UA (0.78, 1.56, 3.12, 6.25, 12.5,
25, 50, or 100 mM). Wells containing only 1% Triton X-100
or PBS were used as positive control and blank respectively.
After incubation at 37degC for 1 h with gentle stirring, the
unlysed RBCs were separated by centrifugation at 10,000 g
for 5 min and the optical density (OD) of the supernatant was
measured at 570 nm. The value was compared with the OD
of the supernatant where full lysis (by Triton X-100) occurred
and the % lysis was calculated. The average value was obtained from triplicate assays.

Recent developments in nuclear medicine for oncology have
centered round investigations on novel specific tumortargeting radiopharmaceuticals for both cancer imaging and
therapy. Progress in nanomedicine has allowed the designing
of tumor-targeted nanocarriers that are able to deliver radionuclide payloads in a site or molecule selective manner to
improve the efficacy and safety of cancer imaging and therapy. These carriers include nanoparticles, liposomes, microparticles, micelles, dendrimers and hydrogels, among others.
Furthermore, use of these specific tumor-targeting carriers in
combination with PET/CT (positron emission tomography/
computed tomography) and SPECT/CT (combined single
photon emission computed tomography/computed tomography), the more recent nuclear imaging multimodalities which
provide high sensitivity and anatomical resolution, is highly
promising and will hopefully allow for earlier tumor detection,
better treatment planning and more effective therapy in the
near future .
Nowadays different natural products obtained from medicinal plants are being widely used for the treatment of different
chronic diseases. Developing therapeutically effective agents
from natural products may reduce risk of toxicity when the
drug is used clinically. UA, a pentacyclic triterpene (Fig. 1)
isolated in abundance from various natural sources, displays
a wide spectrum of pharmacological properties including antibacterial , antitumor , cytotoxic , and antiallergic 
activity. The promising tumor preventive activities of UA is
reported to be due to blocking of cell cycle progression in G1
phase triggering apoptosis as determined by DNA fragmentation . In vitro studies revealed that UA could effectively induce apoptosis of human gastric cancer cell line through inhibition of the expression of cyclooxygenase 2 and activation of
caspase 3 . Studies by other workers in this field also demonstrated the promising inhibitory effects on human colon
cancer cell lines , melanoma cell lines , endometrial cell
lines , etc. However clinical application of UA is restricted
because of its limited water solubility leading to poor pharmacokinetics and low bioavailability. Therefore it is necessary to

Radiolabeling of UA and UA- NPs

UA and UA-NPs were radiolabeled with technetium (99mTc)
by direct labeling approach using stannous chloride dihydrate
as reductant as per the reported method with some modification . Briefly, aqueous pertechnetate (100-150 MBq/mL)
was added separately to UA solution (2 mg in 0.5 mL ethanol)
and UA-NPs suspension (equivalent to 0.16 mg UA in 0.5 mL
water), and 30 mL of freshly prepared stannous chloride
dihydrate solution (20 mg in 10 mL nitrogen purged water)
was added to each. These were incubated at room temperature under inert atmosphere for 15 min and stored in this
condition for further studies. Radiolabeling efficiency was determined by ascending thin layer chromatography (TLC)
using 2.5 x 10 cm silica gel coated aluminium sheet as stationary phase. The plates were spotted with labeled compounds
and developed in 100% acetone as mobile phase. The strip
was cut into five parts and the radioactivity in each part was
determined in a well type gamma scintillation counter
(Electronic Corporation of India, Model LV4755,
Hyderabad, India) to ascertain the free pertechnetate present
in the sample.

to higher tumor uptake of the nanoparticulated formulation.
Neither of the radiolabeled formulations exhibited any significant uptake in stomach during the period of study, indicating
no significant decomposition of the complex in animal system.
The relatively high tumor uptake and significantly high
tumor muscle ratio of the radiolabeled UA-NPs resulted in
