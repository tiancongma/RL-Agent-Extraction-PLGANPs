ABSTRACT
Purpose Ursolic acid (UA), a pentacyclic triterpenoid extract-
ed from plants, shows promising inhibitory effect in different
tumor bearing cell lines. In the present study we fabricated
UA loaded PLGA nanoparticles (UA-NPs) as the drug carrier
and thoroughly evaluated in vitro and in vivo the differential
tumor targeting effects of UA and UA-NPs in B16F10 mela-
noma cells.
Methods Ursolic acid loaded PLGA nanoparticles were pre-
pared by emulsion solvent evaporation technique and evalu-
ated for particle size, polydispersity, zeta potential and drug
release potency. MTT assay as well as flow cytometric and
confocal microscopic analyses were done in B16F10 mouse
melanoma cell lines. Formulations were labeled with
technetium-99m to evaluate the biodistribution and perform
scintigraphic imaging studies following intravenous adminis-
tration in tumor bearing mice model.
Results Single emulsification technique produced smooth
spherical nanoparticles of small size with relatively narrow size
distribution (154 ± 4.56 nm). On B16F10 cell line, the formu-
lation showed higher cytotoxicity compared to the free drug
due to increased in vitro cellular uptake. The formulation was
successfully radiolabeled and remained substantially (>90%)
stable when incubated (37°C, 6 h) separately in normal saline
or freshly collected rat serum or histidine solution. The
radiolabeled UA-NPs exhibited slower blood clearance and
comparatively high uptake in tumor region as evidenced by
biodistribution and scintigraphic studies.
Conclusions The in vitro and in vivo studies have proved the tumor
targeting potential of UA-NPs in B16F10 melanoma cell lines.

INTRODUCTION
Recent developments in nuclear medicine for oncology have
centered round investigations on novel specific tumor-
targeting radiopharmaceuticals for both cancer imaging and
therapy. Progress in nanomedicine has allowed the designing
of tumor-targeted nanocarriers that are able to deliver radio-
nuclide payloads in a site or molecule selective manner to
improve the efficacy and safety of cancer imaging and thera-
py. These carriers include nanoparticles, liposomes, micropar-
ticles, micelles, dendrimers and hydrogels, among others.
Furthermore, use of these specific tumor-targeting carriers in
combination with PET/CT (positron emission tomography/
computed tomography) and SPECT/CT (combined single
photon emission computed tomography/computed tomogra-
phy), the more recent nuclear imaging multimodalities which
provide high sensitivity and anatomical resolution, is highly
promising and will hopefully allow for earlier tumor detection,
better treatment planning and more effective therapy in the
near future (1).
Nowadays different natural products obtained from medic-
inal plants are being widely used for the treatment of different
chronic diseases. Developing therapeutically effective agents
from natural products may reduce risk of toxicity when the
drug is used clinically. UA, a pentacyclic triterpene (Fig. 1)
isolated in abundance from various natural sources, displays
a wide spectrum of pharmacological properties including an-
tibacterial (2), antitumor (3), cytotoxic (4), and antiallergic (5)
activity. The promising tumor preventive activities of UA is
reported to be due to blocking of cell cycle progression in G1
phase triggering apoptosis as determined by DNA fragmenta-
tion (6). In vitro studies revealed that UA could effectively in-
duce apoptosis of human gastric cancer cell line through inhi-
bition of the expression of cyclooxygenase 2 and activation of
caspase 3 (7). Studies by other workers in this field also dem-
onstrated the promising inhibitory effects on human colon
cancer cell lines (8), melanoma cell lines (9), endometrial cell
lines (10), etc. However clinical application of UA is restricted
because of its limited water solubility leading to poor pharma-
cokinetics and low bioavailability. Therefore it is necessary to
develop effective drug delivery systems to improve the thera-
peutic efficacy of UA.
There are several approaches to improve drug bioavail-
ability. Among these, nanoencapsulation using PLGA (Poly-
lactic-co-glycolic acid), a biocompatible polymeric
nanocarrier, is a promising one. PLGA is a well-known bio-
degradable and biocompatible polymer approved by the US
Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and the European
Medicine Agency (EMA). It has been used in a variety of
biomedical devices and tissue engineering scaffolds. Different
therapeutics have been encapsulated in PLGA nano- and mi-
croparticles for several purposes such as vaccination or car-
diovascular and cancer treatments (11). PLGA-NPs bind
drugs with poor solubility and extravasation through the tu-
mor vasculature by the enhanced permeability and retention
effect. The objective of this study was to develop, for the first
time, UA-NPs and test its tumor targeting potential in B16F10
tumor bearing animal model. The nanoparticles were charac-
terized in terms of size and charge by transmission electron
microscopy (TEM), scanning electron microscopy (SEM), and
dynamic light scattering (DLS). Drug loading, entrapment
efficiency, in vitro release profile and interactions with the poly-
mer were also investigated. In vitro cytotoxicity study was per-
formed in B16F10 melanoma cell line by MTT assay and
cellular uptake was carried by confocal microscopy and flow
cytometric analysis. The free drug UA and the UA-NPs were
radiolabeled with technetium-99m by direct radiolabeling ap-
proach using stannous chloride dihydrate as reductant and
radiolabeled UA-NPs was physicochemically characterized.
Biodistribution studies in B16F10 tumor bearing Balb/c mice
were done using optimized technetium-99 m labeled UA-
NPs. Gamma scintigraphy imaging of the tumor bearing mice
was carried out after injecting radiolabeled nanoparticles to
ascertain the localization in tumor. The results are discussed in
the following sections.

Preparation of UA-NPs
UA-NPs were prepared by a single emulsion solvent evapora-
tion technique as described previously with minor modifica-
tion (12). In brief, UA (5 mg) was dissolved in acetone (1 mL)
with gentle heating, then cooled and treated with PLGA
(50 mg). The solution was added to an aqueous solution
(12 mL) of PVA (2%) and immediately sonicated (for 2 min)
under cold condition using a microtip probe sonicator set at
55 W of energy output (Labsonic Sonicator, B. Brown,
Germany). The oil-in water emulsion was allowed to stir at
room temperature for 4–5 h for the removal of acetone. The
prepared UA-NPs were recovered by ultracentrifugation
(30,000 rpm, 4°C) for 30 min (Sorvall Ultraspeed
Centrifuge, Kendro, USA) and washed thrice with double
distilled water to remove PVA and unencapsulated free UA.
The pellet obtained was lyophilized for 6 h to obtain a free
flowing powder.
Nanoparticles were also prepared by the multiple emulsifi-
cation method (13). Briefly, UA (10 mg) was dissolved in 2 mL
of DCM-acetone mixture (1:1) by gentle heating; the solution
was cooled and treated with PLGA (100 mg). To this solution
0.5 mL of 3% PVA solution in water (w/v) was added
dropwise, and the mixture was homogenized at room temper-
ature at a speed of 16,000 rpm for 4 min to form the primary
emulsion. This was added dropwise during 6 min to 75 mL of
1.5% (w/v) PVA solution in water with continuous homoge-
nization using a homogenizer. The solution was allowed to stir
at room temperature for 3–4 h to remove organic solvents.
The emulsion was sonicated for 10 min in a bath sonicator
and filtered through filter paper (11 μm) to remove aggre-
gates. Then the particles were separated by centrifugation at
16,000 rpm for 20 min at 4°C (Sorvall Ultraspeed Centrifuge,
Kendro, USA), washed twice with double distilled water to
remove the excess of PVA and free drug, and lyophilized for
further use.
Blank nanoparticles were produced in a similar manner
without adding drugs. FITC was used as a fluorescent marker;
0.1 mL of its stock solution (0.4% w/v in ethanol) was added
to the PVA solution during emulsification to obtain FITC
labeled UA-NPs. The prepared nanoparticles were lyophi-
lized for further use.
Characterization of Nanoparticles
Particle Size Distribution and Zeta Potential Analysis
The lyophilized UA-NPs were reconstituted with Milli-Q wa-
ter prior to the measurements. The average particle size, size
distribution, and polydispersity index (PDI) of the prepared
nanoparticles were determined by Photon Correlation
Spectroscopy using a Malvern NanoZS (Malvern
Instruments, Worcestershire, United Kingdom). Zeta
Potential was measured at 25°C using the same instrument.
All the measurements were performed in triplicate; average
values of three experiments were calculated and reported.
Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM)
One drop of UA-NPs suspension (1 mg/0.5 mL Milli-Q wa-
ter) was placed over a carbon coated copper grid and dried in
air; the images were visualized using transmission electron
microscope (FEI, Tecnai G2 Spirit Bio TWIN,
Czech Republic) operated at about 210–240 V with a fre-
quency range of 50–60 Hz .
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)
The surface morphology of UA-NPs was also assessed by
SEM (Jeol JSM-6360, Japan) operated at 20 kV accelerating
voltage. The lyophilized nanoparticles were placed on a car-
bon tab mounted on a SEM specimen stub coated with pal-
ladium before observation.
Drug Loading and Encapsulation Efficiency of UA-NPs
The concentration of UA in nanoparticle formulation was de-
termined using a Waters Associates HPLC system, equipped
with a Waters 2487 dual wave length absorbance detector and
a XTerra RP-18 analytical column (5 μm, 250 × 4.6 mm). The
mobile phase consisted of methanol:acetonitrile (80:20), the
column was eluted at a flow rate of 0.6 ml/min, and monitoring
was done at 210 nm. An aliquot of UA-NPs suspension was
added to a mixture of methanol : acetonitrile (1:1, 2 mL),
vortexed, sonicated, and centrifuged; the clear supernatant
was filtered and analysed by HPLC. Measurement was done
using a standard solution of UA in methanol (5–100 μg/mL).

Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FTIR)
To determine the possible chemical interaction occurring be-
tween the drug and the polymer matrix, FTIR spectra of UA,
UA-NPs and PLGA in the range of 400–4000 cm−1 were
studied employing an FTIR spectrophotometer (Bruker,
Tensor-27).
DSC Experiments
Physicochemical compatibility between the drug and the poly-
mer matrix was determined using a differential scanning cal-
orimeter (Pyris Diamond TG/DTA, Perkin Elmer,
Singapore). For this study, accurately weighed samples were
placed in a platinum crucible and heated over a temperature
range from 0 to 320°C at a constantly increasing rate of
10°C/min in a nitrogen gas environment with a flow rate of
150 mL/min.
X-ray Diffraction Study
The X-ray powder diffraction (XRD) patterns of free UA and
UA-NPs were obtained with a X-ray diffractometer (Model
D8, Bruker AXS, Germany) using CuKα radiation (40 Kv,
30 mA).
In vitro Drug Release
UA-NPs (10 mg) were dispersed in 10 mL of phosphate-
buffered saline containing Tween 80 (50 μL) and incubated
at 37°C in an orbital shaker at 120 rpm. At predetermined
time intervals, the tube was centrifuged; then 1 mL of super-
natant was withdrawn and replaced with same volume of re-
lease media. The collected samples were suitably diluted (if
necessary) and analyzed by HPLC as described in
Section 2.3.4.
Long-term Stability Study
UA-NPs prepared by single emulsion solvent evaporation
technique were stored at 4 ± 2°C for 3 months. After storage
for 3 months samples were analysed to determine particle size
and zeta potential value as per methods described earlier.
Experiments were done in triplicate.
In vitro Cytotoxicity Studies
The effect of free UA and UA-NPs on the viability of B16F10
cell line was determined by MTT [3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-
yl)-2,5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide] assay (14). The B16F10
mouse melanoma cells were seeded in 96 well plates contain-
ing growth medium (1 × 104 cells per well) and incubated (for
24 and 48 h) at 37°C in humidified air containing 5% CO2.
The cells were exposed to blank nanoparticles, free UA, and
UA-NPs containing UA in different concentrations (5, 25, 50,
75, 100 μM). After 24 and 48 h of incubation, media contain-
ing the drug was removed. Then 10 μL of MTT solution
(5 mg/mL in PBS) and 90 μL of medium were added to each
well, incubated for another 4 h before the addition of
dimethylsulfoxide (150 μL), and the optical density (OD) at
540 nm was measured using a microplate reader (GENios,
Austria). Cytotoxicity was expressed in terms of IC50 calculat-
ed from the cell viability data representing the drug concen-
tration in which cell growth was inhibited by 50%.
In vitro Cellular Uptake
Confocal Microscopy
Cellular internalisation of FITC loaded UA-NPs was investi-
gated by means of confocal laser scanning microscopy (Andor
spinning disc confocal microscope, UK) (15). B16F10 mouse
melanoma cells (1 × 105) were incubated in cover slips placed
inside 35 mm cell culture dish. After 80% confluence was
reached in each dish, the medium was replaced with FITC
loaded UA-NPs (equivalent to 25 μg UA per dish) and incu-
bated at 37°C for 4 or 8 h. After the medium was removed,
the cells were washed with PBS (three times) and subjected to
nuclear staining with DAPI (4,6-diamino-2-phenylindole) for
5 min. The cells were again washed with PBS (two times) and
the cover slips were mounted with 60% glycerol mounting
medium on a slide. These were viewed and imaged under
confocal laser scanning microscope using FITC filter (λex
492, λem 518 nm).
Fluorescence Activated Cell Sorting (FACS)
Flow cytometric analysis was used to quantitate the cellular
uptake of fluorescently labeled UA-NPs in B16F10 melanoma
cells (16). Fluorescent nanoparticles were prepared by incor-
porating FITC in UA-NPs as described earlier (materials and
methods section). Six well plates were seeded with 1 × 106 cells
per well and the cells were incubated at 37°C for 24 h to allow
cell attachment. Nanoparticles (25 μg/mL UA concentration)
were added to the cells and incubated for 0.5, 1, 2, 3 and 4 h.
After the incubation period, media were removed and the
cells were washed twice with phosphate buffer saline (PBS)
to remove all the unbound nanoparticles. These were then
harvested by trypsinization and centrifuged at 254 g for
2 min at 4°C. Finally, the cells were resuspended in 500 μl
of PBS and stored on ice until analysis. The percentage of
fluorescent cells in the population and the fluorescence inten-
sity were measured using a flow cytometer (FACS Canto II™
cell sorter, BD Biosciences) using FACS Diva software (BD
Biosciences). The increase of fluorescence in the cells treated
with nanoparticles relative to that in the untreated control

cells incubated with particles and that of the untreated cells.
Flow cytometer channel voltage and gain were maintained

Hemo-compatibility
The hemolytic potentials of UA-NPs were assessed after incu-
bation with rat RBCs in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS).
About 3 mL of fresh blood was collected from rat (Sprague
Dawley) in an EDTA-tube and was centrifuged for 5 min
(1000 g) at 4°C. Red blood cells (RBCs) were separated from
the plasma and washed three times with PBS (pH 7.4) to
remove serum proteins if present. Then 100 μL of the suspen-
sion (1%) of RBC in PBS was plated into each well of the 96-
well plate and treated with the nanoparticle suspension with
increasing concentration of UA (0.78, 1.56, 3.12, 6.25, 12.5,
25, 50, or 100 μM). Wells containing only 1% Triton X-100
or PBS were used as positive control and blank respectively.
After incubation at 37°C for 1 h with gentle stirring, the
unlysed RBCs were separated by centrifugation at 10,000 g
for 5 min and the optical density (OD) of the supernatant was
measured at 570 nm. The value was compared with the OD
of the supernatant where full lysis (by Triton X-100) occurred
and the % lysis was calculated. The average value was obtain-
ed from triplicate assays.
Radiolabeling of UA and UA- NPs
UA and UA-NPs were radiolabeled with technetium (99mTc)
by direct labeling approach using stannous chloride dihydrate
as reductant as per the reported method with some modifica-
tion (17). Briefly, aqueous pertechnetate (100–150 MBq/mL)
was added separately to UA solution (2 mg in 0.5 mL ethanol)
and UA-NPs suspension (equivalent to 0.16 mg UA in 0.5 mL
water), and 30 μL of freshly prepared stannous chloride
dihydrate solution (20 mg in 10 mL nitrogen purged water)
was added to each. These were incubated at room tempera-
ture under inert atmosphere for 15 min and stored in this
condition for further studies. Radiolabeling efficiency was de-
termined by ascending thin layer chromatography (TLC)
using 2.5 × 10 cm silica gel coated aluminium sheet as station-
ary phase. The plates were spotted with labeled compounds
and developed in 100% acetone as mobile phase. The strip
was cut into five parts and the radioactivity in each part was
determined in a well type gamma scintillation counter
(Electronic Corporation of India, Model LV4755,
Hyderabad, India) to ascertain the free pertechnetate present
in the sample.
In vitro Stability Studies of Radiolabeled UA and UA-NPs
In vitro stability of 99mTc-labeled UA and UA-NPs was deter-
mined in normal saline, freshly collected rat serum and histi-
dine solution (10−2 M) as per the previously reported method
(18). Briefly, the radiolabeled complex (0.1 mL) was mixed
with 0.9 mL of normal saline, serum or histidine solution
and incubated at 37°C for 24 h. The samples were withdrawn
at regular intervals (1, 2, 4, 6 and 24 h) to check for any
dissociation of the labeled complex by ascending thin layer
chromatography as described above.
In vitro Cell Binding and Internalisation Study
The ability of 99mTc-labeled UA and UA-NPs to bind and
internalize in B16F10 mouse melanoma cell lines was studied
following the reported method (19). Briefly, one million cells
per well were cultured with DMEM medium at 37°C for 24 h.
After the addition of about 40 kBq of 99mTc labeled UA-NPs
to the medium, the cells were incubated at 37°C in 5% CO2
for different periods of time. Cellular internalisation was
stopped at various time points (0.5, 1, 2, 4 h) by removal of
the medium followed by washing twice with ice-cold phos-
phate-buffered saline (pH 7.2). Cell surface-bound
radioligand was removed by washing the cells with ice cold
acidic buffer (0.05 mol/L glycine solution, pH adjusted to 2.8
with 1N HCl) for 5 min at 0°C. The cell-associated radioac-
tivity that was not removed by this procedure was used as the
internalized radioactivity. Finally, cells were treated with 1 N
NaOH at 37°C for 10 min to detach them from plates and
centrifuged. The supernatant was collected and the radioac-
tivity of every fraction was measured on a γ-counter. The
percent of internalized (cell pellet) radioactivity was calculated
by dividing it by the total radioactivity associated with the cells
(surface-bound + internalized) and multiplying by 100. All
experiments were repeated thrice and the results were
expressed as the mean ± standard deviation.
In vivo Evaluation
Blood Clearance Study in Rats
Blood clearance of 99mTc–labeled UA and UA-NPs was stud-
ied in healthy Sprague Dawley rats weighing 275–300 g (n = 3)
(20). To the anaesthetized animal, radiolabeled complex (2–
4 MBq, 0.05 mL) was administered through precannulated
femoral vein. Blood samples (0.2 mL) were withdrawn from
the other precannulated femoral vein at different time inter-
vals ranging from 2 min - 4 h. The samples were weighed and
counted in the gamma scintillation counter. The percent
injected dose/g (% ID/g) of blood sample at each time interval
was calculated and plotted against respective time interval to
give the blood disappearance curve.

Biodistribution Study in B16F10 Tumor Bearing Mice
The biodistributions of 99mTc–labeled UA and UA-NPs were
evaluated in B16F10 tumor bearing mice (Balb/c strain, male,
body weight 25–30 g). The tumor model was developed by
intramuscularly injecting freshly collected B16F10 cells
(2 × 107 cells) suspended in saline to the thigh of the right hind
leg of each mouse. A palpable tumor was developed after 15–
20 days and the animals were used for biodistribution studies.
The animals were well hydrated by intraperitoneal adminis-
tration of saline (0.9%, 2 mL) for 1 h followed by the injection
of 99mTc-labeled compounds (0.05 mL, 10–12 MBq/kg) via
tail vein. At 4 and 6 h post injection, the animals were eutha-
nized and blood was collected by cardiac puncture into pre
weighed counting vials. The animals were then dissected and
other tissues (liver, spleen, lungs, intestines, kidney, heart,
stomach) were excised, washed with normal saline to free from
adhering tissues, and transferred into counting vials. The sam-
ples were counted (gamma scintillation counter) against suit-
able diluted aliquot of the injected solution as standard and
the results were expressed either as % ID/g of tissue or % ID/
organ. The national regulations for the care and use of labo-
ratory animals have been observed in this study according to
the protocol approved by the Social Justice and
Empowerment Committee for the Purpose of Control and
Supervision of Experiments on Animals (CPCSEA),
Government of India, New Delhi.
Scintigraphic Imaging Studies
99mTc–labeled UA and UA-NPs (50 μL, 4–5 MBq/animal)
were injected via tail vein to well hydrated, B16F10 melanoma
tumor bearing Balb/c mice. The animals were anaesthetized
(intramuscular ketamine injection), scintigraphic imaging
studies were performed under GE Infinia Gamma Camera
equipped with Xeleris Work Station, and static images were
acquired at 4 h post-injection.
Statistical Analysis
The data are presented as a mean of at least three indepen-
dent experiments, with error bars indicating the standard de-
viations. Statistical analysis was performed by one-way
ANOVA. The p-values of the experimental results were found
to be <0.05 (statistically significant).
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
Encapsulation of UA in UA-NPs
In recent years various attempts have been made by many
researchers in the development of novel drug delivery systems
using nanoparticles to minimize toxicity, increase stability and
specificity, increase bioavailability, and achieve sustained re-
lease of different hydrophilic and hydrophobic drugs.
Depending on the physicochemical nature of the polymer
and the drug to be loaded, several techniques have been
adopted for the preparation of nanoparticles. Among them,
the emulsion solvent evaporation method is most frequently
used for hydrophobic drugs. In this study, the solution of UA
and PLGA in acetone was sonicated for emulsification into an
aqueous phase containing PVA as surfactant. If performed
under cold condition, sonication induces an increase in tem-
perature and restores the integrity of drug molecule. After
solvent evaporation, the precipitated nanoparticles were
washed several times with distilled water to remove the sur-
factant and unencapsulated free drug. Multiple emulsion sol-
vent evaporation was also employed to prepare UA-NPs.
Characterisation of UA-NPs
Table I shows the mean particle size, yield, encapsulation
efficiency, drug loading and zeta potential of UA-NPs. The
average size of the particles as determined by DLS experiment
was 154 ± 4.56 nm when prepared by single emulsion solvent
evaporation and 295 ± 9.23 nm if prepared by multiple emul-
sion solvent evaporation method. The latter were compara-
tively bigger in size, and thus exhibited higher drug loading
and encapsulation efficiency in comparison to the former. The
yield of the nanoparticles as obtained by two different emul-
sification techniques was comparable.
Nanoparticles exhibited narrow size distribution (Fig. 2a)
with polydispersity index (PDI) of 0.29 when prepared by
single emulsification technique but comparatively broad par-
ticle size distribution with PDI of 1.0 if obtained by multiple
emulsification technique. The zeta potential value of nanopar-
ticles was approximately −18.4 mV when they were prepared
by single emulsification method (Fig. 2b), but −12.2 mV
(approx.) when prepared by multiple emulsification method.
Comparatively higher zeta potential values obtained in the
former process reveals strong electrostatic repulsion which
minimizes aggregation and renders stability to the formula-
tion. Single emulsification technique produced smooth spher-
ical UA-NPs of comparatively small size with relatively nar-
row size distribution; these were used for further characteriza-
tion and evaluation.
The UA-NPs formulation was stored at 4 ± 2°C for
3 months, particle size, zeta potential and PDI were evaluated.
The particle size of nanoparticles slightly increased to 164 nm
and subsequent rise in polydispersity index. This could be due
to the formation of aggregates during storage but change was
not very significant. The zeta potential of UA-NPs prepared
by single emulsion solvent evaporation technique also did not
change significantly during storage.

SEM characterization (Fig. 2c) of UA-NPs (prepared by
single emulsion solvent evaporation method) revealed that
particles were smooth and spherical. UA-NPs morphology
was also evaluated by TEM imaging (Fig. 2d), which showed
that NPs were almost spherical with a size of about 100 nm
and corroborates the results (154 nm) obtained by DLS exper-
iments. Inner portion of the spherical particles appeared dark
which could be due to drug loading.
Figure 3 exhibits the FTIR spectra of blank PLGA, free
UA, the physical mixture, and UA-NPs. The free UA sample
shows an intense sharp peak at 1690 cm−1 corresponding to
the carbonyl group and a broad band at 3429 cm−1 corre-
sponding to hydroxyl function. The spectrum of PLGA con-
tains a peak at 1757 cm−1 which is attributed to carbonyl
group stretch and at 1457 cm−1 for C-H stretching. The spec-
trum of the physical mixture displays peaks resulting from
simple superposition of IR spectra of free UA and PLGA. In
the case of UA-NPs, the peaks of UA were not very intense;
this could be due to the relatively low drug concentration in
the nanoparticles. The spectral analysis did not reveal any
major shift or loss of functional group peaks. This study sug-
gests that during nanoencapsulation there was no chemical
interaction between drug and polymer, as the characteristic

In vitro Drug Release Study
The drug release profile of UA-NPs is shown in Fig. 5a. The
graph represents a biphasic release pattern with an initial
burst effect followed by a sustained and controlled release.
Initial burst release was observed during the first 4 h showing
nearly 30% release of encapsulated UA. This rapid release
continued for almost 24 h and was accompanied by slow
and sustained release of UA from UA-NPs. Around 70.66
± 4.186% of UA was released from nanoencapsulation over
the period of 15 days, thus providing a controlled and
sustained release pattern.
Hemolysis Assay
To assess the in vivo utility of nanoformulation as a carrier for
UA, the haemolytic activity of UA-NPs in different concen-
tration ranges was determined. Nanoparticle formulations
containing UA in increasing concentration ranges (0.78 to
100 μM) were used for study. The results showed negligible
hemolytic activity in the formulations containing UA between
0.78 to 25 μM, whereas detectable percentage of hemolysis
(12–18% at 1 h incubation) was observed at higher concen-
tration ranges (50 to 100 μM) of UA (Fig. 5b). UA-NPs did not
exhibit any significant hemolysis in the tested concentration
range. From the results of the assay, it could be concluded that
UA-NPs may be used safely for intravenous delivery of UA.

The Cytotoxic Activity
UA-NPs, free UA, and drug free PLGA NPs were evaluated
by assessing cell viability using MTT assay on B16F10 mouse
melanoma cell line. The study was conducted using different
concentrations (ranging between 5 and 100 μM) of drug load-
ed nanoparticles containing UA. Two sets of experiments
were done for 24 and 48 h durations. The effect was more
pronounced after 48 h incubation. Cytotoxicity was expressed
in terms of % cell viability against concentration. Both free UA
and UA-NPs displayed concentration dependent cytoxicity
(Fig. 7a). The IC50 values of free UA and UA-NPs were 60
and 18 μM respectively following 48 h incubation with the cell
line (n = 3). UA-NPs exhibited dose-dependent activity in
comparison to free drug. A higher concentration of UA may
be delivered to the intracellular space due to small size of the
nanoparticle. Sustained release of the drug from inner poly-
mer matrix of nanoparticulated formulation resulted pro-
nounced cytotoxic effect (21). Better efficacy and lower IC50
were observed for UA-NPs whereas no cellular cytotoxic ef-
fects were observed when drug free PLGA NPs were exposed
to cell line; this confirms the safe nature of the copolymer.

CONCLUSION
Nanoparticulated drug delivery systems have recently
attracted considerable attraction for targeted drug deliv-
ery of various anticancer drugs ranging from synthetic
compounds to natural products. However the evaluation
of their biological performance is still highly challenging.
Anticancer potentials of UA has been reported by var-
ious workers in this field. We reported an efficient UA
delivery system using the biocompatible polymer PLGA
as the drug carrier. Flow cytometric analysis as well as
confocal microscopic studies revealed substantial cellular
uptake (B16F10 cell line) of UA-NPs. In vitro drug re-
lease study revealed slow and sustained release of the
drug which could be exploited for potential therapy.
MTT assay revealed the pronounced cytotoxic effect of
the formulation. All these proved the clinical signifi-
cance of the UA loaded nanoparticulated drug delivery
system. The tumor targetability of the nanoformulation
was verified by 99mTc-labeling. Significant tumor uptake
and gradual washout indicate the potentiality of the
formulation for cancer therapy.
