Rationally developed core–shell polymeric-lipid hybrid nanoparticles as a delivery vehicle for cromolyn sodium: implications of lipid envelop on in vitro and in vivo behaviour of nanoparticles upon oral administration †
Ravi R. Patel , Gayasuddin Khan , Sundeep Chaurasia , Nagendra Kumar and Brahmeshwar Mishra * Department of Pharmaceutics, Indian Institute of Technology (Banaras Hindu University), Varanasi-221005, U.P., India. E-mail: bmishrabhu@rediffmail.com ;  Fax: +91 542 2368428;   Tel: +91 542 6702748
First published on 9th September 2015
Abstract
In the present study, cromolyn sodium (CS), a highly water soluble molecule was encapsulated into rationally designed, core–shell polymeric-lipid hybrid nanoparticles (PLHNs) for enhancing its oral bioavailability, by improving its intestinal permeability through lymphatic uptake. The CS encapsulated PLGA–lecithin based core–shell PLHNs (CS–PLHNs) were engineered by a double emulsification solvent evaporation method and optimized using a response surface methodology based “Quality by Design” approach. The Box–Behnken experimental design was imperatively enforced to enhance encapsulation of CS inside PLHNs without compromising particle size. Optimized CS–PLHNs exhibited a particle size of 227 ± 3.8 nm and EE of 57.8 ± 1.32% with unimodal size distribution. The physico-chemical characterizations of CS–PLHNs suggested the encapsulation of CS in an amorphous form inside PLHNs without any interactions. The morphological studies pointed towards the existence of smooth, spherical core–shell architecture of CS–PLHNs, which extended release up to 48 h by a controlled diffusion process. The optimized CS–PLHNs exhibited remarkable stability at different environmental conditions as well as in biological milieu . An ex vivo intestinal permeation study showed that the permeation of CS was significantly improved by encapsulating it inside PLHNs compared to that of pure CS, which was additionally confirmed by the in vivo intestinal uptake study using confocal microscopy. A pharmacokinetic study in rats further exhibited 11.9 fold enhancement in the oral bioavailability of CS after its incorporation into PLHNs. In a nutshell, PLHNs can serve as a superior therapeutic carrier system by imparting lipophilicity to potentially obtain the high oral bioavailability of CS, which can further be extended towards numerous hydrophilic drug molecules.
1. Introduction
Over the past decade, different nano-scale vehicles in size range of 10–1000 nm, have already been proven to be an efficient approach for oral delivery of various therapeutic molecules. 9 Amongst them, more attention has been paid to the polymeric nanoparticles 9,10 solid lipid nanoparticles 11,12 and liposomes 13 for enhancing the absorption across the GIT on account of their ability to permeate through the gut wall barrier by specific absorption mechanisms. 10,13,14 Unfortunately, CS being a hydrophilic molecule, the encapsulation inside polymeric nanoparticles as well as solid lipid nanoparticles is foremost challenge, owing to its rapid partitioning into external aqueous phase during the preparation. 10,12,15 Alternatively, liposomes have shown tremendous potential to encapsulate hydrophilic drugs into the aqueous core with high loading. It has also been reported that liposomal encapsulation imparts lipophilicity through lipid bilayer, which could facilitate the passive transport of hydrophilic drug across the GIT barrier. 4,16 However, oral delivery potential of liposome is limited by their erratic and unpredictable absorption profile as well as inability to retain their structural integrity at absorption site. 3,17
In order to circumvent these thorny limitations of existing delivery systems, an advanced, polymeric-lipid hybrid nanoparticles (PLHNs) based tunable, robust architecture has been impressively proposed, in which a lipid layer envelopes the polymeric core. PLHNs are colloidal hybrid carrier systems, which comprise of positive attributes of both, liposomes as well as polymeric nanoparticles in a single vehicle. 18 The polymeric core confers the structural integrity to the PLHNs whereas the lipid envelop imparts the biomimetic shield. The enhanced encapsulation yield for hydrophilic drug could be anticipated by lipidic monolayer coating over the surface of the polymeric core during the preparation. It has also been reported that molecular barrier conferred by lipid, impedes the faster diffusion of hydrophilic drug into an external aqueous phase and hence, potentially increases drug encapsulation yield. 19 Literature survey reveals that number of PLHNs has been prepared to enhance the encapsulation of hydrophilic drug, compared to plain polymeric nanoparticles without any lipidic surfactant. 14,19 Moreover, PLHNs have also shown prolongation of the drug release owing to their core–shell type of structure, which restricts inward penetration rate of water. 20 Previous studies have described the use of PLHNs for enhancing the oral permeability as well as bioavailability. 14,21,22 The enhanced oral permeation of PLHNs is attributed to their absorption through paracellular and transcellular route via enterocytes as well as selective uptake by M-cells of Peyer's Patches (PP). 21,22 To our knowledge, there have been no reports available in literature pertaining oral bioavailability enhancement of CS, by preparing nanocarrier system till date.
In this perspective, present study was aimed at the utilization of the potential of PLHNs for oral delivery of CS, which would encapsulate the hydrophilic CS with high payload and would improve the GIT permeation of CS via selective absorption by M cells of PP as well as endocytic uptake through enterocytes, resulting in an enhanced bioavailability. CS encapsulated PLGA–lecithin based core–shell PLHNs (CS–PLHNs) were prepared by double emulsification solvent evaporation method (W 1 /O/W 2 ) and optimized using “Quality by Design” approach to meet the critical quality attributes for the final product. A 3-level, 4-factor, Box–Behnken experimental design (BBD) was employed to investigate the main and interactive influence of independent variables on physicochemical properties of CS–PLHNs. Desirability based numerical optimization technique was utilized for optimization of CS–PLHNs. The optimized CS–PLHNs were characterized for their various solid-state properties, morphological properties and evaluated for in vitro drug release study as well as stability study. Ex vivo and in vivo intestinal permeation study were executed to assess the permeation performance of CS–PLHNs by confocal microscopy. Eventually, pharmacokinetic study was conducted to evaluate the potential of PLHNs as an effective oral delivery carrier system for CS.

Statistical analysis revealed that lipid/polymer ratio ( X 1 ) and concentration of polymer ( X 4 ) affects positively whereas concentration of surfactant ( X 2 ) and organic phase/aqueous phase ratio ( X 3 ) provides negative effect on particle size ( Fig. 1(A)–(D) ). The lipid/polymer ratio ( X 1 ) appeared to be one of the most dominant variables amongst all, which positively influences particle size owing to its direct effect on viscosity. The significant increment in particle size at higher lipid/polymer ratio could be attributed to the reduction of the shearing efficiency of stirrer at higher viscosity which resulted in thick covering of the polymeric core by lipid coat. Additionally, higher lipid/polymer ratio also favors the formation of liposome due to the presence of high amount of lipid, above its critical micellar concentration and coexistence of liposomes with PLHNs might be responsible for measuring the enhanced particle size. 18 Contrary, the remarkable drop off in the particle size was noticed at higher surfactant concentration ( X 2 ), which might be due to reduction of the interfacial tension between the dispersed phase and dispersion medium. 31 Moreover, high surfactant concentration also offers droplet stabilization against coalescence during the emulsification process by interfacial localization and protects from impinging with each other in order to grow into bigger ones. 32 Similarly, organic phase/aqueous phase ratio ( X 3 ) showed inverse relationship with particle size. This could be attributed to the fact that large amount of organic solvent would decrease the viscosity and thereby, impart high shear stress, which would result in breakdown of emulsion droplets without any coalescence. 23,33 The particle size increased significantly with increase in polymer concentration ( X 4 ) might be because of triggering of the polymer–polymer interaction coupled with viscosity of the dispersed phase, which would result in formation of coarse dispersion with marked density difference. According to Stoke's low, the resultant density difference propels particle collisions and thereby, aggregations which are difficult to be broken up using the same energy of mixing. 31,33
Based on the F -test in the proposed quadratic model, the model with F -value of 27.80 ( p < 0.0001) implied that the quadratic model was statistically significant for relating the results of EE. The value of correlation coefficient for EE was found to be 0.9653, indicating good correlation. The higher value of adjusted R 2 (0.9306) and predicted R 2 (0.8323) with little difference confirmed the adequacy of the model with high significance. Additionally, the suitability of the model with excellent fitting to data was demonstrated by the higher F -value (1.40) which implied that the lack of fit was non-significant ( p = 0.3977). Further, lower value of coefficient of variation (1.76%) indicated excellent precision and reliability of the regression model. All the above consideration indicated that the present regression model could be used to navigate the design space. 23
As depicted in Fig. 1(E)–(H) , the lipid/polymer ratio ( X 1 ) was found to increase EE of CS–PLHNs. Perhaps high lipid/polymer ratio provided thick lipidic barrier surrounding the polymeric core, which would have curtailed the diffusion of the drug towards the external aqueous phase. 19 Conversely, EE was found to decrease with an increase in surfactant concentration ( X 2 ). This could be explained by the fact that the higher surfactant concentration increases drug solubility by reducing interfacial tension and increasing partition of drug into aqueous phase, which in turn substantially lowers EE. 34,35 The organic phase/aqueous phase ratio ( X 3 ) was also negatively related to EE, probably as a result of enhanced partition of the drug into aqueous phase due to decreased viscosity. 35 The reduction in viscosity facilitates rapid diffusion of drug during emulsification and evaporation step owing to high shear stress, which ultimately results in CS–PLHNs with low payload. 23 However, concentration of polymer ( X 4 ) showed positive effect on EE of CS–PLHNs, which accordant with published literature that higher EE was obtained with higher polymer concentration due to rise in viscosity as well as faster solidification. 34 Additionally, increased polymer concentration would increase diffusion path length by forming larger sized CS–PLHNs and also form interfacial viscous diffusion barrier, which would further favor the encapsulation by restricting the drug diffusion. 36
The quadratic model was found to be statistically significant with F -value of 71.04 ( p < 0.0001) for fitting the results of PDI. The high R 2 coefficient (0.9861) suggested the goodness of fit of the model for predicting the results and over 98% of the total variations are well explained by the regression model. The adequacy of the selected regression model was supported by the minimal difference between predicted R 2 (0.9239) and adjusted R 2 (0.9722) value. The non-significant lack of fit ( F -value = 5.62; p = 0.0554) confirmed the suitability of the model with satisfactory fitting to data. Additionally, low value for coefficient of variation (5.13%) indicated the reliability of the quadratic model with adequate precision. Therefore, the proposed model could be employed in order to navigate the design space. 23
The influence of the independent variables on PDI can be evaluated by observing the Fig. 1(I)–(L) . The lipid/polymer ratio ( X 1 ) exhibited significant positive influence on PDI owing to its direct impact on the thickness of the lipid covering over the polymeric core. Higher lipid/polymer ratio would accelerate the aggregation by suppressing their native surface charge, which would result uneven size distribution. Moreover, formation of single and multilamellar liposomes at higher lipid concentration also enforces polydispersity. 23 Nevertheless, significant drop in polydispersity was noticed at elevated surfactant concentration ( X 2 ) and organic phase/aqueous phase ratio ( X 3 ), could be attributed to the marked reduction in the interfacial tension, as reported earlier. The reduction in interfacial tension between aqueous phase and organic phase ensures good emulsification process, imparting homogeneity to CS–PLHNs. 27 The polymer concentration ( X 4 ) exhibited positive relationship with PDI on account of its direct impact on emulsification efficiency. Higher polymer concentration could favor the formation of coarse dispersion due to lack of sufficient energy to overcome the viscous forces as well as lack of sufficient surfactant, which could ultimately impose the polydispersity. 37

3.5 Morphology study
The surface morphology of CS–PLHNs was further determined by AFM study in order to confirm the morphological aspect revealed by HR-TEM. The topographic and three-dimensional (3D) AFM micrographs of CS–PLHNs, generated by the atomic level interaction between a sharp tip and the surface of CS–PLHNs are depicted in Fig. 5(B) and (E) . The particle size and surface morphology determined by AFM was in accordance to HR-TEM study. The particle size of CS–PLHNs, measured by AFM was almost below 250 nm. AFM images showed uniform spherical shaped CS–PLHNs having smooth surface without any visible crevices or pores. The probable reason for smooth surface could be the uniform covering of lipid over the surface of polymeric core, which has resealed the pores generated by the diffusion of organic solvent during the preparation.
Further, core–shell type of architecture of CS–PLHNs was confirmed by CLSM study. The CLSM micrograph of fluorescent CS–PLHNs, generated by visualizing under confocal microscope showed clear differentiation of two regions of different material ( Fig. 5(C) and (F) ). The green color represented the polymeric core whereas very light circle surrounding the green color represented the colorless lipid envelop, which corroborates well with the findings of HR-TEM study.
