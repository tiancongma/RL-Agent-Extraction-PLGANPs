A B S T R A C T
Significant financial losses are incurred annually in commercial aquaculture due to the opportunistic pathogen
Aeromonas hydrophila outbreaks in warm-water fish species. Thus, an appropriate vaccine and an efficient de­
livery system must be developed to combat such infectious diseases effectively. This study focused on developing
an outer membrane protein (OMPs)-based nanovaccine employing the double emulsion method, integrating it in
a biodegradable polylactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA) polymer. The resultant nanoparticle, carrying the recombi­
nant protein Omp23 (rOmp23), had a size of 346.5 nm, an impressive 74.45 % encapsulation efficiency, and a
polydispersity index of 0.318. Additionally, the spherical shape of the PLGA-Omp23 nanoparticles was confirmed
by scanning electron microscope. Further, to evaluate their in vivo efficacy, tilapia fish were administered the
vaccine-incorporated feed orally for 21 days post-challenge with the target pathogen. Cytokine expression levels
were monitored at intervals to track the activation of targeted immune genes and to assess nonspecific immune
response parameters. The obtained relative percentage survival of 73 %, highlighted the therapeutic promise of
the PLGA-encapsulated OMP-based nanovaccine. This study demonstrates the utility of nanoparticle system
designed to encapsulate the outer membrane protein as a potential oral fish vaccine for infectious diseases. This
strategy could be an efficient, cost-effective, and least stressful mode of vaccination to manage infectious
diseases.
1. Introduction
Aquaculture is the world’s most rapidly expanding food sector,
boasting a global production of 85.3 million tons in 2019 [1,2], which
increased to 122.6 million tonnes in 2020 [3]. Fish farming, a
billion-dollar industry, stands out as one of the fastest-growing sectors in
animal food production, providing a stable source of income for millions
and playing a vital role in ensuring food security and driving economic
development [4]. However, the most formidable challenge confronting
this industry remains the widespread occurrence of diseases leading to
annual global losses surpassing $10 billion [5]. Aeromonas hydrophila, an
opportunistic pathogen of warm water fish, primarily causes disease
outbreaks in freshwater aquaculture, leading to global food and nutri­
tional insecurity and the accompanying economic loss [6,7].
Geographically, A. hydrophila infections are reported worldwide, span­
ning more than 20 countries across six continents [8]. While antibiotics
or chemotherapeutics are commonly used for fish disease treatment,
they are associated with negative consequences such as drug resistance,
consumer safety due to residues, and environmental impact [9]. Against
this background, vaccination is a highly effective strategy for thwarting
a wide range of bacterial and viral infections, and actively fostering
environmental, social, and economic sustainability within the global
aquaculture production landscape [10]. Starting from the initial reports
in the 1940s, numerous vaccines have been developed, significantly

2. Materials and methods
2.1. Bacterial cultures and molecular confirmation
Aeromonas hydrophila strain (A. hydrophila Aero B COFM), a diseased
fish isolate, was retrieved from the institute repository maintained at
−80 ◦C in an ultra-low freezer (PHCbi, Japan). The culture was activated
by inoculating in nutrient broth and incubating at 30 ◦C for 24 h, then
subculturing on nutrient agar and incubating at 30 ◦C for 24 h. Genus-
specific confirmation was done by PCR for target genes, namely the lip
gene [25], aer gene [26], and ompW gene [27] (Table 1). ATCC 49140
was used as the reference strain of A. hydrophila in this study.
2.2. PCR amplification of the omp23 gene
The genomic data of A. hydrophila bearing GenBank accession
number CP027804.1 was retrieved to identify and target the omp23 gene
sequence and to design three primers: F1, F2, and R (Table 1). Primers
F1 and R facilitated full-length gene amplification, cloning, and DNA
sequencing. At the same time, F2-R combination, was used to exclude
the signal peptide-encoding area and for cloning and expressing only the
omp23 gene region. PCR reactions were conducted in 30 μL volumes
with 1X Taq amplification buffer (HiMedia, India), 10 mM dNTPs (2.5
mM each), 20 pmol of each primer, and 1 U of Taq polymerase (HiMe­
dia). The template, ranging from 200 to 400 ng, and nuclease-free water
served as the negative control. PCR included an initial denaturation at
95 ◦C for 5 min, followed by 30 cycles of denaturation at 95 ◦C for 1 min,
annealing at 52 ◦C for 1 min, extension at 72 ◦C for 1 min, and a final
extension at 72 ◦C for 10 min. Resultant PCR products were electro­
phoresed on 1.5–2 % agarose gels, followed by gel staining with
ethidium bromide (5 μg/mL) and imaging using a Bio-Rad gel docu­
mentation system (Bio-Rad, USA).
2.3. Cloning and sequencing of the omp23 gene
Following amplification using F1 and R primers, the PCR product of
the full-length open reading frame (ORF) of the omp23 gene was purified
using a Qiagen PCR purification kit (Qiagen, Germany). The purified
PCR product was cloned using the Qiagen PCR cloning kit, following the
manufacturer’s protocol (Qiagen, Germany). Positive colonies were
identified using blue-white screening on Luria Bertani (LB)-ampicillin-X-
gal plates containing 100 μg/mL ampicillin and 2 % X-gal [28,29].
Selected white colonies were confirmed by PCR using the F1 and R
primers and sequenced for inserts in PCR-positive recombinant plas­
mids, (M/S Eurofins Genomics India Pvt Ltd, Bengaluru, India). The
obtained nucleotide sequences were subsequently analyzed for similar­
ity using the basic local alignment search tool (BLAST-NCBI).

amplified using F2 and R primers. The resultant PCR product was pu­

The transformants were selected by plating the cells on LB agar plates

2.5. Purification of recombinant Omp23
The purification of rOmp23 protein was done using nickel-
nitrilotriacetic acid (Ni-NTA) affinity chromatography (Qiagen), hav­
ing six histidine tags (His6) [27,28]. The purity was assessed in 12 %
SDS-PAGE gel, and protein concentration was determined using the
bicinchoninic acid (BCA) protein assay kit (Sigma-Aldrich, USA) using a
spectrophotometer (Multiskan SkyHigh microplate spectrophotometer,
ThermoFisher, USA). For further characterization, Western blotting was
performed according to the method established by Towbin et al. [31].
The target protein was identified using a mouse anti-His antibody
(diluted 1:1000 in PBS, Biolegend, USA). Interaction of the protein
bands with the antibody on a nitrocellulose membrane (Pall Corpora­
tion, USA) was visualized by subsequent incubation with an anti-mouse
horseradish peroxidase conjugate (diluted 1:2500 in PBS, Promega,
USA). Chemiluminescent signals were generated using enhanced
chemiluminescence substrates (ECL Western blotting substrates,
Bio-Rad, USA), and the resulting bands were visualized utilizing a
chemiluminescence documentation system (MINI HD9, UVITEC, UK).
2.6. Preparation of nanoparticle and characterization
The encapsulation of rOmp23 in PLGA NPs (PLGA Mn: 7000–17000
MW, Sigma-Aldrich) involved the W1/O/W2 double emulsion solvent
evaporation technique as described previously [28,32]. In this process,
10 mg of the protein was dissolved in a buffer solution (100 mM
NaH2PO4, 10 mM Tris-Cl, 8 M Urea, pH 4.5) and emulsified in the
organic phase, which consisted of 0.9 mL of dichloromethane (DCM),
100 mg/mL PLGA, and 0.5 mg/mL Pluronic F-127 in 3 mL of polyvinyl
alcohol (25 mg/mL PVA). The primary emulsion was created by soni­
cation for 20 min on ice using a sonicator (Qsonica, USA). The outer
aqueous phase was then added, and sonication was continued for an
additional 20 min in ice chill condition to form the secondary emulsion.
added to a 40 mL solution of 25 mg/mL PVA and stirred for 20 h at
ambient temperature (27◦c) using a magnetic stirrer until DCM
completely evaporated. Protein-loaded PLGA NPs were isolated and
washed twice with ultracentrifugation. Finally, the obtained pellets
were dispersed in 1X PBS and lyophilized (Zirbus Technology, Ger­
many). The hydrodynamic diameter and polydispersity index of the NPs
was estimated using a zeta sizer (SZ-100V2, Horiba Instruments Ltd,
Japan). The lyophilized samples were appropriately diluted with PBS
(pH 7.4), and aliquots from each batch were examined in dynamic light
scattering (DLS) cuvettes. Analysis was performed in triplicate for each
preparation, for size distribution and polydispersity index using DLS at a
scattering angle of 90◦at a temperature of 25 ◦C, and standard de­
viations were calculated for each measurement. Additionally, scanning
electron microscopy (SEM) (ZEISS Gemini, Australia) was done at the
National Institute of Technology Karnataka, India, to study the
morphology of the NPs.
2.7. Encapsulation efficiency measurement and in vitro protein release
study
To evaluate the integrity of the protein and to ascertain its encap­
sulation efficiency, the rOmp23-containing particles were dissolved in a
solution of 0.5 % w/v SDS (pH 7.4), prepared by dissolving SDS in a 0.1
M NaOH solution [28,33]. The protein amount encapsulated within the
nanoparticles was calculated by subtracting the concentration of free
protein in the supernatant from the total protein loaded during NP
preparation, by the formula of Rauta et al. [34]: Encapsulation effi­
ciency (%) = (Total amount of protein - Free protein)/(Total amount of
protein) x 100. To study the release kinetics of the protein from the
nanoparticles, the protein-loaded nanoparticles were incubated at 28 ◦C
in 1X PBS, and samples were drawn at specified intervals (0, 3, 6, 9, up to
48 h). Following centrifugation at 12,000 rpm for 10 min, 500 μL of the
supernatant was collected, and an equal volume of PBS was added
(Dubey et al., 2016 a). The released rOmp23 protein in the collected
samples was quantified using the BCA method (Pierce™ BCA protein
assay kit, ThermoFisher).
2.8. Fish and bacterial strains
Tilapia fish, Oreochromis niloticus, weighing approximately 9–11 g,
were obtained from the College of Fisheries, Mangalore, India, and
carefully transported in oxygenated bags to the experimental site. They
were acclimatized for a month before commencing the experiments, and
were confirmed for the absence of Aeromonas, Edwardsiella, and Vibrio
infections. Skin samples from five randomly selected fish were collected
for bacterial isolation, and genotypic identification by PCR. The fish
were kept in aerated tanks, and commercial feed was given during the
period. The tank water was cleaned twice weekly to maintain a healthy
environment and minimize the risk of disease outbreaks.
2.9. Vaccine preparations for oral delivery
The Omp23 encapsulated nanoparticle was meticulously blended
with finely ground and sieved commercial fish feed with a dose of 10 μg/
g of fish body weight to form a uniform dough. This moist vaccine-feed
mixture was extruded using a hand extruder and allowed to air-dry at
room temperature (32 ◦C). Subsequently, the prepared dry pellets were
stored at 4 ◦C until ready for use. Fish were fed with vaccine once in a
day that accounted for 1 % of the fish’s body weight for 21 days.
2.10. Determination of lethal dose (LD50) of A. hydrophila
The LD50 value of A. hydrophila on tilapia was determined using
thirty fish evenly distributed among three separate tanks, each con­
taining ten fish. A control tank that contained ten fish was also main­
tained. Viable counts of A. hydrophila were determined using the surface
spread method on tryptone soya agar by plating ten-fold serial dilutions
of an overnight culture. The culture was centrifuged, resuspended in
sterile phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), and diluted to achieve three
consecutive dilutions with viable counts of 1.6 × 108, 1.6 × 107, 1.6 ×
106, and 1.6 × 105 cells per 0.1 mL. These dilutions were injected
intraperitoneally into the fish, and mortality was observed for up to one
week. The obtained LD50 value was subsequently calculated for exper­
iments assessing protection against challenge.
2.11. Vaccination, challenge, and relative percentage of analysis
The health assessment of O. niloticus was based on clinical observa­
tions, including surface morphology, pathological signs, and monitoring
abnormal swimming behaviour. Four fish were sampled for external and
detailed pathological examinations of internal organs. The fish were
divided into four groups, each comprising 45 fish. Group 1 served as the
control group and received commercial feed. Group 2 was fed empty NPs

survival (RPS) was calculated over 14 consecutive days using the for­
mula outlined by Kole et al. [35]: RPS = {1 - (mortality in the immu­
nized group/mortality in the control group)} × 100 %.
2.12. Non-specific immune response studies
2.12.1. Collection of serum
Following vaccination, blood samples were drawn from protein feed
group (rOmp23), empty nanoparticle feed group, rOmp23-NP, and
commercial feed group (control), at multiple time points: 7, 14, 21, and
49 days of post-vaccination (dpv), according to the protocol of Sir­
iyappagouder et al. [36].
2.12.2. Myeloperoxidase activity
Myeloperoxidase activity was studied as per the method of Quade
et al. [37] and Behera et al. [38]. The optical density (O.D) of the
mixture was measured at 450 nm using a spectrophotometer (Multiclan
SkyHigh microplate spectrophotometer, USA).
2.12.3. Anti-protease activity
The serum antiprotease activity was assessed using the colorimetric
method of Sahoo et al. [39]. Percentage inhibition was calculated using
the formula: Percentage inhibition (%) = (O.D. reference – O.D. sample)
× 100/O.D. reference as per Yadav et al. [40].
2.12.4. Bacterial agglutination activity
The agglutination test was performed in U-shaped microtitre plates
[38]. The titre was expressed as the reciprocal of the highest serum
dilution, showing agglutination of the bacterial cells.
2.13. RT-PCR detection of the expression of immune-related genes in the
vaccinated fish
To study the expression patterns of immune-related genes in vacci­
nated fish, intestine and kidney tissue samples were collected at various
time intervals (0, 7, 14, and 21 days). Total RNA extraction was done
using the TRIzol (Invitrogen, USA) method. The obtained RNA from
samples were diluted in RNase-free water, and their concentration and
purity were determined using a spectrophotometer (NanoPhotometer®,
Implen, USA). Quantitative polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR) was
performed to quantify TNF-α, CD4, IFN- γ, IL-10, and IL-1β gene
expression levels in the intestine and kidney. The one-step TB Green
PrimeScript RT-PCR kit (Takara, Japan) was used for RT-qPCR analysis
on a CFX Opus 96 Real-Time PCR platform (Bio-Rad, Singapore). Gene-
specific primers used are listed in Table 1, with the ELF-1α gene serving
as the internal control. Relative gene expression of each group was
calculated using the formula: Relative expression = 2^(-ΔΔCt), where
ΔCt represents the difference between the sample Ct value, the ELF-1α Ct
value, and ΔΔCt indicates the difference between the ΔCt value of the
vaccinated and the control group [41]. Triplicate data points were used
for analysis.
2.14. Statistical analysis
The RT-qPCR results were subjected to two-way ANOVA for statis­
tical significance. GraphPad Prism, version 8.4.3. (GraphPad, CA, USA),
was utilized for the data analysis. For statistical analysis, the RT-qPCR
results were examined by two-way ANOVA to determine statistical sig­
nificance. GraphPad Prism, version 8.4.3. (GraphPad, CA, USA), was
employed for data analysis.
3. Results
3.1. PCR identification of omp23 of A. hydrophila
The gene omp23 was confirmed by amplifying the target sequence
using F1 and R primers, resulting in a distinct single band of 627 bp.
Confirmation of the exclusion of the signal peptide region was achieved
by visualizing a PCR-amplified product of 567 bp (Fig. 1).
3.2. Cloning and expression of rOmp23
Upon analysis, the DNA sequence corresponding to the omp23 gene
product was deposited in GenBank and assigned accession number:
MZ044977. Optimal expression of rOmp23 was observed 4 h post-
induction with 0.2 % arabinose. The molecular weight of the resulting
Omp23 deduced sequence was observed to be 37 kDa on 12 % SDS-
PAGE (Fig. 2). Following purification, the recombinant protein exhibi­
ted high purity on SDS-PAGE, and Western blot analysis showed a
distinct band, thus confirming it as Omp23.
3.3. Encapsulation and characterization
Size distribution analysis revealed the empty PLGA NPs to be in the
size range of 209 ± 36.1 nm (Fig. 3A), while the rOmp23-encapsulated
NPs were larger with a size range of 346.5 ± 15.1 nm (Fig. 3B). The
polydispersity index was calculated to be 0.308 ± 0.02. The SEM images
depicted the spherical structure of the formulated NPs (Fig. 3C). The
encapsulation efficiency of rOmp23 in PLGA NPs was found to be 74.45
%. In vitro release showed approximately 63 % of the encapsulated
protein released within 48-h (Fig. 3D).
3.4. Non-specific immune response studies
After vaccination, the non-specific immune response parameters in
tilapia fish were assessed in the different groups (rOmp23, Empty NP,
rOmp23-NP, and Control) at various post-immunization time intervals.

Following vaccination, RT-qPCR analysis was performed to examine
the expression levels of immune genes in both the intestine and kidney

3.6. Challenge study
The RPS in vaccinated and control groups was evaluated in the
challenge experiment. The day following the challenge, clinical signs
that presented as darkened body colour and skin ulcers leading to
gradual mortality were observed in both the control and the group fed
with empty NP. Mortality rates gradually increased, becoming pro­
nounced four days post-challenge. Fig. 7 illustrates the survival rates in
all the groups, and the corresponding RPS values, which were 73 % for
the NPG, 46.42 % for the PG, and 10 % for the ENPG groups.

PBS for 48 h showed an exponential rise in the antigen release after 25 h.
The release was 45 % in 48 h, higher than the 34 % of rOmpW release
obtained by Dubey et al., [44]. The kinetics of rOmpW in vitro release
follows a typical pattern, beginning with a rapid initial release of anti­
gens within 24 h followed by a continual release over an extended
period. A follow-up on the release test, if continued after 48 h, could
have revealed a sustained continuous release of the rOmpW antigen
from encapsulated NPs [44]. This demonstrates the ability of PLGA to
release the antigen slowly over time, which is necessary to strengthen
the immunological response. The SEM image of the nanoparticle showed
it to be spherical in shape and uniform, similar to the observations by
Rauta et al. [34]. The 74.45 % encapsulation efficiency for PLGA NPs
obtained in this study was higher than the 53.56 % shown by Dubey
et al. [44]. High encapsulation efficiencies enable NPs to convey the
antigens effectively in vivo [45]. The formulation of the nanovaccine was
optimized in this study based on particle size, polydispersity index,
encapsulation efficiency, and in vitro release kinetics. Moreover, these
conditions provided a high encapsulation efficiency (74.45 %) and
sustained release profile over 24 h, which are essential for effective
antigen delivery and prolonged immune stimulation. The optimized
conditions balanced both physical stability and biological efficacy, as
evidenced by the elevated cytokine and antibody responses in tilapia.
Post-vaccination response at different time intervals was studied to
evaluate non-specific immune parameters in the four experimental
groups, (rOmp23, Empty NP, rOmp23-NP and Control), each aimed at
eliciting varying levels of immune response compared to the control.
The group treated with NP showed heightened immune response
compared to the others. In fish, innate immunity serves as a critical first
line of defence for protection. The pivotal role of innate immunity in fish
is paramount for initial pathogen defense, guiding subsequent adaptive
immune responses [46]. Myeloperoxidase (MPO), primarily produced
by neutrophils, constitutes a crucial innate immune system molecule. It
plays a significant role in combating bacterial threats by leveraging its
antibacterial effects. MPO’s action on halide ions in the presence of
H2O2 generates hypohalites, thus effectively eliminating bacteria [46].
This study observed an early increase in MPO levels, consistent with
previous findings, which was evident at ten days post-immunization
(dpi) in the immunized group. This observation suggests that the anti­
genic preparations in this study stimulate both specific and non-specific
components of the immune system, working synergistically to confer
protection. Fish serum typically contains an array of protease inhibitors
and antiproteases aimed at curtailing pathogen invasion [47]. Bacterial
pathogens often secrete diverse proteases that play a role as virulence
factors. These enzymes boost the pathogen’s virulence, simultaneously
breaking down host proteins and supplying crucial precursor amino
acids for bacterial growth and replication. The sera derived from
BF-immunized fish displayed varying degrees of anti-protease activity,
ranging from 60 % to 80 % during different days of analysis. Our study

In this study, changes in the immune defence system upon vaccina­
tion against A. hydrophila resulted in the upregulation and down­

related gene expression patterns in fish is relatively recent, leading to
a better understanding in future of immune response to pathogens. TNF-
α and IL-10, identified as crucial components of innate immunity [50,
51], play significant roles. The activation of innate immunity mediated
by these cytokines likely influences the initiation of specific adaptive
immune responses [41]. IL-10 is recognized as an anti-inflammatory
cytokine that mitigates inflammation by blocking chemokine receptors
and impeding the effects of pro-inflammatory cytokines, thus reducing
potential host damage [52]. It is also associated with B cell-triggered
immunoglobulins [53]. In this study there was a gradual increase in
IL-10 and TNF-α expression levels in the intestine of the vaccinated
group. Upregulation of IL-10 was initially observed in the kidney during
the early post-vaccination period in the nano vaccine administered
group. This upregulation potentially helps maintain a delicate balance
between pro- and anti-inflammatory responses, as Jiang et al. [54]
suggested. Moreover, IL-10 contributes to synthesizing a multifunctional
cytokine inhibitory factor, which aids in immune-suppressive functions,
significantly hinders proinflammatory cytokine responses such as IL-1β,
TNF-α, and IFN-γ, preventing tissue damage. Studies in fish have re­
ported increased IL-10 mRNA expression levels due to various immu­
nostimulants, linked to transcriptional changes in innate immune cells,
like macrophages [55]. TNF-α and IL-10 are crucial components of
innate immunity [56,57]. Activation of these cytokines potentially
primes the onset of specific adaptive immune response [58]. TNF-α gene
upregulation in the intestine on the 21st-day post-vaccination in the
nanovaccine-administered group. On the other hand, TNF-α gene upre­
gulation occurred in the kidney before vaccination, followed by a
gradual decrease in gene expression. Previous reports have highlighted a
similar pattern wherein an initial rise in IL-1β and TNF-α expression
occurred shortly after bacterial infection, followed by decline [59]. IL-10
functions as an anti-inflammatory cytokine, pivotal in reducing
inflammation [52]. Its role in minimizing host damage involves blocking
chemokine receptors and curbing the impact of pro-inflammatory
cytokines. Additionally, IL-10 has links to B cell-induced immunoglob­
ulins [53]. Our study showed a gradual increase in IFN-γ, an important
immunoregulatory molecule in innate and adaptive immunity. An
upregulation of IFN-γ expression in both the intestine and kidney of the
vaccinated group was observed on 14th day, thus suggesting that the
nanovaccine probably stimulates both innate and adaptive responses,
potentially fostering the activation of immune-related macrophages [60,
61]. In a study by Lindenstrøm et al., [62], IL-10 and IL-1β expression
levels in the intestine and kidney exhibited variation being significantly
low between weeks 2 and 4 and relatively elevated during weeks 6 and
8. Saurabh and Sahoo [63], observed the upregulation of IL-1β in trout
skin as a response to the parasite Gyrodactylus derjavini infection. In the
Indian major carp, Catla catla infected with A. hydrophila, IL-1β
expression significantly decreased, suggesting potential pathogen toxin
release during different phases of attachment. This process seems to play

critical role of innate immunity in fish through oral vaccination. Zhang

While the current study demonstrates the feasibility of expressing
and purifying the target protein, a potential limitation lies in the scal­
ability of the procedure. For practical or industrial applications, large
quantities of high-purity protein are required, which can have chal­
lenges in terms of cost, time, and technical efficiency. The yields ob­
tained under laboratory conditions may not readily translate to large-
scale production due to constraints in expression efficiency, solubility,
or purification throughput. Therefore, additional optimization strate­
gies, such as enhancing expression systems or employing bioprocess
engineering techniques, may be necessary to overcome these limitations
and make the process viable for broader application.

5. Conclusion
The findings of this study underscore the potential of utilizing PLGA
encapsulated with outer membrane protein Omp23 as a promising
vaccination strategy against aeromonad infections of fish. The encap­
sulation process demonstrated efficient antigen release kinetics, sus­
tained release over time, and protection against acidic conditions, all of
which are essential for preserving the integrity of the antigen. The
ability of the nanoparticle to trigger a heightened immune response,
particularly the non-specific immune parameters such as myeloperox­
idase and anti-protease activity, signifies its capability to bolster the
host’s defense mechanisms. The upregulation of crucial immune-related
genes like IL-10, TNF-α, IFN-γ, and IL-1β in vaccinated fish suggests a
robust activation of both innate and adaptive immunity, critical for
combating pathogen invasion and establishment. The enhanced
expression of genes associated with antigen presentation, such as CD4
and MHC-II, further supports the notion of improved immune response
elicited by the vaccination. The RPS of 73 % post-challenges in the group
administered with the rOmp23 nanovaccine highlights its effectiveness
in conferring protection compared to the control groups, which dis­
played signs of infection and subsequent mortality. These outcomes
emphasize the pivotal role of innate immunity, especially in the context
of oral vaccination in fish. In conclusion, the study demonstrates that the
PLGA-encapsulated Omp23 nanoparticle vaccine possessed promising
attributes: efficient antigen delivery, innate and adaptive immunity
activation, and significant protection against fish aeromonad infection.
These findings pave the way for developing effective and long-lasting
vaccination strategies in aquaculture for other infectious agents and
promote blue transformation goals advocated by FAO.
