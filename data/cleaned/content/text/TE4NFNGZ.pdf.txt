In vitro degradation of poly (D, L-lactide-co-glycolide)
nanoparticles loaded with linamarin
Ahmed S. Hussein1,2, Norhaﬁzah Abdullah1, Fakhru’l-Razi Ahmadun1
1Department of Chemical and Environmental Engineering, Faculty of Engineering, Universiti Putra Malaysia, 43400
Serdang, Selangor, Malaysia
2Advanced Materials and Nanotechnology Lab., Institute of Advanced Technology, Universiti Putra Malaysia, 43400
Serdang, Selangor, Malaysia

Abstract: Linamarin-loaded poly (lactide-co-glycolide) (PLGA) nanoparticles (NPs) were prepared by the double emulsion
solvent evaporation technique. The formulated PLGA (50:50) and PLGA (85:15) NPs were spherically shaped, having an
average particle size < 190 nm, drug entrapment efﬁciency (50–52%) and zeta potentials ranging from −25 to −30 mV.
Interestingly, all formulated PLGA NPs exhibited a controlled biphasic release proﬁle. Polymer degradation was investigated
in the current research to determine the major degradation products and then the polymer biocompatibility as well as safety.
The PLGA NPs degradation behaviour was investigated by measuring water uptake, mass loss, change of pH of the
degradation medium, morphological changes, and lactic and glycolic acid concentrations. Gravimetrical methods, pH meter,
scanning electron microscope and high-performance liquid chromatography were employed, respectively. PLGA (50:50) NPs
were found to degrade faster than PLGA (85:15) NPs. With regard to water uptake, mass loss and pH change, the
degradation behaviour of PLGA (50:50) NPs was signiﬁcantly (ρ < 0.05) different from that of PLGA (85:15) NPs. A
complete degradation of PLGA (50:50) NPs was achieved after 102 days, whereas, only about 60% of PLGA (85:15) NPs
were degraded within the same period. Complete degradation and release of the degradation products naturally by the body
ensures safety of the delivery carrier.
1
Introduction
Owing to the multiple limitations of conventional drugs, such
as solubility, low bioavailability, compatibility and toxicity,
there have been strong efforts in the development of
polymeric drug delivery systems. Although these delivery
systems might be biodegradable or non-biodegradable, the
latter has gained less attention. An ideal polymer to be
used in drug delivery systems should be biocompatible,
biodegradable and non-toxic [1, 2]. The applications of
biodegradable
polymers
in
the
biomedical
ﬁeld
for
controlled drug delivery have received signiﬁcant interest
in
the
past
few
decades.
Among
the
biodegradable
polymers,
polyesters
have
been
the
most
widely
investigated
ones.
Among
the
polyesters,
polylactide,
polyglycolide and their copolymers are the most popular
choices as carriers because of their good degradation as
well as their approval for clinical use [3–5]. The PLGA
copolymer undergoes degradation in aqueous mediums for
both in vitro and in vivo through the cleavage of its
backbone ester linkage [6]. A good understanding of the
biodegradation phenomenon and an approval of the cellular
and
tissue
responses
are
fundamental
prerequisites
in
designing and developing any successful biodegradable
delivery system containing drugs. Furthermore, to assess
the drug release kinetics of any drug delivery system, it is
mandatory to characterise its degradation and erosion
behaviour. Since a change in the surface area of the colloid
takes place as soon as the degradation process starts, it is
essential to control the degradation rate to ensure an even
dosage delivery of the drugs [7].
Generally, degradation rates of the polyesters depend on
many factors including type and component of polyester,
molecular
weight,
molecular
weight
distribution,
crystallinity as well as macroscopic geometry [8]. An
important prerequisite for the hydrolytic degradation of the
polymeric devices is their wetting behaviour. Release of the
drugs from these devices was found to be controlled by
diffusion and an erosion mechanism. After incubation of
the
nanoparticles
(NPs)
into
the
dissolution
medium,
desorption of the surface bound drug occurs ﬁrst followed
by diffusion of both water into and linamarin out from the
NPs, which mainly take place through the NPs walls. As
the
process
continues,
the
degradation
process
will
predominate because of chain relaxation and the formation
of more pores on the surfaces of the NPs. Water penetration
and contact angle are the most important factors in
describing the wettability of the delivery device [9]. The
complete degradation of the polymeric NPs into water
soluble degradation products, such as lactic and glycolic
acids, observed in this study after 3 months ensures a
complete release of the encapsulated drug from the matrix.

Linamarin (α-hydroxyisobutyronitrile-β-D-glucose) is a
cyanogenic glucoside found in the leaves and roots of
plants, such as lima beans, ﬂax and abundantly in the
cassava plant (Manihot esculenta Crantz). Linamarin is a
water-soluble molecule and can be hydrolysed by acid or
enzyme to liberate a toxic chemical ‘hydrogen cyanide’
(HCN), which is the main source of toxicity of linamarin if
found in high concentrations. Some previous researches
have proven that linamarin exhibits a good cytotoxicity
effect towards HT29, MCH-7 and HL-60 [10, 11]. This
cytotoxicity has been mainly attributed to the toxic HCN
that is liberated from the hydrolysis of linamarin. Therefore
this HCN can also harm normal (healthy) cells if both the
dosage form and administration of linamarin is not well
formulated. To circumvent this problem, linamarin has been
encapsulated into PLGA NPs to control its release, and,
consequently, toxicity. The main objective of the current
research is to investigate the degradation behaviour of the
formulated linamarin loaded PLGA NPs to ensure complete
release of the hosted drug as well as the safety of the
delivery system. The selection of PLGA (50:50) and PLGA
(85:15) copolymers was because they have been the most
investigated copolymers in drug delivery systems. They also
provide an opportunity to investigate the effect of changing
(lactide (LA)/glycolide(GA)) composition on the PLGA
degradation.

Materials and methods
2.1
Materials
Poly (DL-lactide-co-glycolide) (PLGA) with two molar ratios
L/G = (50:50), MW 40 000–75 000; and L/G = (85:15), MW
50 000–75 000; polyvinyl alcohol (PVA), average MW 16
000 and 98% hydrolysed; and dichloromethane (DCM)
were
purchased
from
Sigma
Aldrich
(Poole,
UK).
Linamarin was delivered by AG Scientiﬁc (USA).
2.2
Preparation of the NPs
Linamarin loaded NPs were prepared using a double
emulsion solvent evaporation (w/o/w) technique [12–16].
DCM and PVA were employed in this research as organic
solvent and surfactant, respectively. The ﬁrst emulsion (w/
o) was prepared by adding an aqueous solution of the drug
(linamarin) to an organic solution of the polymer (PLGA)
previously
mixed
with
surfactant
solution
then
homogenised for 3 min (Heidolph DIAX 900, Germany).
The ﬁrst emulsion was then introduced into a new aqueous
solution of the surfactant and homogenised for another 3
min
to
obtain
the
double
emulsion
(w/o/w).
After
evaporation of the organic solvent, the suspensions were
centrifuged at 12 000 rpm to separate the NPs (Eppendorf
Centrifuge 5810R, Germany), washed well using distilled
water to remove any free drug and/or PVA. Finally, the NPs
were frozen for 20 h and freeze dried for 36 h (EPSILON
18D Freeze Dryer, Denmark).
2.3
Drug release
The drug release was investigated by placing a weighted
sample into stoppered conical ﬂasks containing phosphate
buffer, pH 7.4, incubated at 37°C and centrifugation rate at
150 rpm. At predetermined time intervals, a sample was
taken from the release medium, centrifuged and 1 ml of the
supernatant was taken for analysis and the removed volume
was replaced by a fresh buffer. Concentration of linamarin
released
was
measured
spectrophotometrically
at
a
wavelength of 545 nm (Spectronic Genesys TM and PC
Model
336003,
Spectronic
Instruments,
USA).
Each
recorded data were an average of three replicates (n = 3).
2.4
In vitro degradation of the NPs
The polymer degradation process was investigated by
determining the change in the surface morphology, water
uptake, mass loss, change in the pH of the release medium,
concentration of lactic and glycolic acids in the medium.
Simply, each sample of NPs was divided into 10 mg
batches, and each batch was placed in screw-capped vials
containing 4 ml of 0.1 M phosphate buffer pH (7.4) and
incubated at 37°C with a constant shaking rate of 150 rpm.
2.4.1 Water uptake and mass loss:
Both water uptake
and mass loss were determined gravimetrically. The initial
mass of a given NPs sample, as indicated in Section 2.3,
was calculated and denoted as Mo. At designated time
intervals, the incubated sample was removed, rinsed with
distilled water, the surface dried using water-absorbent
paper and the wet mass of the sample was measured
immediately (Mw). The sample was then freeze dried and
kept in desiccators until constant weight (Md) was achieved.
The percentage of water uptake was calculated according to
(1). Meanwhile, the percentage mass loss was calculated
using (2) [17–19]. Each recorded data were an average of
three replicates (n = 3).

3
Results and discussion
The formulated linamarin-loaded PLGA NPs were spherically
shaped; unimodal with average size ranged from 160 to 186
nm (Fig. 1). The observed zeta potentials ranging from −25
to
−30 mV
indicate
relatively
good
stability
of
the
formulated
PLGA
in
their
emulsions.
Linamarin
was
physically incorporated with PLGA and most of the drug
was encapsulated into the NPs core surrounded by the
polymer; however, some linamarin molecules were attached
onto the NPs surfaces. However, most of the water soluble
drugs
like
linamarin
suffer
low
drug
encapsulation
efﬁciency (DEE), it is good that our PLGA NPs exhibited
reasonably high DEE ( > 50%). NPs with high DEE ensure
that small quantity of NPs is required to convey a sufﬁcient
drug concentration.
3.1
Drug release
Interestingly, all the three different formulations, two from
PLGA (50:50) NPs and one from PLGA (85:15) NPs
exhibited some burst release followed by controlled drug
release proﬁles that lasted for days (Fig. 2). Since drug
release mostly starts by diffusion process, thus the DEE
might also be indirectly related to degradation of PLGA.
Desorption of the surface bound drug, as well as diffusion
of the encapsulated drug molecules from the NPs, creates
more channels and/or pores on the surface of the NPs. No
doubt this will improve the diffusion of more water into the
NPs, release more of the drug molecules, and, ﬁnally, lead
to degradation of the polymer. Thus, complete degradation
of the biodegradable polymeric delivery systems, such as
PLGA NPs, ensures the release of the entire entrapped drug
from the NPs as well as the fate of the polymer and its
degradation
products.
Polymer
degradation
into
biocompatible end products ensures the elimination of these
degradation products from the body, and, ﬁnally, the safety
of the delivery system. Therefore a good understanding of
the polymer degradation is a prerequisite for successful
development
of
any
polymeric
drug
delivery
system
designed for controlled release.
3.2
Degradation of PLGA NPs
Three formulated batches, two of PLGA (50:50) NPs and one
PLGA (85:15) NPs were employed in the current research to
investigate the polymer degradation behaviour. These two
copolymers were selected to study the effect of changing
the polymer composition (hydrophobicity) based on the LA/
GA ratios on the degradation process. Accordingly, water
uptake, mass loss, pH changes, concentration of degradation
products
(lactic
and
glycolic
acids)
and
morphology
changes of the PLGA NPs were examined.
3.2.1 Water uptake: Generally, the incubated NPs swell
by water from the surrounding medium, and drug molecules
diffuse
out
of
the
particles.
Consequently,
polymer
degradation starts, and, thus, the greater the swelling the
more degradable the polymer will be. Thus, water uptake is
a good gravimetrical technique to determine the wettability
and degradability of a polymeric delivery system. The water
uptake proﬁle is shown in Fig. 3 where it is obvious that
there is an initial period of slow water uptake, which lasted
for nearly 1 week for both batches of PLGA (50:50) (a) and
(b) NPs and one batch of PLGA (85:15) NPs. In the second
week, the water uptake values were about 10.5, 11.8 and
8.7% for PLGA (50:50) (a) and (b) NPs and PLGA (85:15)
NPs, respectively. The increase in water uptake was slow
until the third week of incubation where the values were
20.2, 22.1 and 14.7%; for PLGA (50:50) NPs batch (a),
batch (b) and PLGA (85:15) NPs batch, respectively.

the ratio of its crystalline to amorphous regions as the
amorphous regions are more easily hydrolysable [20].
3.2.2 Mass loss:
The mass loss technique is a simple but
rather effective technique to characterise the erosion of a
biodegradable polymeric matrix. As the mass loss proﬁle is
linear for surface eroding devices only, mass loss proﬁles
allow an immediate assessment of the type of erosion that a
polymer matrix is undergoing, that is, whether it is surface
or bulk erosion [21]. It was generally stated that during the
early stages of the degradation process, only a decrease in
the molecular weight of the polymer takes place, without
any signiﬁcant weight loss. Moreover, the polymer degrades
to
a
critical
molecular
weight,
and
its
produced
water-soluble
fragments
will
be
released
into
the
surrounding aqueous medium [22]. The proﬁles of mass
loss for both batches of PLGA (50:50) NPs and one batch
of PLGA (85:15) NPs, as a function of the incubation
(hydrolysis) time carried out in this research, are shown in
Fig. 4.
It is clear that there is an initial period of no weight loss that
lasted for 1 week (induction stage) for all three batches where
no soluble products formed, then followed by a little
reduction in mass of the NPs. Meanwhile, considerable
mass losses were observed (36.8 and 40.7%) at the third
week and (58.8 and 56.3%) at the ﬁfth week of incubation
for PLGA (50:50) (a) and (b) batches, respectively. The
mass loss observed with PLGA (85:15) NPs was only
18.8% at third week and 41% at ﬁfth week of incubation.
At the end of the sixth week of incubation the masses
remaining for the PLGA (50:50) NPs were only 31.7%
(68.3% mass loss) for batch (a) and 35.4% (64.6% mass
loss) for batch (b). However, for the PLGA (85:15) NPs,
about 51.2% of the mass remained (only 41.8% mass loss)
after 6 weeks of incubation. These results indicate that
PLGA (50:50) copolymer degrades and loses mass faster
than PLGA (85:15). The measured mass loss of PLGA
(50:50) NPs is signiﬁcantly different (ρ < 0.05) from that
of the PLGA (85:15) NPs. Our results are in accordance
with the degradation order that was reported previously as
50:50 > 75:25 > 85:15 at all time-points beyond a week
[23]. Other similar ﬁndings were also previously reported
when a fast mass loss calculated of about (80%) for the
PLGA (50:50) microspheres was observed after the seventh
week of incubation, as compared with only 10 and 1% for
PLGA
(75:25)
and
poly(D,L-lactide)
(PDLLA)
microspheres, respectively [24].
Generally, in the ﬁrst phase of degradation, a signiﬁcant
amount of water-soluble low-molecular-weight oligomers
are formed. Later, these degradation products diffuse out
from the polymer matrix, and, consequently, this leads to

Owing to the fact that great mass loss starts from the 2nd
half of the degradation process, our samples are considered
to undergo bulk degradation rather than surface eroding.
Several previous studies have also stated that PLGA is a
bulk-degradable copolymer [6, 19, 25]. Interestingly, in line
with these earlier ﬁndings, the samples of the present
investigation, exhibited a noticeable mass loss because of
degradation starting from the third week, especially for
PLGA (50:50). From all these ﬁndings it could be said that
mass loss decreases with the increasing lactide content in
the copolymer because of the increase in copolymer
hydrophobicity.
3.2.3 Change of pH of the degradation medium:
A
particular feature of the PLGA degradation is the formation
of acidic degradation products, namely, lactic and glycolic
acids. Accumulation of these acidic monomers increases
acidity and reduces the pH of the medium [20, 26].
Therefore the change in pH of the degradation medium was
also investigated, as shown in Fig. 5. A great change in pH
might affect the living cells (the smallest basic structural
and functional unit of all known living organisms). It may
also affect degradation of the polymer and thus release of
the encapsulated drug as well as its kinetic mechanism. It is
important to note that PLGA is polyester and its hydrolytic
degradation is known to be catalysed by protons. This
means
that
in
highly
acidic
mediums,
the
polymer
degradation increases leading to a greater reduction in the
pH and acidity of the medium. Thus, increasing pH
(increasing basicity) of the incubation medium would
slightly decrease the degradation rate of the polymer [27].
Therefore the pH of the medium should be carefully
monitored to be within the acceptable physiological limits
to ensure that the activity of both the cells and enzymes are
not altered since both are non-monotonic pH dependent.
Generally, a decrease in the pH with incubation time was
observed for all three batches (two PLGA (50:50) and one
PLGA (85:15) NPs). For the 1st week, the pH change was
small because of the limited formation of water-soluble
oligomers, while starting from the second week, a notable
decrease was observed, especially for PLGA (50:50) NPs.
This decrease in pH is mainly because of the formation of
aqueous oligomers, such as L-lactic acid, D-lactic acid and
glycolic acid. Since these degradation products are acidic
and retained in the incubation medium, then any polymer
degradation will result in a further reduction in pH. After 3
weeks of incubation, the reduction in pH was from 7.4 to
7.1, 7.1 and 7.2 for PLGA (50:50) NPs batches of (a) and
(b) and PLGA (85:15), respectively. By the end of the sixth
week of incubation, the ﬁnal pH values were 6.8, 6.7 and
7.1 for PLGA (50:50) NPs batches of (a) and (b) and
PLGA (85:15), respectively. The change in pH observed
with PLGA (50:50) NPs is signiﬁcantly different (ρ < 0.05)
from that of their counterpart PLGA (85:15) NPs.
Since the change in pH results from the acidic degradation
products, the slow decrease in the pH observed for the PLGA
(85:15) NPs compared with the PLGA (50:50) NPs could
mainly
be
attributed
to
the
slow
water
uptake
and
degradation rate of the former polymer. However, the
change in pH observed in this current research (from 7.4 to
the range of 6.7–7.1) is considered unlikely to alter the
body biological activity; the authors believe that more
control of the pH is still required. This could be achieved
by changing the polymer composition, which, consequently,
will modify the polymer degradation, or by adding a pH
modiﬁer that should be biologically safe (does not harm
healthy cells) to maintain the pH within the physiological
limits regardless of the polymer degradation.

PLGA. These ﬁndings are in accordance with the enhanced
degradability of PLGA (50:50) NPs compared with PLGA
(85:15) NPs observed in the current research.
Since the PLGA degradation products (lactic and glycolic
acid) are body metabolisable moieties [25, 28], would
further break down into carbon dioxide and water [29]
then easily and naturally release from the body. This
ensures the safety of our PLGA-based formulated NPs as
drug delivery carriers since no toxicity is expected because
of
the
accumulation
of
any
foreign
substances.
The
ﬁndings of this research correlate with those reported in a
previous study, where the PLGA (50:50) delivery system
was incubated for 3 weeks and the principal water-soluble
acids detected were lactic, lactoylactic and glycolic acids
[30].
Besides
these
water-soluble
acids
as
the
main
degradation products, other unknown oligomers were also
previously detected as impurities in the PLGA degradation
products [31].
However,
although
the
PLGA
copolymer
is
water-insoluble, it is hydrolytically unstable and can be
degraded by attacking its ester bonds, and, consequently, a
random chain scission occurs, resulting in lactic and
glycolic
acids.
These
degradation
products
are
easily
metabolised in the body to water and carbon dioxide via the
Krebs cycle [29, 32]. These ﬁnal degradation products can
easily be eliminated from the body through the natural
pathways, hence safety of the delivery system is guaranteed.
Moreover, the complete polymer degradation also ensures
release of the entire encapsulated drug from the NPs [3].
3.2.5 NPs morphology changes: For biodegradable
polymers, it is important to investigate the morphological
changes over time as the polymer erodes. PLGA is a
copolymer
consisting
of
(LA)
with
crystalline
and
hydrophobic
nature,
and
(GA)
with
amorphous
and
hydrophilic nature. Hydration generally increases hydrolysis
and fragmentation of the parent particles. Meanwhile, poly
(glycolide) (GA) is the hydrophilic part of the polymer; the
higher the amount of this particular part the polymer
becomes more hydrophilic and susceptible to hydration.
The SEM images revealed an apparent change in NPs
surface morphology with incubation time (Fig. 9).
The deterioration in the smoothness and spherical shape of
the NPs is dependent on the rate of polymer degradation. The
PLGA (50:50) NPs retained their structure and integrity for
up to 30 days before starting to agglomerate at 37 days.
The
agglomeration
became
predominant
and
reached
maximum after 50 days. After 60 days of incubation, no
clear NPs were found, instead, ﬂakes were observed. The
ﬂakes started to degrade after 72 days of incubation, and
nearly 70–80%; of the polymers were degraded after 90
days of incubation time. However, the PLGA (85:15) NPs
retained their structure even after 37 days of incubation,
without any noticeable agglomeration compared with the
PLGA (50:50) NPs. Clearly, the PLGA (85:15) NPs only
started to agglomerate after 50 days of incubation and most
of the NPs remained in the medium even after 60 days. The
rate of PLGA (85:15) NPs agglomeration increased after 72
days and some degradation was observed after 92 days.
These results indicate that PLGA (50:50) NPs degrade
faster than their counterpart PLGA (85:15) NPs. The
amorphous regions are more degradable than the crystalline
regions because of the compact structure of the latter, which
retards the penetration of the dissolution ﬂuids into the
system (NPs) as well as the diffusion of the degradation
products from the NPs. Degradation of the amorphous
regions is also expected to create some pores and crevices
on the NPs, which, consequently, will enhance degradation.
Therefore the fast degradation of the PLGA (50:50) NPs
could be attributed to their enhanced amorphous and
hydrophilic nature, whereas the slow degradation rate
observed for the PLGA (85:15) NPs could be attributed to
their strong hydrophobic nature because of the high poly
(lactide)
content,
which
reduces
its
wettability
and
degradation [6, 20, 24, 33–36].
The crystallinity of the polymer may also play a signiﬁcant
role in modulating the degradation rate. Therefore it could be
expected that the matrix forming polymer would rapidly
undergo the transition, that is, from a glassy state to a
rubbery one, upon contact with the phosphate buffer [37].
Generally, the amorphous polymers do not present a close
packed
organisation
and
are
more
hygroscopic
than

speciﬁc applications.

PLGA (50:50). Generally, the batches of PLGA (50:50)
NPs signiﬁcantly (ρ < 0.05) differ in their degradation
behaviour from the PLGA (85:15) NPs. PLGA (50:50)
