ORIGINAL ARTICLE
Exploring paclitaxel‑loaded adenosine‑conjugated PEGylated PLGA
nanoparticles for targeting triple‑negative breast cancer
Dasharath Chaudhari1 · Kaushik Kuche1 · Vivek Yadav1 · Rohan Ghadi1 · Tushar Date1 · Nallamothu Bhargavi1 ·
Sanyog Jain1 
Accepted: 29 November 2022 / Published online: 17 December 2022
© Controlled Release Society 2022
Abstract
In present investigation, we developed paclitaxel (PTX)-loaded adenosine (ADN)-conjugated PLGA nanoparticles for
combating triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC), where ADN acts as a substrate for adenosine receptors (AR) that are
overexpressed in TNBC. Using synthesized PLGA-PEG-ADN, PTX-loaded nanoparticles (PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs)
were prepared via emulsion diffusion evaporation process that rendered particles of size 135 ± 12 nm, PDI of 0.119 ± 0.03,
and entrapment-efficiency of 79.26 ± 2.52%. The NPs showed higher %cumulative release at pH 5.5 over 7.4 with Higuchi
release kinetics. The PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs showed ~ 4.87- and 5.22-fold decrease in %hemolysis in comparison to
free PTX and ­Intaxel®, indicating their hemocompatible nature. The ADN modification assisted cytoplasmic internaliza-
tion of particles via AR-mediated endocytosis that resulted in ~ 3.77- and 3.51-fold reduction in ­IC50 and showed apoptosis
index of 0.93 and 1.18 in MDA-MB-231 and 4T1 cells respectively. The pharmacokinetic profile of ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs
revealed higher AUC and t1/2 than ­Intaxel® and ­Nanoxel® pharmacodynamic activity showed ~ 18.90-fold lower %tumor
burden than control. The kidney and liver function biomarkers showed insignificant change in the levels, when treated with
PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs and exhibited no histological alterations in the liver, spleen, and kidney. Overall, the optimized
particles were found to be biocompatible with improved anti-TNBC activity.
Keywords  Adenosine · Paclitaxel · Triple-negative breast cancer · PLGA nanoparticles · Adenosine receptor
Introduction
Breast cancer (BC) is a complex disease and comprises of
collective diseases rather than being a homogeneous condi-
tion. Hence, finding a generalized therapy for managing BC
is not a wise strategy [1]. This has resulted in several clinical
and pre-clinical investigations to identify types/subtypes of
BC so that, a potential treatment strategy can be developed
with respect to the molecular targets, observed in BC. For
example, estrogen receptor (ER) or progesterone receptor
(PR) expressing BC exhibit hormone-dependent growth.
Thus, employing hormonal therapy for combating such type
of BC can assist well in shrinking the tumor before it is
surgically removed with low chances of reoccurrence [2].
However, devising a strategy for a type of BC which does
not express ER, PR, and HER2 receptors or so called “tri-
ple negative breast cancer” (TNBC) is difficult [3]. Due to
such molecular framework, TNBC has poor prognosis and
the systemic chemotherapy (e.g., paclitaxel, capecitabine,
eribulin, ixabepilone) is the only “go through” strategy in
early and/or metastatic stages [4]. Further, due to no-defined
molecular targets in TNBC, issues like, high risk of relapse,
short progression-free survival, and overall survival tend to
persist at clinical level. This leads to higher mortality rate
in patients diagnosed with TNBC.
In chemotherapy, taxanes along with anthracycline
is the most preferred treatment regime along with other
agents listed by international guidelines. But the major
problem with this strategy is the lack of ability to deliver
therapeutic agents specifically to TNBC cells [5–7]. There
were trails conducted for evaluating targeted therapeutics
against TNBC but majority of them showed insignificant
improvement in therapy [5]. Thus, many attempts have

Materials and methods
Materials
Paclitaxel (PTX) was procured as a gift sample from USP
Fresenius Kabi, India. PLGA, i.e., Resomer RG 502 H
[Poly(D,L-lactide-co-glycolide) 50:50; MW-7-17kD], was
purchased from Evonik, GmbH. ­NH2-PEG-COOH was pro-
cured from Jenkem Technology, USA. 1-Ethyl-3-(3 dimeth-
ylaminopropyl) carbodiimide (EDC), N-hydroxysuccinimide
(NHS), anhydrous dichloromethane, anhydrous dimethyl sul-
foxide, 1-hydroxy-7-azabenzotriazole (HOAt), Annexin V
Apoptosis Detection Kit, diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI),
di-isopropylcarbodiimide (DIC), MTT, dipyridamole, and
all other chemicals were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich,
Burlington, MA, USA. Acetonitrile (HPLC grade), chloro-
form (HPLC grade), diethyl ether, methanol (HPLC grade),
and ethyl acetate (HPLC grade) were procured from Fischer
Scientific, USA. Accurex Biomedical Pvt. Ltd., India, pro-
vided additional assay kits, including alanine transaminase
(ALT), aspartate transaminase (AST), blood urea nitrogen
(BUN), and creatinine. Throughout the studies, deionized
and Millipore-filtered (pore diameter 0.22 m) water was
used. All other chemicals and reagents were purchased from
local vendors and were of analytical grade.
Preparation of ADN‑conjugated PLGA nanoparticles
Synthesis of PLGA‑PEG‑ADN conjugate
PLGA-PEG-ADN was synthesized in two steps as described
in Fig. 1A. To begin with, PLGA (2 g) was dissolved in
8 mL of anhydrous dichloromethane (DCM) to which 2 mL
of DCM containing EDC (92 mg) and NHS (135 mg) was
added drop wise [20–22]. The reaction mixture was stirred
for 24 h under neutral ­N2 atmosphere at room temperature
(RT). The intermediate (PLGA-NHS ester) formed was pre-
cipitated by adding 50 mL ice cold diethyl ether. The precip-
itate was collected and dried overnight under vacuum. The
collected precipitate (500 mg) was dissolved in anhydrous
DCM in to which DCM solution containing ­NH2-PEG-
COOH (125 mg) and N, N-diisopropylethylamine (14 mg;
DIPEA) solubilized separately was added dropwise. The
reaction was allowed to proceed for 12 h under neutral ­N2
environment at RT. The synthesized PLGA-PEG co-polymer
was then precipitated using ice cold ether followed by wash-
ing and drying (under vacuum). This PLGA-PEG copolymer
was used for preparing PTX-loaded PEGylated PLGA nano-
particles (PTX PEG-PLGA NPs).
In second step, PLGA-PEG-COOH (1 g) was solubilized
in 20 mL anhydrous dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) containing
ADN (20 mg), EDC (92 mg), and NHS (135 mg), and stirred
for 24 h at RT under ­N2 atmosphere. The product formed
(PLGA-PEG-ADN) was precipitated (ice-cold diethyl ether),
washed (with methanol), filtered, and dried in similar fash-
ion as described previously. The synthesized PLGA-PEG-
ADN conjugate was characterized using FTIR and 1H NMR
(DMSO-d6, 500 Hz) (details are given in supplementary
material) and was used for preparing PTX-loaded nanopar-
ticles (PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs).
Preparation and optimization of PTX‑loaded PLGA NPs
PTX-loaded PLGA NPs were prepared using emulsion dif-
fusion evaporation method, with minor modifications. Dif-
ferent types of stabilizers and their concentrations (DMAB,
polyvinyl alcohol (PVA), ­Tween® 80, and PluronicTM
F-127) were screened. The process parameters like sonica-
tion cycle (at different amplitudes of sonication, i.e., 25, 30,
35, and 40%) and theoretical drug loading (2.5, 5, 7.5, and
10% w/w) were thoroughly optimized. For details, kindly
follow the supplementary file Sect. 2.
For preparing nanoparticles, PLGA (50 mg) and PTX
(theoretical loading- 7.5% w/w) were dissolved in 2.5 mL
of ethyl acetate at RT. The organic phase was directly mixed
with 5 mL of optimized stabilizer aqueous solution (PVA,
2% w/v). The emulsion was stirred at 1000 RPM for 20 min
followed by probe sonication under optimized amplitude
(35%) and interval (sonication time: 120 s; 5 s on/off) (Refer
supplementary file Sect. 2). The emulsion formed was added
to 25 mL of distilled water and was stirred continuously, so
as to diffuse the organic solvent followed by evaporation.
Solvent evaporation results in nanoprecipitation and the
formation of PTX-loaded PLGA NPs. The particle size, PDI,
and Z-potential of the diluted nanoparticles were determined
using dynamic light scattering (Nano ZS Malvern, UK). The
% entrapment efficiency (%EE) of drug was determined

Storage stability of lyophilized PTX ADN‑PEG‑PLGA
NPs
The storage stability for freeze-dried PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA
NPs was performed using the accelerated stability protocols
as per ICH guidelines [24]. The, freeze-dried PTX ADN-
PEG-PLGA NPs were transferred to 5-mL glass vials, sealed
with plastic caps, and were kept in a stability chamber at
temperature 30 ± 2 °C and relative humidity (65 ± 5%) for
6 months. After that, the PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs were
examined for any changes in particle size, PDI, and %EE.
In vitro drug release
The in vitro release of PTX from prepared PLGA NPs was
investigated using dialysis bag method at pH 7.4 and pH
5.5 to mimic the blood and tumor environment, respectively
[25, 26]. The lyophilized nanoparticles equivalent to 1 mg
of entrapped PTX was dispersed in 500 µL of release media
and transferred to dialysis bag (MWCO 12,000 Da, Sigma-
Aldrich). The dialysis bags were submerged in 15 mL of
phosphate buffer saline pH 7.4 and acetate buffer pH 5.5
containing ­Tween® 80 (1%v/v) separately to maintain the
sink condition. The dialysis assemblies were kept on shaker
bath at 100 strokes per minute at 37 °C. At predetermined
time points, 1 mL aliquots were withdrawn from release
medium and were replaced with equal volume of fresh
release medium. The collected aliquots were then quantified
for PTX concentration using validated RP-HPLC method.

In vivo pharmacokinetic study
The pharmacokinetic study was performed on healthy female
Sprague Dawley (~ 200 g) rats procured from Central Animal
Facility (NIPER S.A.S. Nagar, India) and animal experiments
were carried out as per the directions of the Committee for
the Purpose of Control and Supervision of Experiments on
Animals (CPCSEA), India (Institute Animal Ethics Commit-
tee approval number: IAEC/19/72). Initially, animals were
acclimatized under the dictated environmental conditions, i.e.,
temperature 25 ± 2 °C; humidity 55 ± 5%RH; 12-h light/dark
cycles. The animals were randomly divided in groups based
on the treatment received like ­Intaxel®, ­Nanoxel®, and PTX-
loaded various PLGA NPs (5 animals in each group). The lyo-
philized PTX-loaded NPs, ­Intaxel®, ­Nanoxel®, and free PTX
(dispersed in the 500 µL deionized water) were administered
at dose 5 mg/kg intravenously through tail vein. The samples
(~ 250 µL) were collected from tail vein at predetermined time
intervals (0.5, 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24, and 48 h) in EDTA-coated
vacutainer tubes. The plasma was extracted by precipitat-
ing the proteins by addition of ACN and methanol, followed
by centrifugation at 3000 RPM for 5 min at 4 °C [30]. The
obtained clear supernatant was subjected to quantification
for PTX concentration using validated RP-HPLC bioanalyti-
cal method, where docetaxel (DTX) was used as an internal
standard. The mobile phase used was ACN:Water (60:40) and
the LOQ for PTX was found to be 122.65 ng/mL. Further,
accuracy of HPLC method was confirmed by performing the
recovery study as per ICH norms at three different concentra-
tions (i.e., 80, 100 and 120%), and average % recovery of PTX
was found to be 98.16%, 99.28%, and 100.93% for 200 ng/mL,
400 ng/mL, and 700 ng/mL, respectively. The drug plasma
concentration–time profile was plotted and various pharma-
cokinetic parameters were calculated using the Kinetica soft-
ware (Thermo Scientific, Waltham, MS).

Qualitative evaluation of bio‑distribution
For this investigation, coumarin-6 (C6)-loaded PLGA NPs

The only change made was the use of C6 instead of PTX.

Statistical analysis
The experiment results are reported as mean standard devia-
tion (SD). ­Prism® (GraphPad Software, Inc. CA, USA) was
used for statistical analysis, with one-way ANOVA followed by
Tukey–Kramer multiple comparison tests and two-way ANOVA

Results and discussion
Preparation of ADN‑conjugated PLGA nanoparticles
Synthesis of PLGA‑PEG‑ADN conjugate
The synthesized product, i.e., PLGA-PEG, PLGA-PEG-
ADN, and PLGA-ADN, were characterized using FTIR and
1H NMR. Detailed explanation of the spectroscopy-based
analysis is given in the supplementary file, Sect. 1.
Preparation and optimization of PTX‑loaded PLGA NPs
The PTX-loaded NPs were prepared by screening surfactant,
in which PVA was found suitable. Later, the concentration
of PVA (2% w/v), amplitude (35%) used for sonication,
and % theoretical drug loading (7.5% w/w) was optimized
(Refer supplementary file, Sect. 2 and Table S1). Based on
the optimized parameters, different PTX-loaded NPs were
prepared (PTX PEG-PLGA-NPs, PTX ADN-PLGA NPs,
and PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs) as shown in Table 1. The
particle size distribution of PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs (PDI
of ~ 0.12) is shown in Fig. 1B. The formed particles showed
spherical morphology with smooth surfaces as shown in
Fig. 1C. Additionally, the obtained size (~ 135 nm) was found
to be in correlation with the NPs observed through HR-TEM.
The lyoprotectants were screened, in which 5% w/v of treha-
lose was observed to exhibit lowest size reconstitution ratio
(supplementary file Table S2). Hence, trehalose was selected
as the suitable lyoprotectant in our further studies.
Storage stability of lyophilized PTX ADN‑PEG‑PLGA
NPs
The accelerated stability study was performed till 6 months.
The lyophilized NPs cake showed no change in terms of
shrinkage or any other change in physical appearance. Fur-
ther, Insignificant (p > 0.05) increase in particle size (from
135 ± 12 to 146 ± 10 nm) and PDI (from 0.119 ± 0.03 to
0.131 ± 0.09) were observed after until 6 months of testing
(Kindly refer supplementary file Table S3).
In vitro drug release
The drug release from NPs demonstrated sustained release
pattern at pH 7.4, wherein PTX PLGA NPs and PTX ADN-
PLGA NPs showed < 15% PTX release and PTX PEG-
PLGA NPs and PTX-ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs showed < 10%
PTX release in 24 h. At the end of 25th day, PTX PLGA
NPs, PTX ADN-PLGA NPs, and PTX PEG-PLGA NPs
showed < 65% PTX release, whereas PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA
NPs showed ~ 59% PTX release (Fig. 1D). However, at pH
5.5, the release rate was observed to be relatively higher than
what was observed at pH 7.4. On day 1, ≥ 20% PTX was
released from non-PEGylated and PEGylated PLGA NPs.
At the end of 25th day, PTX PLGA NPs, PTX ADN-PLGA
NPs, and PTX PEG-PLGA NPs showed ≥ 80% PTX release
and PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs showed ~ 73% PTX release
(Fig. 1E). The % cumulative drug release profile shown by
the prepared NPs at pH 7.4 and 5.5 was different. The faster
release at pH 5.5 was observed because the acidic pH acceler-
ates the hydrolysis of ester bond between lactic acid and gly-
colic acid subunits of polymer [34]. Additionally, drug release
from PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs was slower than PTX PEG-
PLGA as drug experiences additional barrier offered by ADN.

Hemolytic toxicity
Haemolytic toxicity profiles of Free PTX, ­Intaxel®, ­Nanoxel®,
and various NPs compared to positive control (Triton-XTM
100) and negative control (PBS, pH 7.4) are depicted in Fig. 1F.
Free PTX and ­Intaxel® showed significantly higher toxicity
(P < 0.0001) as compared to negative control PBS. On the other
hand, there was insignificant difference (P > 0.05) in % hemolysis
between other tested formulations and PBS control group. How-
ever, ­Nanoxel® showed no significant difference in hemolytic
potential when compared with PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs.
Also, the morphology of RBCs observed through SEM
exhibited similar trend as observed in spectrophotometric
absorbance method. The SEM images of RBCs incubated
with respective groups is depicted in Fig. 1G. The PLGA
NPs and ­Nanoxel® exhibited similar morphological attrib-
utes of RBC when compared with vehicle group, indicating
suitable hemocompatibility. The hemotoxicity observed for
­Intaxel® was found to be correlating with our previously
published data [18, 19, 40]. Additionally, the hemotoxicity
results can be well correlated with the in vitro release data.
The PLGA NPs restrict the direct exposure of drug to the
RBCs by sustaining the release for longer duration, whereas
in the case of ­Intaxel®, which is a PTX solution-based sys-
tem, there is a direct exposure of PTX with RBCs leading to
increase in % hemolysis. Similarly, ­Nanoxel® being an NP-
based delivery system hinders the direct exposure of PTX
with RBCs that result in its low hemolytic potential [25].
Cell culture studies
Qualitative and quantitative cellular uptake
In qualitative cellular uptake, C6-loaded PLGA NPs and free C6
were incubated with TNBC cells and observed using CLSM,
the images are shown in Fig. 2A. The ADN-conjugated NPs
showed higher fluorescence than free C6 and NPs without ADN
modification. This indicates that ADN assists in improving the
internalization of PLGA NPs which is desirable. To confirm
the role of ADN receptors in internalization, dipyridamole was
used to block the ADN receptors on TNBC cells. The blockade
of ADN receptors resulted in drastic decrease in fluorescence
in both the cell lines that were incubated with C6-loaded ADN-
PEG-PLGA NPs. This inferred that the ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs
exhibit ADN receptor-mediated internalization [36].
The quantitative cellular uptake also revealed similar results
as that of qualitative cellular uptake. Higher PTX content was
observed from the cells incubated with ADN-modified PLGA
NPs than ADN non-modified NPs, as shown in Fig. 2B. The
time-dependent internalization showed that after 2 h of incuba-
tion, there was insignificant increase in PTX content within the
cell lysate. This could have been observed due to the saturation
of ADN receptors that resulted in insignificant increase in par-
ticle uptake when incubated beyond 2 h. Additionally, the role
of ADN receptor in internalization of PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA
NPs was confirmed as significantly reduced PTX levels were
observed from cells that were incubated with ADN receptor
blocker. Thus, the cellular uptake and ADN receptor blockade
assay strengthens our hypothesis of using ADN as targeting
ligand for developed PLGA NPs [41].
Cytotoxicity study
The effect of free PTX and PTX-loaded PLGA NPs on %
cell viability is shown in Fig. 3A. In both the cell lines,
PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs showed superior cytotoxicity
than the ADN non-modified PLGA NPs and free PTX.
The PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs showed ­IC50 value of
3.42 µg/mL and 3.16 µg/mL in MDA-MB-231 and 4T1
cells respectively. If the efficacy is compared with free
PTX, then ~ 3.77- and 3.51-fold reduction in ­IC50 values
were observed for PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs in MDA-
MB-231 and 4T1 cells respectively. Similar comparison
of ­IC50 values of other tested groups with free PTX is
shown in Fig. 3B. The cytotoxicity data was found to be
in accordance with the results observed in cellular uptake.
The cellular uptake indicated that the ADN-modified
PLGA NPs were able to internalize more effectively in
TNBC cells in comparison to non-modified cells due to
ADN receptor-mediated uptake [41].
Thus, higher cellular uptake would have resulted in
higher PTX content inside the cell resulting in increased
cytotoxicity in the case of PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs.

In vivo pharmacokinetic study
The pharmacokinetic profile, i.e., the plasma drug concen-
tration vs the time profile of PTX when delivered as free
PTX solution, as PTX-loaded PLGA NPs, or through mar-
keted formulations like ­Intaxel® and ­Nanoxel®, are shown

(107,894 ± 4081 ng/mL × h) was significantly higher than its
marketed counterparts, ­Intaxel® (21,521 ± 927 ng/mL × h),
and ­Nanoxel® (54,635 ± 2576 ng/mL × h). The AUC(0-∞) of
PTX PLGA NPs (66,562 ± 3042 ng/mL × h) was also found
to be significantly lower than PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs.
The t1/2 of PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs (23.76 ± 3.17 h)
was found to significantly higher than that of ­Intaxel®
(4.35 ± 0.95 h) and PTX PLGA NPs (13.45 ± 2.11 h).
However, the PTX PEG-PLGA NPs and PTX ADN-
PEG-PLGA NPs exhibited an improved AUC(0-∞) and
t1/2 (p < 0.01) in comparison to the marketed formulation
­Nanoxel®. The half-life of PTX was increased by ~ 3.09-,
2.09-, 6.92-, and 5.46-fold in the case of PTX PLGA NPs,
PTX ADN-PLGA NPs, PTX PEG-PLGA NPs, and PTX
ADN-PEG-PLGA NP, respectively, when compared to
­Intaxel®. A probable reason for such observation is that the
PLGA NPs exhibit a sustained release behavior (as observed
in in vitro release study) that prevents the PTX clearance
[43]. Additionally, PEGylation of NPs help in improving
the MRT of particles leading to improved exposure of PTX
to desired site of action. The MRT of PTX ADN-PEG-

higher than MRT of ­Intaxel® (5.47 ± 1.39 h), ­Nanoxel®

Conclusion
The developed PTX-loaded PLGA NPs, having ADN as the
targeting ligand and PEG as the linker, showed improved
anticancer activity in comparison to free PTX and marketed
counterpart ­Intaxel®. The ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs achieved
loading of 7.5% w/w, which is sufficient for in vivo dos-
ing and for achieving the desired therapeutic concentration.
The particles exhibited pH-dependent release behavior,
i.e., slow release at physiological pH and higher release at
acidic (tumor microenvironment) pH. Such slow-release
behavior at physiological pH prevented direct exposure of
PTX to RBCs and exhibited hemocompatibility. The ADN
modification over PLGA NPs render higher internalization
of particles that resulted in > 3.5-fold reduction in ­IC50 val-
ues in TNBC cells. Further, the ADN modification allowed
particles to exhibit higher apoptosis index in TNBC cells
when compared to non-modified PLGA NPs and free PTX
group. The PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs exhibited improved
pharmacokinetic parameters (AUC, MRT, and t1/2) in com-
parison to ­Intaxel®, other non-targeted and non-PEGylated
PLGA particles.
ADN modification assisted PLGA NPs in achieving
tumor specific distribution and retention which resulted
in lower organ toxicity and insignificant alteration in the
kidney and liver biomarker levels when compared to con-
trol group. Further, PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs showed
superior anti-cancer efficacy (in terms of tumor volume
reduction and % tumor burden) when compared to all other
tested groups including ­Intaxel® and ­Nanoxel®. Overall, the
developed strategy, i.e., to involve ADN as targeting ligand
for TNBC, has been successfully explored with promising
results and can be explored further for managing TNBC.
