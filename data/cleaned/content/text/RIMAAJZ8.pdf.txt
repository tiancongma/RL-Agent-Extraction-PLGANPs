INTRODUCTION
The application of nanotechnology in the biomed
ical field is undergoing rapid progress, especially in the
production of nanocarriers for drug delivery. The
nanometer sizeranges of these delivery systems offer
distinct advantages for drug delivery [1]. Diverse
nanocarriers, such as polymeric nanoparticles, poly
meric micelles, niosomes, nanoemulsions, lipid nano
particles, liposomes and nanogels have been utilized to
deliver various types of drugs [2]. Along with diverse
drug delivery systems, polymeric nanoparticles are
excellent carriers for drug delivery due to their unique
properties such as stability, suitable size, and appropri
ate drug release [3, 4].
According to previous authors, nanoparticles have
been extensively investigated in drug delivery systems
because their particle size range from 10 to 1000 nm
which is acceptable [5]. During the past decade,
amphiphilic copolymers have attracted considerable
attention not only in basic science, but also in biomed
ical use, particularly for drug carriers [6]. Amongst all
the polymers, biodegradable and biocompatible poly
mers like PLGA have immense potential for diverse
biomedical application, particularly in the areas of
diagnosis and tissue engineering, and as drug delivery

agents [7ŌĆō9]. The two main problems that hinder the
application of polymeric nanoparticlebased drug
delivery systems in biological fields are that of the best
possible formulation with appreciable drug loading
and the desired sustained release profile [10], and the
toxicity of the nanoparticulate system [11]. The tox
icity of nanoparticles is minimized by using biode
gradable materials.
PLGA is one of the most successfully used biode
gradable and biocompatible polymers. The hydrolysis
of PLGA produces lactic acid and glycolic acid [12].
These are easily metabolized by the body via the tricar
boxylic acid cycle [13]. PLGA is approved by the
United State Food and Drug Administration (US
FDA) and European Medicine Agency (EMA) in var
ious drug delivery systems and it commercially exists
with different molecular weights and copolymer com
positions [14, 15].

AbstractŌĆöNanoscience have become an extended field worldwide because nanomaterials offer various novels
and exclusive properties. The properties at the nano scale are dependent on the size, the morphology, and the
components. There are various types of nanocarrier systems presently being investigated and explored for drug
delivery applications. Polymeric nanoparticles are promising drug delivery systems whose formulation and opti
mization can be profitably conducted by theoretical methods. The different synthetic polymers are successfully
used to prepare polymeric nanoparticles. Poly(lacticcoglycolic acid) (PLGA) has attracted much attention
due to its attractive characteristics such as biodegradability and biocompatibility. The aim of this study was to
prepare PLGA nanoparticles containing hydrochlorothiazide by using the emulsionsolvent evaporation
method and to study the effect of different formulation variables such as sonication time, polymer content, the
amount of surfactant, volume of aqueous phase and drug content. The size and shape of nanoparticles were
characterized by Dynamic light scattering (DLS) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM). The Zeta
potential study was also performed to understand the surface charge of nanoparticles. The drug release from
drug loaded nanoparticles was studied by dialysis bag method and the in vitro drug release data was also studied
by various kinetic models. Hopefully we produced spherical shape sustained release hydrochlorothiazide loaded
PLGA nanoparticles with a size range under 300 nm with zeta potential ŌĆō27.9 mV.

The IUPAC name of hydrochlorothiazide is 6chloro
1,1dioxo3,4dihydro2H1,2,4benzothiadiazine
7sulfonamide. It is frequently used to treat hyperten
sion, congestive heart failure, and the prevention of
kidney stones [16].
On the basis of literature reviewed, it was found that
several authors reported the preparation of PLGA
nanoparticles loaded with different model drugs, but
none of them previously reported the preparation and
optimization of PLGA nanoparticles containing
Hydrochlorothiazide by emulsionsolvent evapora
tion method.
The purpose of this study was to develop and char
acterize a new formulation of Hydrochlorothiazide
using polymer based nanoparticles. Nanoparticles
were synthesized using an emulsionsolvent evapora
tion technique and the effects of sonication time,
polymer concentration, amount of surfactant, volume
of aqueous phase and drug content was examined for
effects on particle size and morphology. The in vitro
drug release and the drug release data of drug loaded
nanoparticles was studied by dialysis bag method and
various kinetic models respectively.
The significance of this work is to explain the effect
of different parameters scientifically which are
responsible to control the size of nanoparticles pre
pared by this method. These results are reproducible
and therefore generalized as standard for the prepara
tion of nanoparticles loaded with other hydrophobic
drugs for sustained drug delivery.
EXPERIMENTAL
Materials
The polymer used to be PLGA (RESOMER┬« RG
504 molecular weight range is (3.8ŌĆō5.4) ├Ś 104 and
inherent viscosity is 0.45ŌĆō0.60 dL/g), with dllactide
to glycolide copolymer ratio as 50 : 50 obtained from
Evonik Mumbai (India). Hydrochlorothiazide drug
was received as gift sample from IPCA Ltd. Mumbai
(India). The surfactant used to be poly (vinyl alcohol)
(PVA) purchased from Sigma Aldrich New Delhi
(India). All other chemicals were of analytical reagent
grade. Purified water of MilliQ quality was used dur
ing the entire experiment.
Preparation of Nanoparticles
Nanoparticles were prepared by using emulsion
solvent evaporation method [17], an organic phase
containing polymer (PLGA) and drug (hydrochlo
rothiazide, usual concentration) was dissolved in ace
tone (usual volume). This organic phase was added to
an aqueous phase consisting of the surfactant (PVA,
usual concentration) to produce an emulsion. This
emulsion was broken down into nanodroplets by the
application of external energy (through an ultrasonic
probe sonicator, Leela Electronics, Mumbai, India)
and these nanodroplets produced nanoparticles by the
evaporation of the organic solvent, which was highly
volatile. Organic solvent was evaporated while mag
netic stirring at 300 rpm on magnetic stirrer (Remi,
India) under atmospheric conditions for 4 h leaving
behind a colloidal suspension of PLGA nanoparticles.
Hydrochlorothiazide loaded nanoparticles were iso
lated by 30 min centrifugation (WX ultra 100 ultracen
trifuge Thermofisher Scientific USA) at 21000 rpm.
Resultant sedimented nanoparticles were collected,
then lyophilized (YSI250, Yorco Freeze Dryer (Lyo
philizer), Yorco Sales Pvt. Ltd., India).
Characterization of DrugLoaded Nanoparticles
The average particle size and zeta potential were
measured by using the Zetasizer (ModelZEN 3600,
Malvern Zetasizer, U.K.) utilizing dynamic light scat
tering (DLS) technique.
The zeta potential of a particle is the overall charge
of the particle which is acquired by it in a particular
medium. In this technique, a voltage was applied
across a pair of electrodes at either end of a cell con
taining the particle dispersion. Charged particles were
attracted to the oppositely charged electrode and their
velocity was measured and expressed in unit field
strength as their electrophoretic mobility. Samples in
triplicate from the prepared suspensions were diluted
in MilliQ water and placed in the measurement cell
for analysis.
Morphology of the drugloaded nanoparticles was
determined by transmission electron microscopy
(TEM) using a TEM Technai Japan. For TEM analy
sis, a drop of the nanoparticle emulsion was placed on
a Cu grid and stained with 2% phosphotungstic acid
(PTA) solution, and then the image was captured.
Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) was
used to assess potential interactions between formula
tion components. The FTIR spectra of polymer
(PLGA), drug (hydrochlorothiazide) and hydrochlo
rothiazide loaded PLGA nanoparticles were recorded
on KBr plates in the scanning range of 400ŌĆō4000 cmŌĆō1.
These spectra were recorded on a FTIR8400S Fourier
Transform Spectrophotometer (Shimadzu, India).

supernatant (w) was then subtracted from the total
amount of drug added (W). Effectively, this subtrac

loaded PLGA nanoparticles was analyzed by dialysis

performed in triplicate.

Effect of Process Variables on Formulation
Characteristics
A number of process variables were assessed to
identify ideal preparation conditions by using emul
sionsolvent evaporation techniques, such as sonica
tion time, PLGA concentration in the organic phase,
surfactant concentration, aqueous phase volume, and
drug content in the formulation. Only a single variable
was altered in each experiment series.
Effect of Sonication Time
In emulsionsolvent evaporation method, the basic
step is applying the external energy to obtain the emul
sion. This energy is provided by sonication. To verify
the effect of sonication time on nanoparticles size and
morphology, it was varied between 1min and 7 min.
(Results are presented in Fig. 2a). In all cases spherical
particles produced, according to TEM result.
Figure 2b shows the TEM image of nanoparticles with
least size in this series of experimental. From the anal
ysis of results, it can be concluded that the increase in
the sonication time leads to a decrease in the nanopar
ticles size from 380 nm to 270 nm. In this technique
emulsification of sample is one of the most significant
steps of the procedure, because an inadequate disper
sion of phases results in bulky size particles. The ulti
mate size of the nanoparticles depends on the drop
size throughout the emulsification procedure; and the
decrease of the emulsion drop size allows the produc
tion of smaller size nanoparticles [23, 24].
Effect of Polymer Concentration
The polymer concentration was changed ranging
from 10 to 17.5 mg/mL of organic phase and the effect
of the initial amount of polymer on the particles size
and shape was studied, while keeping all process param
eters at standard conditions. According to results,
increasing the polymer concentration ranging 10 to
17.5 mg/mL in the organic phase led to an increment in
nanoparticles size respectively 300 to 410 nm. (Results
are presented in Fig. 3a). On raising the polymer
amount, particles size were enhanced. This type of
observation was also concluded by other authors for
PLGA and other polymers like PLA [25, 26].

Effect of Aqueous Phase Volume
In emulsionsolvent evaporation method aqueous
phase volume is one of the important factors for prep
aration of drug loaded nanoparticles. Aqueous phase
volume varied from 40 to 50 mL while maintaining
other processing variables at standard conditions.
Some authors have reported the preparation of
PLA/PLGA nanoparticles by using salting out method
along with highspeed stirring. They have used ace
tone for preparation of organic phase and thus con
cluded that size of nanoparticles increases with the
increment in aqueous phase volume to organic phase
volume ratio [28]. Here we explore the influence of
volume of aqueous phase on the particle size of drug
loaded PLGA nanoparticles by emulsionsolvent
evaporation method. With augmenting the aqueous
phase volume 40 to 50 mL by keeping fixed amount of
organic phase (5 mL), the size of nanoparticles
extends from 350 nm to 430 nm (results are presented
in Fig. 5a) and TEM image in Fig. 5b. Enhancing the
aqueous phase volume in this method enlarges the size
of nanoparticles, because the applied energy is fixed
which is causing the breakdown of droplets but the
total volume of the system enhances with increasing
the volume of the aqueous phase. The same amount of
energy was then distributed in a larger volume of sys
tem which resulted in the less droplet breakdown and
production of larger nanoparticles.
Effect of Drug Concentration
In this step the inclusion of drug (hydrochlorothi
azide) into PLGA nanoparticles was analyzed. The
amount of hydrochlorothiazide used was varied 1.5 to
2.5 mg/mL of acetone, by keeping all other processing
variables at standard conditions. It can be concluded
that on raising the amount of drug, the size of nano
particles extends from 270 nm to 355 nm; the results
are summarized in Fig. 6a. This can be explained by
the fact that on augmenting the amount of drug in
organic solvent, viscous nature of organic phase
increases, which makes the mutual dispersion of both
phases difficult and produces bigger size nanoparti
cles. TEM experiments (Fig. 6b) showed that the
shape of particles remained spherical in all cases.

CONCLUSION
In the present work the emulsionsolvent evapora
tion method was used to produce spherical hydrochlo
rothiazide loaded PLGA nanoparticles. It demon
strates the potential procedure for controlling the size
of nanoparticles. In this method the process of nano
particle formation was directly proportional to the
reduction of globule size due to the fast diffusion of
organic phase. For the success of this method, the sig
nificant step is the initial stage of the process. The size
of droplets forming during this stage and their stability
are important factors. Preparative variables such as
sonication time, polymer concentration, surfactant
concentration, aqueous phase volume and drug con
tent could be the crucial factors for the production of
PLGA nanoparticles. The release of drug from these
nanoparticles takes place in a sustained manner in
comparison to pure drug. These data are reproducible
and generalized for production of PLGA nanoparti
cles of other hydrophobic model drugs by this method.
