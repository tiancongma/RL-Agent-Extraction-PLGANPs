Abstract
The blood‚Äďbrain barrier (BBB) separates blood from brain tissue, making drug delivery to the brain a significant chal¬≠
lenge. This study aims to develop and optimize a novel nanoparticle system that may overcome the BBB and have a
potential effect on glioblastoma. Ketorolac tromethamine (KT)-loaded poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) RG 503-H
nanoparticles were prepared using water/oil/water (W/O/W) emulsification solvent evaporation. KT-loaded nanoparticles
have a particle size of 155.5‚ÄČ¬Ī‚ÄČ2.08¬†nm, a zeta potential of -12.9‚ÄČ¬Ī‚ÄČ1.23 mV, a polydispersity index of 0.111‚ÄČ¬Ī‚ÄČ0.035, and an
encapsulation efficiency of 53.46%. A significant portion of the drug (>‚ÄČ90%) was released in 6¬†h and completed within
24¬†h. When 3% (w/v) mannitol and trehalose were used as cryoprotectants, the nanoparticles remained physically stable.
Additionally, it has been discovered that KT solution is cytotoxic at high doses to Rat Brain Glioblastoma (RG2) cells,
and KT-loaded nanoparticles are more effective than drug solutions at lower doses.

Introduction
Primary brain tumors have considerable morbidity and death
rates as part of the central nervous system (CNS) tumors [1].
Gliomas are the most common type of brain tumor and are
classified into four grades related to their clinical progres¬≠
sion and histology [2]: non-malignant grades I (pilocytic)
and II (low-grade, well-differentiated), and the malignant
grades III (anaplastic) and IV (glioblastoma multiforme)
[3].
Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) is the most lethal and
prevalent primary brain tumor [4], accounting for 48.3% of
primary malignant brain tumors and 57.3% of all gliomas
[5]. GBM‚Äôs prevalence is higher in older adults than in chil¬≠
dren, and incidence rises with age (the highest rate observed
between 75 and 84 years). Also, men have the GBM 1.58-
fold more than women [5]. The incidence of GBM in Europe
changes depending on population diversity and ranges from
3/100,000 to 5/100,000, while in the USA, it is 3.2/100,000
[6]. Survival after the diagnosis of GBM is generally less
than 20 months, and for the recurrent cases, roughly less
than one year [7].
GBM‚Äôs outline is the immense oxygen deprivation in the
tumor center that causes sweeping necrosis in addition to
superior vascular proliferation and enhanced permeation
capacity of tumor cells [3]. Also, another GBM characteris¬≠
tic is a higher intratumoral heterogeneity [8]. GBM core and
margin tissue display dissimilar molecular features. That‚Äôs
why GBM treatment has gained acceptance as the hardest
defiance in cancer [9].
Conventional treatments, such as chemotherapy, sur¬≠
gery, radiotherapy, and a combination of chemotherapy and
radiotherapy, showed insufficient effects for the remedy
heretofore [4]. Insufficient treatment is related to various
factors, such as tumor heterogeneity, target selection, and
inadequate passage of chemotherapeutic agents through the
blood-brain barrier (BBB) and into tumors [10]. The BBB
separates peripheral circulation from the central nervous
system (CNS) and regulates the outputs and inputs of the
brain. All macromolecules and over 98% of small molecules
(<‚ÄČ400 Da) are ineligible for penetration from the BBB [11].
Despite the fact that there is quite an advancement in the new
chemotherapeutic agent discovery and delivery systems for

Nanoparticles are systems that enable therapeutic and
diagnostic drugs to penetrate target tissues better, control
the release of the active substance they contain, and provide
long-term effects due to the particle sizes, thereby increas¬≠
ing treatment efficacy and reducing side effects [12, 13].
Developing poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) nanopar¬≠
ticles loaded with anticancer agents constitutes a significant
advancement in cancer therapies [14]. These nanoparticles
have been designed to target tumor cells specifically, there¬≠
fore mitigating the potential harm inflicted on healthy cells
and diminishing the occurrence of side effects [15]. The
utilization of nanoparticles enables the regulated release of
drugs over a specific duration, hence providing prolonged
therapeutic efficacy and minimizing the frequency of
administration [12, 16]. From this point of view, they dem¬≠

brain tumors.
Arachidonic acid is converted into prostaglandins by
rate-restricting enzymes, which are called cyclooxygen¬≠
ases (COX). At present, COX has two isozymes: COX-1
and COX-2. While COX-1 is fundamentally produced in
various types of tissue, COX-2 is frequently produced in
inflammatory and multiple cancer cells [17‚Äď19]. Studies
have shown that inflammation is closely related to cancer,
suggesting that eliminating inflammation may be a viable
strategy for preventing and treating cancer [20, 21]. COX-2
inhibitors may provide benefits in combating several can¬≠
cers, depending on their antitumor effects, such as celecoxib
and rofecoxib. The long-standing utilization of COX-2
inhibitors might reduce the prevalence and risk of several
cancers. In one study, celecoxib, an anti-inflammatory drug,
has been shown to have an anticancer effect [22]; in another
study, it was combined with a chemotherapeutic agent and
enhanced its antineoplastic activity [23]. Nonsteroidal anti-
inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) are extensively utilized
for diminishing pain and inflammation worldwide. Even
though the structure of the NSAIDs varies, all of them have
antipyretic, anti-inflammatory, and analgesic efficacy [24].
Ketorolac tromethamine (KT) is a compound consist¬≠
ing of the tromethamine salt of ketorolac. KT is soluble in
water and exhibits additional characteristics, including a pH
of 7.4 and an osmolality of 290 mOsmol/kg. Its pKa value
is 3.5, and it has a molecular weight of 376.41 [25]. KT is
an NSAID that impacts both COX enzymes, which impede
prostaglandin production. KT significantly affects COX-1
activity, as well as that of COX-2‚Äôs; on the other hand, it
has no selectivity for COX-1 over COX-2 [26]. KT exhibits
a high oral bioavailability of 90%; however, its biological
half-life is relatively short, ranging from 3 to 4¬†h [27]. As a
result, it necessitates increased dosing frequency to maintain
therapeutic levels. Meanwhile, the penetration ability of KT
is poor from the BBB [28].
In this present study, KT-loaded PLGA nanoparticles
were designed, and in vitro characterization studies (such as
particle size, surface properties, encapsulation efficacy, in
vitro release, and) and cryoprotectants (mannitol, dextran,
trehalose, glucose, sucrose) effects on particle size, poly¬≠
dispersity index (PDI), and zeta values were determined.
Furthermore, the effect of designed formulations on the via¬≠
bility of RG2 cells was evaluated for possible use in GBM
treatment.
