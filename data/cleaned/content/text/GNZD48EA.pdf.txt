Continuous Supercritical Emulsions Extraction: Packed Tower
Characterization and Application to Poly(lactic-co-glycolic Acid) +
Insulin Microspheres Production
Nunzia Falco, Ernesto Reverchon,* and Giovanna Della Porta
Department of Industrial Engineering, University of Salerno, Via Ponte don Melillo, 1-84084, Fisciano (SA), Italy
ABSTRACT: The continuous supercritical emulsions extraction (SEE-C) process uses a countercurrent packed tower for the
continuous extraction of the organic solvents from emulsions. The continuous operation allows the formation of microspheres
that are recovered at the bottom of the tower in the form of a water suspension, avoiding coalescence problems and batch-to-
batch repeatability, typical of traditional processes. In this work, the packed tower used for SEE-C was characterized from a
ﬂuidodynamic point of view: ﬂooding conditions and the allowable density diﬀerence were calculated from the experimental data.
Then, an active principle (insulin) was tested for its encapsulation in poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) microspheres, starting
from a double emulsion: particle size, morphology, and encapsulation eﬃciency of the produced microdevices were evaluated.
Spherical, not collapsed microspheres with a mean diameter between 1.8 μm (SD ± 0.9) and 4.8 μm (SD ± 2.8) and
encapsulation eﬃciencies up to 70% were obtained. The very fast solvent extraction rate did not inﬂuence the particle
morphology but had a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the size distribution of the precipitates which were always smaller than the starting
droplets. A solvent residue (ethyl acetate) lower than about 600 ppm was obtained in all the experiments performed.
1. INTRODUCTION
Eﬃcient delivery of pharmaceutical compounds to target cells
or organs is receiving considerable attention in medicine. In the
last years, biodegradable microspheres have been extensively
investigated as controlled release dosage forms. Most traditional
devices are realized suspending the bioactive agents in a
polymeric monolith and surgically implanting them into the
body or encapsulating the bioactive agents in polymeric
microspheres and injecting them subcutaneously.1 In the case
of proteins, their intrinsic physicochemical properties severely
hinders the development of new protein-based therapeutics.2
One major reason is the inherent instability of proteins, when
they are exposed to the usual conditions during microparticle
fabrication, using conventional emulsion/solvent removal
techniques.
The most common method for the encapsulation of proteins
into biodegradable microspheres is the formation of a water-in-
oil-in-water (w-o-w) double emulsion followed by the
evaporation of the solvent contained in the oil phase. This is
a simple technique which shows several drawbacks; indeed (1)
it is diﬃcult to control size and distribution of the
microspheres, due to the long processing times required;3,4
(2) proteins, due to their hydrophilic nature, tend to diﬀuse
into the continuous external aqueous phase during the
evaporation step, leading to protein loss and, therefore, to
lower drug encapsulation eﬃciency;5 (3) the process is
intrinsically discontinuous and problems of batch-to-batch
reproducibility are common as well as scale-up diﬃculties to
industrial dimensions. A high batch reproducibility is
particularly relevant for biomedical and pharmaceutical
applications because the approval of a manufacturing process
by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) requires the
demonstration that all batches give a product with identical
characteristics; (4) the three-dimensional structure of proteins,
which is crucial for their biological activity, can be damaged by
heat and mechanical stresses, also determining aggregation and
formation of insoluble precipitates.6,7
To overcome the solvent evaporation/extraction limitations,
supercritical extraction of emulsions technology (SEE) was
recently proposed in the literature. Some authors reported the
use of supercritical carbon dioxide (SC-CO2) to eliminate the
organic solvent from oil-in-water emulsions for the preparation
of drug microparticles of megesterol acetate or cholesterol and
for the formation of lipid microspheres charged with
ketoprofen and indometacin.8,9 Other authors produced poly
lactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA) microspheres with mean sizes
ranging between 1 and 3 μm, loaded with diﬀerent anti-
inﬂammatory drugs.10,11 These studies conﬁrmed that, due to
the enhanced mass transfer of supercritical ﬂuids, the extraction
of the oily phase of an emulsion can be very fast (minutes
instead of hours) and, therefore, only slight modiﬁcations of the
droplets distribution are produced during solvent elimination.
The major limitation of the SEE is that the process remains
intrinsically discontinuous, sharing this inconvenience with
traditional solvent evaporation/extraction techniques. Indeed, a
batch of emulsion can be treated for each run by SEE, with
problems of repeatability of the batches and reduction of the
process yield due to the material lost.10 For these reasons, a
new SEE conﬁguration has been proposed (SEE-C) using a
countercurrent packed tower,12,13 which takes advantage of the
similarities of this process with the classical absorption/
stripping procedure, in which mass transfer between the liquid
and the gaseous phase is obtained. The major diﬀerence with

The objective of this work is ﬁrst to characterize the packed
tower used for SEE-C from a ﬂuid dynamic point of view.
Indeed, the hydrodynamic behavior strongly depends on
parameters, such as, the density diﬀerence of the two phases,
the kind of internal packing, and the relative amount of the
phases ﬂowing in the tower. Then, using the optimized
conditions, the process is applied to the production of
controlled-size protein-loaded biopolymer microspheres. Poly-
(D,L-lactic-co-glycolic acid) 50:50 copolymer (PLGA 50:50)
has been chosen as the biopolymer, due to its safety in
biomedical preparations and its approval for human use.14
Insulin has been chosen as the model protein to be
encapsulated within PLGA microspheres for its wide use in
the treatment of diabetes mellitus.15−19 Characterization of the
produced microspheres in terms of morphology, size
distribution, and encapsulation eﬃciencies is also proposed.
2. EXPERIMENTAL METHODS
2.1. Materials and Emulsion Preparation. CO2 (99.9%
SON; Naples, Italy), polyvinyl alcohol (PVA; mol wt, 30 000−
55 000; Sigma-Aldrich Co.; Milan, Italy), ethyl acetate (EA;
purity 99.9%; Carlo Erba Reagents; Milan, Italy), glycerol
(GLY; purity 99%; Sigma-Aldrich Co.; Milan, Italy), poly-
(lactic/glycolic) acid (PLGA 50:50; RESOMER RG 504H; mol
wt, 38 000−54 000; Boehringer Ingelheim; Germany), insulin
from bovine pancreas (INS; purity 99.9%; Sigma-Aldrich Co.;
Milan, Italy), triﬂuoroacetic acid (TFA; Carlo Erba Reagents;
Milan, Italy), and HPLC grade acetonitrile (Carlo Erba
Reagents; Milan, Italy) were used as received.
2.2. Double Emulsion Preparation. Water-in-oil-in-water
(w1-o-w2) emulsion (1:19:80 w/w) was prepared to encapsu-
late insulin in PLGA because this protein is sparingly soluble in
water and insoluble in organic solvents. Therefore, to entrap
insulin by the w1-o-w2 emulsion method, it is mandatory to set
the aqueous pH below the insulin isoelectric point (pI = 5.5),
where the basic amino groups become protonated.20
A known amount of insulin was dissolved into 1 mL of an
aqueous/TFA GLY solution (250 μL of GLY and 750 μL of
water with 0.1% TFA) containing a PVA concentration of
0.04% w/w and used as the inner water phase (w1); a known
amount of PLGA was dissolved into EA to form an organic
solution (o), which was used as the oil phase. The water phase
was mixed with the oily phase to form a primary w1-o emulsion
upon 90 s of sonication using a digital ultrasonic probe at 50%
of the amplitude (model S-450D, Branson Ultrasonics
Corporation, Danbury, CT). Then, the ﬁrst emulsion was

2.3. Continuous Supercritical Emulsion Extraction
(SEE-C) Apparatus Description. A schematic representation
of the SEE-C process layout is reported in Figure 1. The
apparatus consists of a packed column with an internal
diameter of 13 mm, in which carbon dioxide (C) is fed from
the bottom of the column using a high-pressure diaphragm
pump (SC_P, mod. Milroyal B, Milton Roy, Pont Saint-Pierre,
France), and the emulsion (E) is delivered from the top of the
column using a high-pressure piston pump (L_P, mod. 305,
Gilson, Villiers-le-Bel, France). The column is formed by three
AISI 316 stainless steel cylindrical sections of 30 cm height
connected by four cross-unions and is packed with stainless
steel packings 4 mm nominal size with 1889 m−1 speciﬁc
surface and 0.94 of voidage (0.16 in. Pro-Pak, Scientiﬁc
Development Company, State College, PA). The apparatus is
thermally insulated by ceramic cloths and its temperature is
controlled by six controllers (TC1-TC6, Gordon J/Series 93,
Watlow, Milan, Italy) at diﬀerent heights of the column. The
extracted solvent is recovered in a separator (S) put
downstream the top of the column in which the pressure is
regulated by a backpressure valve (V5, 26-1700 series, Tescom,
Selmsdorf, Germany). A rotameter (R, model N5-2500, ASA,
Sesto San Giovanni, Italy) put at the exit of the separator
measures the CO2 ﬂow rate delivered. The microspheres
suspension is continuously collected from the bottom of the
column. The start-up of the extraction process begins delivering
SC-CO2 from the bottom of the column until the operating
pressure is reached. Then, the emulsion delivery is started at
the top of the column and the two ﬂow rates are set at ﬁxed
values.
2.4. Droplets and Microspheres Morphology and Size.
The droplets formed in the emulsions were observed using an
optical microscope (model BX 50 Olympus, Tokyo, Japan)
equipped with a phase contrast condenser. A ﬁeld emission-
scanning electron microscope (FE-SEM model LEO 1525, Carl
Zeiss SMT AG, Oberkochen, Germany) was used to study the
morphology of the produced microspheres. A sample of
powder was dispersed on a carbon tab previously stuck to an
aluminum stub. Samples were coated with gold (layer thickness
250 Å) using a sputter coater (model 108 A, Agar Scientiﬁc,
Stansted, U.K.).
Droplet size distributions (DSDs) and particle size
distributions (PSDs) were measured by dynamic light
scattering (DLS, model Mastersizer S, Malvern Instruments
Ltd., Worcherstershire, U.K.). The Mastersizer S software uses
Mie theory to produce an optimal analysis of the light energy
distribution and to obtain the size distribution of the particles.
Analyses were performed immediately after the preparation of
the emulsion and of the microsphere suspension, using several
milligrams of sample. The distributions proposed in this paper
are the mean of several DLS analysis.
2.5. Solvent Residue Analysis. Ethyl acetate content in
the ﬁnal suspensions was analyzed to determine the eﬃciency
of solvent removal from the emulsion during SC-CO2
extraction. The solvent residue was measured using a head
space sampler (model 50 Scan, Hewlett & Packard, Palo Alto,
CA) coupled to a gas chromatograph interfaced with a ﬂame
ionization detector (GC-FID, model 6890 Agilent series,
Agilent Technologies Inc., Wilmington, DE). Ethyl acetate
was separated using a fused-silica capillary column 30 m length,
0.25 mm internal diameter, 0.25 μm ﬁlm thickness (model DB-
1, J&W, Folsom, CA). GC conditions were oven temperature at
40 °C for 8 min. The injector was maintained at 180 °C (split
mode, ratio 1:1), and helium was used as the carrier gas (7 mL/
min). Head space conditions were equilibration time 60 min at
100 °C, pressurization time 2 min, loop ﬁll time 1 min. Head
space samples were prepared in 10 mL vials ﬁlled with 3 mL of
suspension. Analyses were performed on each sample in three
replicates. Calculation of the concentration of ethyl acetate in
microsphere samples was based on the standard calibration
curve obtained using diﬀerent EA/water solutions at known
concentrations (8, 80, 800 ppm).
2.6. Protein Loading Determination. The amount of
protein loaded into the microspheres was measured by
accurately weighing 10 mg of dried microspheres, dissolved
in 600 μL of acetonitrile in centrifuge tubes and sonicated until
complete transparency. Then, 1400 μL of water containing
0.1% TFA (0.1% aqueous TFA solution) were added to the
sonicated mixture to dissolve insulin completely. The remaining
undissolved PLGA was separated by centrifugation at 2000 rpm
for 2 min. The clear supernatant was then withdrawn, and the
protein concentration in the resulting clear solution was directly
analyzed at room temperature by HPLC (model 1200 series;
Agilent Technologies Inc.) equipped with a LiCrosphere C18
column (250 mm × 4.6 mm), packed with 5 μm particles of
100 Å pore size. The mobile phase was composed of an
acetonitrile/water mixture (32:68 v/v) containing 0.1% v/v
TFA. The ﬂow rate was 0.9 mL/min, the injected volume was
20 μL, and the detecting wavelength was 214 nm. The amount
of measured insulin, calculated from a calibration curve, was
then converted in the eﬀective insulin loaded into micro-
spheres.

2.7. Correlations for Flooding Point Calculation.
Flooding point can be calculated using standard procedures
based on the use of graphs reported in the literature. The ﬁrst is
the “Generalized Pressure Drop Correlation” (GPDC),21 that
describes the balance between the up-ﬂowing gas momentum
force and the gravitational forces acting on the liquid droplets;
it is a function of the physical properties of the gas and of the
liquid stream; therefore, mass ﬂow rates, gas and liquid
densities, viscosity of the liquid, as well as a packing factor
must be known. The other two correlations tested are the
“Brunner correlation”, that is the GPDC correlation modiﬁed
by Brunner for towers operating with supercritical CO2,22 and
the “Random packing correlation”(RPC).23 The correlation
proposed by Brunner allows one to calculate ﬂooding points for

packed columns with structured packings. Particularly, the gas
capacity factor on the ordinate is a measure of the gas velocity
necessary to suspend a liquid droplet, and the ﬂow parameter
on the abscissa rates the kinetic energy of the liquid phase to
the kinetic energy of the gaseous phase. The “Random packing
correlation” depends on liquid and gas ﬂow rates, surface area
of packing per unit lower volume, fractional void volume of dry
packing, liquid viscosity, local acceleration due to gravity, liquid
and gas densities. This last correlation shows three separate
curves: the intermediate curve can be used for random
packings, the upper curve is used for stacked packings, and
the lower curve is useful for indicating an approximate lower
limit when random packings are loaded.
3. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
3.1. Selection of the Operative Conditions and
Flooding Calculations. In the classical interpretation of a
liquid extraction process, mass transfer is concentrated at the
interface between the two ﬁlms formed by the liquid and gas
phase.24 This schematization can be also applied to the SEE-C
process; therefore, mass transfer between the liquid and the
dense gas can take place at the interface between the ﬁlm of
liquid moving downward on the surface of the packing and the
ﬁlm of SC-CO2 moving upward in the column. Because of the
interactions between the two countercurrent ﬂow rates, mass
transfer between the two phases is activated; but, variations in
the top and bottom product compositions are observed until
steady state conditions have been obtained.
The use of packed towers is commonly not recommended in
processes involving the presence of a solid phase; indeed, it is
expected that the solid precipitates from the liquid phase and
deposits on the packing surface, producing a progressive
reduction of the mass transfer and, subsequently, the blockage
of the process. SEE-C preliminary experiments demonstrated,
instead, that at least for particles up to about 50 μm diameter,
the solid does not deposit on the packings, since the
microspheres remain suspended in the falling ﬁlm of liquid
(i.e., the emulsion external phase) inside the column; the
presence of the surfactant on the particles and on the packing
also allows the particles to slip on the solid surfaces.12
In a countercurrent packed column, eﬃcient mass transfer
conditions are reached when the liquid ﬂowing down over the
packing begins to be held in the void spaces between the
packing; this is called the loading condition and the pressure
drop in the column begins to increase. Further increases in gas
or liquid velocity will produce further increases of the pressure
drop, until the liquid completely ﬁlls the void spaces in the
packing, hindering the gas ﬂow; therefore, the liquid cannot
ﬂow down through the column and it is forced to ﬂow up,
together with the gas stream. This condition is referred to as
ﬂooding and the column is no more operative. Given a packed
column, for a ﬁxed gas rate, there is a deﬁnite liquid rate above
which the column will ﬂood; similarly, at any given liquid rate,
there is a deﬁnite gas rate above which the column gives
ﬂooding. Moreover, the ﬂooding point depends on the density
diﬀerence between the two phases involved. When emulsions
are used in the column, it may be also possible to have ﬂooding

however, in that case, structured packings have been used that

interactions with the liquid. We have used, instead, very small

3.2. Insulin-Loaded PLGA Microspheres: Size Tailoring
and Morphology. In the second part of the work, we applied
the SEE-C process to the production of insulin-loaded PLGA
microspheres. For all the SEE-C experiments, extraction
pressure and temperature conditions were ﬁxed at 80 bar and
38 °C and a L/G ratio of 0.1 was also selected, according to the
previous discussions and to the following considerations: (1)
the temperature is compatible with several drugs or peptides to
be encapsulated and lower than the PLGA glass transition that
occurs at about 40 °C;26 (2) EA is completely miscible in SC-
CO2;27 (3) the solubility of the EA-water external phase in SC-
CO2 is very small and can be neglected.28−30
The microsphere size is mainly related to the emulsion
formulation parameters: indeed, the decrease of the stirring rate
or the increase of the polymer concentration in the oily phase
during the emulsion preparation can produce larger drop-
lets.11,31 In this work, the inﬂuence of the emulsion stirring rate
was measured on droplets/microspheres size and size
distribution, decreasing the stirring rate from 2800 to 1000
rpm, to explore the possibility of microspheres size tailoring. A
constant PLGA concentration of 10% w/w in the organic phase
was used for all these experiments.
Examples of the emulsions produced at diﬀerent stirring rates
are shown by the optical microscope images reported in Figure
4 (left side). All the emulsions were formed by stable,
noncoalescing droplets. After SC-CO2 solvent extraction in
the continuous tower, microspheres were produced that
maintained the spherical shape of the starting droplets and
formed no aggregates, as shown in the corresponding FE-SEM
images also reported in Figure 4 (right side). DSDs of the
emulsions and PSDs of the related microspheres, obtained by
laser scattering analysis, are reported in Figure 5. The
experimental evidence is that the microspheres are smaller
and the distributions are narrower than the droplet mean sizes
and distributions from which they were generated, due to a
shrinkage of the droplets during solvent elimination.
The PSDs curves are comparatively reported in Figure 6 and
conﬁrm the general evidence obtained in Figures 4 and 5.
However, further information can be qualitatively deduced
from this ﬁgure and quantitatively measured by the data
summarized in Table 4: the mode of the three distributions is
very similar; whereas, the PSD strongly enlarges when the
stirring rate is reduced. This result is in good agreement with
those observed by other authors31 and may be explained as
follows: there is a minimum droplet size distribution that can
be stabilized by the surfactant molecules and it is reasonable
that, at higher energy inputs, this lower bound is gradually
approached.
3.3. Insulin Encapsulation Eﬃciency. Encapsulation
eﬃciency of proteins within polymeric microspheres is a
complex phenomenon controlled by several factors. There are
usually three main factors which are considered to aﬀect the
drug encapsulation eﬃciency during emulsiﬁcation and solid-
iﬁcation of double emulsions: (1) coalescence between the
inner water phase and the outer water phase, leading to the
leakage of the drug into the outer water phase. Therefore, the
more stable the inner water phase, the higher is the drug
encapsulation eﬃciency. For example, insulin molecules are
highly loaded in PLGA microspheres when a hydrophilic
component is added to the primary solution. In the speciﬁc
emulsions prepared in this work, glycerol was added to the
inner water phase because it is more eﬃcient due to its higher
viscosity.32 (2) Coalescence and break-up of double emulsions
also leads to the leakage of the drug; (3) diﬀusion of the drug
from the inner to the outer water phase through the oil phase,
during the processes of droplets formation and solvent
removal.5,33
In this work, the eﬀect of the microsphere size on the
encapsulation eﬃciency was investigated: when insulin was
entrapped in larger microspheres, higher encapsulation
eﬃciencies were obtained (Table 5). This result is also
conﬁrmed by traditional process literature data, where it is
reported that when insulin was entrapped in large micro-
particles, larger entrapment eﬃciencies were obtained.34,35
However, SEE-C is more eﬃcient than solvent evaporation,
since the encapsulation eﬃciencies obtained in this work (up to
70%, see Table 5) are larger than those observed in previous
traditional studies.36
Ethyl acetate residues varying between 300 and 600 ppm
were measured in all the PLGA/INS suspensions recovered
after the continuous process. These values are considerably
lower than the residual EA limits acceptable in pharmaceuticals,
ﬁxed at 5000 ppm by the European Pharmacopoeia, conﬁrming
the eﬃciency of the SEE-C process in solvent removal.
4. CONCLUSIONS
SEE-C processing oﬀers a new strategy in the biopolymer
microspheres production with a better particle size control and
good encapsulation eﬃciency and is especially useful when
thermolabile compounds, such as proteins, are processed.
Insulin-loaded PLGA microspheres have been produced in a
robust and reproducible mode in only few minutes. Greater
product uniformity, higher throughput with small plant
volumes, and the elimination of the batch-to-batch repeatability
problems have been obtained, together with lower solvent
residue and good drug encapsulation eﬃciency. Drug release
studies from these microspheres are in progress, together with
insulin stability tests. No insulin degradation products have
until now been found during its release from the PLGA
microspheres.
