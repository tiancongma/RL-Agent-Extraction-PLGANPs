## Materials Methods
In order to overcome the MDR of SF and improve the antitumor efficacy

of HCC, we developed a nano delivery system based on poly (lactide-

glycolic acid copolymer) (PLGA)-TPGS matrix for SF delivery (SPTNs).

On the one hand, TPGS as a surfactant was used in the preparation of

PLGA nanoparticles to improve the encapsulation efficiency of SF. On

the other hand, TPGS can improve the tumor cellular uptake and

aggregation of antitumor drugs as P-gp inhibitor by reversing MDR.

Intravenously administrated SPTNs can spontaneously accumulate at

the tumor site by EPR effect. Then, the released TPGS suppressed the

expression of the MDR1 gene and P-gp, which facilitated the diffusion of

SF into the tumor cells. This research focused on whether the

incorporation of TPGS into PLGA nanoparticles could improve the

antitumor efficacy against MDR in vitro.

SF (> 99.9% purity) and TPGS were obtained from Shanghai Yuanye

Biomaterial Co., Ltd (Shanghai, China). PLGA (8.8-11.7 kDa) was

purchased from Daigang Biomaterial Co., Ltd (Jinan, China). 3-(4,5-

Dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5 diphenyltetrazolium bromide (MTT) was

provided by Solarbio Life Sciences Co., Ltd (Beijing, China). TRIzol

reagent (Roswell Park Memorial Institute 1640) was obtained from

Thermo Fisher Scientific (Waltham, MA, USA). Dimethyl sulfoxide, DAPI,

sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS), antifade reagents, penicillin, streptomycin,

Annexin V-FITC apoptosis detection kit and BCA protein assay kit were

supplied by Beyotime Biotechnology Co., Ltd (Shanghai, China). Fetal

bovine serum (FBS) was obtained from Sigma (St. Louis, MO, USA).

Coumarin-6 (C6) was obtained from Baoman Biotechnology Co., Ltd

(Shanghai, China). The Quanti Nova SYBR green RT-PCR kit was

purchased from Qiagen (Dusseldorf, Germany).

(Thermo 3111, Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA).

In Vitro Drug Release

The in vitro release of SF from SPTNs was studied by the dialysis

the end of the treatment, 20 mL of MTT solution was added into each well

for further 4 h to stain the cells. After removing the MTT solution, the

incubation medium was replaced with 150 mL of dimethyl sulfoxide. The

reader (Molecular Devices, Sunnyvale, CA, USA) at 490 nm. The cell

variability and half-maximal inhibitory concentration (IC50) were

calculated as previously reported .

Cell Apoptosis Assay

To study the changes about the nucleus, we seeded cells (1 x 105) into

wells of 6-well plates and incubated for 24 h. Cells were treated with SF

solution, SPVNs and SPTNs at a concentration of 20 mM for 24 h,

whereas the culture medium was added in the control group. The cells

were washed thrice with PBS and fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde for 20

min. Next, 0.2 mg/mL of DAPI solution was added to each well to stain

the cell nucleus for 10 min, and the cells were subsequently washed with

PBS. Finally, scanned images were obtained using a fluorescence

inverted microscope (Olympus IX81; Olympus Corporation, Tokyo,

Japan).

For qualitative analysis of cell apoptosis, cells were seeded in 6-well

plates at the logarithmic phase and incubated overnight. After removing

the medium, we added different preparations into the cells. The cells

were washed thrice with PBS. Then, 10 mL of 2.5% Annexin V-FITC

binding buffer and 5% propidium iodide were added at room temperature

in the dark. Intracellular apoptosis was measured through flow cytometry.

HepG2/MDR cells in the logarithmic growth phase were seeded in 6-well

plates and treated with the following drugs for 24 h: SPVNs, SPTNs,

RPMI 1640 medium (without FBS) and verapamil (P-gp inhibitor). Next,

cells were washed thrice with PBS. 1 mL of Rho123 (5 mM) was then

added into cells and incubated for 2 h in the dark. Finally, the cells were

collected in a flow tube and examined through flow cytometry.

SPTNs Inhibit MDR1 Gene and P-gp Expression

To explore whether the treatment with SPTNs has effect on reversing the

MDR of cancer cells, quantitative real-time polymerase chain reaction

(qRT-PCR) was performed to examine the expression levels of MDR1

mRNA. TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA) was used to isolate

total RNA from cells, and cDNA was synthesized from total RNA by

using the Revert Aid First Strand. qRT-PCR was performed using the

SYBR Green PCR master mix in the ABI Step One Real-Time PCR

system. GAPDH was used as an internal control. The sequences of

primers used in PCR are listed in Table S1. Furthermore, the protein

level of P-gp was also detected by western blotting.

Total protein was extracted by washing with PBS and subsequent lysis

with RIPA buffer (Beyotime, China). The supernatant was collected after

centrifugation at 12,000 rpm for 20 min at 4degC. The BCA protein

quantitative kit (Beyotime, China) was used to analyse the protein

concentration. Equivalent amounts of the protein (20 mg/lane) were

separated through 15% SDS-PAGE and transferred to PVDF

membranes. The membrane was incubated in 5% skimmed milk and

subsequently cultured with the primary monoclonal antimouse P-gp

overnight at 4degC. The next day, the membrane was treated with

horseradish peroxidase-labelled secondary antibodies for 1 h at room

temperature. Finally, image master software was used to analyse the

density of specific protein bands.

In Vivo Antitumor and Safety Evaluation

HepG2/MDR tumor-bearing Babl/c mice (male, 16-18 g) were used to

investigate the antitumor efficacy of SPTNs in inhibiting tumor growth.

We injected 0.1 mL of cell suspension (1 x 107 cells/mL) in the right

axillary vein to establish a tumor model. After inoculation for 7 days, mice

were randomly divided into six groups with eight mice in each group

intravenously injected with saline, free SF, SPVNs (6 mg/kg) and SPTNs

(3 mg/kg, 6 mg/kg and 9 mg/kg). All groups were administered injections

every 3 day for 21 days. The body weight and tumor volume was

recorded every other day. We measured the length-diameter (a) and

short-diameter (b) of tumors by using a vernier caliper every other day

and then calculated them using the equation: (a x b2)/2. At day 21,

subcutaneous tumors were surgically excised and isolated. The mouse

tissue was embedded in paraffin, sectioned and then stained with

haematoxylin and eosin (H&E). Finally, we observed and photographed

the sections under a microscope. The tumor inhibitory rate (%) was

calculated as follows:

$$ \mathrm{Tumor}\ \mathrm{inhibitory}\ \mathrm{rate}\left(\%

\right)=\left(1-\frac{W_{\mathrm{drug}}}{W_{\mathrm{control}}}\right)\ast

100 $$

where Wdrug is the tumor average weight of the drug group and Wcontrol is

the tumor average weight of the control group.

Statistical Analysis

SPSS (version 17.0) was used to perform the data analysis. All data

were expressed as mean +- standard deviation (SD). One-way ANOVA

was used to determine statistical significance. The difference was

considered statistically significant at *p < 0.05, **p < 0.01 and ***p <

0.001.

SPTNs exhibited a stronger cytotoxicity than SPVNs and SF groups. The

IC50 of SPTNs was decreased by 4.27-fold compared with SPVNs after

48-h treatment (3.90 mM vs. 16.67 mM), which was associated with the

higher cellular uptake and probably beneficial to drug delivery inside the

favourable encapsulation efficiency of 90.35%. SPTNs were almost

spherical in shape with uniform particle size (215.70 +- 0.36 nm), narrow

polydispersity index (0.27 +- 0.02) and negative surface charge (-26.01 +-

0.65 mV). In the cellular uptake assay, the intracellular coumarin-6 (C6)

fluorescence of TPGS component-based PLGA nanoparticles (C6-PTNs)

was 1.63-fold higher relative to that of PVA component-based PLGA

nanoparticles (C6-PVNs). The half-maximal inhibitory concentration and

apoptosis ratio of SPTNs against HepG2/MDR cells were 3.90 mM and

75.62%, respectively, which were notably higher than free SF and

sorafenib-PLGA-PVA nanoparticles (SPVNs). The anti-drug efflux

activities of SPTNs were assessed by the intracellular trafficking assay

using verapamil as a P-gp inhibitor. SPTNs could effectively inhibit the

drug efflux in tumor cells detected by flow cytometry, and suppressed

relative MDR1 gene as well as P-glycoprotein expression in tumor cells.

Attributed to the MDR reversion effect of SPTNs, the in vivo antitumor

efficacy experiment showed that SPTNs significantly inhibited the tumor

growth of HepG2/MDR xenograft-bearing nude mice, and obviously

reduced the toxicity against liver and kidney compared with SF

treatment. In summary, SPTNs, as highly efficient and safe antitumor

nano delivery systems, showed promising potential for hepatocellular

carcinoma therapy through reversing P-glycoprotein-mediated MDR.

Preparation of SPTNs

SPTNs were prepared through an ultrasonic emulsion solvent

to screen out the optimal prescription. For preparation of SPTNs, SF (3.2

mg) was dissolved in 8 mL of dimethyl sulfoxide and mixed with 2 mL

acetone containing PLGA (37.0 mg). Then, probe sonication (90 W, 5

min) was performed to obtain the primary emulsion of the compound.

Subsequently, the primary emulsion was slowly poured into TPGS (1.24

mg) mixed with deionized water (55.6 mL) at a flow rate of 0.5 mL/min.

The resultant dispersion was simultaneously sonicated at 200 W for 5

min in a pulse mode to obtain O/W emulsion. At room temperature, the

obtain O/W emulsion was used a rotary evaporator to evaporate organic

solvent for 4 h to obtain SPTNs. The excess TPGS was removed

through ultracentrifugation at 10,000 rpm for 10 min at 4degC. PVA was

used to replace TPGS to obtain SPVNs. Blank nanoparticles were

was used to replace SF to prepare C6-PTNs and C6-PVNs.

As depicted in Figure 1D, SPTNs exhibited a typical two-phase drug

Cellular Uptake Study

We used the C6 to replace SF in PLGA-based nanoparticles to

investigate cellular uptake. As shown in Figure 2A and B, the intracellular

In Vivo Antitumor and Safety Evaluation

To validate the advantages of combining TPGS into PLGA-based

nanoparticles, the antitumor efficacy was performed through intravenous

injection of SPTNs on the HepG2/MDR cell-bearing mice, with saline, SF

and SPVNs as the controls. As shown in Figure 6A, the saline group

showed the fastest tumor growth within 21 days. The treatment of

SPVNs and SPTNs exhibited an obvious inhibition of tumor growth

compared with SF, which could be attributed to an improved intratumoral

accumulation of nanoparticles based on EPR effect. With the

incorporation of TPGS, the medium dose of SPTNs (6 mg/kg) further

inhibited the tumor growth compared with SPVNs (6 mg/kg) highlighting

that TPGS facilitated a prolonged retention in the tumor cells and a more

efficiently antitumor effect of SPTNs. As shown in Figure 6A, the tumor

size of saline, SF (6 mg/kg) and SPVNs (6 mg/kg) groups enhanced by

4.76-, 4.21- and 2.97-fold during the treatments, respectively. By

comparison (Figure 6B), the tumor size only grew up 1.85-fold after

treatment with SPTNs (6 mg/kg). At the end of the treatment, the tumors

of mice were isolated (Figure 6B), and the tumor weight and mice weight

were respectively measured to evaluate the tumor inhibition rate (Figure

6C and D). The tumor weight values of mice treated with SPTNs (6 mg/

kg, 9 mg/kg) were 0.69 +- 0.04 g and 0.39 +- 0.02 g, respectively, which

were obviously lower than those in other treatments. Notably, the tumor

inhibition rate of high dose of SPVN (9 mg/kg) treatment was 65.42 +-

2.24%, which was the highest among all the groups (Figure 6D),

verifying the high-efficiency antitumor effect of SPTNs against

hepatocellular carcinoma. Next, we evaluated the potential toxicity of the

various groups against normal organs. The saline group was made into

H&E-stained sections (Figure 7), and we found low levels of

inflammatory infiltration in the liver and kidney sections of SF-treated

mice (as shown in black arrows). However, there were no obvious

pathological changes of the heart, liver, spleen, lung and kidney in the

SPTNs group.

## Conclusion
In this study, we successfully synthesized and applied PLGA-TPGS

nanoparticles as SF carriers (SPTNs) for overcoming MDR. SPTNs

rapidly released SF when exposed to the acidic environment. As a P-gp

inhibitor, TPGS increased the EE of PLGA nanoparticles and facilitated

the cellular uptake of drugs. SPTNs could effectively inhibit tumor cell

proliferation and induce cell apoptosis in a low drug concentration.

Furthermore, SPTNs exhibited an enhanced antitumor activity compared

with SF and SPVNS in vivo. This study provides a PLGA-TPGS nano

delivery strategy for overcoming MDR in chemotherapy of hepatocellular

carcinoma.
