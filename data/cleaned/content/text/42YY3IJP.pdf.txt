Abstract
Ketoconazole (KTZ), an antifungal agent used to treat localized or systemic fungal infections by
inhibiting ergosterol synthesis, exhibits restricted efﬁcacy within eukaryotic cells owing to its
elevated toxicity and limited solubility in water. This study aims to improve the biological
activity and overcome cytotoxic effects in the renal system of the hydrophobic KTZ by
incorporating it into poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) nanoparticles (NPs) utilizing
biomaterial nano-engineering techniques. KTZ-loaded PLGA NPs (KTZ-NPs) were prepared by
single emulsion solvent evaporation method and characterized by using dynamic light scattering
(DLS), electrophoretic light scattering (ELS), Fourier transform-infrared (FT-IR) spectroscopy
and scanning light microscopy (SEM). Particle size and zeta potential of KTZ-NPs were
determined as 182.0 ± 3.27 nm and −27.4 ± 0.56 mV, respectively. Antifungal activity was
analyzed with the time-kill and top agar dilution methods on Candida albicans (C. albicans) and
Aspergillus ﬂavus (A. ﬂavus). Both KTZ and KTZ-NPs caused a signiﬁcant decrease in A. ﬂavus
cell growth; however, the same effect was only observed in time-killing analysis on C. albicans,
indicating a methodological difference in the antifungal analysis. According to the top agar
method, the MIC value of KTZ-NPs against A. ﬂavus was 9.1 μg ml−1, while the minimum
inhibition concentration (MIC) value of KTZ was 18.2 μg ml−1. The twofold increased
antifungal activity indicates that nanoparticular drug delivery systems enhance the water
solubility of hydrophobic drugs. In addition, KTZ-NPs were not cytotoxic on human renal
proximal tubular epithelial cells (HRPTEpCs) at fungistatic concentration, thus reducing fungal
colonization without cytotoxic on renal excretion system cells.
Keywords: antifungal activity, biocompatibility, controlled release systems, ketoconazole,
nanoparticles, PLGA

modulating procedures or the increase in the patient popula-

Nowadays, these high mortality rates in fungal infections can
be reduced with the use of broad-spectrum antibiotics, but it is
known that these antibiotics have high toxicity, too [10].
Ketoconazole, (KTZ; 1-[4-[4-{[2-(2,4-dichlorophenyl)-2-
(imidazol-1-ylmethyl)-1, 3-dioxolan-4yl] methoxy} phenyl]
piperazin-1-yl] ethan-1-one; ﬁgure 1), is a hydrophobic chiral
imidazole antimycotic drug, which is categorized as Bio-
pharmaceutics Classiﬁcation System (BCS) class II com-
pound [11]. It is used in the treatment of topical or systemic
fungal infections (Aspergillosis, Pneumocystis pneumonia,
Candidiasis, Cryptococcosis, etc) as broad-spectrum anti-
biotic [12, 13]. KTZ shows its effect by blocking the synth-
esis of ergosterol, the primary fungal membrane sterol. This
effect occurs because of the blockage of C-14 α-demethylase,
a cytochrome P-450 enzyme that converts lanosterol to
ergosterol. Due to the blockage, the function of the membrane
impairs, its permeability increases, and it increases the leak-
age of amino acids, small ions, and proteins from the fungi,
leading to cell death [11, 12, 14–17]. The effectiveness of
ketoconazole treatment in urinary tract infection caused by
Candida has been demonstrated in clinical study [18]. How-
ever, high toxicity in eukaryotic cells [19, 20] and low water
solubility [21–23] of KTZ impair its pharmacodynamic
properties, thus the biological activity of KTZ [19]. In a study
performed by El-Baky et al it was shown that KTZ has efﬂux
pump inhibition activity. Additionally, it is known that most
of the compounds (e.g. reserpine, omeprazole, phenothiazine)
exhibit this activity at concentrations that are toxic to eukar-
yotic cells [19].
The biological activities of antimicrobial agents can be
effectively enhanced by encapsulating drugs into nanos-
tructures.
Superior
physicochemical
properties
such
as
enhanced
solubility
and
stability,
speciﬁc
site-targeted
distribution, and sustained release can be achieved by loading
antimicrobial agents into nano-sized carrier systems [24, 25].
In addition, the side effects of the drug can be minimized, it
can be targeted through its speciﬁc accumulation in the
infected area, the therapeutic index can be expanded, ther-
apeutic life can be extended due to its slow elimination,
antimicrobial resistance can be reduced, protection against
chemical degradation can be increased and the frequency of
administration can be reduced [24, 26, 27]. Among the new
drug delivery systems, polymeric nanoparticles have been
recognized as up-and-coming carriers of antimicrobial agents
[24]. Polymeric nanostructures have several advantages such
as simple production techniques, higher stability compared to
other colloidal carriers, high therapeutic agent loading ability,
and biodegradability [27].
Various drugs and natural products used can cause
nephrotoxicity resulting in acute kidney failure. One of the
major problems in drug development studies and clinical use
of existing drugs is renal toxicity [28]. In preclinical studies,
2% of drug development failures, 19% of Phase 3 failures,
and 20% of acute renal failure are due to nephrotoxicity [29].
It has also been reported that drug-induced kidney injury is
responsible for 14%–26% of acute kidney injury cases [30].
One of the common sites of drug-induced kidney injury is the
proximal tubule cells. Because they are both exposed to cir-
culating chemicals and have important roles in carrier-medi-
ated drug clearance [28].
PLGA, a Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved
polymer [31], is biocompatible with long-term durability and
convenient for application via parenteral injection and used
frequently in controlled release studies with polymeric NPs.
The PLGA NPs have higher stability, improved water solu-
bility, and high drug loading capacity compared to other
nanocarriers. PLGA NPs improve the biological activity of
many drugs by controlled release properties [32].
In the present study, we aimed to synthesize KTZ-loaded
NPs efﬁciently and compare their antifungal activity with free
KTZ. Furthermore, we hypothesized that the high nephro-
toxicity of KTZ may be modulated via encapsulation. For this
purpose, KTZ-loaded nanostructures were prepared with a
single emulsion-solvent evaporation method and then the
prepared NPs were characterized in detail and their antifungal

2.2. Preparation of KTZ-loaded PLGA NPs
KTZ-loaded PLGA NPs were synthesized using the oil-in-water
(o/w) single emulsion solvent evaporation method as previously
described [33]. Firstly, KTZ and PLGA were dissolved in etha-
nol-DCM and DCM, respectively. The KTZ and PLGA solutions
were mixed and vortexed to ensure to obtain organic phase
homogeneously. This organic phase was emulsiﬁed with aqueous
PVA solution (3% w/v) by sonication using a microtip probe
sonicator (Bandelin Sonopuls, Germany) over an ice bath. The
obtained emulsion was added dropwise to 0.1% w/v PVA
solution and stirred for 4 h on a magnetic stirrer at room temp-
erature where organic solvents were removed under constant
stirring. The prepared NPs were precipitated by centrifugation
(40 min., 9000 rpm), washed three times with ultrapure water,
and then freeze-dried. Free NPs were prepared by the same
method without using KTZ. All lyophilized NPs were stored at
−20 °C until used for further analysis.

The dynamic light scattering method was

2.3.3. FT-IR analysis.
FT-IR spectra of free KTZ, PLGA, and
freeze-dried nanoparticles were obtained in the attenuation total
reﬂectance (ATR) mode using an FT-IR spectrophotometer
(Shimadzu, Japan) for chemical analysis of functional groups
present in NPs and to conﬁrm encapsulation of KTZ into NPs
[37]. The spectra were obtained from 4000 to 600 cm−1 and
4 cm−1 resolution with 16 scans per sample.

The surface morphology, shape, and

Zeiss EVO LS 10 (Germany) scanning electron microscope at
an acceleration voltage of 7.00 kV.

3. Results and discussion
3.1. Preparation and characterization studies
Free-NPs and KTZ-NPs were prepared using the single
emulsion solvent evaporation method. The encapsulation
efﬁciency of KTZ-NPs was calculated by determining the
amount of unentrapped KTZ from the supernatant obtained
after centrifugation by the indirect method. The particles were
then freeze-dried and weighed to determine the reaction yield
and KTZ loading capacity. The particle size and zeta potential
of NPs were measured using dynamic and electrophoretic
light scattering methods, respectively. Table 1 shows the
synthesis parameters and RY, EE, LC, particle size, PDI, and
zeta potential values of prepared nanostructures.
The gravimetrically determined reaction efﬁciency was
61.08% for KTZ-NPs and 53.80% for free NPs. EE and LC
of KTZ–NPs were calculated as 56.04% and 21.07%,
respectively.
Figure 2 shows the particle size (a) and zeta potential (b)
distribution of KTZ-NPs. The particle size of Free-NPs and
KTZ-NPs were found as 141.5 ± 1.47 and 182.0 ± 3.27 nm,
respectively. An increase in the particle size was observed
after the encapsulation of KTZ into NPs. It can be seen in
ﬁgure 2(a) that the prepared KTZ-NPs size range belongs to a
single population (100% density). At the same time, the low
PDI value (0.23 ± 0.004) conﬁrms that monodisperse parti-
cles are produced, and the particle size is homogeneous. Zeta
potential values of Free-NPs and KTZ-NPs were measured as
−27.2 ± 1.98 mV and −27.4 ± 0.56 mV, respectively. Zeta
potential is one of the key physicochemical properties of
the stability of nanostructures, which depends on several
parameters, including the type of drug encapsulated, the
PLGA end group, and the type of emulsiﬁer [43]. The mea-
sured negative zeta potential value is due to the presence of
ionized carboxyl groups (because of acid-terminated PLGA)
on the surface of the particles [44] and non-ionic PVA used as
an emulsiﬁer during synthesis [39]. A high (extremely
positive or negative, >± 30 mV) zeta potential value ensures
long-term colloidal stabilization by preventing aggregation of
nanostructure through electrostatic interactions (repulsive
forces) [45]. Thus, it contributes to biological activity by
preventing protein adsorption of nanostructure and their
interaction with serum components.
FT-IR spectra of KTZ, PLGA, and KTZ-loaded PLGA
NPs (KTZ-NPs) were evaluated for the analysis of functional
groups in nanostructures and conﬁrmation of encapsulation of
KTZ into PLGA NPs. Comparative FT-IR spectra KTZ,
PLGA, and KTZ-NPs are given in ﬁgure 3.
In the FT-IR spectrum of PLGA and KTZ-NPs given in
ﬁgure 3, the bands at 2993 and 2989 cm−1 belong to C–H
stretching of CH2 and C–H stretching of -CH respectively.
The band at 1750 cm−1 belongs to the C=O stretching
vibration of the ester bond, the bands at 1166 and 1086 cm−1
belong to the C–O stretch, and these bands are the char-
acteristic peaks of the PLGA molecule. The KTZ showed the
characteristic peaks of C=O stretching vibration of the car-
bonyl group, C=C aromatic stretching, tertiary amine groups,
and C–Cl stretching at 1647 cm−1, 1510 cm−1, 1244 cm−1,
and 814 cm−1 respectively [46, 47]. However, the signiﬁcant
decrease in the intensity of the main peaks of KTZ in the
spectrum of KTZ-NPs and the predominance of characteristic
peaks of PLGA indicate that KTZ is encapsulated into PLGA
nanoparticles. However, the low intensity of the C=C aro-
matic bond at 1510 cm−1 in the FT-IR spectrum of KTZ-NPs
indicates that a small amount of KTZ is adsorbed on the
surface of particles.
The surface morphology and shape of KTZ-NPs were
analyzed by SEM and the obtained SEM micrograph was
given in ﬁgure 4. The images of KTZ-NPs conﬁrmed the
production of spherical-shaped particles with smooth surfaces
and the size and uniformity of size (low PDI value, 0.23 ±
0.004) obtained by the dynamic light scattering method.
3.2. In vitro KTZ release study
The in vitro release of KTZ-NPs was evaluated in PBS buffer
at pH 7.4 by dissolution method. The release proﬁle of KTZ
was followed for 96 h and the graph showing the cumulative
percentage of KTZ released as a function of time is given in
ﬁgure 5.
As shown in ﬁgure 5, the release graphic of KTZ was
typically diphasic. KTZ-loaded nanoparticles showed initial
burst release reaching 60% in the ﬁrst 2 h. Burst release of
drug molecules from particles is generally observed due to the
rapid diffusion of drug localized near the NP surface or
adsorbed directly to the surface. In the second phase, the drug
release reached 79.78% and then the release speed slowed
down. Nearly linear sustained release was observed between
48 and 96 h intervals and 84.28% of the KTZ in the NPs was
released at the end of 96 h. A similar release pattern of
ketoconazole from PLGA nanoparticles has been reported by
other researchers [48].

it has been reported that antifungal agent/drug-loaded poly-

size of the produced nanoparticles, an increase in efﬁciency in
nanoformulation is an expected result.

4. Conclusion
Ketoconazole is a broad-spectrum synthetic imidazole anti-
fungal approved by the FDA [55] for the treatment of sys-
temic fungal infections [56]. Its aqueous solubility is not
sufﬁcient (0.087 mg l−1 at 25 °C) [57] to dissolve the entire
dose in gastrointestinal ﬂuids under normal conditions and is
not absorbed systemically after topical application. Studies
have shown that KTZ has toxicity in mammalian cells and
tissues when used at high oral therapeutic doses [58, 59]. It
has also been reported to have toxic effects on the liver after
oral administration [60].
In this study, PLGA-based polymeric nanoparticles were
synthesized with low particle size and size distribution (182.0
± 3.27 nm and 0.23 ± 0.004, respectively) and high surface
charge (−27.4 ± 0.56 mV) by single emulsion solvent eva-
poration method for an effective carrier system design of
hydrophobic ketoconazole. KTZ and KTZ-NPs caused a
signiﬁcant reduction in A. ﬂavus cell growth; however, the
same effect was not observed on C. albicans. According to
the results of the top agar dilution method, the MIC value of
KTZ-NPs was 9.1 μg ml−1, while the MIC value of KTZ was
18.2 μg ml−1 against A. ﬂavus. Antifungal activity against A.
ﬂavus twofold increased with the long-term release of keto-
conazole (approximately 96 h) despite low-dose exposure.
Nephrotoxicity evaluation showed that KTZ NPs resulted in
higher cell viability compared to free KTZ treatment. This
indicates that the encapsulation of KTZ into nanostructures
improves its biocompatibility. It is thought that these outputs
will provide important beneﬁts in future therapeutic applica-
tions. In addition, in further studies, the surface of NPs pro-
