ABSTRACT

The purpose of this research was to develop a new kind of GA-CS/sPLGA complexed
Microspheres delivery system for CuB. In this work, star-shaped poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid)
(sPLGA-COOH) and chitosan modified with glycyrrhetic acid (GA-CS) were successfully
synthesized. The GA-CS/sPLGA complexed Microspheres for CuB was prepared by emulsion
evaporation method. Futhermore, CuB-loaded Microspheres formulations were optimized. The
release behaviour of CuB-loaded Microspheres in vitro were also examined. It was showed that
CuB-loaded amount of Microspheres could reach 58.78%±1.288 by adding GA-CS into the
external water phase. The size of corresponding CuB-loaded Microspheres was 370±16.14 nm in
diameter, and zeta potential was negative 10.86±0.4790 mV measured by laser light particle
size/zeta potential analysis. The results indicated that the complexed GA-CS/sPLGA Microspheres

1. INTRODUCTION

CuB belongs to the family of tetracyclic
triterpenes, which can be extracted from the
calyx melo of Cucumis melo L., a Chinese
medicinal plant [1]. Cucurbitacin used clinically
contain mostly CuB and CuE, which are effective
against chronic hepatitis and primary liver
carcinoma.
However,
their
nonspecific
cytotoxicity and insolubility in water had hindered
their clinical applications [2,3]. To overcome such
limitations, nanometer scale devices were used
for CuB entrapment [4-6], such as liposomes and
solid lipid nanoparticles.

The most commonly used polymer in controlled
release dosage forms were poly lactic acid
(PLA), PLGA, poly (lactic-co-glycolic acid), poly
caprolactone (PCL) and Chitosan (CS) etc.
Polylactic-co-glycolic
acid
(PLGA)
is
FDA-
approved biodegradable polymer. Owing to its
excellent biocompatibility, PLGA is often used for
drug delivery [7]. It was reported that both drug
entrapment and release performances can be
improved by using star PLGA polymer as drug
carrier [3,8]. Chitosan (CS) is a kind of amino
polysaccharide
with
high
affinity
for
cell
membrane and is widely used as drug carriers
[9,10]. In addition, chitosan has many reactive
groups on the surface, such as amino groups,
which can be used for grafting appropriate ligand
to obtain the active targetability.

In this article, four-arm star poly(lactic-co-glycolic
acid) and chitosan modified with glycyrrhetic acid
(a targeting ligand) were synthesized. The
Chinese Traditional Medicine (Cucurbitacin B,
CuB) was used as a model drug and the drug-
loaded
GA-CS/sPLGA
Microspheress
were
constructed by electrostatic interaction of positive
GA-CS and negative sPLGA. It is expected that
the complexed Microspheres will have great
potential to both raise the drug-loaded amounts
and improve the drug release behavior [11,12].

2. EXPERIMENTS

2.1 Materials

Glycolide and D,L-lactide were purchased from
Beijing Yuan-Sheng Rong Technology Co., Ltd.
(China). PTOL (AR) and PVA (molecular weight,
30,000-70,000) were purchased from Beijing J&K
Scientific Ltd. (China). Chitosan (low molecular
weight,
85%
deacetylation
degree)
was
purchased
from
Jinan
Haidebei
Marine
Bioengineering
Ltd.
Co.
(China).
Succinic
anhydride (AR) was purchased from TCI
Development Ltd. Co. (China). Sn(Oct)2 was
purchased from Sigma Company. Glycyrrhetic
acid (purity 99%) was purchased from Fujie
Pharmaceutical Co. Ltd. (China). Cucurbitacin B
(purity 95.5%) was purchased from Tianjin
Institute of Pharmaceutical Research Ltd. Co.
(China). All other chemicals were of analytical
grade and all materials were used without further
purification.

2.2 Instruments

1H-NMR spectra were performed on a Varian
UNITY-plus 400 spectrometer. Tetramethylsilane
was used as an internal standard. Both GA-CS
and sPLGA were dissolved in CDCl3 solvent.

UV-vis absorption spectra of the samples were
recorded at room temperature using a UV-2401
UV-vis spectrophotometer (SHIMADZU).

The mean size and zeta potential of drug-loaded
Microspheres were determined by a 3000HS

Malvern instrument (Worcestershire, UK). All the
measurements were repeated three times.

The amount of CuB contained in the samples
were determined by SofTA, CoMetro-6000 HPLC
equipped with a Hypersil ODS C18 column
(200 mm×4.6 mm, 5 µm). All the measurements
were repeated three times.

2.3 Synthesis and Characterization of
star-PLGA-COOH

Lactide
and
glycolide
(monomer),
PTOL
(initiator) and Sn(Oct)2 (catalyst) ) were added in
dichloromethane to meltingly polymerize in a
sealed tube [3,13]. The stoichiometric ratios
were
monomer:initiator:catalyst=10000:100:1
mol/mol).
Before
heating,
the
tube
was
repeatedly passing nitrogen gas to remove trace
air. The reaction was conducted for eight hours
at 160°C in oil bath. After the reaction finished,
the
reaction
mixture
was
dissolved
in
dichloromethane, and pure star-PLGA-OH was
obtained by precipitation twice with anhydrous
methanol, and vacuum dried overnight.

The detailed synthetic procedure of star-PLGA-
COOH was as follows. Two times amount of
succinic anhydride reacted with star-PLGA-OH
for 24 h at room temperature under continuous
stirring. The solvent (1,4-dioxane) was removed
after the reaction finished. Then the concentrated
solution was dissolved in hot water and extracted
three times with dichloromethane. Finally, star-
PLGA-COOH
was
obtained
by
methanol
recrystallizing
and
vacuum
dried,
whose
structure was further characterized by 1H-NMR.

2.4 Preparation and Characterization of
Chitosan modified with Glycyrrhetic
Acid

According to the reference [14,15], carboxyl
group of glycyrrhetic acid was activated with
DCC/NHS, then a condensation reaction was
conducted between chitosan and the activated
ester of glycyrrhetic acid. The structure of
product was characterized by H1-NMR.

2.5 Preparation
of
Drug-loaded
Complexed sPLGA Microspheress

Briefly, 160 mg sPLGA-COOH and 40 mg CuB
were dissolved in 2 mL dichloromethane as the
oil phase, and an apropriate amount of GA-CS
(or CS) was dissolved in 20 mL 2% PVA solution
as the water phase. Under ultrasound action the
oil phase was slowly dropped into water phase to
form a stable emulsified solution in an ice bath
(0°C). The obtained solution was then poured
into a large amount of 2% PVA solution, and the
mixed solution was continually stirred for 6 h to
volatilize dichloromethane completely. Pure CuB-
loaded
Microspheres
were
obtained
by
centrifugation at 3,500 rpm for 30 min, followed
by washing with 2% aqueous solution of ethanol
(3×10 mL) and vacuum drying for 48 hours.

Drug entrapment efficiency, drug-loaded amount,
particle size and zeta patential were chosen as
indexes, and the preparation formulation of drug-
loaded Microspheres was optimized by changing
the amount and types of chitosan added in the
external water phase.

2.6 Determination of the Content and
Entrapment Efficiency of CuB

5 mg CuB-loaded Microspheres were put into a
10 mL volumetric flask. Then 2 mL of
dichloromethane was added to dissolve the
Microspheres
completely
under
ultrasound
action. Finally, 8 mL of methanol was added and
the solution was filtered through 0.22 µm
microporous
membrane.
After
abandoning
primary filtrate, subsequent filtrate was collected.
CuB concentration in the filtrate was determined
by HPLC under the following chromatographic
conditions
[Chromatographic
column:
Shim-
pack VP-ODS C18 (200 mm×4.6 mm, 5 µm),
mobile phase of methanol: water (70:30), flow
rate: 1 mL·min-1, column temperature: 30°C,
detection wavelength: 228 nm, injection volume:
20 µL]. Based on the standard curve of CuB and
the following formulas, the drug-loaded amount
and entrapment efficiency were calculated.

The amount of drug-loaded Microspheress = The
drug weight contained in the Microspheres/The
total weight of Microspheres×100%;

The entrapment efficiency = The drug weight
contained in the Microspheres/The total weight of
the drug×100%.

2.7 In vitro Release Investigation of Drug-
loaded Microspheres

10 mg of drug-loaded Microspheres were
dispersed in 10 mL PBS solutions. The solution
was then placed in the dialysis bags. The release
medium was PBS solution (containing 0.5%
SDS) at pH 7.4 in 37°C water bath oscillator.
Samples were withdrawn at appropriate time

Zhang et al.; BJPR, 9(6): 1-8, 2016; Article no.BJPR.22575

4

intervals and we added an amount of PBS
solution
equal
to
that
withdrawn.
CuB
concentration of the supernatant was determined
by HPLC after centrifuging. The cumulative
release curves were drawn by calculating
cumulative release amount at each time interval.

2.8 Statistical Analyses

All experiments were repeated at least three
times. Results are expressed as means ±
standard deviation. A difference between means
was considered significant if the p value was less
than or equal to 0.05.

3. RESULTS AND DISCUSSTION

3.1 Characterization
of
sPLGA
and
Chitosan Modified with Glycyrrhetic
Acid

The structure of star-PLGA-OH and star-PLGA-
COOH was confirmed by 1H-NMR. The 1H-NMR
spectrum of star-PLGA-OH was shown in Fig. 1.
The
chemical
shifts
of
δ=1.27
and
3.7
corresponded to CH3 and CH of the initiator
PTOL respectively. The chemical shift of δ=1.58
and 5.17 corresponded to CH3 and CH of lactide
respectively.
The
chemical
shift
4.82
corresponded to CH2 of the glycolide. The rest
peaks were impurities peaks. The results showed
that the glycolide/lactide copolymer had been
synthesized successfully. After the reaction of
star-PLGA-OH with succinic anhydride, the peak
of δ=2.72 appeared in the spectrum of Fig. 1b,
which corresponded to CH2 chemical shift of
succinic
anhydride
(δ=2.62)
and
indicated
successful synthesis of star-PLGA-COOH.

New peaks at 2.5~2.7 ppm was shown in
spectrum C compared with spectrum D in Fig. 1.
These new peaks corresponded to the methyl,
GA methyl and methylene protons respectively,
so it was inferred that GA-CS polymer had been
successfully synthesized.

The UV-vis spectra of GA, CS and GA-CS in
solution were examined. The methanol solution
of GA had a characteristic absorption peak at
248 nm, while the GA-CS in methanol solution
had a characteristic absorption peak at 258 nm,
which was characteristic absorption of GA.
However CS solution had no obvious absorption
in the 200~400 nm, which furtherly support that
coupling reaction of GA and CS was successful.
3.2 GA-CS/sPLGA
Complexed
Micro-
spheress´ Preparation and the Effect
of
GA-CS
Concentration
on
the
Microspheress´ Properties

Microspheres drug delivery systems are of
considerable therapeutic interest. Currently, this
field is dominated by the use of poly(lactide-co-
glycolide)
type
Microspheress.
Copolymer
poly(lactic-co-glycolic
acid)
(PLGA)
nano-
technology has been approved by the US FDA
for the use of drug delivery, diagnostics and other
applications
of clinical and basic science
research,
including
cardiovascular
disease,
cancer,
vaccine
and
tissue
engineering.
However, because of the deficiency of suitable
functional groups on their surface, conventional
PLGA Microspheres lack the possibility of
surface modifications for specialized targeting or
other purposes. Such modifications are thought
to
greatly
improve
the
effectiveness
of
Microspheres delivery systems.

To solve this problem, sPLGA and chitosan
modified with glycyrrhetic acid were synthesized
and
sPLGA
complexed
Microspheres
was
constructed by means of chitosan and modified
chitosan with glycyrrhetic acid. The decision to
choose glycyrrhetic acid as a targeting ligand
was that glycyrrhetic acid accumulated in rat
hepatocytes by a ligand-receptor interaction and
had
good
hepatoma
cellular
targeted
efficiency [14-17].

The effect curves of varied GA-CS amounts
added in the external water phase on the
properties
of
CuB-loaded
complexed
Microspheres was shown in Fig. 2. The zeta
potential of Microspheres increased as the
amount of the GA-CS with positive potential was
added gradually in negative sPLGA system.
Meanwhile the size of the PLGA Microspheres
increased as GA-CS concentration increased in
the external water phase. Most importantly, the
adding of GA-CS improved the amount CuB-
loaded of Microspheres. It was shown that the
amount of drug loaded was 58.78%±1.288 when
80% GA-CS was added. However continuously
increasing of GA-CS concentration led to highly
viscous solutions and broad size distribution of
Microspheres. Statistical evaluation proved that
no significant difference was observed for zeta
potential
and
the
size
of
CuB-loaded
microspheres by adding GA-CS in the Fig. 2
(p<0.05). Similarly, no significant difference
bserved for drug-loaded amount and drug
entrapment efficiency in the Fig. 2 (p<0.05).

3.4 In vitro Release Study of CuB-loaded
Microspheres

In vitro drug release profile reveals fundamental
information on the Microspheres´ composition
and structure. The release curves of two kinds of
CuB-loaded Microspheres in vitro was shown in
Fig. 3. The cumulative release amount of drug-
loaded Microspheres decreased when GA-CS
added in the external water phase. The burst
release reduced and the release rate had the
tendency of slowing down. When the release
time was close to 300 h, the cumulative releasing
amount hadn’t reached 70% yet. This was
because the complexing of GA-CS with sPLGA
Microspheres restricted the drug’s diffusion
through the Microspheres. The propertities
in vivo of CuB-loaded microspheres, such as
pharmacokinetic parameters or adverse effects
will be further studied in subsequent work.

4. CONCLUSIONS

In this work, we developed a new kind of drug-
loaded complexed Microspheres from sPLGA-
COOH and GA-CS by electrostatic interaction.
The
ligand
GA
was
introduced
to
the
Microspheres by adding GA-CS in the external
water phase. We have shown that GA-CS could
be used as a stabilizer and a auxiliary emusifier
to prepare sPLGA complexed Microspheres by
emulsion evaporation
method.
The
results
showed that the complexed Microspheres had
higher CuB entrapment efficiency, smaller drug
burst release and a slower sustained release
than the uncomplexed ones. In summary,
sPLGA/GA-CS
complexed
mirospheres
are
suitable drug carrier for CuB and these results
are helpful in subsequently drug targeting
experiment.
