A B S T R A C T
Postbiotics are substances released or produced by the metabolic activity of microorganisms that have a bene­
ficial effect on the host. Among the postbiotics, butyrate and propionate stand out for their excellent anti-
inflammatory and immunomodulatory properties in the fight against intestinal diseases. However, these sub­
stances have some limitations as they are low molecular weight compounds that have limited clinical use due to
unfavorable pharmacokinetics, non-selective pharmacological action, poor palatability and unpleasant odor.
Encapsulation in microparticles, designed for specific delivery to the colon, is a relevant strategy to prevent
premature degradation or release of nanoparticles during their passage through the upper gastrointestinal tract,
allowing for the targeted drug delivery to the colon by oral administration. In this study, we developed an oral
multifunctional system based on nano-in-microparticles, processed by supercritical emulsion extraction to ach­
ieve targeted colonic release of butyrate and propionate. The nano-in-microparticles exhibited a diameter of
about 1340 nm, an encapsulation efficiency of approximately 39 % for propionate and 36 % for butyrate. They

1. Introduction
There are more than 1000 bacterial species in the human intestine,
and more than 50 bacterial genera reported [1]. The large intestine
houses the most significant number of bacteria in the gastrointestinal
tract (GIT) (approximately, 1011-1012 cells per gram). The intestinal
microbiota plays an important role in preserving the health of the host,
establishing nutrients, achieving energy balance, modulating the im­
mune response and protecting from pathogens. In this way, a favorable
balance of the intestinal microbiota is maintained, promoting the host’s
health. Several studies have shown that prebiotics, probiotics, and
probiotic metabolites, known as “postbiotics”, can improve the ho­
meostasis of the intestinal microbiota, maintaining the integrity of the
intestinal barrier, modulating the immune response and exerting a
beneficial effect on the health of the host [5,16,25,46].
The term postbiotic is related to functional bioactive compounds,
generated as fermentation products that are beneficial to the health.
This term can be considered a broad term, encompassing the synonyms
referents to these components of microbial fermentation. Thus, post­
biotics include many different constituents, including metabolites,
short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), microbial cell fractions, functional
proteins, extracellular polysaccharides (EPS), cell lysates, teichoic acid,
and peptidoglycan-derived muropeptides [15,25,37,44,48]. Among the
postbiotics mentioned, SCFAs have shown great immunosuppressive
potential in inflammatory bowel diseases.
SCFAs are derived from the fermentation of polysaccharides of plant
origin by the natural intestinal microbiota. The main SCFAs include
butyric, acetic, and propionic acid, responsible for the formation of fatty
acid salts (e.g., acetate, propionate, and butyrate) [40,49].
It is worth noting that there are currently commercially available
butyrate and propionate supplements; however, due to their physico­
chemical properties, the amount of active ingredient that reaches its
available form in the intestine is limited, making it unfeasible to use in
the prevention and treatment of diseases, due to the dose concentration/
response. Thus, despite the notable advantages of SCFAs, they have
some limitations such as low molecular weight that limits their clinical
use due to unfavorable pharmacokinetics, in addition to nonspecific
effects, poor palatability, and unpleasant odor [41]. Therefore, it is
necessary to develop colon-specific delivery systems that overcome the
limitations of these active ingredients and increase their concentration
gradient in the colon for the application in the prevention and treatment
of intestinal diseases.
The upper region of the GIT imposes many barriers for the active
substance to reach the colon. The association of bioactive encapsulation
strategies in nanostructured systems and the incorporation of these
systems into microparticles has been widely explored in the literature
([9,10]; W. [23,29,34]). The protection of the system, as well as its
vectorization to the colon, can be achieved through the microencapsu­
lation of nanocarriers using pH-resistant polymers, also representing an
adequate strategy for protecting it against pH variations in the GIT [9,
10,2].
Traditional techniques frequently used to produce nano/micropar­
ticles are: spray drying (Q. [24,47]), ionic gelation [9,10,26],
freeze-drying [43], interfacial polymerization, solvent evaporation [3],
coacervation [19], and inclusion complexation [4]. However, these
methods show numerous disadvantages, such as: (1) difficulty in
controlling the average diameter and morphology, (2) high poly­
dispersity index, (3) need for high temperatures for the drying process,
compromising the integrity of bioactive compounds, (4) low yield, (5)
long processing time, (6) high cost, (7) low drug encapsulation effi­
ciency, (8) post-processing steps to remove monomers, by-products,
organic solvent residues and surfactants, and (9) compromised stabil­
ity [3,39]. To overcome these problems, techniques assisted by super­
critical CO2 (SC-CO2) have been explored.
Supercritical assisted atomization (SAA) [8], supercritical anti­
solvent precipitation (SAS) [36], supercritical emulsion extraction (SEE)
[32], and supercritical assisted electrospray [14], are among the most
frequently used. These approaches benefit from the unique properties of
CO₂ in supercritical conditions, such as high diffusivity, low surface
tension and adjustable density, which make it possible to develop
optimized processes compared to conventional technologies, such as
evaporation of organic solvents or spray drying. Among the main ben­
efits, there is the complete removal of residual organic solvents, avoid­
ing the toxicological risks associated with the use of volatile solvents,
which is especially relevant in pharmaceutical and food applications
[18]. In addition, operation at moderate temperatures - generally below
45◦C - allows an efficient encapsulation of thermolabile compounds,
such as short-chain fatty acids, without thermal degradation [28].
Another crucial aspect is the precise control over particle size, shape
and polydispersity, since CO₂ pressure, temperature and saturation pa­
rameters can be adjusted to promote favorable conditions for nucleation
and homogeneous drying, resulting in more uniform and reproducible
particles [38]. From an environmental point of view, supercritical CO₂ is
a green solvent: it is non-flammable, non-toxic, economically viable,
widely available and recyclable, contributing to the sustainability of the
process and meeting the requirements of green chemistry [20].
In addition to these technical and environmental aspects, the scal­
ability of supercritical technology also stands out, as it has already been
consolidated in industrial processes such as the extraction of caffeine,
lipids and natural flavors. This demonstrates its viability for tran­
sitioning from the laboratory to large-scale production without
compromising the quality of the material obtained. In the specific
context of the continuous supercritical emulsions extraction (SEE), the
technique allows almost monodisperse microparticles to be obtained,
with a low tendency to agglomeration, high homogeneity and high
encapsulation efficiency, characteristics that often overcome the chal­
lenges faced in conventional methods, such as wide size distribution and
particle collapse [7,31]. Thus, the use of supercritical CO₂ in particle
production offers a set of synergistic advantages involving technological
precision, environmental safety and industrial viability, consolidating
itself as a superior approach to traditional encapsulation techniques.
Poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) is a polymer widely used in the devel­
opment of drug delivery systems and has been approved by the Food and
Drug Administration (FDA) and the European Medicines Agency (EMA)
as a safe and certified substance for clinical application. It is biode­
gradable, biosafe, biocompatible, versatile in formulation and func­
tionalisation. PLGA-based carriers guarantee the bioavailability of the
encapsulated drug by protecting it from unwanted degradation in the
physiological environment, ensuring sustained and targeted delivery or
facilitating the diffusion of the drug into the intracellular environment
and reducing its side effects [12].
Therefore, the objective of this work is to provide a proof of concept

2. Materials and methods
2.1. Materials
Poly-lactic-co-glycolic-acid (RESOMER 504 H; ratio 50:50; Mw
38,000–54,000) was acquired from Evonik Industries (Denmark).
Butyrate (catalogue code: B5887), propionate (catalogue code: P1880),
phosphoric acid (PhA), and Tween® 80 were purchased from Sigma-
Aldrich (USA). Ethanol, acetonitrile (ACN), ethyl acetate (EA), and po­
tassium chloride were supplied from Carlo Erba Reagents (Italy). Hy­
aluronic acid was purchased from Vitafarma (Brazil). Carbon dioxide
(CO2, >99 % purity) was purchased from Morlando Group Srl (Naples,
Italy). Lecithin-sodium was acquired from Lipoid (Germany). Labrafac™
was purchased from Gatefoss´e (France). SnakeSkin™ Dialysis Tubing
(10 K MWCO) was acquired from Thermo Scientific (USA).
2.2. Development of nanoparticles containing butyrate and propionate
Nanoparticles (NPs) were produced using emulsification and solvent
evaporation technique. The aqueous phase consisted of the dispersion of
hyaluronic acid (HA) at a concentration of 0.5 mg/mL−1, with the in­
clusion of polysorbate 80 (1.5 % v/v). The oily phase consisted of
organic solvent (1 mL; ethanol), SCFAs (for active nanoparticles; 0.5 µg/
mL−1), lecithin (0.0136 g) and Labrafac™ (0.150 mL). In summary,
during the process, the oil phase was added to the aqueous phase, drop-
by-drop, in an ice bath, under cavitation. This step was carried out in a
sonicator (Branson Ultrasonic, model S-450D) equipped with a microtip
for 10 min, amplitude 20 %. Subsequently, NPs were kept under heating
(40 ◦C) and magnetic stirring at 800 rpm for 30 min. Then, NPs were
centrifuged (Thermo Scientific, model IEC CL30R) at 3600 rpm using an
Amicon® Ultra-15 50 kDa, for 15 min to concentrate them for the
following incorporation into PLGA microparticles.
2.3. Physicochemical characterization of nanoparticles
2.3.1. Analysis of the average diameter, Zeta potential and polydispersity
index of NPs by DLS
The polydispersity index (PdI) and the average particle size were
determined using the dynamic light scattering (DLS) technique, using
Zetasizer Nano-S® equipment (Malvern Instruments), at a detection
angle of 173º and at a temperature of 25 ºC. The Zetasizer nano-S v7.11
software was used to calculate the random diffusion of the system, and
the results were converted into the particles’ hydrodynamic diameter
and size distribution, using the Stokes-Einstein equation.
The Zeta potential (ZP) of the system was calculated using a Zetasizer
Nano-S particle analyzer, with an angle of 173º, through the electro­
phoretic mobility technique employing the Smoluchowski equation.
2.3.2. Analysis of diameter, span index and particle concentration by NTA
The nanoparticles’ diameter, span index, and concentration were
determined by tracking the Brownian movement, using the Nanosight
nanoparticle analyzer (Malvern Instruments), at 25 ºC. The samples
were diluted in ultra-purified water in a 1:1000 (v/v) ratio, placed in a
1 mL syringe, and loaded into the system. Analyses were performed
using NTA 3.0 software. All measurements were performed in triplicate.
2.3.3. Encapsulation efficiency (EE%)
An aliquot of the formulations containing butyrate or propionate was
centrifuged at 3600 rpm in an Amicon® Ultra-15 50 kDa, for 15 min.
The concentration of the encapsulated drug was analyzed using a pre­
viously validated chromatographic method (Agilent LC, model 1100 and
1200 series). The drug encapsulation efficiency was calculated accord­

2.4. Preparation of the emulsion containing nanoparticles
Two types of emulsions were prepared to evaluate the best condi­
tions for developing microparticles containing NPs; a single oil/water
emulsion (o/w; ratio 20:80) and a double water/oil/water emulsion
(w1/o/w2; ratio 1:9:90). The oil phase of the o/w emulsion was prepared
by dispersing PLGA (5 %, w/v) in EA; the external aqueous phase con­
sisted of EA-saturated water containing Tween 80 (0.6 %, w/v) and
25 % (v/v) of the concentrated NPs suspension. To prepare the single
emulsion, the oil phase was slowly poured over the aqueous phase under
high-speed agitation (Silverson Machines Ltd., model L4RT) for 3 min at
a stirring speed of 4000 rpm.
The oil phase of the w1/o/w2 double emulsion was prepared by
dispersing PLGA (5 %, w/v) in EA; the suspension of NPs containing the
postbiotics was used as the internal aqueous phase. The w1 phase was
mixed with the oil phase to form a primary w1/o emulsion by vortexing
(Velp Scientifica) at maximum speed for 1 min. The primary emulsion
was, then, slowly added to the EA-saturated aqueous solution containing
Tween 80 (0.6 %, w/v) by high-speed stirring (Silverson Machines Ltd.,
model L4RT) for 5 min at 2300 rpm. All emulsions were processed by
SEE immediately after preparation.

involving counter-current extraction of the solvent in the oil phase of the
emulsion by supercritical CO2. The plant consists of several sections as
shown in the layout of Fig. 1. The extraction protocol is detailed in
previous studies [30,35]. The prototype apparatus consists of a
stainless-steel packed column (1889 m−1 specific surface and 0.94 void;
Pro-Pak, Scientific Development Company), where CO2 is fed from the
bottom of the column using a high-pressure diaphragm pump (Milton

All experiments were repeated twice (N = 2).
2.6. Solvent evaporation (SE)
The solvent evaporation (SE) technique was also applied to the
emulsions to compare the results of conventional and supercritical
techniques. To do this, the emulsions were stirred at 120 rpm for 4 h at a
temperature of 37 ◦C in a laminar flow to allow the solvent to be
removed by evaporation. The suspension obtained was washed, centri­
fuged and collected on a membrane filter, following the same protocol
described for SEE process.
2.7. Characterization of the nano-in-micro system
2.7.1. Size distribution and morphology analyses
The droplets of the emulsions obtained were analyzed using an op­
tical microscope (Olympus, model BX 50). The droplet size (DS) and
particle size (PS) distributions were measured by dynamic light scat­
tering (DLS; Malvern Instruments, model Mastersizer S and Zetasizer
Nano-S) after preparation of the emulsion and suspension. Size distri­
butions were obtained by averaging 10 measurements. In addition to the
size distribution, the particle shrinkage factor percentage (SF%) was also
calculated from the ratio between the mean particle diameter and the
mean droplet diameter.
To analyze the morphology, the particles were placed on a double
adhesive carbon tape and coated with gold (250 Å thickness) using a
sputter coater (Agar Scientific, model 108 A). They were, then, analyzed
using a Field Emission-Scanning Electron Microscope (FE-SEM; Carl
Zeiss, model Supra 35).
2.7.2. Encapsulation efficiency (EE%)
The encapsulation process was performed according to the meth­
odology described by Gimenez-Rota et al. [13] with adaptations. Briefly,
a known mass (approximately 75 mg) of particles was dissolved in
0.2 mL of EA to disrupt PLGA and disperse the encapsulated molecules.
Then, 1.8 mL of water saturated with EA was added to precipitate the
polymer. The suspension was then centrifuged at 6500 rpm for 30 min
and the supernatant was collected, kept under magnetic stirring for 3 h
to evaporate the solvent and then collected and filtered to assess the
concentration. The concentration assessment was carried out using high
performance liquid chromatography (HPLC; Agilent LC, models 1100
and 1200 series) equipped with a Prodigy ODS3 column (100 Å, 5 μm
250 ×4.6 mm). The mobile phase used was 90:10 ACN:Water acidified
0.1 % PhA; the flow rate was 0.8 mL/min. The injection volume was
20 μL for each test and the detection wavelength was 216 nm. A cali­
bration curve was generated for each postbiotic. The percentage

2.7.3. Analysis of interactions via spectroscopy in the Fourier transform
infrared region (FTIR)
The characteristic vibrations of the bonds corresponding to the
structure of the particles were evaluated by vibrational absorption
spectroscopy in the infrared region (FTIR). The spectra of the systems
were obtained using a spectrometer (Shimadzu, model IRTracer-100)
under the following conditions: percentage transmittance (%T), reso­
lution of 1 cm−1 and spectral range of 46 analyses from 500 to
4000 cm−1 using the KBr wafer transmission technique.
2.7.4. Energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX)
Elemental analysis and element mapping were carried out using a
FE-SEM equipped with Energy Dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX;
Oxford Instruments, model INCA Energy 350). The particles were pre­
viously placed on a double-sided adhesive carbon tape and then coated
with chrome (EMITECH, model K575X). EDX analysis was carried out to
assess the presence of active ingredients in the particles through the
Sodium atoms present in the butyrate and the propionate.
2.8. In vitro release studies
The evaluation of the in vitro release of butyrate and propionate from
nanoparticles and nano-in-microparticles was carried out under mag­
netic stirring, at a shaking speed of 200 rpm and a temperature of 37 ◦C
± 0.4. A mass of nanoparticles or nano-in-microparticles was added to
the snakeskin dialysis membrane and incubated in the dissolution me­
dium. To mimic the pH variations that occur throughout the GIT,
different media were used: hydrochloric acid buffer (HCl/KCl; pH 1.2) to
simulate gastric fluid (for 120 min), phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) to
simulate intestinal fluid (120–360 min) and phosphate buffer (pH 6.8)
to simulate colonic fluid (360–720 min). Aliquots of 0.5 mL were
removed at predetermined times (with immediate replacement of the
medium), filtered through a 0.22 μm cellulose acetate membrane and
the amount of released postbiotics was quantified by HPLC. All analyzes
were performed in triplicate.
Mathematical models were applied to evaluate the drug release
mechanisms. The release was analyzed by adjusting (SigmaPlot 14.0
software) to different mathematical models (Korsmeyer - Peppas,
Higuchi, First order, Hixson - Crowell, Baker - Lonsdale and Weibull)
using the highest adjusted coefficient of determination (R2).
2.9. Statistical analysis
Data are presented as mean ± SD (standard deviation). The analysis
was performed by ANOVA followed by multiple comparisons applying
the Tukey method, using Sigma Plot 11.0 and GraphPad Prism 5 soft­
ware. Differences between groups were considered statistically signifi­
cant when p < 0.05.
3. Results and discussion
3.1. Production and characterization of nanoparticles
To encapsulate the postbiotics in NPs, we chose to use the emulsifi­
cation technique followed by evaporation of the solvent. This process
started with the preparation of an oil-in-water (o/w) emulsion. In NPs
developed in this study, the organic phase was prepared by combining
ethanol, Labrafac™, and soy lecithin as an emulsifier.
The used Labrafac™ consists of caprylic and capric acid triglycerides
and is commonly used as a vehicle in parenteral formulations. Its pres­
ence in NPs was selected to promote the retention of postbiotics, even
after the evaporation of the solvents. Soy lecithin was incorporated to
facilitate the emulsification process. This emulsifier is of natural origin
and consists of a mixture of phospholipids, such as phosphatidylcholine,

lecithins with a higher fatty acid content tending to have a higher
negative charge density due to the ionization of carboxylic groups [17].

According to the results obtained, NPs exhibited an average diameter
ranging between 149 nm and 165 nm by DLS and between 92 nm and
142 nm by NTA, with no significant difference between the two tech­
niques. In addition to the average particle diameter, NTA technique also
provides data related to particle concentration that is an important in­
formation considering the subsequent encapsulation of nanoparticles
into microparticles. The nanoparticles exhibited an optimal concentra­
tion, within the ideal working range (106-1010) [45].
The particle diameter range obtained in this study is particularly
favorable for colon drug release applications. Nanoparticles of this size
tend to have a longer residence time in the gastrointestinal tract and a
better permeation in the mucosa. Furthermore, particles with a diameter
of less than 200 nm are more likely to avoid early absorption in the
upper portions of the gastrointestinal tract, increasing the likelihood
that they will reach the colon in an intact form, especially when incor­
porated into microparticles. In addition, the observed PdI values indi­
cate a relatively narrow and homogeneous size distribution.
ZP is an essential property directly linked to the stability of nano­
structured systems and the understanding of biological interactions. ZP
values above + 30 mV or below −30 mV in NPs indicate a high ioni­
zation rate and, as a result, a greater electrostatic repulsion between
nanoparticles. Under these circumstances, aggregation can be reduced,
improving the electrostatic stability of the systems [6]. In this study, it is
possible to observe that NPs presented a high density of negative charge
due to the presence of D-glucuronic acid and N-acetyl-D-glucosamine in
HA that make the polymer strongly anionic.
From EE% results, it is possible to observe that NPs showed a larger
propionate encapsulation (80.51 %) when compared to butyrate NPs
(58.41 %). It is suggested that this occurs due to the structural size of
butyrate and because they are larger molecules when compared to
propionate; however, in both cases, particles were efficient for encap­
sulating postbiotics, exhibiting a good EE%.
3.2. SEE encapsulation of propionate and butyrate NPs in PLGA
microparticles
The different emulsions prepared and processed by SEE (o/w and w/
o/w) were evaluated and compared to select the best condition for the
co-encapsulation of NPs containing butyrate and propionate. The
operating conditions of the equipment were selected according to a
previously optimized method [13,30]. The main obtained results are
summarized in Table 2.
The results show that, despite the same concentration of PLGA and
surfactant, the variation in the kind of prepared emulsion had a direct
influence on the physical properties and encapsulation of NPs. The
particles prepared by single emulsification (SEEO/W) were characterized
by a reduced mean diameter compared to those prepared by double
emulsification (SEEW/O/W) and also showed a low encapsulation effi­
ciency of NPs (17.8 % for propionate and 20.2 % for butyrate). These
results are confirmed by the size distribution graph (Fig. 3), where it can
be seen that the particles obtained by single emulsification showed a
population between 100 and 200 nm, suggesting that it was a popula­
tion formed by unencapsulated NPs.
The particles developed from the double emulsion process exhibited
larger droplet and particle diameters that can be beneficial for
improving the encapsulation performance of NPs, as well as acting as a
physical barrier to protect the active ingredients in the upper portions of
the GIT. These results were confirmed by the encapsulation efficiency
analysis, where the particles showed a high encapsulation efficiency of
the postbiotics of around 39 % for PROP and 36 % for BUT. This result
can be considered an excellent percentage when it comes to the co-
encapsulation of nanoparticles and is higher than that found in the
literature for other methods, where the encapsulation efficiency of
polymeric nanoparticles in microparticles generally ranges from 12 % to
25 % ([10,3]; W. [22,27,42]).
Shrinkage was observed in both particles processed by SEE; but, the
shrinkage factor was higher in the double emulsion. This observation
can be justified by the fact that, in these types of emulsion, the con­
centration of droplets formed in the primary emulsion is generally
limited by the amount of surfactant and oil phase, which allows for a
limited number of micelles and, consequently, larger droplets are pre­
pared. The shrinkage rate of SEEW/O/W agrees with other studies ob­
tained by SEE from double emulsions [13,30].

3.3. Comparison between SE and SEE
To compare the results of conventional and supercritical techniques,
the previously selected emulsions were also treated using the solvent
evaporation technique. The main obtained results are shown in Table 3.
The results obtained from the emulsions processed by SE show that
the application of this conventional technique allowed for the formation
of larger diameter carriers (2350 ± 910) compared to SEE technique,
also shown in Fig. 5. This result suggests that these particles may have
undergone a physical instability during development, known as Ostwald
maturation, which is responsible for promoting droplet aggregation. In
SEE processing, the rapid removal of solvent in the oil phase by SC-CO2
promotes a rapid stiffening of the polymer during particle formation,
preventing this instability. SEM micrographs also show that many par­
ticles were empty, which explains the low encapsulation efficiency of
this technique. All these results agree with previous studies [11,30].
3.4. Spectroscopy in the Fourier transform infrared region (FTIR) and
energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX)
FTIR analysis was carried out as a preliminary assessment of the
molecular structure of the constituents of the particles and the possible
chemical interactions between the particles and NPs containing the
postbiotics (Fig. 6A). For propionate and butyrate, the main absorption
bands were at approximately 2960 cm−1, related to the C-H stretching
vibration, and at about 1650 cm−1, related to the C––O stretching vi­
bration. From the PLGA spectrum, a C-O-C stretching peak was observed
around 1100 cm−1. The CH2 stretching was observed around
1400–1450 cm−1, whereas the C––O stretching vibration was noted at
1790 cm−1. C-H, -CH2 and -CH3 gave subtle signals between 2800 and
3000 cm−1.
FTIR spectra of the particles prepared by SEE showed the main PLGA
and postbiotic peaks, but attenuated. The C––O stretching vibration at
1790 cm−1, that was present in the PLGA spectrum, also appeared in the
particles. The typical signal for postbiotics around 1650 cm−1 was also
present in the particles obtained by SEE, and there were also substitution
signals between 2800 and 3000 cm−1. It is suggested that these spectral
changes, such as peak attenuation, are related to the formation of
hydrogen bonds and supramolecular interactions between PLGA mole­
cules and NPs, consolidating the formation of microparticles containing
NPs.
To demonstrate the presence of NPs in PLGA microparticles, the
samples processed by SEE were characterized by EDX analysis that
verified the detection of Sodium (Na) present in the sodium butyrate and
sodium propionate of NPs (Fig. 6B).
Although sodium is an extremely water-soluble element, that would
justify the loss of a large part of it in the aqueous phase of the emulsion,
the results obtained by EDX showed the presence of Sodium in particle
maps, confirming the encapsulation of NPs. These results, together with
those presented above, demonstrate the potential of encapsulating NPs
containing butyrate and propionate in PLGA microparticles by SEE.
3.5. Release profile study
The therapeutic performance of drugs for colonic delivery is directly
correlated to their release profile, mimicking the physiological condi­
tions of delivery. To understand the release profile of NPs and nano-in-
microparticles given the different pH variations of the GIT, particles
behavior was evaluated at pH 1.2, pH 7.4, and pH 6.8 (Fig. 7).
NPs release curve shows that, although NPs control the release of the
active ingredients, all samples completely released the active ingredient
before reaching the colonic pH (pH 6.8), confirming the importance of
encapsulating NPs in microparticles to protect the particles in the con­
ditions of the GIT.
The microparticles were highly effective in controlling the release of
the postbiotics in the GIT. Release rates were approximately 15 % at
acidic pH for both compounds, 30 % in phosphate buffer pH 7.4, and
they were only fully released after 24 h in a simulated colonic envi­
ronment. It is suggested that this behavior is related to the more compact
and organized PLGA matrix, making it difficult for the molecules to
diffuse. In addition, the ability of these particles to protect the active
compound at gastric pH is remarkable, showing greater protection than
those developed using nano-in-microparticles by other techniques [10,
29,3].

Information).
