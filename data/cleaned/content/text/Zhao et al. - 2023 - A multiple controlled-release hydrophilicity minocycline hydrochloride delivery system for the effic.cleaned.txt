2.2. Preparation and in vitro evaluation of MH-NPs and MH-NPs-in-gels

2.2.1. Preparation of MH-NPs
Approximately 12.0 mg MH was dissolved in 1.0 mL distilled water
as aqueous phase I. Then, 15.0 mg sodium dextran sulfate (DS) and 5.0
mg CaCl2 were dissolved in 1 mL distilled water as aqueous phase II.
Next, 60.0 mg PLGA (GA:LA = 50:50, 10 kDa) was dissolved in 4.0 mL
dichloromethane and was equally divided into two parts to obtain two
oil phases. Aqueous phases I and II were respectively dropped into two
oil phases in an ice-water bath to obtain two W/O primary emulsions by
the ultrasonic probe (SCIENTZ-IID, Scientz Biotechnology Co., Ltd,
Ningbo, China) at 285 w for 2 min. Two W/O primary emulsions were
mixed and sonicated with an ultrasonic probe at 285 w for 2 min to
obtain an ultimate emulsion, which was then dropped into 20.0 mL of
1% sodium cholate solution and sonicated with an ultrasonic probe at
285 w for 5 min to prepare the W/O/W double emulsion. Finally, The
MH-NPs were obtained after removing the organic solvent in double
emulsion by rotational evaporation (EYELA-N-1300S, Tokyo Rikakikai
Co., Ltd, Japan). The MH-NPs powders were obtained by freeze-drying
after mixing MH-NPs with sucrose and lactose.

To gain insight into the reasons for the different release behavior of
formulations, the release kinetics of MH from the MH-in-gels, MH-NPs,
and MH-NPs-in-gel were investigated to predict the possible mechanism
of release. A regression coefficient value (r2) close to 1 indicated a better

3.4.1. The release kinetics of MH-in-gels
The release of MH from MH-in-gels followed the first-order kinetics
(r2 = 0.9606), indicating that it was controlled by concentration gra
dients (Lisik and Musial, 2019). Therefore, the MH diffusion through the
system was greater than the hydrogel bulk erosion, and drug molecules
were mainly released by diffusing through the hydrophilic channels of
the hydrogel.  This also led to difficulties in
controlling the release behavior of hydrophilic MH through hydrogels
alone.

2.3.1. Animals
Forty male Sprague Dawley (SD) rats (6-8 weeks old, 190 +- 10 g)
were used in the experiments, which were provided by Liaoning
Changsheng Biotech Co., Ltd. (Benxi, China). All animal experiments
followed the Guiding Principles of Shenyang Pharmaceutical University
and were approved by the ethical committee of Shenyang Pharmaceu
tical University. (Ethical No.: CSE20201113).

High glucose feeding (Keyes and Jordan, 1964) and silk ligation of
animal teeth  were used to induce periodontitis
in this study. All SD rats were kept for one week for adjustable feeding
before the experiment and had free access to food and water. Forty SD
rats were randomly divided into five groups (n = 8): A (healthy), B
(control), C (MH-in-gels), D (MH-NPs), and E (MH-NPs-in-gels). Except
for group A, the remaining 4 groups of 32 SD rats were used to construct
periodontitis models. The 2% inhaled isoflurane was used to anesthetize

lator, and then a 0.2-mm-diameter orthodontic steel wire was ligated
around the left maxillary first molar for 4 weeks. During this period, the
32 rats were fed with high glucose feeding, and the ligatures were
checked daily after the application. If the ligature disappears, a new
ligature will be placed. On the 4th week, the 100 mL of MH-in-gels, MH-
NPs, and MH-NPs-in-gels (containing 1.0 mg MH) were injected into the
periodontal pockets of the SD rats in groups C-E once a week for 4
weeks, respectively. The severity of periodontal disease was evaluated
by the following parameters once a week: gingival index (GI), peri
odontal pocket depth (PPD), tooth mobility (TM), and weight. Values of
PPD were measured using a round-ended probe with a tip of 0.3 mm in
diameter. The scoring criteria of GI and TM were shown in S 1. All SD
rats in groups A-E were executed by deep anesthesia at week 8 to obtain
maxillary periodontal tissues.

3.1.2. Construction of nanogenerator
The control of particle size was also a key step in nanoparticle
preparation. A nanogenerator was built and combined with the double

shown in Fig. 1A, three substances (DS, MH, and CaCl2) were prepared
into two W/O primary emulsions, and then two primary emulsions were
mixed and ultrasonicated to prepare the ultimate emulsion. Subse
quently, the ultimate emulsion was made into a double emulsion, and
the MH-NPs were obtained after removing the organic solvent. After
mixing of the two primary emulsions, three substances in the internal
aqueous phase contacted and complexation reaction occurred. After
sonication, the MH, Ca2+, and DS in the water phase were dispersed into
small droplets within the ultimate emulsion. This led to a low content of
MH, Ca2+ and DS in each droplet, followed by a rapid end of the
complexation reaction to form complexes with small particle size. The
small complexes were finally encapsulated by PLGA to obtain MH-NPs at
the nano level. Therefore, the droplets of the ultimate emulsion that
control the final formation of the complexes were also regarded as
nanogenerators, which was a critical factor in controlling MH-NPs par
ticle size.
Furthermore, the particle size of MH-NPs kept increasing with longer
mixing time. (Table 2) This was because that without sonication, the
complexation reaction would continue until one of MH, calcium ion, and
DS was completely consumed, and then the resulted complexes were
difficult to be disrupted by sonication. The earlier the sonication began
after mixing was, the lower extent of complexation reaction was. This
resulted in a smaller particle size of the internal droplets in ultimate
emulsion and decreased the amount of complex encapsulated in each
droplet. It further caused the smaller particle size of the complexes at the
end of complexation within the ultimate emulsion, so the MH-NPs with
smaller particle sizes were obtained.
The MH-NPs obtained under optimal conditions had the appearance
of a yellow suspension (Fig. 2A) and were evenly dispersed and non-
adherent to each other, and also had a smooth and full surface
without holes, as revealed by the TEM and AFM images. (Fig. 2A and C).
Meanwhile, the particle size of MH-NPs were 109.2 +- 47.25 nm and
138.7 +- 77.58 nm measured by DLS before and after freeze-drying
(Fig. 3A and B). Before freeze-drying, the particle size shown in the
TEM and AFM was smaller than that in the DLS. This was because DLS
indicated the overall size of the particle and the surrounding diffuse
layer of the particle in the aqueous media, however, TEM and AFM
analysis were attributed to the particle size alone under dry conditions.
(Yan et al., 2020a; Yan et al., 2020b) In contrast, after freeze-drying, the
particle size of MH-NPs observed by AFM and TEM (Fig. 2B and D) were
larger than that observed by DLS. This was because some aggregation
occurred between the freeze-dried nanoparticles, and the freeze-dried
protectant covered the surface of the nanoparticles after freeze-drying.
This resulted in larger particle size shown by TEM and AFM with
direct observation of the powder after freeze-drying. (Fu and Zhang,
2017) After re-dispersion, the freeze-dried protectant dissolved and
most of aggregates were re-dispersed between nanoparticles, so that

into temperature-sensitive gels, the obtained MH-NPs-in-gels not only
presented good retention and penetration, but also achieved multiple
controlled release of MH. This allowed MH to be consistently and deeply
delivered into the periodontal tissue, facilitating the treatment of peri
odontitis. It was further verified in periodontitis modelling rats that MH-
NPs-in-gels were highly effective in anti-inflammatory and bone repair

3.1. Preparation and characterization of MH-NPs

3.1.1. Formation of MH-Ca2+-DS complex and MH-NPs
In our study, the insoluble complex was constructed by MH, Ca2+,
and DS to achieve efficient encapsulation of MH. The selected three
substances (DS, MH, and CaCl2) might have interaction forces with each
other. The positively charged ammonium group in MH could form
electrostatic interaction with the negatively charged sulfate group in DS.
The hydroxyl group in DS could potentially form hydrogen bonding with
the hydroxyl, carbonyl, and amide groups in MH.  In
addition, Ca2+ could chelate with MH , and also
had electrostatic interaction with oppositely charged DS. However, no
insoluble complex was formed when any two of the three substances
(DS, MH, and CaCl2) were mixed, suggesting that DS or CaCl2 alone was
unable to form an insoluble complex with MH. Whereas the mixture
solution containing DS and CaCl2 in the reaction container immediately
became insoluble after adding MH solution, which indicated that the ion
pairing agents composed of CaCl2 and DS could form insoluble IIP
complexes with MH. Typically, one MH molecule had two metal ion
binding sites.  Therefore, as shown in Fig. 1A, after
the CaCl2 was bound to DS via electrostatic interaction, every MH
molecule was linked to two Ca2+ by ionic interaction. This caused the
hydrophilic hydrochloride in MH was replaced by the ion pairing agent,
thus forming the insoluble complex.
Then the optimal mass ratio of MH, CaCl2, and DS forming MH-NPs
was investigated to control the drug loading and encapsulation effi
ciency of MH-NPs. As shown in Table 1, the average particle size range
of MH-NPs was 100-120 nm measured by DLS, which were prepared
with different MH, CaCl2 and DS mass ratios. The optimum amounts of
MH, CaCl2 and DS were 12.0 mg, 5.0 mg, and 15.0 mg respectively, with
the highest drug loading (9.59%), the lowest PDI (0.136), and the
encapsulation efficiency of 95.58%. Any further increase or decrease in
amounts of MH, CaCl2, or DS resulted in a decrease in drug loading,
indicating that as the amount of three substances changes, the interac
tion within the complex gradually achieved equilibrium and drug
loading reached the highest level. Any increase or decrease in the
amounts of the three substances would break this equilibrium and
reduce the drug loading. More importantly, the MH was efficiently
encapsulated in the PLGA nanoparticles by insoluble complexes
construction.

3.5. Cell biocompatibility

After studying the in vitro release behavior and release mechanism of
microspheres, we further investigated the cytotoxicity of MH, PLGA,
MH-Ms, and temperature sensitive gels by CCK-8 assay. As shown in
Fig. 5E, only approximately 56% and 57% of the cells could respectively
survive for 1 day in 500 mg/mL of MH solution and MH-NPs, which was
caused by the too high MH concentration, while the cell viabilities of
MH, PLGA, Gels and MH-NPs at a concentration of 1- 200 mg/mL were
greater than 97%. This indicated that the MH, PLGA, Gels and the MH-
NPs were all low toxicity and biocompatible.
