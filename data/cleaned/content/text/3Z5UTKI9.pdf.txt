A B S T R A C T
The N-iodomethyl-N, N-Dimethyl-N-(6,6-diphenylhex-5-en-1-yl) ammonium iodide (C6I) is a new chemical en­
tity (NCE) with demonstrated in vitro and in vivo antileishmanial activity. As many others NCE, C6I is poorly
water soluble (Class II of the Biopharmaceutical Classification System), thus its oral administration is potentially
compromised. To overcome the issue, nanotechnology is a real and useful tool. Moreover, nanotechnology can
improve C6I internalization by macrophages, the drug targets, therefore increasing its antileishmanial activity.
The present work aimed to develop a potential suitable oral nanodrug delivery system for C6I to improve its
antileishmanial activity. For this purpose, polymeric and solid lipid nanoparticles were developed. Polylactic-co-
glycolic acid polyester (PLGA) were prepared by nanoprecipitation while solid lipid nanoparticles made of
glycerol tripalmitate (Trp) and glyceryl tristearate (Trs) were obtained by emulsion–solvent evaporation method.
Physicochemical characterization of all formulations was performed, as well as stability studies. A lyophilization
procedure was also established. Biological activity was evaluated by antileishmanial activity and cytotoxicity in
vitro assays.
Five types of commercial PLGA to encapsulate C6I were tested. The best results were obtained using PLGA
752S and D-α-Tocopheryl polyethylene glycol 1000 succinate (TPGS), the water-soluble form of vitamin E.
Particles around 220 nm in diameter and encapsulation efficiency (EE) up to 60% were obtained. Solid lipid
nanoparticles based on Trs and Trp lipids were also in the nanoscale showing a low polydispersity index and EE%
>60%. Both types of nanoformulations, polymeric and lipidic, thd C6I cytotoxicity in human and hamster
macrophages cell cultures. In vitro assays with PLGA loaded-C6I showed a noticeable improved antileishmanial
activity on intracellular amastigotes of L. (V.) panamensis, with the following EC50 values: PLGA «< Trs-SLN «
free C6I < Trp-SLN. In conclusion, PLGA nanoformulation could be a suitable candidate for preclinical evaluation
in animal model.
1. Introduction
Leishmaniasis is a neglected tropical disease that affects millions of
people worldwide [1]. Around 20 species of Leishmania can cause the
infection, in which this intracellular parasite invades macrophages. The
disease presents mainly three different clinical forms: visceral,
monocutaneous, and cutaneous leishmaniasis (CL). The CL is the most
common form and generally causes aberrant lesions on the infected in­
dividuals, bringing to them a permanent social stigma and psychological
suffering [1,2]. Leishmania spp. Is an intracellular parasite and infects
macrophages [1,2]. Pentavalent antimony salts are still considered the
first-line drug treatment. They present serious drawbacks including:

costs [2]. Meanwhile, the access to miltefosine, the only oral drug
approved for the treatment of leishmaniasis, is still strongly limited,
despite the efforts from institutions after the patent expired [3].
Therefore, there is an urgent needed to develop new drugs to treat
Leishmaniasis, which not only can target the parasite inside the mac­
rophages, but also provides more effective and affordable treatment for
patients.
In the search for new molecules with antileishmanial potential, new
choline analogs from the family of the halomethylated quaternary
ammonium salts were developed and demonstrated promising activity
[4]. Our group identified a series of 24 halomethylated and
non-halomethylated quaternary ammonium salts, being N-iodome­
thyl-N, N-dimethyl-N- (6,6-diphenylhex-5-en-1-yl) ammonium iodide
(C6I) the lead compound for the treatment of experimental CL [5–7].
The selection was based on the antileishmanial activity, meaning the
ability to inhibit the choline/ethanolamine kinase of L. major, at a
concentration lower than 20 μg/mL (on Leishamania (Viannia) pan­
amensis) [4,7].
In an attempt to deal with these challenges, different strategies have
been developed. In this sense, nanotechnology is a promising new
approach in the treatment of leishmaniasis. Encapsulating the drugs into
nanoscale systems, the physicochemical, biopharmaceutical and toxi­
cological properties significantly change. In the last decade, several
studies have been shown the application of nanosystems for treating
leishmaniasis, such as metallic, polymeric and lipid nanoparticles (NPs),
as well as liposomes and nanocrystals [8,9]. Currently, amphotericin B is
an example of the usage of this approach. The classical formulation
displays adverse effects on kidney and liver that restrict its use only to
some cases that did not respond to the first line treatment, that is based
on the intramuscular administration of meglumine antimoniate (MA),
while the liposomal form of amphotericin B (Ambisome®, Gilead Sci­
ences, Inc.) improves its clinical and biopharmaceutical profile [10].
Presently, this is the unique liposomal product approved for the treat­
ment of leishmaniasis, however it is not widely affordable because of its
high cost.
Polylactic-co-glycolic acid polyester (PLGA), a copolymer of lactic
and glycolic acids approved for pharmaceutical use by the FDA and the
EMA, is one of the most used polymers due to its biodegradability and
biocompatibility. Polymer-based NPs are widely used in experimental
models for the administration of active molecules, since they have been
successfully used as a passive delivery system for various intracellular
pathogens [11–13]. The potential of primaquine-loaded poly-­
alkylcyanoacrylate (PACA) NPs were 21 times more effective than free
primaquine to eliminate L. donovani [14]. Similarly, nanoencapsulated
miltefosine has shown greater activity against intracellular amastigotes

Nano-D-amphotericin B treatment elicited also an increased reduction in
the number of parasites and cell viability regarding the free amphoter­

the free molecules, and this implies in a reduced frequency of
administration.
On the other hand, solid lipid nanoparticles (SLN) are aqueous
colloidal dispersions with a matrix composed of biodegradable lipids
that are solid at room temperature. Many of these lipids are long chain
fatty acids, mono-, di- and triglycerides, sterols and waxes, with phos­
pholipids, poloxamers, polysorbates and sodium cholate or glycocholate
as surfactants that can solubilize lipophilic drugs and stabilize the
nanodispersions [17]. The SLN have become an alternative for drug
delivery because of their low cost and easy large-scale production and
storage. Recent advances in SLN formulation have improved the
lymphatic uptake and oral bioavailability of entrapped molecules. For
example, nano drug delivery systems can increase therapeutic efficacy
by releasing leishmanicidal drug substances in macrophage-rich organs,
such as the bone marrow, liver and spleen, improving the activity on the
target and reducing the toxic effects of several antileishmanial
substances [18]. Our group has demonstrated expertise in SLN and
developed a Precirol® lipid based-nanosystem [19] for C6I oral
administration. Results showed improved in vitro and in vivo anti­
leishmanial activity of C6I-loaded Precirol® SLNs, with EC50 values
40-fold lower than free C6I in animal model after a daily dose of 30
mg/kg/day for 28 days.
Furthermore, both PLGA NPs and SLN present controlled release
properties suitable for prolonged oral drug delivery, decreasing doses
and treatment duration [20]. Therefore, the goal of the present study
was to investigate the in vitro antileishmanial activity and cytotoxicity of
C6I-loaded PLGA NPs and SLN as potential nanocarriers for oral
administration.

2.3. Synthesis of nanoparticle
2.3.1. C6I-PLGA NPs
PLGA NPs were obtained by nanoprecipitation method [21] with
modifications [22–24]. Five PLGA references with different molecular
weight, lactic: acid ratio and carboxylic groups (Resomer® RG 502,
502H, 504, 504H and 752S) were assayed in order to select the most
suitable for C6I vehiculization. Hydrophilic and lipophilic surfactants as
Pluronic® F-68 (for the aqueous phase) and Span® 60 and TCP (for the
organic phase) were tested. Forty-five mg of polymer and 15 mg of

Span® 60 were co-dissolved in 3 mL of acetone. For loaded NPs, C6I was
dissolved in the polymer solution at 10% w/w polymer/drug. Blank NPs
(without drug) were used as control. The organic solution is dropwise
added at 5 mL/min using a syringe pump (Harvard Apparatus Ltd,
Edenbridge, UK) into 9 mL of 0.5% Pluronic® F-68 aqueous solution
under magnetic stirring. The NPs dispersion occurred under agitation for
4 h at room temperature until complete solvent evaporation. For the
selection of the surfactants, 15 mg of Span® 60 with different TPGS
concentrations were tested. The Pluronic® F-68 surfactant was main­
tained at 0.5%. The NPs suspensions with particle diameter lower than
300 nm, without aggregates and/or precipitates were selected. After
solvent evaporation, NPs were collected and washed by centrifugation at
10,000 g for 60 min at 4 ◦C (Eppendorf 504R centrifuge, Eppendorf AG,
Germany).
2.3.2. C6I-SLN NPs
In a first stage, the solubility of C6I in the molten lipids Trp and Trs
was determined. It was performed using a slight modification of a pre­
viously described method [24,25]. Briefly, the solid lipids Trp and Trs
were melted at 10 ◦C above each respective melting point, in a
controlled temperature water bath. Small amounts of C6I were then
successively added until the saturation of the lipid was achieved, cor­
responding to an excess of solid C6I that persisted for more than 8 h.
Each determination was carried out in triplicate.
The NPs of Trp and Trs loaded with C6I (C6I-Trp and C6I-Trs,
respectively) were prepared by the emulsion-solvent evaporation
method using Trp or Trs as lipid component, and sodium deoxycholate,
Tween® 20 and soy lecithin as co-surfactants. Trp or Trs (50 mg) was
dissolved with lecithin (360 mg) and mixed with C6I (3 or 5 mg) in
dichloromethane (organic phase), which was then added to the aqueous
phase containing Tween® 20 and sodium deoxycholate. The dispersion
step was performed by sonication for 3 min (Branson Sonifier 250, USA)
followed by 5 min of stirring at 12,500 rpm using a Silverson High Speed
Mixer L4RT (Silverson Machines, Chesham, UK). The NPs dispersion was
maintained by stirring for approximately 4 h at room temperature until
the evaporation of dichloromethane. The unbound C6I was separated
from the NPs dispersion by size exclusion chromatography in a PD-10
column (Bio-Rad Laboratories) using PBS pH 7.4 [26].
2.4. Characterization methods of PLGA and SLN NPs
The particle size and distribution were analysed by photon correla­
tion spectroscopy (PCS) using a Zetasizer Nano S (Malvern Instruments,
UK). Samples were maintained in polystyrene cuvettes and measure­
ments were performed at 25.0 ± 0.1 ◦C after dilution of the NPs in pu­
rified water (1:100). The results were expressed as mean diameter and
polydispersity index (PI). The surface charges (zeta potential, ZP) of the
C6I-PLGA, C6I-Trs, C6I-Trp and blank NPs were determined based on
the mobility of the particles in an electric field using a Zetasizer Nano Z
(Malvern Instruments, UK). For this step, 100 μL of the samples were
placed in a specific cuvette with 900 μL purified water. All measure­
ments were performed in triplicate. After dilution, the aspect and
morphology of PLGA and SLN NPs were determined by scanning elec­
tron microscopy (SEM, XL-30 Royal Philips Electronics, Amsterdam).
2.5. C6I encapsulation efficiency and drug loading
The drug content of the PLGA, Trs and Trp NPs was determined
indirectly by measuring the amount of non-encapsulated drug. After
centrifugation of the recently prepared NPs, the supernatant was
recovered and the amount of free C6I was measured by spectropho­
tometry in a microplate reader at 233 nm (UV-1700 Pharmaspec, Shi­
madzu, Japan). The supernatant from blank PLGA, Trs and Trp NPs were
used as blank. The quantification method was validated according to the
International Guide for Validation of Analytical Procedures (Interna­
tional Conference on Harmonisation - ICH), topic Q2 (R1) 2005 [27].
The calibration curve was constructed over the range 10–0.25 mg/mL
(r2 = 0.9990, limit of detection: 0.1 mg/mL, limit of quantification: 0.3
mg/mL). The encapsulation efficacy percentage (EE%) and C6I drug
loading percentage (DL%) was calculated according to the following

2.7. Stability
Colloidal stability of NPs in biological environments at different
storage conditions is critical to the design of optimal preparations, and
to clarify the fate of these nanosystems after administration. Thus, the
stability of PLGA NPs and SLN was evaluated at high and low temper­
atures, different pH solutions and lyophilized conditions. In these
evaluations, the mean particle diameter, ZP values and drug loading
were assessed.
2.7.1. SLN stability
2.7.1.1. Storage conditions. Fresh solutions of PLGA, Trs and Trp NPs
(blank and loaded with C6I) were stored at 4 ◦C (±2 ◦C). SLNs particles
were stored for 2 months. PLGA NPs were stored for 2 and 6 months also
[24–26]. Mean particle diameter, PI, ZP and DL were determined after
separation of the unbound C6I by spectrophotometry in a microplate
reader at 233 nm [29].

synthesis of SLN, a deep thermal stability study was carried out for fresh
Trp and Trs NPs. Samples were tested under a programmed heating-
cooling cycle. The samples were properly diluted with purified water
(1:100) in a quartz cell, and particle size analysis was performed while

2.9. pH effect on the NPs
It is known that the pH of aqueous dispersions of PLGA NPs can affect
the chemical stability of the polymer. A brief study was carried out to
determine PLGA NPs stability under different simulated biological en­
vironments. Blank and C6I-loaded PLGA NPs were incubated in 1 mL of:
(i) phosphoric acid buffer pH 1–1.2; (ii) phosphate buffer pH 6.8; (iii)
phosphate buffer pH 7.4, and (IV) 0.9% of saline solutions. In all cases, 1
mL of aqueous dispersion PLGA NPs was added and the mixtures were
incubated at 37 ◦C (sample suspension:buffer volume rate was 1:10).
The particle size was determined at 15 and 30 min, and following 1, 2, 3,
4, 6, 7 and 24 h.
2.10. Lyophilization: effect of cryoprotector on PLGA NPs
The influence of cryoprotectors on the quality of freeze-dried for­
mulations has been widely investigated and its use is often necessary to
reduce particles aggregation and obtain a better re-dispersion of the
freeze-dried product [30,31]. To preserve PLGA NPs, different concen­
trations of trehalose as cryoprotectant were tested. Once the trehalose
was added, the particle size was measured. The sample that maintained
the particle size was selected and freeze-dried (Criodos-50, Telstar In­
dustrial SL, Terrassa, Spain) at −80 ± 0.5 ◦C and 0.057 mbar, to obtain a
fine powder. All formulations were prepared in triplicate.
2.11. In vitro cytotoxicity activity
The cytotoxicity of free C6I, blank NPs (Trs, Trp and PLGA) and
loaded NPs (C6I-Trs, C6I-Trp and C6I-PLGA) was evaluated in vitro on
primary cultures of human monocyte-derived macrophages (MDMhu)
and golden hamster peritoneal macrophages (MPha). Cytotoxicity was
also evaluated in Caco-2 human intestinal cells (ATCC® HTB-37™) and
Detroit 551 human fibroblasts (ATCC® CCL-110™). Cytotoxic effect
was determined by cell viability assay using the MTT (3-(4,5-dime­
thylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide) enzymatic micro
method [32]. Briefly, cells grown at 100,000 cell/100 μL in RPMI-1640
supplemented with 10% FBS, 1% antibiotics (10,000 units of penicillin
and 10 mg/mL streptomycin). Then, they were exposed to 100 μL of the
corresponding concentrations (200, 50, 12.5 and 3.125 μg/mL) of NPs
(loaded or blank) or free C6I. After 72 h of incubation at 37 ◦C with 5%
CO2, 10 μL/well of MTT solution (0.5 mg/mL) was added to each well
and plates were incubated at 37 ◦C for 3 h. The reaction was stopped by
the addition of 100 μL/well of isopropanol solution at 50% with sodium
dodecyl sulphate at 10%, following 30 min of incubation. Cell viability
was determined by the amount of formazan produced according to the
color intensity (absorbance), recorded as the optical density (O.D) ob­
tained at 570 nm in a Varioskan reader (ThermoFisher Scientific, MA,
USA). Untreated cells were used as a viability control, and doxorubicin
was used as a control for cytotoxicity. At least two independent assays
were performed. The results were expressed in percentage as the mean
lethal concentrations (LC50), calculated by Probit analysis [33], as
follows:
% viability = (O.D exposed cells / O.D unexposed cells) x 100
From that, the percentage of cell growth inhibition was determined
as:
% inhibition = 100 - % Viability.
The toxicity was defined according to the LC50 values, accordingly:
high cytotoxicity: LC50 < 100 μg/mL; moderate cytotoxicity: 100 μg/
mL < LC50 < 200 μg/mL; and low cytotoxicity: LC50 > 200 μg/mL [4–7,
32].
2.12. 2.9 In vitro antileishmanial activity
The antileishmanial activity of the blank NPs, C6I-loaded NPs and
free C6I was evaluated on intracellular amastigotes of L. (V.) panamensis
transfected with the gene of the green fluorescent protein (MHOM/CO/
87/UA140-EGFP) [34]. The effect of each formulation was determined
according to the inhibition of infection observed and the decreased
parasite load within the macrophages. In summary, human U-937 cells
at a concentration of 3 × 105 cells/mL in complete RPMI 1640 medium
and 0.1 μg/mL phorbol 12-myristate 13-acetate were infected with
promastigotes using the rate 15:1 parasites per cell, and then incubated
for 3 h at 34 ◦C and 5% CO2. Afterwards, cells were washed twice with
PBS to remove non-internalized parasites. One mL of complete 1640
RPMI medium was added, and the cells were incubated for 24 h to
ensure multiplication of intracellular parasites. After 24 h of infection,
the medium was replaced by fresh medium containing each type of NPs
at concentrations of 50, 12.5, 3.125 and 0.78 μg/mL, following incu­
bation. After 72 h, the effect of each formulation on the parasite load
was determined. Cells were detached from the dish with trypsin-EDTA
solution (250 mg). The recovered cells were centrifuged at 1100 rpm
for 10 min at 4 ◦C, the supernatant was discarded, and the cells were
washed with 1 mL of cold PBS and centrifuged at 1100 rpm for 10 min at
4 ◦C. The supernatant was discarded, the cells were suspended in 500 μL
of PBS and analysed by flow cytometry (FC 500MPL, Cytomics, Brea, CA,
USA), counting at least 10,000 events. The antileishmanial activity was
determined according to the reduction of the number of viable parasites
within the infected cells obtained for each formulation and at each
concentration. The number of parasites for each concentration of each
type of NPs was calculated using the following formula:
% Infection = (MFI of infected and treated cells / MFI of untreated infected
cells) × 100
Then, inhibition percentage was calculated as:
% Inhibition of parasites = 100 - % Infection
All assays were performed in triplicate, in two independent experi­
ments. The results of antileishmanial activities were expressed as the
median effective concentrations (EC50) measured by Probit method
[32]. The activity of each compound was established according to the
EC50 values as: high activity: EC50 < 25 μg/mL; moderate activity: 25
μg/mL < EC50 < 50 μg/mL; and potentially not active: EC50 > 100
μg/mL [4–7].

3. Results
3.1. Synthesis and characterization of PLGA and SLN NPs
3.1.1. PLGA NPs
Five polymers were tested in standard conditions with the purpose of
selecting excipients for the nanoformulation, previously to the synthesis
of this NP. The stability of the PLGA NPs suspensions was dependent on
the nature of the polymer. After 4 h of evaporation of the solvent, the
stability of the suspension was observed macroscopically, and the size of
the particles was measured. No sediments or aggregates were observed
macroscopically in the suspension of the NPs elaborated using
Resomer® GR 752S. For the rest of the Resomer® assayed, particles got
into visible aggregates during acetone evaporation step at room tem­
perature. The particles were larger than 2 μm, therefore difficult to re-
suspend, so they were discarded.
Differences were also observed between blank and loaded NPs
elaborated with Resomer® GR 752S and Span® 60 (Hydrophile-Lip­
ophile Balance (HLB) 4.7) as a surfactant. Results are summarized in
Table 1. Smaller diameters were obtained for blank NPs (≈300 nm),
while loaded NPs showed diameters higher than 1000 nm (formulations
#1 and #2).
Best nanoformulation was selected based on the criteria of size
(<300 nm), hence the assay of different combinations of surfactants.
First, TCP (HLB 13) was included as surfactant in the organic phase. The
concentration of Span® 60 (HLB 4.7) was the same. Using a 5% w/w of
TPGS (formulations #3 and #4), diameters decreased around 50% (from
1320 nm to 688 nm), while using 10% of TPGS (formulations #5 and
#6) diameters decreased around 70% (from 1320 nm to 441 nm), with a
narrow size distribution (PI ≈0.1). Nevertheless, higher concentrations
of TCP (15% and 20%) (Formulations #7 - #10) did not reduce NPs size.
Moreover, the replacement of Span® 60 by Pluronic® F-68, keeping
10% of TPGS, decreased particle sizes to ≈220 nm (formulations #11
and #12).
The zeta potential (ZP) was moderately affected by the final NP
composition. The formulations containing TPGS showed reduced ZP
values, indicating that there was some drug on the surface of the
particles.
The formulation selected was #12, which presented the best profile
according to the criteria (size <300 nm, suitable for oral formulation).
The SEM image of #12 showed the particle size smaller than 1 μm,
corroborating the PCS results. (Fig. 1A).
3.1.2. Characterization of Trs and Trp NPs
The C6I-Trp and C6I-Trs SLN were prepared using the emulsion-
solvent evaporation method indicated above. After preparation of C6I-
Trp and C6I-Trs NPs, lipid saturation was observed using an initial
amount of C6I of 5 mg. Especially in these conditions, the EE% was 50%
for Trp and 63% for Trs. The PI was superior to 0.3, and the zeta po­
tential was affected by the electrical charge of the internalized com­
pound, hence the size was increased. Reducing the initial amount from 5
to 3 mg, EE% increased up to 65–67% for both formulations. For these
same formulations, particles around 104–107 nm in diameter with a
narrow size distribution (PI ≈0.25) were obtained. In general, the ZP
values the relation was inverse with drug concentration: the higher the
drug load, the lower the ZP values and the best concentration of com­
pound was 3 mg (P < 0.05). (Table 2).
In a morphologic study by SEM, C6I-Trs NPs was selected for higher
EE% in comparison to Trp. Those formulations showed spherical and
homogeneous particles. Nevertheless, some aggregates were also
observed as a result of the sample preparation process (a thin film of gold
was spotted over the samples in high vacuum under low pressure)
(Fig. 1B-C).
3.2. C6I release studies
PLGA NPs showed a C6I time-dependent release profile, reaching
asymptotic values at ≈24 h. In the first hour, more than 50% of the C6I
is released at physiological pH 7.4. For example, for Trp NPs, after 8 h of
assay, about half of the drug concentration was released. The release
profile of C6I in Trs and PLGA NP was similar. The obtained values
corresponding to f1 and f2 for both formulations were 3.86 and 66.58,
respectively. According to Diaz and cols. [35], f1 values up to 15 (0–15)
and f2 values greater than 50 (50–100) ensure the “sameness” or
“equivalence” of the two dissolution profiles. Therefore, both release
profiles (Trs and PLGA NPs) can be considered similar.
After 20 h, the amount of C6I released was >94% from Trs NPs and
90% from Trp NPs. Release profile was more sustained in Trp NPs, then
becoming exponential until 22 h (Fig. 2).
In vitro release profiles were fitted to several kinetic models widely
used (zero order, first order and Higuchi model). To test the pertinence
of the release models employed, the Akaike Information Criteria (AIC)
were used. The AIC are a measure of the best fit based on maximum
probability. Table 3 shows the correlation coefficients (r2) and AIC
values obtained after fitting.
The Higuchi model provided the best adjustment curve for all the
NPs with the higher determination coefficients and smallest AIC values
for all the NPs tested, indicating that the release mechanism was mainly
diffusional.

3.3. Stability
3.3.1. SLN
3.3.1.1. Storage conditions. According to the results obtained in the
previous section, 3 mg loaded SLN were selected to continue with the
stability studies. The stability of blank and 3 mg loaded Trs and Trp NPs
was assessed by measuring the changes on diameter and ZP. The
refrigerated storage conditions 4 ◦C (±2 ◦C) caused minor changes in the
diameter of loaded Trs and Trp NPs after two months of storage, whereas
the ZP decreased significantly (P < 0.05). (Table 4).
3.3.1.2. DLS and DCS tests. Moreover, a deep study of the effect of
temperature on the diameter of Trp and Trs NPs were evaluated by DLS,
applying a heating gradient from 25 to 90 ◦C, then cooling down to the
initial temperature at the same rate. The diameter measurements were
performed every 0.5 ◦C/min by Zetasizer Nano S (Malvern Instruments,
UK). When Trp and Trs NPs were subjected to a temperature increase, a
gradual reduction in diameter throughout the heating step was
observed. The initial diameter value was not recovered after a cooling
phase. The formulation composed of Trp was the most affected, reducing
its diameter from 104 to 85 nm in the temperature range of 70–80 ◦C.
The melting point of Trp is lower than the melting point of Trs, which
withstand a high temperature than Trp NPs. In the same way, Trs NPs
suffered the same decreased of diameter around 60 ◦C. Despite these
variations in diameter, the values were always within the nanorange
(Fig. 3A-B).
The raw lipids presented melting points between 70 ◦C and 80 ◦C.
The melting point of C6I was 160.1 ◦C. However, for loaded C6I-Trp and
Trs NPs, the melting point peak of the drug was not detected. Perhaps it
can be explained by a structural change of the drug within the particles
from its crystalline form to an amorphous or molecularly dispersed state.
For all formulations, the melting points of the Trs and Trp were lower
than the melting points of the bulk lipids. Fig. 3C shows the thermo­
grams of each component of Trp and Trs NPs (blank and loaded NPs).
PLGA stability.
3.3.1.3. Storage conditions. According to the results displayed in
Table 1, formulations #11 and #12 were selected for this study. The
stability test did not show significant variations in monitored parame­
ters (diameter, PI, ZP, EE and DL) after 6 months of storage under
refrigeration (P < 0.05) (Table 5).
3.3.1.4. pH effect. The chemical stability of PLGA is greatly affected by
pH. This was confirmed with formulation #12, which revealed polymer
degradation by hydrolysis at extreme conditions of pH and temperature.
In the present work, it was also studied how different pH buffered values
can affect the diameter of loaded C6I-NPs.
Phosphoric acid buffer (pH 1.2, with and without pepsine), phos­
phate buffer (pH 6.8), phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) and 0.9% saline solu­
tion were used. Results are summarized in Fig. 4. No variations on
particle diameter after incubation at pH 1.2 (with or without pepsine)
and 0.9% saline solution. However, after the incubation of the NPs in
phosphate buffer at pH 7.4 and at pH 6.8, significant size variations were
observed.
3.3.1.5. Lyophilization: effect of cryoprotector on PLGA NPs. Freeze-
drying is a widely used process for drying and improving the stability of
various pharmaceutical products, including NPs. The process involved
three steps: freezing, ice sublimation and desorption of unfrozen water.
There are many factors that can affect the quality of the final product
after freeze-drying: type and the concentration of the cryoprotectant,
nature of the surfactant, chemical groups attached to the NPs surface, or
the type of polymer used to elaborate the NPs.
In the present study, trehalose was selected as one of the most useful
cryoprotectant to protect PLGA NPs during the stressful freezing step.
The influence of trehalose/NP ratio on diameter was studied in formu­
lation #12, (i) after the freezing step, and (ii) at the end of the freeze-
drying process. Table 5 shows the results obtained. Fresh NPs (formu­
lation #11) were ≈220 nm in diameter and showed narrow size distri­
bution (PI ≈0.1).
The presence of cryoprotectant significantly reduced the diameter of
the particles. Percentages of trehalose greater than 0.06% did not
significantly affect the diameter of the NPs after freezing and thawing
(Table 6). Using this trehalose ratio, particles were easily redispersed in
distilled water.

4. Discussion
In the search of novel therapeutic actives against CL, we have
developed a choline analog from the family of halomethylated quater­
nary ammonium salts [5]. We selected N-iodomethyl-N,N-dime­
thyl-N-(6,6-diphenylhex-5-en-1-yl)ammonium iodide (C6I) as lead
compound from a series of 24 molecules mainly because of its in vitro
leishmanicidal activity [5]. Additionally, the lead compound was also
selected because of its physicochemical, pharmacokinetic and pharma­
ceutical properties that make it suitable for oral administration [6].
However, this compound presents a main physicochemical disadvan­
tage: its low aqueous solubility which could difficult the dissolution
process in the gastrointestinal (GI) tract and therefore could affect its
oral bioavailability [6–19]. In this scenario, the inclusion of C6I in a
suitable nanoacarrier could be improved its oral delivery. Currently,
polymeric or lipid-based NPs seem to be the most promising approaches
[9].
NPs are solid and spherical structures with size smaller than 1 μm.
Currently, a wide variety of drugs have been marketed using nano­
formulations with the purpose of improving the bioavailability of the
drug in affected tissues or cells and avoiding toxicity [34]. Additionally,
NPs confer protection from degradation in biological fluids, allowing
drug administration by different routes [10].
The herein described PLGA NPs presented homogeneous diameters
and were suitable for oral administration, as previously reported by
Abdelwahed et al., (2006) [30]. Although some studies reported the
independence of the type of PLGA polymer over particle size and system
stability [36], C6I was only compatible with the Resomer® RG 752S.
Particles obtained using other presentations of this polymer (502, 502H,
504 and 504H) resulted into aggregates and precipitated, which
impaired resuspension. Polymer selection is an important part of the
design of products, once variations in the proportion of glycolic or lactic
acid can affect the interaction between the C6I compound and the
polymer. Resomer® RG 752S is the most hydrophobic polymer of all
those that were tested. Possibly, the affinity of the C6I was mediated by
hydrophobic type interactions, although some authors have reported
ionic interactions between different polymers and the encapsulated
compounds [37–38]. In any case, our results clearly indicate that
drug-polymer interactions should be studied in a case-by-case scenario
and emphasize the need of thorough studies for every drug candidate.
The balance of forces (hydrophilic-lipophilic) of the excipients
constituting the formulation could determine the behavior of the active
pharmaceutical ingredient encapsulated. These forces establish the
loading efficiency and physical stability of the NPs and should be taken
into account in formulation design [39]. The effect of excipients was
demonstrated by formulation #2 where the surfactant Span® 60 and C6I
showed an evident interaction and the diameter increased when NPs
were loaded with the compound. The replacement of Span® 60 for TPGS
resulted in particle size reduction and changes in the aspect of the
formulation (without aggregates). Other studies have shown

Therefore, both compounds could compete for the hydrophobic re­

contribute to increase particle size (formulations #2, #4, #6, #8 and

these studies, formulation #12 was selected for further in vitro screening.
Another parameter that could affect the stability of nano­
formulations was the zeta potential. The electrical charge of a compound
could modify the charge of a particle. For example, the ZP of formulation
#11 was −27.1 ± 1.0 mV, while for formulation #12 the ZP changed to
−20.6 ± 1.49 mV (6). Despite the ZP being outside the desired range
(near +30 or −30 mV), the obtained values still indicate suitable
colloidal stability, considering the steric stabilization given by the sur­
factants used [43].
Regarding the stability of formulation #12, the results did not show
significant variations in monitored parameters after 6 months of storage
under refrigeration conditions, when compared to freshly prepared NPs.
In relation to the release profile of PLGA NPs, it is known that this
polymer is degraded by hydrolysis, which in turn is pH dependent. So,
the C6I release at pH 7.4 showed a biphasic profile, typical of this
polymer, probably due to diffusion-degradation. The release phase was
associated to loss of C6I at the surface and a subsequent degradation
phase of the polymer, as well as a diffusion of the drug through the
polymer matrix [44].
Regarding Trs and Trp NPs, the solubility of C6I in these lipids was
low and the saturation of the system during formulation studies was
observed with 5 mg of compound. However, decreasing the quantity of
C6I in the system to 3 mg, greater encapsulation efficiency around 65%
was achieved. Several studies have shown the loading capacities of these
lipids with different compounds and systems, especially to load qua­
ternary compounds with low polarity. The high concentration of C6I
could affect the reorganization of lipid maybe for the electrical charge of
the compounds, which could decrease the capacity of encapsulated
molecules [45–47].
One disadvantage related to Trs and Trp NPs is the effect of tem­
perature on the reorganization of the crystalline structure, which can
influence the presence of the active compound in the particle and pro­
motes its release. In the present study, it was possible to verify the
protective role of lecithin against the direct effects of temperature, as
previously demonstrated by other authors [48]. This effect could be
verified in the Trs and Trp NPs when comparing results obtained in the
stability studies related to the effects of temperature and DLS, where the
changes were not significant concerning particle size and loading ca­
pacity. The electrical surface properties of the particle were affected by
the presence of the compound leading probably a reorganization of lipid
matrices with subsequent rearrangement of the compound in the
formulation [46]. This reorganization of lipid nanoparticle matrix was
demonstrated by the DSC studies. These results could be explained
because of potential defects in the lipid network, leading to a decrease of
their crystallinity in comparison to bulk counterparts [49].
The in vitro release of C6I from the NPs occurred rapidly in the first
hours, releasing more than 50% of the formulation payload. The drug
release from the NPs appeared to have two components with an initial
fast release of about 50% at the first 24 h of sampling time. This was
followed by a slower exponential release of the remaining drug. The fast
initial compound release from lipid matrices could probably be
explained because of the presence of drug molecules adsorbed or close to
the surface of SLN. Comparing the release profiles from lipid particles, it
can be observed two things: (i) C6I was released faster from Trs than Trp
NPs, (ii) from Trs NPs, the release profile was more sustained, likely
because of a stronger interaction of the compound with Trs, since this
lipid is more hydrophobic than Trp. Finally, for both lipids the expo­
nential delayed release may be attributed to diffusion of the dissolved
drug within the NPs core into the dissolution medium [50].
In addition, these NPs improved the dissolution profile according to
Fernandez et al. (2018) for C6I free [19]. Previous works in the literature
demonstrated that decreasing particle size can lead to an increased
dissolution rate and higher oral bioavailability of poorly soluble com­
pounds. This phenomenon could be explained by Noyes-Whitney and
Ostwald-Freundlich principles: the particle size in the nanometer range
may cause an increased dissolution rate and saturation solubility for a
nanoformulation [51].
No cytotoxicity was observed for Trs, Trp and PLGA NPs loaded with
C6I in comparison with the free C6I. In contrast, the antileishmanial
activity increased, especially for formulation using PLGA, that was 30
times more active than the free C6I. The second most active nano­
formulation was C6I-Trs NPs followed by C6I-Trp NPs. These different
activities of both lipids could be related to differences between the
release profiles, faster in C6I-Trs than in C6I-Trp NPs.
The effect on the activity of the compound may be due to multiple
reasons, since there are reports of the benefits of using NPs as a vehicle
for leishmanicidal drugs [12,13]. In one hand, the PLGA NPs may pro­
vide protection to fragile molecules against enzymatic hydrolytic
degradation [40]. On the other hand, it is known that particles 200–300
nm in diameter are more susceptible to be phagocyted by macrophages
and C6I-PLGA NPs are in those diameters [52]. Another factor to take
into account is the acid pH of the phagolysosome environment that fa­
vors the autocatalytic degradation of PLGA and the subsequent release
of the drug, thus ensuring a localized effect [53, 54].
Finally, the use of TPGS as a complement in the treatment of leish­
maniasis has also been tested, where the disease has been associated
with a production of reactive oxygen species and the gradual exhaustion
of TPGS and ascorbate [55]. In the case of Trs and Trp nanoformulations,
the potentiating effect of the activity of the antileishmanial compound
after being encapsulated in these solid lipids has been documented
previously. The use of lipids to improve the bioavailability of poorly
soluble compounds has also been studied [48]. Several hypotheses may
explain how the SLN improve the effectiveness of compound, one of
them, the nanoparticles may increase the dissolution of compounds in
physiological solutions [6; 53]. Additionally, many studies describe
several properties of SLN over conventional formulations to improve the
biopharmaceutical profile – increased solubility, enhanced stability,
improved epithelium permeability and bioavailability, prolonged
half-life, tissue targeting, and minimal side effects). Finally, this kind of
nanoformulation maximize the antileishmanial activity [45, 48].
5. Conclusion
The present work investigated different nanoparticle systems to
encapsulate C6I, a highly active antileishmania compound, but highly
cytotoxic and with moderate solubility in water. A comparative study of
polymeric and SLN performance regarding characterization, stability
cytotoxicity and in vitro antileishmanial activity was performed. The
study showed that PLGA and Trs nanoparticles loaded C6I presented
leishmanicidal activity in vitro with a dose several times lower than the
free active C6I compound. PLGA formulations showed the highest se­
lective index (IS > 350). So, the C6I dose loaded into PLGA NPs needed
to eliminate the parasite keeping alive the cells were the lowest. The
cytotoxicity effect of the formulation on the relevant cell lines (Caco-2
human intestinal cells and Detroit 551 human fibroblasts) was almost
null. Nevertheless, a more deep in vitro study on these cell lines will be
necessary in order to understand nanoparticle-cell interactions and their
ability to cross the intestinal barrier. Finally, the possibility to obtain a
