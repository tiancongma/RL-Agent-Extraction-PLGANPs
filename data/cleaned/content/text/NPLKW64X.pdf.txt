A B S T R A C T
Hypothyroidism is a chronic condition combated by a daily oral supplementation of levothyroxine. In
addition to the need for frequent dosing, oral administration may result in variable absorption of the
drug leading to a failure in achieving normal thyroid function. Therefore, the development of a long-
acting injectable system capable of delivering the drug is necessary. This work was aimed at develop-
ing sustained release microparticles loaded with levothyroxine. The microparticles were produced
through the emulsiﬁcation-solvent evaporation method using 2 grades of biocompatible and biode-
gradable polyesters: poly(ᴅ,ʟ-lactide-co-glycolide) (PLGA) and poly(ᴅ,ʟ-lactide) (PLA). Both polymers
produced microparticles with very similar sizes (1.9 mm) and zeta potential values (around -22.0 mV).
However, PLA microparticles had a signiﬁcantly higher drug loading (6.1% vs. 4.4%, respectively) and
encapsulation efﬁciency (36.8%, vs. 26.1%, respectively) when compared to PLGA counterparts. While
both types of microparticles displayed a biphasic release pattern in vitro, a slower rate of release was
observed with PLA microparticles. Moreover, a similar biphasic release pattern was found in vivo, with
an initial phase of rapid release followed by a slower phase in the subsequent 10 days. These results
indicate the possibility of developing levothyroxine loaded polyester microparticles as a potential
long-acting thyroid hormone replacement therapy.

Introduction
Conventional dosage forms, although widely available, are limited
by their inability to achieve targeted or extended drug release.1,2 This
has led to the development of drug delivery systems capable of
encapsulating drug molecules within miniscule carriers that can tar-
get speciﬁc tissues and control the release of the encapsulated
drug.3,4 Targeted delivery achieved by these systems has resulted in
the selective accumulation of actives within speciﬁc tissues, sparing
the rest of the body.2,4 Furthermore, because the delivery of the drug
is directed, a much smaller dose is needed to achieve therapeutic
effects.5,6 The smaller needed doses, targeted delivery, and extended
release that these systems offer have resulted in diminished experi-
enced side effects and greater patient compliance when compared to
conventional dosage forms.7 This superiority of targeted drug carriers
has made them very attractive systems for drug delivery.8
Microparticles (also known as microspheres), one of the most
commonly used drug delivery systems,2 are spherical particles
that have a diameter which falls within the micron range, i.e.
1−1000 mm.9-11 These microparticles form when actives are homo-
geneously dissolved or dispersed within a continuous internal matrix
composed of polymeric material.9 The characteristics of the poly-
meric materials used to encapsulate actives aid in the ability of these
systems to ﬁne-tune drug release.12,13 Therefore, careful selection of
polymeric materials is vital.
Polymers used to formulate microspheres can fall within one of
two categories: biodegradable or non-biodegradable materials.2 Both
are capable of encapsulating a wide range of actives14,15; however,
non-biodegradable materials can accumulate in the body resulting in
toxicity.16 This observed toxicity has resulted in a more extensive use
of biodegradable polymers.2

In recent years, the use of the polyester polymers poly(ᴅ,ʟ-lactide-
co-glycolide) (PLGA) and poly(ᴅ,ʟ-lactide) (PLA) to synthesize poly-
meric microparticles has ballooned as a result of their favorable
characteristics.15,17,18 The hydrophobic polymers are capable of
encapsulating a plethora of actives with vastly different chemical
properties.3,19−22 They can sustain the release of encapsulated actives
for periods of time that range from a few weeks to several
months.12,13,18,21 Additionally, they are not only biocompatible but
also biodegradable within biological media.23,24 As a result of these
favorable qualities, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has
approved twelve polyester-based microsphere products for drug
delivery.3,20,25
The American Thyroid Association deﬁnes hypothyroidism as a
condition in which the thyroid gland is underactive.26 This results in
a serum deﬁciency of thyroid hormones like thyroxine (T4) which in
turn brings about a pronounced deceleration of the entire body’s
metabolic processes. Constipation, depression, exhaustion, forgetful-
ness, weight gain, muscle cramps and weakness, menstrual irregular-
ities, and sensitivity to cold temperatures are only some of the varied
symptoms associated with hypothyroidism.
Hypothyroidism is a chronic condition requiring lifelong replace-
ment of levothyroxine, a synthetic form of the T4 hormone. Daily oral
supplementation of the drug in the form of tablets is conventionally
used to combat the deﬁciency.27 In some cases, however, symptoms
persist even with supplementation. This could be attributed to the
patient’s (1) improper intake of the medication with foods and che-
lating agents that limit its absorption,28−32 (2) non-adherence,33 or
(3) coexisting malabsorptive disorders or other concomitant dis-
eases.28 The resulting subtherapeutic serum levels of levothyroxine
eventually lead to a failure in achieving normal biochemical thyroid
function.32
Several different formulations with varying routes of administra-
tion have been developed to combat these issues. Orally administered
liquid solutions of levothyroxine and drug loaded soft gel capsules
were found to be particularly promising.34−36 They were able to cir-
cumvent malabsorption as a result of gastrointestinal diseases and
interactions with other medications.37 Furthermore, liquid solutions
could be given to patients who experience swallowing difﬁculties.
However, these formulations do not offer the convenience of a sus-
tained release system. Therefore, the need to explore the potential of
parenteral long-acting formulations has become necessary.38−40
More recently, the work of several groups has been aimed at sus-
taining levothyroxine release through its loading into 3-(amino-
propyl) triethoxysilane modiﬁed porous silicon nanoparticles,41 or
chitosan nanoparticles.42 However, the use of biodegradable, biocom-
patible, and FDA approved polymers such as PLGA and PLA is more
realistic and closer to clinical translation.
For these reasons, formulating levothyroxine within a polyester-
based delivery system capable of circumventing the absorptive disad-
vantages of the oral route and capable of extending release, thereby
diminishing the need for patient adherence to daily dosing, would be
greatly advantageous. In this work, the ability of polyester polymers
to encapsulate levothyroxine within microspheres and then control
its release after parenteral administration in animal models will be
investigated.
Materials
All the materials obtained for this work were used as received
without any modiﬁcations. Two polyester polymers that were
obtained from Sigma-Aldrich, Germany were utilized to form the
microparticles. These polymers are Resomer RG 502 H (PLGA;
molecular weight: 7−17 kDa; and lactide to glycolide ratio: 50:50)
and Resomer R 202 H (PLA; molecular weight: 10−18 kDa; and lac-
tide to glycolide ratio: 100:0). The microparticles were loaded with
levothyroxine sodium salt pentahydrate (Sigma-Aldrich, China).
Methanol (Romil Pure Chemicals; Cambridge, Great Britain); acetone
(BBC Chemicals for Lab; China); lecithin of soya (BBC Chemicals for
Lab; China); mineral oil (Az Chem for Chemicals; Germany); and n-
hexane (BBC Chemicals for Lab; China) were all used in the micropar-
ticle production process. Potassium chloride (BBC Chemicals for Lab;
China); potassium dihydrogen phosphate (Scharlab S.L.; Spain);
sodium chloride (Biochem Chemopharma; France); and disodium
hydrogen phosphate dihydrate (Iso Lab Chemicals; Wertheim, Ger-
many) were dissolved in distilled water to produce the phosphate
buffered saline (PBS) needed for zeta potential measurements. To
produce the medium in which drug release from the microparticles
was studied, sodium hydroxide pellets (VWR BDH Prolabo; Belgium)
were dissolved in distilled water. Finally, HPLC grade acetonitrile
(Macron Fine Chemicals; USA) and phosphoric acid (Romil Pure
Chemistry; Cambridge, Great Britain) were used for the analysis of
the drug.
Methods
Microparticle Synthesis and Puriﬁcation
The microparticles were produced using the emulsiﬁcation-sol-
vent evaporation method. To produce the internal phase of the emul-
sion, 50 mg of PLGA or PLA were dissolved in 3 mL of acetone. A one
mL solution of methanol containing 10 mg of sodium levothyroxine
was added dropwise to the acetonic polymer solution as it was stir-
ring on a magnetic stirrer. The mixture was formed in a vial that was
closed tightly before and after the addition of the drug solution to
prevent solvent(s) evaporation. The external phase was produced by
dissolving an appropriate amount of lecithin in mineral oil to form a
0.5% w/v solution. Emulsiﬁcation took place when the drug loaded
polymeric solution was added to 10 mL of the external phase in a
centrifuge tube and then emulsiﬁed by simple shaking for 1 min.
Once emulsiﬁcation had taken place, the emulsion was left stirring
on a magnetic stirrer operating at 400 rpm overnight to allow for
complete solvent(s) evaporation. Sodium levothyroxine is light sensi-
tive; therefore, all solutions were produced/ kept in amber glass vials
or beakers covered with aluminum foil.
The next day, 10 mL of n-hexane were added to each formula in
order to dilute the mineral oil and aid in the puriﬁcation of the
microparticles. The mixture was then centrifuged at 4500 rpm for
15 min (Centurion Scientiﬁc Ltd. UK). The supernatant was dis-
carded, and the precipitated particles were further washed with
another 10 mL of fresh n-hexane. The mixture was centrifuged again
at 4500 rpm for another 15 min. After the 15 min had elapsed, the
supernatant was discarded once more, and the microparticles were
left to dry. Once dry, the microparticles were collected, weighed,
and stored appropriately.
Determination of Microparticle Size, Polydispersity and Zeta Potential
Microparticle size and polydispersity were determined using
dynamic light scattering (Zetasizer Nano-ZS, Malvern Instruments,
UK). Minute samples of the dried microparticles were suspended in
1 mL of distilled water by sonication using an ultra-probe sonicator
(UP200Ht, Hielscher Ultrasonics, Germany). Ten second pulses of
50 % continuity at 20 % amplitude and 100 W were used to break up
any agglomerates. All readings were conducted in triplicates.
The zeta potential of the produced microparticles was determined
using electrophoretic light scattering analysis (Zetasizer Nano-ZS,
Malvern Instruments, UK) in the same method detailed previously.
The only difference is that the dried microparticles were suspended
in 1 mL of PBS (pH = 7.4) instead of in water.

Microparticle Morphology by Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM)
The surface morphology of the produced microparticles was
assessed using SEM (Tescan, Czech Republic). This was done by
mounting the dried microparticles on an aluminum stub with con-
ductive double-sided carbon tape. The surface morphology of the
microparticles was then visualized using SEM.
Determination of Microparticle Encapsulation Efﬁciency and Drug
Loading
The encapsulation efﬁciency and drug loading were determined
directly by dissolving the produced microparticle formulations and
analyzing the amount of drug released. To do so, each produced for-
mulation was initially dissolved in 1 mL of acetone in a tightly closed
amber glass vial. After 1 ho, the vials were opened to allow for solvent
evaporation. Once the acetone had evaporated, 10 mL of 0.01 N NaOH
were added to dissolve the drug. The polymer has limited solubility
in the NaOH. The mixtures were then left overnight in a shaker incu-
bator operating at 75 rpm and 37 °C (Daihan Labtech, Korea, model
number LSI-3016A) so that complete drug solubilization can take
place. The next day, all mixtures were centrifuged at 4000 rpm for
10 min (Centurion Scientiﬁc Ltd.,UK), and the supernatant was col-
lected for analysis using high performance liquid chromatography
(HPLC).
The drug concentration of each solution was determined by corre-
lating the area under the curve (AUC) of drug peaks from chromato-
grams to those obtained from a calibration curve constructed using
standard levothyroxine sodium solutions in 0.01 N NaOH. Next, drug
loading and encapsulation efﬁciency were determined using Eqs. (1)
and 2, respectively. All measurements were conducted in triplicates
to calculate mean values and standard deviations.

HPLC analysis (Shimadzu HPLC, model LC-2030C PLUS 3D) of levo-

acquisition was conducted using the LabSolutions LCGC program. For

The mobile phase was composed of a 40:60 v/v mixture of acetoni-

ﬂow rate, and temperature were set at 20 mL, 1.5 mL/min, and 25 °C,

Statistical analysis of drug loading and encapsulation efﬁciency
was performed by conducting an unpaired t-test (GraphPad Prism
version 9.0). Results were considered statistically different when the
p-value was less than 0.05.
Determination of Microparticle in Vitro Release
The in vitro drug release from microparticles was determined
through the dialysis method. The entire weight of microparticles pro-
duced from each formulation (54 § 3 mg) was suspended in 3 mL of
0.01 N NaOH and ﬁlled into dialysis bags with a molecular weight
cut-off point of 10−12 kDa. The dialysis bags were submerged in
27 mL of 0.01 N NaOH, and the vials were stored in a shaker incubator
operating at 37 °C and 75 rpm (Daihan Labtech, Korea, model number
LSI-3016A). At predetermined time points, 1 mL samples of the dialy-
sate were obtained and immediately replaced with fresh, pre-
warmed media. Release samples were analyzed by HPLC with
appropriate dilutions as detailed in the previous section. At the end
of the experiment, the amount of the drug left within the micropar-
ticles was determined by the method described for drug loading and
encapsulation but using only 5 mL 0.01 N NaOH instead of 10 mL. All
measurements were conducted in triplicates to calculate mean values
and standard deviations.
It is important to note that in vitro release was conducted under
alkaline conditions as opposed to physiological conditions. This is
because levothyroxine demonstrates the most optimal stability43,44
and solubility under alkaline conditions. Physiological conditions
would have resulted in faster drug degradation and the inability to
obtain sink conditions.
Determination of Microparticle in Vivo Release
The in vivo drug release from PLGA microparticles was deter-
mined by measuring serum total thyroxine (T4) levels prior to and
after the injection of loaded microparticles into rats. PLGA micropar-
ticles were chosen as a representative formulation. Adult female Rat-
tus albus were obtained from the animal house unit at the University
of Jordan. All animals were kept in a well-ventilated room at
22 § 2 °C with a 12 h light-dark cycle. They were also provided with
a standard rate diet and ad libitum water. The rats, weighing 130
−140 g, were randomly divided into 2 groups (n = 4), and treated
with either (i) drug-free PLGA microparticles (negative control), or
(ii) drug-loaded PLGA microparticles. The drug-loaded microparticles
had a drug dose of around 0.4 mg levothyroxine. A speciﬁc amount of
each microparticle formulation (around 8 mg) was dispersed in
0.5 mL PBS (pH 7.4) and injected subcutaneously into each rat. At pre-
determined time points, blood samples were collected using a plastic
micro-hematocrit capillary tube (length 75 mm; volume 45 mL)
introduced in the retro-orbital venous sinus of the rat’s eye directed
towards the opposite ear.45 The collected blood samples were then
placed in Eppendorf tubes and kept refrigerated for serum collection.

Results and Discussion
Microparticle Physiochemical Characterization
The size of levothyroxine sodium loaded microparticles produced
through the emulsiﬁcation-solvent evaporation method using PLGA
and PLA was determined by dynamic light scattering. As can be seen
in Table 1, the produced microparticles have a size of 1.9 mm regard-
less of the type of polymer used. Particles of this size can be easily
administered parenterally for prolonged drug release.46
Furthermore, these microparticles exhibit a wide particle size dis-
tribution as illustrated by the high polydispersity index (PDI) values.
This is regularly associated with polyester microparticles synthesized
from an emulsion.47,48
A high polydispersity in particle size can be linked to an unfavor-
able release proﬁle.49 The smaller microparticles within the polydis-
perse population have a greater surface area to volume ratio when
compared to their larger counterparts. This greater ratio would lead to

As for the zeta potential of the microparticles, both polymers pro-
duce particles that have a negative surface charge. This charge can be
attributed to the free carboxylic acid groups at the ends of the poly-
meric chains, in addition to any new ones that formed upon partial
polymeric hydrolysis.13,48 Such zeta potential values would offer
good stability of the produced microparticles.
The surface morphology of representative microparticles was
assessed using SEM. As shown in Fig. 1, the produced microparticles
are spherical with smooth surfaces. Moreover, the microparticles
exhibit a wide particle size distribution, which is consistent with the
DLS results.
Microparticle Drug Loading and Encapsulation Efﬁciency
The ability of PLGA and PLA of relatively similar molecular weights
to encapsulate levothyroxine sodium within microspheres was inves-
tigated. Drug loading and encapsulation efﬁciency results can be seen
in Fig. 2.
Both PLGA and PLA microspheres were prepared using the same
method and produced microparticles of a very similar size and poly-
dispersity. Their drug loading and encapsulation efﬁciencies were,
however, signiﬁcantly different. Microspheres prepared using PLGA
had a drug loading of 4.4 § 0.2 % and an encapsulation efﬁciency of
26.1 § 1.5 %. Meanwhile, those prepared using PLA had a drug load-
ing and encapsulation efﬁciency of 6.1 § 0.2 % and 36.8 § 1.2 %,
respectively.
Accordingly, results demonstrate that with an increase in poly-
meric hydrophobicity, a signiﬁcant (p value = 0.0023) increase in
drug loading and encapsulation can be obtained. The more hydro-
phobic nature of the PLA is due to the presence of additional methyl
groups.12,51 Such an increase in hydrophobicity would provide a bar-
rier against drug outﬂux upon implant formation. This barrier
reduces drug loss and thereby increases drug loading and encapsula-
tion efﬁciency.
Microparticle in Vitro Release
The release of levothyroxine sodium from loaded PLGA and PLA
microspheres was conducted. The results can be seen in Fig. 3. Both
polymers exhibited an initial phase of rapid drug release. Four hours
into the experiment, PLGA and PLA microspheres had released almost
identical amounts of the loaded drug (i.e., 27.6 % and 27.7 %, respec-
tively). Thereafter, the release patterns diverge, and a deceleration of
the release rate is seen with PLA microspheres. On day two, 87.7% of
the drug had been released from PLGA microspheres as compared to
66.0% from PLA microspheres. Release was terminated on day four
when the cumulative drug released from PLGA microspheres was
96.2 %, while that from PLA microspheres was 82.0 %. This demon-
strates the ability of PLA to control the rate of drug release over a lon-
ger period of time. Additionally, such a pattern of biphasic release is
commonly seen with polyester-based microparticles.52
Two main mechanisms govern drug release from polyester-based
drug delivery systems: diffusion and degradation/erosion.53 While
initially the rate of drug release is said to be mainly controlled by dif-
fusion of the drug through water ﬁlled pores; during the later stages,
the rate is determined by polymeric degradation/erosion.53
The overlapping initial phase of rapid release exhibited by micro-
spheres of both polymers suggests that a similar large proportion of
the drug was present at or near the surface of the microspheres. The
dissolution of the drug at the surface, and the diffusion of drug mole-
cules near the surface of the microspheres would take place promptly
upon microparticle hydration or in vivo administration.18
After that initial phase however, PLA was capable of controlling
the rate of release more efﬁciently than PLGA. This took place even
though both polymers produced microspheres of similar sizes, and

PLA microspheres were loaded with more levothyroxine sodium. The

hydrophobicity of PLA. An increase in hydrophobicity would result in
a slower rate of polymeric hydration and water-ﬁlled pore formation.
The slower the rate at which these pores form and coalesce into large
enough channels that allow for drug release, the more sustained the
release of the hydrophilic levothyroxine sodium is. Furthermore, PLA
has a greater number of methyl groups when compared to PLGA.
These methyl groups provide additional steric hindrance, further
impeding water penetration, polymer degradation/erosion, and drug
release.12,51
Microparticle in Vivo Release
The in vivo release behavior of PLGA microparticles was investi-
gated using a rat model by measuring serum total T4 levels at differ-
ent time points. Total T4 represents both the free and bound forms of
T4. The latter form, which is bound to thyroid binding globulin, is not
readily available for uptake by cells like the free form and thus repre-
sents a storage pool with a longer half-life.54
As can be seen in Fig. 4, drug-free (blank) PLGA microparticles,
which were used as a negative control, exhibited no change in the
serum total T4 concentration throughout the entire study. On the
other hand, drug-loaded PLGA microparticles resulted in an initial
phase of rapid release during the ﬁrst day. This rapid release is dem-
onstrated by the signiﬁcant increase in the total T4 concentration
from a basal concentration of 3.8 mg/dL at the time of injection to
82.3 mg/dL. The rapid increase was followed by a phase of slower
release over the next 10 days as evident by the decrease in total T4
concentrations. After that, T4 concentrations returned to their basal
level. Overall, the in vitro release behavior was found to be in good
harmony with that seen in vivo.
Similar results have been previously reported. In a comparable
study,55 in vivo release from subcutaneous implants loaded with lev-
othyroxine was investigated. It was found that the implants provided
detectable plasma T4 levels for a duration of 4 weeks with concentra-
tions ranging between 5 and 20 ng/mL. A similar biphasic release pat-
tern has also been noted when the release of levothyroxine from oral
solutions and soft capsules was studied.36 An initial phase of rapid
release that lasted 2−4 h was followed by another phase of slower
release over the next 24 h. The extent of these phases is shorter than
those obtained from subcutaneous implants55 and the microparticles
within this work due to the nature of oral formulations.
When extrapolating the obtained results from animal models to
expected behavior in humans, it is important to note a few things.
There is a signiﬁcant difference in the body surface area between rats
and humans.56 Additionally, the plasma T4 half-life is about 12 h in
rats and 5−9 days in humans.57 Due to the signiﬁcantly greater body
surface area and half-life in humans, it is expected that a less rapid
initial release phase and a longer second phase of slower release will
be demonstrated in humans. However, further human studies are still
needed.
Conclusion
Both PLGA and PLA microspheres were prepared using the same
emulsiﬁcation-solvent evaporation method and produced micropar-
ticles of a very similar size, polydispersity, and zeta potentials. How-
ever, the more hydrophobic PLA microparticles had a signiﬁcantly
higher drug loading and encapsulation efﬁciency and had the ability
to control drug release for a longer period of time. Both in vitro and in
vivo release behaviors displayed a biphasic pattern with an initial
phase of rapid release followed by a later phase of slower release.
These results indicate that the development of injectable, long-acting,
levothyroxine loaded microparticles is not only possible, but could
also potentially be used as a hormone replacement therapy for hypo-
thyroidism.
