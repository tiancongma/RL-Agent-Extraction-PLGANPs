In Vitro BSA Release Profile of DPAP Formulation

The in vitro drug release profile of DPAP formulation was
investigated over a pH range of 6-8 at 37 8C and 150 rpm shaking
speed. Figure 11 shows no initial burst release of drug molecules
after the first 24 h at 37 8C, demonstrating the capability of the
DPAP formulation to harbor and retain the drug molecules
without premature diffusive escape. The drug release profiles
proceeded with a plateau for the different pH systems. The
experimental results indicate that the release pattern of encapsulated drug molecules is highly dependent on the molecular
properties of the drug and degradation mechanism of the DPAP
formulation. Hence, the presence of internal pores and composition of the polymeric particles largely affect the diffusion rate of
drug molecules to achieve a sustained release profile whilst the
degradation rate of the outermost polymeric layer defines the
initial burst release of encapsulated drugs from the DPAP
formulation. Protonation and deprotonation of aptameric linkage
with the PEI polymeric layer can significantly influence the
degradation rate of DPAP formulation at different pH levels. A
significant protonation of PEI occurs under acidic pH, which
impacts the linkages between the aptamer and PEI layer, resulting
in enhanced DPAP degradation. The degree of protonation
and subsequent weakening of aptamer-PEI linkage decreases with
an increasing pH. Hence, the effect of pH is critical to maintaining
the desired release kinetics of DPAP formulation.

## Conclusion
The work reports the synthesis and characterization of an
enhanced delivery system combining functional biophysical
attributes
of
aptamer
and
biopolymeric
particles
to
form
multifunctional DPAP micro-particles. Layer-by-layer DLS analysis validated the synthesis of DPAP particulates with tailored
D average hydrodynamic size and positively charged surface
for enhanced targeting and intracellular uptake at malignant sites.
The DPAP system demonstrated to be pH-dependent, indicating its
modifiable endosomal degradation kinetics to release encapsulated drugs in a controlled delivery mechanism. DPAP microparticles also showed stability under varying physiological
temperature. The high encapsulation efficiency, strong binding
strength, and high decomposition temperature of DPAP demonstrate
an
effective
polymeric
vehicle
that provides
strong
protection and shielding characteristics to its payload from harsh
physiological environment as well as prevent drug leakage and
initial burst release. The multilayer DPAP formulation can be
molecularly engineered to carry different active biomolecules and
aptamers in unique compartments of its structural architecture to
simultaneously target various malignant cells. In vitro release data
shows the controlled and sustained drug release profiles under
varying pH conditions, and that pH is an influential parameter in
maintaining specific drug release characteristics. Nonetheless,
further in vivo investigations of DPAP formulation are mandatory
to prove its efficacy in mammalian systems.
