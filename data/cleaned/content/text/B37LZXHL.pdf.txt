Some hydrophobic natural drugs with high added values are promising in clinical applications, while
their poor water-solubility becomes a major restriction for the bioavailability. To overcome this problem,
the size-reducing strategy is a favorable approach. Herein, model paclitaxel (PTX)@poly (lactide-co-
glycolic acid) (PLGA) microcapsules are fabricated by an intensiﬁed supercritical extraction of emulsion
(SFEE) method. The phase behaviors of carbon dioxide (CO2)/emulsion system during extraction are
investigated to interpret the effects of operation parameters on the extraction. Besides, the emulsion for-
mulas on microcapsule properties are examined experimentally as well. The results indicate that super-
critical parameters above the mixture critical point (> 8 MPa at 313 K, for example) enables the great
phase mixing of oil phase and CO2, thus the effectiveness of emulsion extraction can be guaranteed.
Besides, the PLGA mass concentration (cPLGA) and water/oil ratio variations are also proved to favorably
adjust the size, encapsulation efﬁciency, and drug loading of microcapsules.

Due to the great inhibition of cancer cell proliferation, paclitaxel
(PTX), a diterpenoid alkaloid, has commonly recognized as a
promising agent for anti-cancer activities [1]. However, the clinic
application of PTX is still severely restricted by the low bioavail-
ability caused by its poor water-solubility [2]. To overcome this
challenge, considerable efforts have been made by researches con-
tinuously. Among these endeavors, size-reducing is internationally
recognized as one of the most effective strategies to increase
bioavailability and efﬁcacy of water-insoluble drugs [3–5]. Besides,
encapsulating drugs with biodegradable polymers to obtain micro-
capsules is another commended method to promote the bioavail-
ability

Emulsions are prefect soft templates to fabricate these drug
microcapsules with great convenience and technical applicability.

size distribution, and great activity maintenance of drugs. Super-
critical extraction of emulsion (SFEE) technology manifests favor-
able adaptability in processing poorly water-soluble substances
as well as encapsulating microcapsules [7–10], and has undergone
a rapid development in recent years. The SFEE process includes the
preparation of the oil-in-water emulsion and oil phase extraction
from emulsions by supercritical carbon dioxide (SCCO2). During
the extraction stage, the oil droplets stabilized by surfactants can
be considered as gas antisolvent precipitators, where the dissolved
substance can be quickly precipitated [9]. Traditional methods to
obtain microcapsules (solvent evaporation, spray drying, in-situ
chemical synthesis, and so on) may suffer from some technical
obstacles, such as high-temperature, poor environmental friendli-
ness, complicated operations, difﬁculty in dispersion and size con-
trol of microcapsules. By contrast, the SFEE can present the unique
advantages. Firstly, the SCCO2 possesses no-toxicity and excellent
miscibility with most organic solvents, thus the SFEE process is
capable in fabricating various kinds of microcapsules with low
residual solvents, and the extracted solvents can be recycled.
Besides, the microcapsules obtained by SFEE can exhibit great dis-
persion and size uniformity, ascribed to the isolation effect of
aqueous phase on the oil droplets and uniform SCCO2 extraction
ﬁeld. The emulsion formulas can be ﬂexibly designed to adjust

the size and structure of microcapsules. Finally, the extraction tem-
perature of SCCO2 is moderate (usually lower than 50 ℃), the dam-
age to active ingredients contained in drugs can be minimized as
well.

Despite this, there are still technical modiﬁcations desired for
conventional SFEE processes. The creaming of static emulsions
can occur as there is the density difference between oil and aque-
ous phases. The SCCO2 dissolution into the oil phase can exacerbate
the creaming, ascribed to the swelling and rapid density decrease
of the oil phase. Hence the guarantee of emulsion stability during
SFEE processing is important. Second, the mass transfer between
CO2 and emulsion is always limited by the low solubility and diffu-
sion of CO2 in the aqueous phase, for which the intensiﬁcation is
necessary for SFEE process. Pleasantly, extraction on ﬂowing emul-
sions and enlargement of contacting surface area between CO2 and
emulsion bulk phase are feasible strategies to settle the above
problems. For instance, the microﬂuidic technology assisted SFEE
method was successfully conducted to obtain PVA and chitosan
particle suspensions by Murakami et al. [11,12] and Luther et al.
[13], the emulsions contact with SCCO2 inside micro-channels in
the form of slug-ﬂow in their experiments. Campardelli et al.
[14] conducted the SFEE process in a packed column and obtained
the lipid particles with low organic residues.
Inspired by the above discussions, a novel SFEE method is pro-
posed to fabricate the model PTX@poly (lactide-co-glycolic acid)
(PLGA) microcapsules in ethyl acetate (EA)/water emulsion. The
PLGA is selected as the polymer matrix, since it is biodegradable
and non-toxic, and has high safety proﬁle [15,16]. These features
make PLGA a more promising candidate as a carrier for encapsu-
lated drugs. The emulsion injection and SCCO2 aeration are con-
ducted in the SFEE method, aiming to intensify the mass transfer
between SCCO2 and oil phase. The emulsion injection enables the
bulk emulsion fragmentation into droplets in the ﬁrst-stage extrac-
tion, the SCCO2 can rapidly diffuses into the oil phase, leading to
the microcapsule precipitation. For the second-stage extraction,
the SCCO2 aeration can further remove the residual EA in emul-
sions. The phase behaviors of emulsion/CO2 system are experimen-
tally observed, and the thermodynamic phase equilibrium results
are also presented to interpret the observations and assist in opti-
mizing supercritical parameters. Besides, the emulsion formulas in
terms of mass concentrations of PLGA (cPLGA) and water/oil ratios
on microcapsule properties are also examined. This study conﬁrms
that the intensiﬁed SFEE method can present favorable technical
applicability and controllability in the preparation of hydrophobic
drug microcapsules.
2. Experiments
2.1. Materials
PTX (purity > 99 %) is supplied by Nanjing Duly Biotech Co., Ltd.
China. PLGA (Mw = 70,000 g/mol) is purchased from Shenzhen Esun
Industrial Co., Ltd. China. Polysorbate 80 is provided by Sinopharm
Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd. China. Deionized water and carbon
dioxide (CO2, 99.5 % purity) are kindly provided by School of Chem-
ical Engineering, Dalian University of Technology. Ethyl acetate
(EA, 99.5 % purity) is supplied by TianDa Chemical Reagent Co.,
Ltd. China. All materials are used as received.
2.2. Formation of EA-in-water emulsions
Under the condition of heating (60 ℃) and stirring, PTX (bioac-
tive compound) and PLGA (encapsulation material) are dissolved
into EA to obtain the oil phase of emulsions, the concentration of
PTX in the oil phase is ﬁxed at 0.01 w/v%, while that of the PLGA
is adjustable (from 1 to 10 wt%). The aqueous phase composed of
deionized water and polysorbate 80 (concentration of 1 wt%) is
prepared concurrently. The aqueous phase should be dissolved to
saturation with EA in advance for EA has a certain solubility in
water. Finally, the oil and aqueous phases are mixed and emulsi-
ﬁed with various volume ratios (from 1 to 5) by a high-speed shear
homogenizer (10,000 rpm for 3 min) to obtain emulsions.

2.3. SFEE processing
Schematic diagram for experimental setups is presented in
Fig. 1, there are ﬁrst-stage and second-stage extractions for the
emulsion in the proposed SFEE process. The CO2 is pressurized by
a high-pressure diaphragm compressor (constant ﬂow rate of 15/
min) and pre-heated in a thermostat water bath, it is then intro-
duced into the high-pressure vessel (inner volume of 500 mL) via
an aeration dispenser at the bottom of vessel cavity. For the ﬁrst-
stage emulsion extraction, the vessel is already ﬁlled with SCCO2
with desired temperature and pressure, and then the prepared
fresh emulsion is fed by a constant ﬂux pump at the ﬂow rate of
10 mL/min, it is then introduced in the vessel by a customized noz-
zle, which is installed at upper part of vessel cavity and has an
aperture of 0.5 mm. The emulsion is then fragmentated to form
droplets, and the SCCO2 rapidly diffuses into the oil phase, causing
that the dissolved PTX and PLGA can be precipitated. For the
second-stage emulsion extraction, the pre-extracted emulsion is
accumulated at the bottom of vessel cavity, the SCCO2 aeration
generates numerous bubbles to establish a uniform extraction
ﬁeld, and the residual solvent in the emulsion can be further
removed. To guarantee the sustained ﬂow of emulsions, the mag-
netic stirring is equipped for the vessel, and the stirring speed is
set to 600 rpm. As the aimed pressure and temperature are
reached, the exhaust valve opening is regulated to maintain the
pressure dynamic stability in the vessel.
As EA is continuously extracted and removed from emulsions
by SCCO2, the PTX@PLGA microcapsules are solidiﬁed and then
suspended in the aqueous phase. The whole procedure for all
experiment runs is lasted for 30 min. The PTX@PLGA microcap-
sules are separated from the obtained suspension and dried over-
night, the suspensions and dried solid samples are retained to be
further characterized.

3. Results and discussion
3.1. Phase behavior investigation in regards to SFEE
The phase behavior evolutions of EA jet in CO2 are investigated
in Fig. 2. The pressure variations can present the signiﬁcant impact
on the jet morphology and phase behavior of EA in CO2 environ-
ment. For pressures below the critical one of CO2 (7.4 MPa), the
jet morphology of EA in CO2 is similar to that in the atmosphere,
i.e., the jet column is broken to form small droplets. The difference
is that the jet morphology transformation in the atmosphere is
mainly regulated by the jet velocity, rather than the pressure. As
the pressure reaches 8 MPa, the core jet length of EA decreases
apparently, ascribed to that the interfacial tension between EA dro-
plets and SCCO2 are much decreased, while the solubility of CO2 in
EA is promoted greatly. Besides, the EA droplets formed by the
fragmentation of jet column becomes more dispersed, and their
descending speed is obviously reduced as well.
Compared with the gaseous CO2, the density and viscosity of
SCCO2 beyond the critical pressure are rapidly increased, thereby
strengthening the buoyancy force and viscous resistance experi-
enced by the EA jet. As the pressure continues to rise to 10 MPa
and 12 MPa, the mixing of EA and SCCO2 becomes remarkable.
Despite the area near the nozzle, there is no obvious phase inter-
face between EA droplets and SCCO2 continuous phase. The surface
tension and latent heat of vaporization can vanish above the mix-
ing critical point of CO2 and EA system, thus the liquid fragmenta-
tion and atomization phenomena observed under subcritical
conditions will disappear as well. The jet morphology and phase
behavior of EA in CO2 can be also inﬂuenced by temperature, of
which the improvement leads to the density and viscosity decrease
of CO2, the buoyancy force and viscous resistance experienced by
descending EA droplets are reduced. As a result, the whole

evolution process of phase behavior of EA/CO2 system can lag
behind with increasing temperature.

Fig. 3 shows the phase behavior evolutions of emulsion jet in
CO2. The bulk emulsion can be fragmentated into numerous small
droplets, and the mass transfer between CO2 and emulsion dro-
plets can be greatly accelerated. The phase behavior evolution of
emulsion jet in CO2 is similar to that of EA jet, which is also mainly
dominated by the surface tension and CO2 solubility. It is notable
that the size of emulsion droplets obtained above 8 MPa becomes
pretty limited, the emulsion jet has transformed into complete
atomization, implying the appropriate operation conditions to con-
duct the SFEE process.
The phase behavior evolutions of CO2 aeration in emulsion are
investigated in Fig. 4. As the pressure increases in 0–––6 MPa,
the oil droplets of EA in the emulsion are gradually dissolved and
swelled with CO2, thus the emulsion volume is enlarged. At
8 MPa (the pressure above the critical one of CO2, 7.38 MPa), the
CO2 undergoes the phase transform from subcritical state to super-
critical state, the solubility of CO2 in EA is signiﬁcantly increased,
when there are obvious three phase layers observed in the visual
ﬁeld, i.e., the transparent SCCO2 layer at the top, the middle EA
layer, and the emulsion bulk phase at the bottom. The middle EA
layer is formed due to the signiﬁcant dissolution and swelling of
SCCO2, which causes a substantial reduction in the EA density. This
phase separation highlights the strong antisolvent effect of SCCO2,
where the presence of CO2 disrupts the stability of the emulsion,
leading to the segregation of its components. As the pressure fur-
ther increases above 10 MPa, the phase interface between SCCO2
and EA layer is vanished, indicating that the EA/CO2 system has
reached a miscible state. In this state, the SCCO2 and EA are fully
mixed, and no distinct three-layer structure is visible. This phase
behavior transition suggests that at higher pressures, the solubility
of CO2 in EA increases continuously, but the interface tension
between them is remarkably reduced, resulting in a homogeneous
mixture. This phenomenon is crucial for understanding the phase
dynamics in SFEE applications, where the supercritical conditions
can be adjusted to achieve desired SCCO2 extraction mode for
emulsions. Besides, it seems that the characteristics of phase
behavior transform earlier at the case of 50 ℃ than those at 40 ℃.

Besides, the phase behavior evolutions of emulsion/CO2 system

The phase behaviors of emulsion/CO2 system can be explained
upon the high-pressure phase equilibrium relationships [17,18].
The pressure enhancement can signiﬁcantly improve the solubili-
ties of CO2 in EA, while the temperature exhibits much weaker reg-
ulation effects. As the supercritical conditions stay beyond the
mixture critical points (MCP) of EA/CO2 system, the EA and CO2
can achieve the miscibility theoretically, i.e., the extraction effect
of SCCO2 on the oil phase of emulsion is optimized. The valves of
MCP of EA/CO2 system at 313 K and 323 K are ∼ 8 MPa and
8.3 MPa. Besides, the diffusivity of solvent in CO2 phase can be
strengthened along with the increasing temperature or descending
pressure [19], when the dissolution of CO2 and EA can be acceler-
ated. The above discussions are capable to interpret the results of
aforementioned phase behavior evolutions.
3.2. PTX@PLGA microcapsule preparation by SFEE
The preparation feasibility of PTX@PLGA microcapsules with
the proposed SFEE method is examined. Fig. 6 shows the SEM
images of raw PTX, PLGA microspheres and PTX@PLGA microcap-
sules obtained by SFEE. To prepare the PLGA microspheres and
PTX@PLGA microcapsules, the supercritical conditions and emul-
sion formulas are ﬁxed: temperature is 313 K, pressure is 9 MPa,
extraction time is 30 min, PLGA mass concentration is 7.5 wt% in
the oil phase, water/oil ratio is 5, and PTX concentration is 0 and
0.01 w/v% in the oil phase, respectively. As shown in Fig. 6, the
raw PTX presents the irregular stick-like shapes and wide size dis-
tribution (Fig. 6a), while the PLGA microspheres exhibit the general
sphericity and little agglomeration (Fig. 6b), ascribed to the great
templating effect of emulsions and isolation effect of aqueous
phase on the oil droplets. For the PTX@PLGA microcapsules
(Fig. 6c), the morphology shows no obvious difference to that of
pure PLGA microspheres, indicating an excellent PLGA encapsula-
tion on the precipitated PTX. The average sizes of the PLGA micro-
sphere and PTX@PLGA microcapsule are presented in Fig. S5, and
the stability of PTX@PLGA microcapsule stored at various temper-
atures is further examined in Fig. S6.

3.3. Effects of SFEE operations and emulsion formulas on microcapsule
properties
For the SFEE process, the experiment setups of supercritical
conditions (temperature (T) and pressure (P)) and emulsion formu-
las (PLGA mass concentrations (cPLGA) in the oil phase and water/oil
ratios (W/O)) are shown in Table 1, the main examined properties
of microcapsules include average size (D.), encapsulation efﬁciency
(E.E.) and drug loading (D.L.).
As presented in Fig. 9, the temperature and pressure are not
capable enough to regulate the size of microcapsules, their impacts
are signiﬁcantly reﬂected in the residual solvent concentrations,
related to the mass transfer in SFEE process. As the temperature
increasing (Fig. 9a), the residual solvent concentration is monoton-
ically reduced from 3.5 mg/L (313 K) to 0.005 mg/L (333 K). The
temperature enhancement can not only improve the diffusivity
of solvent in CO2 phase but also accelerate EA evaporation from
CO2/aqueous phase interfaces. In Fig. 9b, the residual solvent con-
centration shows a trend of ﬁrst descending and then increasing
with the pressure improving, of which the minimum value is
3.5 mg/L at 9 MPa. The CO2 undergoes phase state evolutions from
gas state, subcritical state, and supercritical state as the pressure
varies from 3 MPa to 6 MPa and 9 MPa, and its dissolution and
extraction abilities to EA are strengthened as well. The EA and
CO2 are miscible with the pressure above the MCP (∼8 MPa at
313 K), the solubility between the two phases is no longer a limit-
ing factor. However, the diffusivity reduction of EA/CO2 system and
viscosity increase of CO2 caused by higher pressures (≥ 9 MP a) can
perform negative impacts on the extraction, leading to the slight
increase of residual solvent concentration.
In Fig. 10, both the temperature and pressure increase can
slightly reduce the encapsulation efﬁciency and drug loading, indi-
cating the poorer PLGA encapsulation and more PTX loss from the
oil phase of emulsions. This PTX loss can be attributed to two ways:
First, SCCO2 can perform weak extraction capacity on PTX, which

has not been encapsulated by PLGA matrix, it can be leaked into
the aqueous phase during the PLGA solidiﬁcation process, ascribed
to that the surfactants can increase the solubility of hydrophobic
drugs in aqueous phase by forming micelle structures [25].

The detailed regulation mechanism of cPLGA and water/oil ratios
on the microcapsule is further analyzed. The effects of cPLGA on the
morphology and size distribution of microcapsules are investi-
gated, and the results are presented in Fig. 11. These microcapsules
possess satisfactory sphericity and dispersion. In the size distribu-
tion images, the y-coordinate indicates the volume proportion of
microcapsules within various size ranges, it is seen that the size
shows an obvious ascending trend with the cPLGA increasing.
Higher cPLGA indicates the higher viscosity and density of oil phase
for emulsions, improving the difﬁculty of oil phase breaking into
droplets during emulsiﬁcation. As a result, the oil phase droplets
with high cPLGA will perform the wide size distribution, leading to
the formation of microcapsules with wide size distribution as well.
In Fig. 12a, the average size of microcapsules increases from ∼ 1.
1 lm  (cPLGA of 1 wt%) to 3.1 lm  (cPLGA of 10 wt%). In Fig. 12b, the
encapsulation efﬁciency of PTX in microcapsules also shows an
improving trend with cPLGA increases from 1 wt% to 10 wt%, i.e.,
14.4 % to 40.9 %, while the drug loading is decreased from 0.8 %
to 0.37 %. As cPLGA increases, the viscosity of the oil phase is
improved, which is not conducive to the PTX mass transfer from
the oil phase to aqueous phase. It is worth noting that there is a
critical overlapping concentration (C*) for polymer solutions. In
dilute polymer solutions, polymer strands are separated from each
other, and the distribution of chain segments in the solution is
uneven. As the actual concentration is higher than C*, the polymer
strands interweave and overlap with each other, hindering the
molecular diffusion of PTX. The C* deﬁnition can be obtained
through empirical correlation equation [26].
The in vitro release proﬁles of PTX@PLGA microcapsules with
different mass concentrations of PLGA are illustrated in Fig. 13,
these release proﬁles present an initial burst of 35 % ∼ 40 % in
the ﬁrst 10 h. This initial burst can be contributed to the tremen-
dous concentration gradient of PTX between the microcapsule
interior and external buffers. In the following hours, the PTX
release rate tends to be stable and the release presents a sustained
manner, the cumulative release percent is increased beyond 50 %.
Besides, the release rate of microcapsules obtained with higher
cPLGA is much slower, attributed to the large size of the capsules
and thick outer encapsulation of PLGA enlarging the resistance of
PTX outward diffusion. The release characteristics greatly coincide
with the Weibull distribution [27], which is representative in

The effects of water/oil ratios on the morphology and size distri-
bution of microcapsules are investigated in Fig. 14. As can be seen,
the average size of these microcapsules presents no apparent dif-
ference, while the size distribution seems to be more concentrative
as the water/oil ratio increasing. Actually, higher water/oil ratio
means the lower distribution concentration of discrete oil droplets
in the continuous aqueous phase, the probability of contact
between different oil droplets is signiﬁcantly reduced, and the
extents of droplet coalescence and accumulation are decreased as
well. Consequently, the microcapsules obtained with the high
water/oil ratio can show the more uniform size distribution.
In Fig. 15a, the average size of microcapsules varies in the range
of ∼ 2.5 lm  to  ∼ 3 lm. In Fig. 15b, both the encapsulation efﬁ-
ciency and drug loading of PTX in microcapsules present the
descending trends with the water/oil ratio increases from 1 to 5.
At high water/oil ratios, the surfactant concentration in the exter-
nal aqueous phase stays much higher than the critical micelle con-
centration (CMC), the micelle presence can greatly improve the
solubility of hydrophobic drugs in the aqueous phase. Promoted
by the concentration gradient, PTX molecules diffuse from the oil

phase droplets to the aqueous phase until the chemical potential of
PTX in both phases reaches the equilibrium, or until the PLGA
solidiﬁcation, hindering further diffusion of PTX molecules. As
the water/oil ratio increases, the rate at which PTX molecules reach
the thermodynamic equilibrium in the aqueous and oil phases
slows down, resulting in more PTX being lost to the outer aqueous
phase, and the encapsulation efﬁciency and drug loading are
decreased. Besides, the in vitro release proﬁles of PTX@PLGA
microcapsules with various water/oil ratios are illustrated in
Fig. 16. As can be seen, these proﬁles also present the typical char-
acteristic of initial burst and then slow release. Attributed to the
similar sizes of microcapsules, the cumulative releases at 50 h
exhibit no signiﬁcant difference, which reaches about 55 %,
respectively.

4. Conclusions
Model PTX@PLGA microcapsules are successfully produced
with the intensiﬁed SFEE method, in which the emulsion injection
and SCCO2 aeration are applied to intensify the mass transfer
between SCCO2 and oil phase (EA) of emulsions. Combined with
phase equilibrium data, the phase behavior investigations prove
that the pressure enhancement can signiﬁcantly improve the solu-
bilities between CO2 and oil phase, and operation conditions above
MCP of CO2/oil system (∼8 MPa and 8.3 MPa at 313 K and 323 K,
respectively) are conducive to conducting the proposed SFEE pro-
cess, when the obtained microcapsules can present stable size dis-
tribution, while further pressure or temperature increase would
not make obvious impact. Besides, the residual EA concentration
decreases rapidly with pressures or temperatures above MCP, the
obtained ﬁnal concentration is as low as 3.5 mg/L. For emulsion
formula regulations, the increase of PLGA mass concentration can
increase the average size and encapsulation efﬁciency of microcap-
sules, but decrease the drug loading. Besides, the increase of water/
oil ratio can reduce the encapsulation efﬁciency and drug loading
of microcapsules, but show little inﬂuence on the average size.
The cumulative release characteristics of microcapsules are mainly
related to the average size, which coincide with the Weibull distri-
bution, indicating that the PTX release follows the Fickian
diffusion.
