A B S T R A C T
The objective of this work was to investigate the effect of microfluidics on the quality attributes of metformin
hydrochloride-loaded poly lactic-co-glycolic acid polymeric particles (MFH-PLGA PPs) when compared to a
traditional double emulsion batch method. The relationship of encapsulation and loading efficiencies, yield %,
particle size, surface morphology, and release profile with process and formulation variables were determined
using design of experiments (DoE). The effects of the dispersal method of the primary (sonication vs. vortex) or
secondary emulsion (microfluidics vs. batch), polyvinyl alcohol concentration (PVA), and drug to polymer ratio
were investigated. The PPs’ size was impacted by both the PVA concentration and the type of primary and
secondary emulsion dispersion methods. Microfluidics significantly increased the PPs’ yield %, particle size,
encapsulation, and loading efficiencies. The higher loaded microfluidic-based PPs had more burst release,
following first-order release kinetics when compared to the lower loaded batch-based particles, which followed
the Korsmeyer-Peppas model for release kinetics. Microfluidic-based PPs exhibited a smooth, porous, more
uniform, and larger particle size with hollow structure than the batch-based PPs with a matrix-like structure. In
conclusion, we have elucidated the effect of microfluidics on the quality attributes of MFH-PLGA PPs and their
comparison to the traditional batch technique.
1. Introduction
Hydrophilic drugs are rapidly eliminated from the body and can be
metabolized before reaching their target organs. Therefore, they suffer
from short half-lives, requiring to be administered multiple times daily
to maintain an effective plasma concentration, which hinders patients’
compliance (Bharali et al., 2003). Hence, their encapsulation in various
drug delivery systems (DDSs), particularly polymeric particles (PPs),
was proposed and investigated to mitigate such challenges. Drug de­
livery in PPs has allowed the controlled (Sousa et al., 2017; Wang et al.,
2017), targeted, or sustained-release of the entrapped molecule (Chen
et al., 2017; Sun et al., 2017). One of the most widely used methods to
encapsulate hydrophilic drugs in PPs is the double emulsion solvent
evaporation method (DE-SE) (Lamprecht et al., 1999; Lee et al., 2016).
Since DEs’ introduction in 1924 by Sefiriz, they have been prepared
using a traditional batch method, requiring shear mixing (Iqbal et al.,
2015b; Lagreca et al., 2020). DE-SE is conducted in two main steps:
emulsification followed by organic solvent evaporation, as summarized
in Fig. 1. Initially, primary emulsification of an aqueous solution of the
drug (W1) with an organic solvent containing the polymer (O) is
accomplished using shear mixing. The primary emulsion (W1/O) is then
shear mixed for a second time in an outer water phase (W2) containing a
polymer or a stabilizer to obtain secondary emulsion (W1/O/W2). The
polymer solidification is induced during the solvent evaporation phase,
which allows for the polymer precipitation at the oil–water interface and
further encapsulation of the hydrophilic drug in the PPs. Finally, the

requires a profound understanding of the effect of formulation and
process variables and their interaction on the fabricated PPs’ quality
attributes (QAs) such as encapsulation efficiency (EE %), loading effi­
ciency (LE %), particle size, and release profile. Polyvinyl alcohol con­
centration in W2 (PVA, acts as a stabilizer) was shown to significantly
affect the particle size (inverse proportionally) and EE % (inverse pro­
portionally) of liraglutide PLGA PPs (Ismail et al., 2019). Yang et al.
observed a similar effect for PVA concentration in W2 on particle size, as
well as affecting the release profile of their protein-loaded PLGA PPs
(increasing PVA concentration resulted in slower release) (Yang et al.,
2001). Polymer concentration was depicted to impact the yielded PPs’
particle size (direct proportionally) (Ibraheem et al., 2014; Iqbal et al.,
2015a; Lamprecht et al., 1999) and similarly affects the EE % (Ismail
et al., 2019; Paulo and Santos, 2019). The in-vitro release profile was
shown to be influenced by the type of organic solvent, polymer con­
centration (inverse proportionally) (Paulo and Santos, 2019), and mo­
lecular weight of lactic acid copolymer (inverse proportionally with %
burst release) (Zilberman and Grinberg, 2008). Some other variables
were less well understood, for example, the primary emulsion dispersion
method between sonication and vortexing (Qi et al., 2014). Researchers
aimed to determine the effect of formulation and process parameters’
variables on PPs’ QAs, with an end goal of optimizing and fabricating
PPs exhibiting certain desired properties. Nevertheless, batch-based
polymeric particles suffer from low EE %, high polydispersity index
(PDI) due to lack of control over the droplet size, and difficulty to scale
up; all of these are attributed to the required shear mixing (Lagreca
et al., 2020; Rezvantalab and Keshavarz Moraveji, 2019).
Microfluidics, a technique allowing the rapid and homogenous
mixing of fluids in microchannels (Whitesides, 2006), represented a
grand step forward for the controlled fabrication of DDSs that require
shear mixing during their batch manufacturing. Previous work from the
Weitz group demonstrated the capability to prepare highly uniform
double (Utada et al., 2005), triple, and other hierarchical emulsions
(Utada et al., 2007) on a drop by drop basis using glass capillary devices,
benefiting from the hydrodynamic instability. They have further
demonstrated its applicability to various emulsion-based applications,
such as microparticle fabrication (Shah et al., 2008). Many research
groups have studied DE droplet engineering using microfluidics,
concentrating on droplets or droplet-particle geometry relationships.
For example, Nie et al. have demonstrated the capability to manufacture
uniform core–shell droplets and further PPs with controlled morphol­
ogies in microfluidic devices, with a PDI not exceeding 2.5 % (Nie et al.,
2005). They were interested in controlling the external and internal
morphology of the PP by varying the flow rates of the input fluids.
Similarly, Nabavi et al. investigated mechanisms to generate DEs and
multi-core DE droplets in a single step focusing on droplet-based vari­
ables (e.g., capillary number) (Nabavi et al., 2017, 2015). All these ef­
forts have given an insight into droplet engineering and microfluidic
application to various DDSs. However, for microfluidics to be further
applied for fabricating hydrophilic drug-loaded microparticles on an
industrial scale, more research is needed to enlighten how it affects PPs’
QAs compared to the industry widely used method, batch method.
Comparing the batch technique to microfluidics in terms of PP QAs was
studied for other systems, such as single emulsion-based PPs (Busatto
et al., 2018) and nanoprecipitation-based PPs (Xu et al., 2009), but less
is known regarding double emulsion-based systems. Moreover, among
microfluidic DE-based processes, fewer studies have been done to
investigate the effect of formulation and process variables on the QAs of
the fabricated PPs, such as particle size, release profile, and LE %, among
others. Amoyav et al. investigated the impact of microfluidic DE poly­
mer type, concentration, and molecular weight, organic solvent type,
and continuous phase flow rate on solely the porosity of the fabricated
porogen-containing PPs (Amoyav and Benny, 2019).
Therefore, the purpose of this work was to investigate:
1. The effect of microfluidics on the PP QAs as compared to the tradi­
tional batch approach.
2. A comparison of the two techniques focusing on screening the effect
of formulation and process parameters variables on the PP’s EE %, LE
%, particle size, yield %, release profile, and surface and inner
morphologies.
The study focused on microfluidics as a method of dispersion of the
secondary emulsion, while the primary emulsion preparation technique
was batch-based. Primary emulsion variables, including dispersion
method, the drug to polymer ratio, PVA concentration in W1, were first
studied for batch-based PPs using a 23 full factorial DoE study design.
Selected primary emulsion variables were included in a second DoE,
comparing microfluidics to batch. Metformin hydrochloride (MFH), a
small, highly water-soluble drug (log P = -1.8) due to its low molecular
weight and high hydrophilicity, was considered a model challenging
hydrophilic drug to be loaded in PLGA PPs.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Materials
PLGA (50:50, Ester terminated, inherent viscosity 0.63 dl/g in HFIP)
was obtained from Lactel, Durect Corp. Inc, United States. MFH was
purchased from Toronto Research Chemicals, Canada. PVA, average
molecular weight 30,000–70,000 g/mol, 87–90% hydrolyzed was ob­
tained from Sigma-Aldrich, United States. Dichloromethane (DCM) was
purchased from J.T. Baker, ACS reagent. All other solvents were of HPLC
grade and were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich.
For fabricating the co-flow glass capillary device, square (ID = 0.9
mm) and round glass capillaries (ID = 0.7 mm, OD = 0.87 mm) pur­
chased from Vitrocom®, United States, were used. The round glass
capillary was pulled using a Micropipette puller (Sutter Instrument Co.,
United States) and polished to the desired nozzle size. Glass pretreat­
ment, treatment, and post-treatment washing were performed as
described elsewhere with some modifications (Levenstein et al., 2016).
The nozzle glass surface was hydrophilic-treated using (3-aminopropyl)
triethoxysilane to ensure proper wetting for emulsion generation by
placing 100 µL of the desired silane grade with the capillaries in a
vacuum desiccator for 60 min. 5-minute epoxy, Devcon®, United States,
was used to glue the co-flow microfluidic device.

2.2. Batch-based MFH-PLGA PPs preparation
MFH-PLGA PPs were prepared using the DE-SE technique schemat­
ically shown in Fig. 1. A 0.25 mL of an aqueous MFH solution (40 or 100
mg/mL), with or without 1 %w/v PVA, was emulsified in 2 mL of PLGA
solution in dichloromethane (DCM) at a concentration of 200 mg/mL to
form the primary emulsion. The primary emulsification was performed
using sonication for 30 seconds (s) on ice or vortexing for 2 minutes
(min). The primary emulsion was then added dropwise to a 300 mL
outer water phase (W2) of 1 %w/v PVA and stirred for 2–3 hours (h) at
room temperature, allowing for DCM evaporation and solidification of
the PLGA PPs encapsulating MFH. MFH-PLGA PPs were then collected
by filtration (Whatman™ Nylon filter, 0.45 µm), washed with deionized
water three times to remove excess PVA, frozen at −80 ◦C in a Revco™
freezer, and then lyophilized using Labcono Freezone® freeze dryer.
Dried MFH-PLGA PPs were stored in closed vials at 4 ◦C for further
characterizations.
2.3. Microfluidic-based MFH-PLGA PPs fabrication
MFH-PLGA PPs were fabricated using microfluidics, as shown in
Fig. 2. The primary emulsion was prepared using sonication, as
mentioned earlier. It was then introduced into the nozzle (dispersal
phase) at 0.05 mL/min in a dripping regime with a 1 % w/v PVA injected
into the square capillary at 0.5 mL/min (continuous phase). The fabri­
cated MFH-loaded PLGA droplets were collected in a petri-dish con­
taining 1 %w/v PVA to avoid coalescence and left in a fume hood for 3 h
in a quiescent state to allow for DCM evaporation. The fabricated so­
lidified microfluidic-based MFH-PLGA PPs were further processed in the
same manner as the batch-based PPs.
2.4. Design of experiments (DoE) studies
Design-Expert software (Version 11, Stat-Ease, Minneapolis, MN)
was used to plan the full-factorial DoE investigation of the primary
emulsion preparation variables and their effects on the QAs of MFH-
PLGA PPs. Selected significant primary emulsion variables were
included in a second full-factorial DoE comparing the batch and
microfluidics as a secondary emulsion dispersion method. A full-
factorial DoE design was selected as it is known to be suitable for such
screening studies (Hanrahan and Lu, 2006). Statistical analysis was
performed using analysis of variance (ANOVA) in which a P-value of less
than 0.05 was considered significant.
2.5. Physicochemical characterization of MFH-PLGA PPs
2.5.1. Particle morphology and size determination
The shape and surface morphology of MFH-PLGA PPs were examined
using Zeiss EVO 50 XVP scanning electron microscope (SEM). Samples
were loaded on a double-coated carbon conductive tape (Ted Pella Inc.,
Redding, CA), mounted on aluminum stubs, and sputter-coated twice
with gold before imaged using SEM at an acceleration voltage of 5–10
kV. The geometric diameter (Dg = X50) of the particles was measured
directly from the SEM images with ImageJ 1.49n software (NIH, USA).
The inner morphology of MFH-PLGA PPs, when done, was examined
for blade-cut particles using SEM and microtome-cut particles using Jeol
JEM-1230 transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and Amscope
ME520TA microscope equipped with an MU-900 camera. The blade-cut
particles were prepared by immersing the PPs in liquid nitrogen, fol­
lowed by cross-sectioning using a blade. The protocol used for micro­
tomy and TEM imaging is detailed in S1.
2.5.2. Encapsulation efficiency (EE %)
The amount of MFH successfully incorporated in the PLGA PPs was
determined using a liquid–liquid extraction method or the in-vitro
dissolution data (release studies). In the liquid–liquid extraction
method, 10 mg MFH-PLGA PPs were added to 2 mL of DCM to dissolve
PLGA, and MFH was extracted using 1 mL of deionized water, stirring at
50 rpm for 48 h. The aqueous layer was withdrawn, and the concen­
tration of MFH was determined using HPLC analysis.
The in-vitro dissolution testing was performed using 10 mg MFH-
PLGA PPs suspended in 1 mL phosphate buffer pH of 6.8 (±0.05) in
Eppendorf tubes fitted into a foam piece, making sure the part con­
taining the solution is submerged in the water bath. They were kept
stirring at 200 rpm at 37 ◦C. At predetermined time intervals, tubes were
centrifuged at 14,000 rpm, and then 1 mL was withdrawn for HPLC
analysis. The removed solution was replaced with a fresh dissolution
medium. Release studies were performed in triplicates (n = 3) for 54
days until no further release was observed. The total amount of drug
released was used to calculate MFH’s EE % in the PLGA PPs. Theoretical
and actual LE % and EE % were calculated using Equations 1, 2 and 3.

2.5.3. In-vitro release profile kinetic modeling
To understand the mechanism by which MFH released from the
loaded particles, we subjected the release profile to fit multiple kinetic
models; zero order, first order, Higuchi, Korsmeyer-Peppas, Baker-
Lonsdale, Hixson-Crowell, and Hopfenberg, using DDsolver software
(Zhang et al., 2010). The goodness of fit parameters and rate constants
were calculated. The goodness of fit of a model was determined based on
R2 adjusted, Akaike information criterion (AIC), and model selection
criterion (MSC).

2.5.5. Thermal analysis
Thermal analysis of MFH-PLGA PPs, pure MFH, PLGA 50:50, and
physical mixtures of both was performed using differential scanning
calorimetry (DSC) (DSC Q1000, TA instruments, DE, United States). A
5–10 mg sample was loaded in aluminum pans, cribbed, and heated
from 0 to 250 ◦C at the rate of 10 ◦C/min under a nitrogen atmosphere.
Differences in heat flow rate were measured against an empty reference
pan.
2.5.6. X-Ray powder diffraction (XRPD) analysis
MFH’s crystallinity in MFH-PLGA PPs was analyzed using XRPD
(Miniflex II Desktop X-ray diffractometer, Rigaku, Japan). Copper K-
alpha radiation was used with a scintillation detector at a generation
voltage of 30 kV and a current of 15 mA. Data were collected by the 2θ
method at a scan speed of 1◦/minute at the range of 5 −
80◦2θ.
2.5.7. HPLC analysis
HPLC analysis was performed using an Agilent 1100 HPLC system
with a Phenomenex Luna® SCX column (250 mm × 4.6 mm, 10 µm, 100
Å) (Macherey–Nagel, Germany) equipped with a guard column filled
with the same packing material and UV detection at 218 nm. The mobile
phase consisted of 100% ammonium phosphate buffer of pH = 3 at a
flow rate of 2.2 mL/min, run with isocratic conditions. The injection
volume was 20 μL and the retention time of MFH’s peak was 8.5 min.
The calibration curve was prepared at 10 µg/mL to 400 µg/mL of MFH.
3. Results
3.1. Primary emulsion variables effect on MFH-PLGA PP QAs
3.1.1. Primary emulsion preparation variables: DoE study
A two-level, full-factorial design (23) was developed to understand
the effect of the primary emulsion dispersion method (factor A), the drug
to polymer ratio (factor B), and PVA concentration in W1 (factor C) on
yield % (Y1), particle size (Y2), EE % (Y3), and LE % (Y4) of MFH-PLGA
PPs prepared via the batch technique. The upper and lower levels of each
variable are summarized in Table 1A. The primary emulsion for each of
the eight planned formulations was prepared according to the settings
mentioned in Table 2, keeping the secondary emulsion preparation
conditions constant across all experiments using stirring for 2–3 h. The
results of all study responses, summarized in Table 2, were analyzed
using Design-Expert, which produces half-normal probability plots (S2)
and Pareto charts to visualize the study variables-response relationship
and significance.
3.1.2. Yield %
The process yield % of the eight batch-based formulations (F1 – F8)
ranged from 65.9 to 81.8 %. The fitted model describing the influence of
variables on the yield % is as follows:
Yield % = 73.54 −4.77B

3.1.6. Surface morphology
All F1-F8 formulations were observed to have spherical-shaped
morphology with polydisperse particle size using SEM, as shown in
Fig. 5Ia and Fig. 5IIa. From comparing Fig. 5I to Fig. 5II, it is noticeable
that the degree of the surface porosity and roughness is correlated pri­
marily to the method of dispersion of the primary emulsion. Formula­
tions fabricated using vortexing (Fig. 5Ib-d) exhibited a less porous and
smooth surface than formulations prepped using sonication (Fig. 5IIb-d),
with a highly porous and rough texture. These findings were consistent
across each of the four formulations fabricated using the same dispersion
method while varying the drug to polymer and the PVA concentration.
From Fig. 5II, it is observed that the sonicated PPs’ degree of porosity is
affected by the drug to polymer ratio. MFH-PLGA PPs prepared using
sonication at a 1:4 drug to polymer ratio (Fig. 5IId and e) have an
extremely porous surface compared to those fabricated at a 1:10 drug to
polymer ratio (Fig. 5IIb and c).
3.2. Comparison of microfluidics to traditional batch technique
3.2.1. DoE study design
Based on our findings in the previous DoE study, sonication, a pri­
mary emulsion dispersion method, was proven to be crucial for maxi­
mizing EE % and LE % and resulting in less poly-dispersed MFH-PLGA
PPs than vortexing. Accordingly, we fixed sonication as the primary
emulsion dispersion method for this second DoE study, comparing
microfluidics to an entirely traditional batch technique. A two-level,
full-factorial 23 DoE design was utilized to determine the effect of the
drug to polymer ratio (factor A), PVA concentration (factor B), and
method of dispersion of the secondary emulsion (factor C) on yield %

3.2.2. Surface and inner morphology
Four microfluidic-based MFH-PLGA PPs formulations (A1, A4-A6)
were fabricated using a co-flow device. The primary emulsion, prep­
ped using sonication, was injected into the co-flow device as the
dispersal phase into a continuous phase of 1 %w/v PVA (Fig. 2). Once
the primary emulsion passed through the nozzle, it formed highly uni­
form DE droplets (Video 1 and Fig. 8a). The DE droplets collected in the
petri dish had a 2D-circle shape with a diameter slightly larger than its
original size coming straight out of the device. The 2D-MFH-PLGA DE
droplets shrank in size during the solvent evaporation phase, forming
3D-spherical droplets (Fig. 8b) while maintaining their size uniformity.
Post-drying and lyophilization, these uniform droplets resulted in uni­
form PPs, as shown in the SEM images in Fig. 9a and b. Microfluidic
(Fig. 9a and b) and batch-based (Fig. 9d and e) MFH-PLGA PPs had
similar surface morphology; spherical particles with porous surfaces.
The pore size analysis using ImageJ software revealed that the
microfluidic-based MFH-PLGA PPs had significantly larger pores than
those produced by the batch process, 13.9 ± 6.22 µm and 2.6 ± 1.10 µm,
respectively. Microfluidic-based MFH-PLGA PPs had a more uniform
particle size with a soft, sponge-like texture, while the batch-based PPs
suffered from high polydispersity with a hard, dense texture. The SEM
images of cross-sectioned MFH-PLGA PPs in Fig. 9c and f revealed a
hollow form for the microfluidic-based PPs with varying skin thick­
nesses; in contrast, the batch-based PPs had a matrix-like structure. The
same matrix-like inner morphology was confirmed from the TEM and
optical microscope images of the microtome sections of the batch-based
particles, as shown in Fig. 10. Unfortunately, the microtomy of the
microfluidic-based MFH-PLGA PPs was not successful because the PPs
popped out of the resin during slicing.
3.2.3. Crystallinity
The XRPD diffractograms comparing pure MFH, unprocessed PLGA
(50:50), microfluidic and batch-based MFH-PLGA PPs are shown in S4.
Pure MFH exhibited multiple characteristic diffraction peaks at 2θ;
12.5◦, 17.9◦, 23.1◦, 24.8◦, and 25.8◦, indicating its crystalline structure.
Unprocessed PLGA showed a curved baseline with no identifiable peaks,
typical for amorphous solids. Likewise, microfluidic and batch-based
MFH-PLGA PPs had no characteristic peaks, suggesting that MFH is
encapsulated in the PLGA PPs in an amorphous or molecularly disor­
dered crystalline phase of molecular dispersion (Shin et al., 2010).
3.2.4. Thermal analysis
The DSC thermograms comparing the physical state of MFH-PLGA
PPs prepped via microfluidics and batch techniques to their individual
components are shown in S5. Pure MFH had an endothermic peak at
231.4 ◦C, corresponding to its melting point (Tm). On the other hand,
pure PLGA 50:50 had a single exothermic peak at 51.61 ◦C, corre­
sponding to its glass transition temperature (Tg). Both MFH-PLGA PPs
made via batch and microfluidics had similar Tg and Tm to the physical
mixture of MFH and PLGA. Hence, indicating only physical interaction
between the drug and the polymer.

PLGA PPs was in good agreement with first-order kinetics, indicating
diffusion of a water-soluble drug from a porous matrix (Ramteke et al.,
2014).
4. Discussion
In a systematic two-phase study, we revealed how MFH-PLGA PPs
QAs differed among microfluidics and batch techniques when the same
primary emulsion was used (i.e., batch-made). This primary emulsion
and its variables were separately studied in the first phase of the study.

PPs QAs

1) primary emulsion dispersion method at vortex and sonication; 2) drug

particle size, EE %, and LE %) were evaluated using a DoE approach.
The primary emulsion dispersion method significantly affected the
EE %, LE %, and particle size of MFH-PLGA PPs (Figs. 3 and 4). Its
observed effects on the EE % and particle size depended on the level of
the drug to polymer ratio used in the formulation. When sonication was
used as the primary emulsion dispersion method, the LE % of the PPs
was maximized to 3.5 % compared to 0.7 % using vortexing. The dif­
ference between sonication and vortexing methods lies in the amount of
shear force exerted in each case. Sonication applies high shear force,
breaking up the W1 phase into very tiny droplets uniformly dispersed in
the O phase. Smaller and better dispersed W1 droplets in the O phase
result in a more viscous primary emulsion (Yushu and Venkatraman,
2006). This viscosity hinders the W1 droplets’ diffusion and leakage to
the W2, resulting in our findings of higher LE % for the sonicated for­
mulations. On the other hand, vortexing exerts a low shear force that is
not sufficient to break up the inner W1 droplets into smaller droplets.
These large inner W1 droplets can easily diffuse out of the less viscous
primary emulsion to the continuous W2 phase, resulting in PPs with
lower LE %.

higher EE % (Fig. 4e) when compared to vortexing, resulting in an

difference can be explained again by the increase in viscosity of the
primary emulsion, hindering the leakage of the drug-containing inner
W1 to the outer W2 phase. Similar trends of LE % and EE % were
observed in other studies when the effects of high and low shear
methods for primary emulsion dispersion were compared (Qi et al.,
2014). The degree to which sonication resulted in an increased EE % of
MFH into the PLGA PPs was dependent on the drug to polymer ratio. The
1:10 ratio resulted in formulations with much higher EE % than the 1:4
ratio of an average maximum of 35.5 % and 17.2 %, respectively. A
higher amount of MFH in the W1, such as in the 1:4 ratio case, increases
the concentration gradient between the W1 and W2 phases, thereby
increasing the amount of MFH diffusing into the W2 and depleting the
amount of successfully encapsulated MFH (Yang et al., 2001). This
means that there is a trade-off between EE % and LE % in batch; very
high EE % can be achieved by lowering the drug concentration or
increasing the polymer concentration. However, the LE % of the fabri­
cated PPs would be compromised (the formulation will be mostly
polymer). Further enhancement of the EE % could be attained by
reducing the concentration gradient between W1 and W2 phases by
decreasing the volume of W2. The latter was not examined in the current
study as the primary emulsion variables were the main focus.
When the two primary batch emulsification techniques were
compared, sonication resulted in MFH-PLGA PPs with similar particle
sizes at both 1:4 and 1:10 ratios, while vortexing led to somewhat larger
particles at the 1:4 ratio when compared to the 1:10 ratio (Fig. 4b). The
same observation for the low shear-mixed PPs was noted by Cheng et al.;
larger particle size at the high drug content (Cheng et al., 2020). On the
other hand, our sonicated particles’ size not being significantly affected
by the drug to polymer ratio agreed with Cun et al.’s findings. (Cun
et al., 2011).
The other variables studied in the formation of the primary emulsion
also impacted the QAs of the product PPs. The drug to polymer ratio
moderately affected the yield % and the particle size and EE % discussed
above. 1:10 ratio resulted in a maximum yield % of 81.8 %, whereas the
1:4 ratio resulted in a maximum yield % of 69.3 %, which can be
attributed to the low EE % of the latter. This effect was largely inde­
pendent of the primary emulsification technique (Fig. 3a and 4a). The
PVA concentration used in the primary emulsification significantly
impacted the particle size of MFH-PLGA PPs, where the addition of PVA
increased the particle size (Fig. 3b and 4c and d). As established in the
literature (Yang et al., 2001), increasing the PVA concentration leads to
an increase in the primary emulsion viscosity, making it more chal­
lenging to disperse the primary emulsion into smaller droplets during
the secondary batch emulsification process, which results in overall
larger particles. Our findings agreed with what was observed by other
researchers (Abdelkader et al., 2018; Ahmad et al., 2020).
The surface morphology noticeably varied among MFH-PLGA PPs
prepared via different primary emulsion dispersion methods. Sonication
resulted in rough and highly porous surfaces, with porosity correlated
directly to the drug to polymer ratio, while vortexing resulted in a
smooth and less porous texture (Fig. 5). This porous surface for the
sonicated PPs matches the capability of sonication to uniformly disperse
the W1 into very tiny droplets in the O phase that ultimately transformed
into pores in the final solidified MFH-PLGA PPs (Qi et al., 2014). As
described earlier, increasing the amount of drug increases the phase’s
viscosity (Rahman et al., 2010), making it more difficult for the finely
dispersed W1 droplets to coalesce. Hence, more porous surfaces were
observed for the 1:4 sonicated formulations when compared to the
formulations at the 1:10 ratio. Vortexing applies less shear force that was
not sufficient to disperse the W1 into smaller droplets, resulting in a
primary emulsion with less viscosity. Hence, the inner W1 can easily
migrate to the continuous W2.

a comprehensive picture of the batch-microfluidics space, the drug to
polymer ratio and PVA concentration remained a part of the DoE in
which a 23 design was employed (Table 3). The use of a commonly
prepared, sonicated primary emulsion in comparing microfluidics to
batch has made the variables’ effects on MFH-PLGA PPs more apparent
and easier to evaluate systematically. For microfluidic secondary
emulsion fabrication, the primary emulsion was dispersed in the W2
phase using the co-flow device (Fig. 2b). The batch secondary emulsion
was prepped by adding the primary emulsion dropwise instantly into the
continuous W2 phase while shear mixing (stirring).
The use of microfluidics affected several aspects of MFH-PLGA PPs
QAs, including EE %, LE %, particle size, yield %, surface and inner
morphologies, and cumulative % release at 1 and 24 h. Microfluidics, a
dispersion method with no need for shear mixing, resulted in MFH-PLGA
PPs with much higher EE % and LE % (Fig. 7d and e). In addition, when
using microfluidics, in contrast with batch, the EE % and LE % increased
with the increased drug to polymer ratio (Fig. 7d). As explained earlier,
increasing the amount of drug in the W1 leads to an increase in the
concentration gradient between the W1 and W2 phases; thus, the drug
diffuses down the concentration gradient at a faster rate due to the
existing shear mixing in batch (Yang et al., 2001). However, in micro­
fluidics, this diffusion rate possibly decreased due to the fact that no
shear mixing was needed during the double emulsification and solvent
evaporation process, in addition to the smaller W2 phase (Fig. 2a).
The particle size of MFH-PLGA PPs was significantly larger and more
monodispersed for microfluidic-based formulations, which varied be­
tween 973.4 and 1011.6 µm, whereas the completely batch-based PPs’
size varied between 235.7 and 436.0 µm. The PVA concentration only
impacted the particle size of MFH-PLGA PPs, when a full batch tech­
nique was used (Fig. 7c). In the literature, it is established that the
emulsion droplet size formed in microfluidic devices depends on input
fluids flow rates, viscosities, and device geometry (Shang et al., 2017). In
our study, the microfluidic-based MFH-PLGA PP size (about 1000 µm)
was close to the square capillary’s inner dimensions (0.9 mm). During
the primary emulsion dispersion into the continuous W2 phase, we
observed that the primary emulsion droplets, once they came out of the
nozzle, enlarged in size and had a diameter close to the inner diameter of
the square capillary, as shown in video 1. This observed droplet diam­
eter minimally increased before it started to decrease during the solvent
evaporation phase. This geometry-related particle size may explain why
PVA concentration did not influence the size of microfluidic-based MFH-
PLGA PPs. The PVA concentration influenced the size of the batch-based
PPs due to the reasons discussed above related to the increased primary
emulsion viscosity at higher PVA concentration, hindering its break up
into tiny droplets by the batch-based secondary emulsion dispersion
method, namely, stirring.
Microfluidics increased the overall yield % of MFH-PLGA PPs when
compared to the batch technique (Fig. 7a and b). A potential explanation
for this is the uniformly large particle size for all microfluidic-based
formulations that may result in fewer mechanical losses upon filtra­
tion. In contrast, the batch-based formulations were much smaller and
more polydisperse, and fine particles were noted in the filtrate. In
addition, a higher yield % would be expected owing to the higher
loading of the microfluidic-based MFH-PLGA PPs.
The SEM micrographs showed that microfluidics resulted in large
uniform MFH-PLGA PPs with large surface pores, while the batch led to
small polydisperse particles that exhibited small pores (Fig. 9b and e).
This large pore size is probably due to the coalescence of the dispersed
W1 in the primary emulsion during the 45-minute microfluidic emulsi­
fication run. This coalescence eventually resulted in a double emulsion
with large internal W1 droplets. These large droplets would translate
into large pores (13.9 ± 6.22 µm) after the complete solidification of the
PPs (Qi et al., 2014). This explanation was further supported when the
internal morphology of the microfluidic-based PPs was examined
(Fig. 9c) and determined to be a hollow structure. On the other hand, the
batch-based primary emulsion was instantly added to the continuous W2
phase, forming the secondary emulsion. This emulsion mostly main­
tained its tiny, well-dispersed W1 droplets structure, which solidified to
particles with a matrix-like structure (Fig. 9f and 10a-d) and small sur­
face pores (2.6 ± 1.10 µm).
Microfluidic-based MFH-PLGA PPs with the hollow structure, larger
pore sizes, and higher LE % exhibited much faster release at 1 and 24 h
than the matrix-like structured batch PPs (Fig. 11). High MFH loading
into the microfluidic-based PLGA PPs resulted in a higher concentration
gradient between the inside of the PPs and the release media. Simulta­
neously, the thin polymeric matrix, in addition to the large pore size,
accounted for less resistance for drug diffusion across the matrix. Hence,
it explains why microfluidic-based PPs had a high burst release, with a
release profile following first-order kinetics down the concentration
gradient. With the diffusion rate dependent on the drug molecule’s
molecular weight, a small molecule such as MFH would suffer more
significantly from rapid diffusion across the PLGA matrix when
compared to larger molecules. In contrast, the matrix-like structure and
the small pores from the fully batch-based process hindered the rapid
leakage of this small molecule into the outer release media.
The drug to polymer ratio influenced the cumulative % release at 24
h in addition to the secondary emulsion dispersion method (Fig. 11e).
The fact that the 1:10 ratio led to a relatively lower burst release than the
1:4 can be attributed to the former’s lower drug concentration gradient
between the inner and outer release media. Hence, the rate by which the
drug was released was slower in the 1:10 case, as similarly reported by
others (Cheng et al., 2020; Mainardes and Evangelista, 2005).
5. Conclusions
The studies detailed in this work were undertaken to investigate the
effect of process and formulation variables on the QAs of a highly hy­
drophilic drug-loaded PLGA PPs, in this case, MFH. Multiple primary
emulsion process and formulation variables were screened using a DoE
approach to determine the most significant factors impacting the QAs of
MFH-PLGA PPs, namely EE % and LE %. This facilitated the comparison
of the effect of microfluidics to the traditional batch technique on the
QAs of MFH-PLGA PPs under the selected primary emulsion variables. In
the primary emulsion screening phase, it was found that the sonication
increased the EE % and LE % and impacted the particle size and the
surface porosity and roughness of MFH-PLGA PPs. The low drug to
polymer ratio increased the yield % and EE % and decreased the particle
size of the vortexed particles, while the high PVA concentration
increased the particle size.
In the microfluidics-batch comparison phase, we found that micro­
fluidics increased the yield %, EE %, LE %, and particle size of MFH-
PLGA PPs over the traditional batch method. The low drug to polymer
ratio decreased the % burst release and increased the yield % owing to a
higher concentration gradient. The PVA concentration effect, while seen
in the batch-based formulations, was not observed under microfluidic
conditions owing to the device-based geometry. Using a simple co-flow
microfluidic device, the EE % and LE % of MFH-PLGA PPs were
improved from 19.1 % and 3.8 %, respectively, for the batch-based PPs,
to 69.6 % and 13.9 % for the microfluidic-based PPs. However, despite
successfully loading MFH into the PLGA PPs using microfluidics – as
targeted, the release profile from the PPs was negatively impacted
because of the hollow porous structure in addition to the high LE %. This
burst release will be tackled in our future studies that will be focused on
engineering PPs using a fully microfluidic system, with the intent of
fabricating a polymer shell with no pores. This will be coupled by
studying the effect of PLGA’s molecular weight and lactic to glycolic
acid ratios.
The current study concluded our initial investigations into the
encapsulation of highly hydrophilic drugs into PPs. Future studies to
determine the effect of formulation and process variables on the QAs of
PPs fabricated using an entirely microfluidic process are planned. These
