A B S T R A C T
Double emulsion process has become highly promising for development of PEG-ylated nanocarriers (NCs) with
co-encapsulated hybrid model agents, i.e., hydrophilic deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) and hydrophobic Thiazole
Orange (TO) dye, in the double compartment structure to protect them from the environmental conditions and to
investigate diﬀerent parameters aﬀecting the size, charge and morphology as well as colloidal and biological
stability of the ﬁnal theranostic nanosystems. Diﬀerent stabilizing agents including surfactants: Cremophor A25,
Cremophor RH 40, Poloxamer 407, di-C12DMAB as well as polymers: PEG-PDLLA, PEG-PLGA, PEG-PCL, were
screened to choose suitable ones for this approach. The average size of the synthesized NCs measured by dy-
namic light scattering (DLS) remained < 200 nm. The encapsulation eﬃciency of the hybrid cargo was con-
ﬁrmed by UV–vis spectroscopy. Morphology and shape of the loaded nanocontainers were investigated by
transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and atomic force microscopy (AFM). Time-depended colloidal stability
studies with DLS and ζ-potential followed by turbidimetric technique allow to select only the long-term nano-
systems to ﬁnal investigation the “stealth” properties of the fabricated PEGylated NCs.

1. Introduction
The active cargo with diagnostic and therapeutic (theranostic)
properties deserve new kinetically stable and biocompatible delivery
systems having a colloidal phase as an essential prerequisite for their
eﬃciency [1,2]. Between the revealed nanocarriers an important role
may be played by nanoemulsions – bluish/translucent isotropic dis-
persions, which comprise water and/or oil nano-domains (with sizes
50–100 nm)
with

physico-chemical properties of nanoemulsions, their physical and che-
mical

interactions

drug

and

structural

agents and properties of the liquid/liquid interface [4–6]. During the
past decade or so, there have been major advances in the application of
nanoemulsion structural design principles to fabricate nanocarriers
with novel functional properties (size, charge, interfacial functionali-
zation and physical state) [2,4,7,8]. These approaches leading to active
agents encapsulation can be divided into three broad categories:
layering, embedding and clustering [2]. Among the mentioned techni-
ques, the double (multiple) emulsion method known to the embedding
process, is considered to be the most important because it permits the
encapsulation of hydrophilic and hydrophobic cargo in the both oily
and aqueous phases [8]. The hydrophobic diagnostic (e.g. organic and
inorganic ﬂuorophores) and hydrophilic therapeutic (e.g. drugs or nu-
cleic acids) agents can be loaded simultaneously or separately in the
double compartment structure and protected from the environmental
conditions. Furthermore they may also migrate from outer to inner
phase, providing a kind of reservoir suitable, for example to increase
spectroscopic and functional properties for targeted NIR-induced pho-
todynamic therapy (PDT) of some cancers or an intercalation of nucleic
acids (DNA or RNA) by some special organic dyes [8–10].
Generally the preparation of W/O/W formulations requires both
two-step emulsiﬁcation process and combination of hydrophilic and
hydrophobic surfactants to stabilize the W/O as well as the O/W in-
terface. However that approach suﬀer from low encapsulation eﬃ-
ciency, high droplet size and polydispersity as well as many destabili-
zation pathways leading to emulsions with short-term stability and low
biological potential [8]. Thus, the main issue when using double
emulsions in theranostic applications is their production in a well-
controlled manner with homogeneous droplet size and application of
biocompatible substrates, by optimizing diverse process variables [9].
So far many formulation approaches have been arranged to improve
double emulsion stability including sophisticated combinations of sur-
factants, the proportions optimization of the two types of emulsiﬁers,
and additions of cosurfactants or copolymers [10–12]. Nevertheless, as
it has been recently proved, the structuring of the nanoemulsion in-
terface by solvent evaporation method using functionalized polymers
(i.e., PEG-ylated polyesters as poly (D,L-lactic acid) – PDLLA, poly(gly-
colic acid) – PGA, polycaprolactone – PCL, and poly (lactic-co-glycolic
acid) – PLGA), may simultaneously improve the long-term colloidal
stability of the encapsulated hybrid cargo as well as extend the func-
tional performance of nanoemulsion based formulations to “stealth”
nanocarriers with unique attributes e.g. non-toxicity and stability in
blood circulation [9,13,14].
The objective of the present work is to design, develop and optimize
the solvent evaporation double emulsion (W/O/W) process in order to
obtain polyester-origin stealth nanocarriers (PEG-PDLLA, PEG-PLGA,
PEG-PCL) for co-encapsulation of hybrid cargo, i.e., model DNA used as
hydrophilic therapeutic agent and Thiazole Orange (TO) applied as
poorly water soluble hydrophobic diagnostic dye. The inﬂuence of
various preparation parameters, such as the type of surfactant and its
hydrophobicity, the type of polymer and the level of the active cargo
loading, on the characteristics of the obtained nanocarriers and their
colloidal and biological stability was investigated to optimize the best
nanosystem formulation for further in vitro and in vivo applications.

2.2. Fabrication of nanocarriers by double emulsion (w/o/w) solvent
evaporation method
Polymeric nanocarriers stabilized by PEG-PLGA, PEG-PCL, PEG-
PDLLA and non-ionic or cationic surfactants for co-encapsulation of
therapeutic (model DNA in the initial concentration of 0.1 mg/ml) and
diagnostic agent (TO in the initial concentration of 0.2 mg/ml) were
prepared by modiﬁed double emulsion (w/o/w) evaporation process
without any pH adjustment [8]. Generally, aqueous internal phase
(with DNA) was emulsiﬁed for 5 min in dichloromethane (containing
TO, PEG-ylated polymer in concentration of 5 mg/ml and di-C12DMAB)
in the ratio 1:4 using a homogenizer with 25,000 rpm. This primary
nanoemulsion was poured into 1% hydrophilic surfactant solution
(Cremophor A25, Cremophor RH 40 or Poloxamer 407) aqueous solu-
tion stirring in a homogenizer for 10 min (25,000 rpm) and immersed
in an ice water bath to create the water-in-oil-in-water (w/o/w)
emulsion. The organic solvent was then evaporated under reduced
pressure in a rotary evaporator (Ika RV 10 digital) and polymeric na-
nocarriers loaded by the hybrid cargo were collected overnight.
2.3. Mean droplet diameter, polydispersity index and surface charge
The nanocarrier size distribution (i.e. the mean hydrodynamic dia-
meter, DH) and polydispersity index (PdI) of the polymeric NCs were
determined by DLS using a Nano Series Zetasizer from Malvern
Instruments, with a detection angle of 173° in optically homogeneous
square polystyrene cells. All the measurements were performed at
25 °C. Each value was obtained as an average of three runs with at least
10 measurements. The surface charge expressed as ζ-potential of the
obtained nanocarriers was measured by the microelectrophoretic
method by the Malvern Zetasizer Nano ZS apparatus. Each value was
obtained as an average of three subsequent runs of the instrument with
at least 20 measurements. The DTS (Nano) program was used for the all
data evaluation.

The imaging of the shape and morphology of the obtained nano-

NCs were placed on Cu–Ni grid and left to dry in 20 h at room tem-
perature. The NCs were stained with 2% uranyl acetate, and then the
image was captured.

emulsion evaporation procedure was successfully designed to obtain
polyester-origin and PEG-ylated (stealth) nanocarriers to co-encapsulate a

According to the general strategy presented in Scheme 1, the w/o/w
double emulsion evaporation approach involves a three-step process. In
the ﬁrst step, aqueous internal phase containing DNA is emulsiﬁed into the
continuous organic phase being a combination of hydrophobic surfactant
(S1), polymer and dye solution forming water-in-oil (w/o) nanoemulsion.
Next, the primary submicron emulsion is poured into hydrophilic surfac-
tant (S2) aqueous solution and immersed in an ice water bath to create the
water-in-oil-in-water (w/o/w) emulsion. Then, the organic solvent eva-
porates through the emulsion/air interface to make the loaded NCs solidify
and hardening. Consequently, for the ﬁrst phase of w/o/w process we
applied a cationic double-chained surfactant didodecyldimethylammo-
nium bromide (di-C12DMAB). As it has been proved before, di-C12DMAB
increases the adsorption density of hydrophobic alkyl chains on the par-
ticle surface providing its enhanced hydrophobicity. Thus, their adsorption
ultimately induced the emulsion phase inversion from O/W emulsion to
W/O emulsion [16]. Furthermore, di-C12DMAB may interact with deox-
yribonucleic acid and form stable DNA–surfactant complexes leading to
condensation of the genetic material and fusion or destabilization of cell
membranes, and thus enhance gene delivery [17]. On the other hand the
eﬀective double-emulsion process requires also an application of a sur-
factant with high hydrophilic–lipophilic balance (HLB) value to stabilize
the second phase of w/o/w formation in tandem with the hydrophobic di-
C12DMAB [8]. Thus we applied three representative of hydrophilic stabi-
lizers, i.e., non-ionic polyethoxylated emulsiﬁers – Cremophor A25
(CrA25) and Cremophor RH 40 (CrH40) as well as non-ionic triblock co-
polymer – Poloxamer 407 (P407). Based on the unique surface properties
and HLB value equal to 14–22, these surfactants can be applied as a
material of choice for stabilized liquid-core nanocarriers at oil–water in-
terface [18]. Finally, for the enhanced NCs stabilization, environmental
protection and biocompatibility, three types of polyester-origin PEG-ylated
polymers were applied in this study (PEG-PLGA, PEG-PCL, PEG-PDLLA),
as bio-safe, biodegradable and pharmaceutical-oriented stealth com-
pounds of the loaded nanocontainer shell [13,14]. The appropriate con-
centrations of surfactants and polymers and the ratio of organic to aqueous
phase were selected according to previous studies [5,8,9,13] or were de-
termined experimentally. Nine types of polymeric nanocarriers (systems
1–9 from Table 1) were thus successfully synthesized. Their mean dia-
meter (DH), polydispersity index (PdI), zeta potential (ζ), encapsulation
eﬃciency of the model therapeutic compound (EEDNA) and diagnostic
agent (EETO), were assessed on the day of the theranostic NCs production
as well as 30 days after their storage in the room temperature. The mean
particle sizes, polydispersity and ζ-potential measurements of the obtained
nanocontainers stabilized by various surfactant and polymer types dis-
played an average size between 143 and 189, PdI of ca. 0.2–0.3 and nearly
neutral surface from −4 mV to −1 mV, corresponding well with data
reported for other polymeric carriers designed mainly for anticancer
therapies [5,19,20]. Concerning the inﬂuence of polymer and surfactant
type on the nanocarrier size, we observe that the smallest nanocarriers
with narrow size distribution (PdI ∼0.22) were obtained for those sta-
bilized by PEG-PLGA and CrA25, while those stabilized by PEG-PCL
showed the broadest range of particle sizes and polydispersity (up to 189
and 0.32 respectively for system 6) regardless of the surfactant type, which
can be caused by longer solubility of that polymer in organic phase during
the emulsiﬁcation process. However, regarding the surfactant inﬂuence on
the NCs DH and PdI, it should be notice that the all nanosystems stabilized
by P407 revealed lowest quality for possible biological application, i.e., the
less unimodal size distribution resulting in PdI from 0.28 (for system 3) to
0.32 (for system 6). Furthermore, those NCs showed also low encapsula-
tion eﬃciency of the active cargo between 72–80%, which is quite low
comparing with the EEs of the nanosystems stabilized by CrA25 (80–95%).

3.3. Impact of PEG-ylation on the nanocarrier biological stability
The goal of eﬀective drug or gene delivery systems is to prolong, localize
and protect the encapsulated cargo with the surrounded environment [14].
After intravenous administration nanoparticles may encounter several spe-
ciﬁc barriers as rapid recognition by the Mononuclear Phagocyte System
(MPS) followed by their clearance from the systemic blood ﬂow that might
prevent those NCs from reaching the intended cancer cells. Thus, the dis-
covery of eﬃcient ways to prolong nanoparticles circulation time by re-
ducing MPS-mediated NC clearance is of major importance to nanomedicine
[14,21]. Poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG) has been shown to successfully confer
protein resistance to a variety of surfaces through both main mechanisms,
i.e., steric repulsion and a hydration or water structuring layer. Moreover,
neutrally charged particles have a much lower opsonization rate followed
by MPS-mediated clearance than those with high surface charge [14].
Consequently to circumvent such phenomenon and increase the further
half-life of the obtained NCs in body ﬂuids, PEG-ylated nanosystems as
potentially invisible to the immune system (“stealth”) have been developed.
The stealth properties of the PEGylated theranostics revealed by the biolo-
gical stability tests (Fig. 4) indicate that obtained NCs not agglomerate
during the incubation in the DMEM supplemented in 10% FBS – a mimic of
the physiological environment widely used for cell culture. It was observed
that upon addition the biological ﬂuid the size distribution of the obtained
nanosystems with a mean diameter value from 143–162 nm changed only
slightly (up to 5 nm for the system 4) proving the PEG-ylated shell pro-
tection from the possible adsorption of serum proteins present also in the
blood circulation system, i.e., albumin, α-globulin, β-globulin, γ-globulin
and IgG. This ﬁnding is important because colloidal stability in serum for
any nanoparticulate system determines the successful delivery of the active
cargo as it prevents particle aggregation, opsonization or embolism from
occurring in the systemic circulation prolonging the nanocarrier half-life
and in vivo biodistribution after parenteral administration [22].
4. Conclusions
According to the obtained results we accomplish that a rationally de-
signed double emulsion process has the potential to be a promising ap-
proach to obtain long-lasting, biocompatible and stealth nanocarriers of
hybrid theranostic cargo with diﬀerent hydrophobicity, i.e., DNA and TO.
The optimized w/o/w encapsulation resulted in NCs with sizes below
200 nm, narrow size distribution,
nearly neutral surface and high
encapsulation eﬃciency for nanosystems primarily with PEG-PLGA shell
and stabilized by Cremophor A25. The biological stability studies provided
in a mimic of the physiological environment, proved the potential of the
PEG-ylated nanosystems to overcome several speciﬁc barriers in circulation
systems after intravenous administration and thus to reach the intended
cancer cells in potential in vivo theranostic applications.
