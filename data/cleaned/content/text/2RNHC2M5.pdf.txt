ABSTRACT:
Linamarin-loaded poly(lactide-co-glycolide) (PLGA)
nanoparticles were prepared by using a double emulsion solvent evaporation
method using polyvinyl alcohol as a surfactant. A two-factor full factorial design,
Box–Behnken design and surface response methodology, were employed to
improve the nanoparticle yield, particle size, and drug encapsulation efﬁciency

polymer concentration, surfactant concentration, and homogenization speed.
Both linear and quadratic empirical models were developed to express each
response parameter as a function of the studied factors. A total of 12 and 17

release and overall drug release of <30% in 120 h of incubation.

Introduction
P
olymeric and gold nanoparticles have been in-
vestigated as drug delivery systems for differ-
ent biomedical applications such as in vitro and in
vitro assay, in vivo imaging, and cancer therapy.1−3
Targeting cancer cells is a major goal for most
researchers who aim to convey a sufﬁcient and
controlled dose of drug to the tumor.4 Linamarin
(Fig. 1), a cyanogenic glycoside (2-β-D-glucopy-
ranosyloxy-2-methylpropanenitrile), is found in cas-
sava (Manihot esculenta Crantz) root cortex and
parenchyma and has good water solubility. The bit-
ter taste of fresh cassava roots is associated with
high levels (>90%) of cyanogenic glycosides, mainly
linamarin.5,6 Cytotoxicity effects of pure linamarin
as well as aqueous and methanol extract of cas-
FIGURE 1. Chemical structure of linamarin.
sava linamarin on Caov3, MCF-7, HT-29, and HL-
60 cells had proved to have a bright future as an
alternative anticancer.7,8 This anticancer activity of
linamarin is attributed to the release of hydrogen
cyanide (CN) upon hydrolysis of linamarin. It is im-
portant to highlight that HCN is toxic and can subse-
quently kill both cancerous as well as normal cells.
Therefore, because of both the high water solubil-
ity of linamarin and toxicity of HCN, it is crucial
to formulate well the drug delivery carrier to en-
sure that only the correct amount of linamarin is
released in the human system at the right time and
area to enhance drug efﬁcacy and avoid any side ef-
fects. Linamarin’s high water solubility represents a
major challenge for its nanoencapsulation using an
aqueous medium. A biodegradable poly(D,L-lactide-
co-glycolide) (PLGA) polymer has been used in the
current research as a carrier polymer to nanoencap-
sulated linamarin.
To improve product or process quality, it is cru-
cial to understand the process behavior as well
as amount of variability and its impact on the
process. Most of the experimental designs involve
changing one variable at a time while maintain-
ing the others at a ﬁxed level. The limitation of
this type of experimentation is that it is unreliable,
time consuming, requires a large number of runs
and resources, and may also give false data be-
cause it ignores the interaction effects between the
investigated process parameters. To overcome this
problem, highly automated statistical tools such as

tages of these statistical tools for empirical modeling,
analysis, and optimization of any process are that all
the parameters can be varied simultaneously over a
set of experimental runs. Moreover, valid, reliable,
and sound conclusions can be drawn effectively and
economically. Experimental deigns such as factorial

highly empirical models and treat the system as a
black box. They relate the inputs and outputs by
empirical correlations that ﬁt reasonably well with
the experimental results and are reliable only within
the region of input–output of these experimental re-
sults. Because of the complexity of the physical and
chemical phenomena involved, each drug delivery
system should be ﬁrst characterized precisely before
carrying out the above-mentioned empirical model-
ing. Therefore, the best physicochemical characteris-
tics of nanoparticles can be obtained by optimizing
their encapsulation process. The aim of this work is
to optimize the process parameters for preparation
of linamarin-loaded PLGA nanoparticles.

98% hydrolyzed polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) with

analytical-grade chemicals, such as dipotassium hy-

EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN
The experimental design for the formulation of
linamarin-loaded PLGA nanoparticles was carried
out in two stages. PLGA nanoparticles prepared
by using the emulsiﬁcation solvent evaporation
method have been found to be inﬂuenced by multi-
ple parameters such as a PLGA (lactide/glycolide)
ratio, polymer concentration, surfactant type and
concentration, solvent nature, stirring rate and time,
and formulation temperature.13−18 A preliminary
scouting study (screening process) was carried out
by preparing some batches to determine the most
signiﬁcant process parameters that greatly affect the
characteristics of the nanoparticles (NPs). After the
screening process, the effect of only two factors,
namely PVA concentration (A) and polymer concen-
tration (B), were investigated using a 22 full factorial
design.19,20 The selected high and low levels were 30
and 50 mg/mL for PVA and 2.5 and 10 mg/mL for
PLGA. The design was carried out in triplicate, two
center points with a total of 14 completely random-
ized experiments. In the second stage, a new pro-
cess parameter, namely homogenization speed (C),
which ranged from 15,000 to 23,000 rpm, was intro-
duced to the best values of the PVA concentration
(A) and PLGA concentration (B) selected from the
ﬁrst stage. Both Box–Behnken design21−23 combined
with RSM19,20 have been employed in the second
stage of optimization. Meanwhile, in both stages the
studied responses are nanoparticle yield, drug en-
capsulation efﬁciency, and particle size. Design Ex-
pert (6.0.4, Stat-Ease Inc., Minneapolis, MN) soft-
ware was employed to design and analyze the data.
The data collected using both designs were analyzed
using ANOVA.22
PREPARATION OF THE NANOPARTICLES
Linamarin-loaded nanoparticles were prepared
using a double emulsion solvent evaporation,
sometimes referred to as the water-in-oil-in-water
(W/O/W) technique. The W/O/W technique em-
ployed in the current research was according to the
one used by researchers.24−27 Meanwhile, the prepa-
ration process was based on the use of a homoge-
nizer (Heidolph DIAX 900, Germany) in a two-step
emulsiﬁcation process.
MORPHOLOGY OF THE NANOPARTICLES
To examine the surface morphology, the freeze-
dried nanoparticles were adhered to a double-sided
adhesive carbon tape placed on an aluminum stub
and then coated with gold–palladium (Baltec SC030
Sputter Coater, Germany). The morphological char-
acteristics of the nanoparticles were evaluated using
a scanning electron microscope (Philips XL30 ESEM,
Eindhoven, Netherlands).

DRUG ENCAPSULATION EFFICIENCY
A nondestructive indirect method was applied to
determine the drug encapsulation efﬁciency (DEE).
First, the amount of free linamarin in both the super-
natant and washings was measured at a wavelength
of 545 nm using a UV-spectrophotometer (Spectronic
Genesys TM and PC model 336003, Spectronic In-
struments, San Diego, CA). Then, an amount of the
encapsulated linamarin was calculated by subtract-
ing the amount of free drug from the total amount
of the drug used in the formulation. The percentage
of DEE was calculated using Eq. (2),29,30:
Drug encapsulation efﬁciency
=
Mass of drug in nanoparticles
Mass of drug used in formulation × 100
(2)
PARTICLE SIZE
The average particle size was investigated us-
ing the photon correlation spectroscopy. The mea-
surements of the particle size were performed as
follows: 10 μL of the nanoparticle fresh emulsion
was well suspended in 1 mL of distilled water to
ensure a complete homogeneity. The average par-
ticle size of the suspension was then measured
using a particle-size analyzer (Nanophox, Sympa-
tec Gmball, System-Partikel-Technik, K¨onigsbrunn,
Germany).
IN VITRO DRUG RELEASE
A weighed quantity of the NPs was suspended
in a closed conical ﬂask containing a speciﬁc vol-
ume of 0.1 M phosphate-buffered saline pH 7.4. The
NP suspensions were then incubated at 37◦C, un-
der a constant shaking rate at 150 rpm. At predeter-
mined time intervals, 1.5 mL liquor of each sample
was removed and centrifuged at 10,000 rpm to sep-
arate nanoparticles. One milliliter of sample super-
natant was placed in a cuvette, and the absorbance
of linamarin was measured at a wavelength of 545
nm (Spectronic Genesys TM and PC model 336003;
Spectronic Instruments). A fresh 1.5-mL buffer solu-
tion was placed in the centrifuge tube and vigorously
shaken to release the nanoparticles that adhered to
the walls and bottom of the tube. Then, the liquor
was returned to the incubation ﬂask.
Results and Discussion
This research beneﬁts from the advantage of
biodegradable PLGA polymer to formulate PLGA
nanoparticles loaded with linamarin as a controlled
release anticancer candidate. Nanoparticulate drug
delivery systems have been chosen as the convenient
carriers to be prepared because of their multiple ad-
vantages such as high stability and drug loading ca-
pacity and good penetration into the cells. At the pre-
liminary factor screening process, two copolymers,
namely PLGA (50:50) and PLGA (85:15), were tested
for the production of nanoparticles. Nanoparticles
produced using PLGA (50:50) were better in terms
of their physicochemical characteristics compared to
nanoparticles prepared using PLGA (85:15); there-
fore, PLGA (50:50) was employed throughout this
work. It was also observed that increasing the vol-
ume of an internal aqueous drug solution increases
the particle size and decreases the DEE. The same
observation was also previously reported by other
researchers.18,31 Therefore, the volume of the internal
aqueous solution was kept at 1 mL throughout all ex-
periments. The internal aqueous solution of volume
<1 mL was excluded due to difﬁculty in dispersing
the concentrated and viscous drug solution into the
organic phase. It is also expected that drug loss on
container walls will increase during its transfer to
the organic solution.

considerably material loss, enhance production, and
reduce manufacturing cost. Interestingly, nearly all
the formulated batches gave yields that ranged be-
tween 40% and 87%. Variations observed with all
formulations indicate that the responses are strongly
affected by the process factors. From the ANOVA ta-
ble, it was found that the factors A, B, C, A2, and
AB are statistically signiﬁcant (p < 0.05). The model
has good correlation coefﬁcients (R 2 = 0.9641 and
adjusted R 2 = 0.9180), indicating that the empirical
model is suitable for the prediction of the response
with respect to the process factors. The data normal-
ity was evaluated using the normal probability plot

concentration from 1% to 5%, whereas the particle
size increased from 137 to 164 nm with an increas-
ing PLGA concentration from 13.3 to 66.7 mg/mL.65
The decrease in the particle size with an increas-
ing PVA concentration has been widely reported by
other researchers.58,60,66−69
The increase in the particle size with respect
to the PLGA concentration could probably be due
to the enhanced viscosity of the organic phase,
and consequently this will reduce dispersion of the
aqueous phase containing a drug into the poly-
mer organic phase. Because the ﬁnal size of the
particles is dependent upon the ﬁrst globule size
generated throughout the emulsiﬁcation process,
coarse emulsions or a greater aggregation num-
ber may be obtained as a result of a high PLGA
concentration and this will lead to larger particles
with wider size distributions.66,70−72 Our ﬁndings
correlate with those found by Le-sheng and col-
leagues, who reported an increase in both particle
size (i.e., from 34.3% to 70%) and DEE (i.e., from
55% to 76%) when they increased the PLGA con-
centration from 15% to 40%. The authors also re-
ported a decrease in the size with the homogeniza-
tion speed.73 A similar observation was also found
by Budhian and coworkers, who reported an in-
crease in the particle size from 200 to 300 nm when
the polymer concentration was varied from 5 to
66.6 mg/mL.74 Meanwhile, the increase in the par-
ticle size with an increasing polymer concentration
for the PLA and PLGA polymers has been widely
reported.3,18,37,61,66,75,76
After investigating the effects of the PVA and
PLGA concentration and homogenization speed on
the yield, DEE, and particle size, the best val-
ues of these parameters for semiﬁnal optimization
were determined as follows: (1) about >48 mg/mL
PVA concentration and <6.5 mg/mL PLGA con-
centration; (2) about >47 mg/mL PVA concentra-
tion and >22,000 rpm homogenization speed; and
(3) about <7.19 mg/mL PLGA concentration and
>20,000 rpm homogenization speed. Based on these
selected values, a numerical optimization using the
desirability approach was applied to locate the opti-
mal settings of the formulation parameters to obtain
the desired responses. Based on the desired nanopar-
ticle properties, numerous formulations were pro-
posed by setting constraints on the independent pro-
cess parameters and responses.
Numerous suggestions were given by the model
but ﬁve formulations were selected and formulated
in triplicate based on the desirability. A comparison
was made on the observed and predicted values of
the NP yield, particle size, and DEE. The predic-
tion error (%) for the responses was calculated using

Conclusions
Successful linamarin-loaded PLGA nanoparticles
were fabricated by using the double emulsion sol-
vent evaporation method. The effect of the most ef-
fective process parameters, namely polymer (PLGA)
concentration, surfactant (PVA) concentration, and
homogenization speed on the NP yield, particle
size, and DEE was evaluated. The optimal values
of the studied process parameters were determined
with the aid of both a two-factor full factorial de-
sign and Box–Behnken design with surface response
methodology (RSM). The ﬁnal optimum values for
the PLGA concentration, PVA concentration, and ho-
mogenization speed were determined at 9.8 mg/mL,
50 mg/mL, and 23,000 rpm, respectively. The ex-
perimental results were found to be in good agree-
ment with those predicted by the empirical model.
Thus it could be concluded that RSM represents an
effective and potential technique for optimization
of linamarin-loaded PLGA nanoparticles. Interest-
ingly, the optimized nanoparticles showed a sus-
tained and controlled drug release (lasting for days)
compared to both the free drug as well as the nonop-
timized drug.
