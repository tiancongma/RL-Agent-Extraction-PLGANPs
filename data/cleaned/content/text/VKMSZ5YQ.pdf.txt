A B S T R A C T
The objective of current research work was to fabricate dissolving microneedles combining ergotamine and
caffeine for synergistic action using controlled release kinetics with better permeability. The method of prepa­
ration for microneedles utilized multiple emulsion (w/o/w) approach by solvent-diffusion-evaporation process
wherein the nano-emulsion of ergotamine and caffeine prepared using PLGA polymer and PVA as a stabilizer.
The PLGA nanospheres were further loaded in polymer matrix of PVA and PVP K-90 and the final mixture poured
in sterile silicon molds of microneedles. The PLGA nanospheres exhibited particle size in narrow range of 280.34
± 6.61 to 416.0 ± 9.67 nm and good colloidal stability with negative zeta potential ranging between −19.08 ±
8.77 to −22.49 ± 8.09 mV. Higher entrapment efficiency (86.21 ± 4.52 %) for ergotamine and controlled
release pattern (49.79 ± 4.16 % at 48 h) displayed by PLGA nanospheres. Similarly, the dissolving microneedles
loaded with PLGA nanospheres showed controlled release pattern for in-vitro and ex-vivo drug release studies
with 52.01 ± 5.71 % for ERM and 87.04 ± 2.44 % for CFE at 48 h whereas ex-vivo release studies illustrated
similar results of 51.08 ± 3.56 % for ERM and 69.2 ± 2.16 % for CFE. The anti-hyperalgesic capability of
microneedles was verified by the acetic acid writhing test, and the non-toxicity of synthetic microneedles was
confirmed by histopathology and serotonin toxicity studies. The novel 3D applicator effectively delivered the
microneedle array into the nasal cavity for systemic action. Therefore, the fabricated rapid dissolving micro­
needles combining two drugs ergotamine and caffeine with use of 3D applicator proved to be a coherent tech­
nique for intranasal delivery of ergotamine in the treatment of migraine.
1. Introduction
Microneedles (MNs) offer an interesting drug transport system for
BCS class II and IV drugs that combine the advantages of non-invasive
transdermal delivery of drugs along with invasive delivery using con­
ventional hypodermic needles (Prausnitz, 2004). MNs are micro-
dimensional needles similar to conventional hypodermic needles ar­
ranged in form of arrays for various interdisciplinary fields to deliver
hydrophilic (e.g. Amoxicillin, fluconazole, sumatriptan, amikacin, zol­
mitriptan, etc.) and lipophilic (e.g. Amphotericin-B, metronidazole,
darunavir, ritonavir, levofloxacin, ciprofloxacin, etc.) drugs including a
diversified range of small and large molecules, proteins, peptides and
vaccines. MNs effectively deliver drugs like insulin for diabetes (Jin
et al., 2018), influenza vaccine for vaccine delivery (Rodgers et al.,
2018), parathyroid hormone as growth hormone (Xie et al., 2017),
doxorubicin and docetaxel for cancer (Ye et al., 2016), methotrexate for
rheumatoid arthritis (Chen et al., 2020; Yao et al., 2019), anti-CGRP
peptide for inflammation (Gupta et al., 2013) and tacrolimus for psori­
asis (Du et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2018; Tekko et al., 2020), across human
skin with minimum pain and tissue damage thus gaining wider patient
acceptance. MNs unlike a transdermal patch penetrate skin by creating
microchannels in the stratum corneum which is main blockade for
transdermal delivery and act with minimal pain and penetration depth
of 80–200 nm without disruption of dermal layer that leads to skin
permeability enhancement and effective delivery of drugs at the target
sites like inflammatory sites or tumor cells (Sabri et al., 2019; Khare,
2021).

Although there is a significant unmet clinical need for a more prac­
tical intranasal drug delivery route, MN technology has yet to be
investigated for nasal administration because of the considerable chal­
lenges that the nose and sinus cavities present to this technology. The
therapeutic dose supplied by microneedles is discovered to be higher
than that of topical formulations; as a result, dose reduction is achiev­
able when utilizing a microneedle nasal delivery system, according to
US patent application US 2013/0085472 A1 (Ngo and Ergotamine,
2022). The nasal mucosa displays as a therapeutically viable alternative
to systemic drug delivery via the olfactory pathway for efficient CNS
transport of drugs in the treatment of Alzheimer’s disease, epilepsy,
brain tumors, trauma, pain control, dizziness, and migraine or other
headache conditions due to its large surface area and highly vascular
nature. An effective way to deliver drugs reaching the olfactory region
through the nasal cavity can be achieved by use of a 3D applicator that
can potentially deliver higher therapeutic efficacy than the topical or
injection technique with ease of usage and minimal side effects. Ergot­
amine, an ergot alkaloid, has been widely used for decades in the
treatment of migraine. However, due to low bioavailability exhibited by
oral (5 %) and rectal routes (17 %) and extensive first-pass metabolism,
a combination of ergotamine with CNS stimulant like caffeine is rec­
ommended for synergistic action (Arnold et al., 2014; Dali and Shende,
2022). Caffeine forms soluble molecular complex with ergotamine
improving its solubility and enhancing its efficacy in migraine treatment
(Ito et al., 2011). Hence the focus of this research work was to fabricate
dissolving microneedles combining ergotamine with caffeine for syn­
ergistic activity using PLGA, polyvinyl alcohol and povidone as biode­
gradable polymeric matrix that degrade completely releasing the
encapsulated drugs offering controlled release rate and drug perme­
ability enhancement. For the management of migraine this study also
proposed to perform the pharmacodynamics and serotonin toxicity
studies and evaluate the anti-migraine potential of fabricated ergota­
mine microneedles using 3D applicator for intranasal administration.
2. Materials and methods
Poly lactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA) (lactide/glycolide ratio 75:25)
was obtained from Evonik, India. Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA; Mw:
85,000–125,000) procured from Spectrum chemicals, USA whereas
polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP K-90; MW: 360,000) from BASF, Germany.
Ergotamine tartrate (ERM) obtained from Teva Czech Industries, Czech
Republic and caffeine (CFE) supplied by Omniactive Health Technolo­
gies, India. Maltose supplied by Molychem Chemicals, Mumbai, India.
Polyethylene glycol (PEG-400, MW: 280–420) was obtained from Merck
(USA). Analytical grade reagents used for all experiments.
2.1. Preparation method for PLGA nanospheres
The nanospheres obtained by employing multiple emulsion
approach i.e., water-in-oil-in-water (w/o/w) using solvent-diffusion-
evaporation process. Initially primary emulsion (w/o) formed that
involved mixing two phases: organic phase and aqueous phase wherein
organic phase comprised of PLGA and ERM dissolved in DCM whereas
aqueous phase constituted of PVA, sodium bicarbonate and CFE dis­
solved in distilled water. For the purpose of creating a primary emulsion,
the organic phase was gradually introduced to the aqueous phase while
being continuously magnetically stirred (Remi, India) at 800 rpm for 15
min. To create multiple emulsion (w/o/w), additional aqueous PVA
solution was added while being stirred at 1200 rpm for 45 min.
The multiple emulsion was then added to distilled water and swirled
gently overnight at room temperature at 600 rpm to allow the DCM to
evaporate. To obtain the nanospheres, the dispersion was centrifuged at
8000 rpm for 30 min at 25oC (Eppendorf, USA). The nanospheres were
then resuspended in distilled water and used for further research. The
composition of formulation is mentioned in Table 1.
2.2. Characterization of PLGA nanospheres
2.2.1. Particle size, ZP and PDI
Malvern Zetasizer from the UK was used to determine the particle
size, ZP, and PDI of the PLGA nanospheres (MNE-01, MNE-02, and MNE-
03). Double distilled water (MilliQ, France) was used to adequately
dilute the formulations MNE-01, MNE-02, and MNE-03, and measure­
ments were made in triplicate.
2.2.2. Entrapment efficiency (%) for PLGA nanospheres
To calculate the percentage of Entrapment efficiency, PLGA nano­
spheres were weighed and introduced to 10 mL of phosphate buffer (PB)
pH 6. Following this, absorbance of aliquots was measured using a
UV–Visible spectrophotometer (Perkin Elmer, USA). The aqueous
formulation dispersions were centrifuged for 30 min at 8000 rpm with a
centrifuge made in Remi, India, and the clear supernatant solution was
filtered through a cellulose membrane filter (Millipore, USA). At
wavelengths of 240 and 273 nm for ERM and CFE, respectively, a
UV–Visible spectrophotometer was used to analyze the filtered solution.

3. Fabrication of microneedles
Microneedles were prepared using a polymer matrix of PVA and
Povidone K-90 and casting the polymer blend loaded with PLGA nano­
spheres in sterile silicon molds. Initially the polymer blend prepared
with PVA and PVP K-90 mixed in a ratio of 50:50 along with maltose in
distilled water to form a clear solution (the polymer matrix constituted
30 % PVA, 30 % PVP K-90 and 10 % maltose). Lastly 0.5 mL of PEG-400
added to above polymer matrix as PEG provides the elasticity to
microneedles. PLGA nanospheres equivalent to 1 mg ERM were mixed
with the polymer matrix and sonicated for 30 min to remove the air
entrapped during stirring method. The 2 × 2 cm2 silicon molds were
carefully filled with this viscous dispersion to prevent air entrapment,
and they were then dried in an oven at 50 ◦C for 45 min. After drying, the
MN array was taken apart, wrapped in aluminium foil, and stored in a
desiccator until further use.
3.1. Characterization of microneedles
3.1.1. SEM analysis
The surface properties of microneedles comprising PLGA nano­
spheres were examined using SEM (Zeiss, Germany). The microneedle
array was mounted on aluminium stubs covered with adhesive tapes and
the surface morphology examined with a beam of electrons.
3.1.2. In-vitro drug release for microneedles
The microneedle formulation was placed on to the glass plates that
were mounted on Franz diffusion cells filled with 20 mL PB pH 6 at 37 ◦C
± 0.5 ◦C. Sample aliquots were withdrawn as per the protocol at pre­
scheduled time points of 30, 60,120, 180, 240, 720 min with last ali­
quots at 24 and 48 h and replenished with same amount of fresh PB pH 6
maintaining the sink conditions. The UV–Visible spectrophotometer
(Perkin Elmer, USA) was used to conduct the in-vitro release investiga­
tion at wavelengths of 240 nm for ERM and 273 nm for CFE.
3.1.3. Ex-vivo release study
Ex-vivo studies for PLGA microneedles were performed in order to
assess the controlled release profile from PLGA nanospheres. Goat nasal
mucosa was obtained from a nearby slaughterhouse in Andheri, Mumbai,
India, for the ex-vivo experiments. The Franz diffusion cell device with the
nasal mucosa placed on it was filled with 20 mL of PB pH 6 and slowly
spun at 300 rpm while the temperature was held at 37◦0.5 ◦C. In order to
maintain sink conditions, aliquots were taken out at predetermined
timepoints of 30, 60, 120, 180, 240, 720 min and final aliquots at 24 and
48 h with replenishment with the same amount of fresh PB. This was done
with the aid of a 3D applicator to introduce the microneedle array into the
excised sample of nasal mucosa. Analysis of the aliquots were carried out
using UV–Visible spectrophotometer (Perkin Elmer, USA) at the wave­
lengths of 240 nm for ERM and 273 nm for CFE.
3.1.4. Attenuated Total Reflection-Fourier transform infrared (ATR-FTIR)
spectroscopy
To evaluate the interactions of drugs and excipients used in the
formulation, infrared spectra of ERM, CFE, PVA, PVP K-90, PLGA and
MN array were examined using an ATR-FTIR spectrophotometer (Perkin
Elmer, USA) with wavelength ranging from 500 to 4000 cm−1 and at a
resolution of 32 cm−1.
3.1.5. Mechanical strength for MNs
The microneedle patch was attached to a cylindrical probe with a
cross-sectional area of 1.5 cm2 attached to the probe of CTX texture. The
measurements were performed by moving vertically downwards at a
speed of 0.1 mm/s. A load of 100 g was applied for 30 s and after
measurements the probe moved upwards. The load vs time graph
determined the hardness in terms of mechanical strength of the micro­
needles. The mechanical strength evaluated based on the low and high
particle content in microneedles.
4. Animal studies
4.1. Pharmacodynamics studies
The pharmacodynamic study was conducted to evaluate the most
significant symptom of migraine i.e., hyperalgesia induced by acetic
acid writhing test. Male Wistar rats weighing 202.4 ± 14.6 g were kept
in an air-conditioned cage system with 5 animals in each group. Prior to
experimentation the rats were adapted to the laboratory conditions for
7 days with free admittance to food and water. Animals were segregated
into 3 groups wherein the first group of animals was applied the
microneedle samples with the help of 3D applicator in both nostrils, the
second group was administered 10 µL of drug solution and the third was
control group receiving 10 µL normal saline solution in both nostrils by
use of micropipette. Post-administration of acetic acid (0.3 % w/v)
intraperitoneal injection, the acetic acid writhing test was used to
evaluate the abdominal constrictions and hind limb stretching that
occurred for 15 min. By counting the number of abdominal contractions
that occurred within 15 min of administering an injection of acetic acid
(0.3 % w/v), anti-hyperalgesic activity was measured.
4.2. Serotonin toxicity studies
Serotonin toxicity is a condition that has the ability to significantly
alter an animal’s behaviour. Serotonin levels that are elevated in the body
cause aberrant neuromuscular function, changes in mental state, and
autonomic hyperactivity. Serotonin levels in the body rise as a result of
taking certain classes of medications, such as serotonin reuptake in­
hibitors (fluoxetine, sertraline, venlafaxine, duloxetine, etc.), anti-
migraine drugs (ergot alkaloids and triptans), MAO inhibitors (phe­
nelzine, selegiline, etc.), and anti-depressants (clomipramine, imipra­
mine, and nortriptyline). In accordance with the authorized protocol from
the Institutional Animal Ethics Committee (IAEC), the goal of this study
was to assess the effect of MNs on animal serotonergic activity. The male
Wistar rats weighing (190–210 g) were divided into three groups (n = 5):
the control group, the ERM-treated group, and the MN-treated group. The
different neuromuscular excitations were quantified as the serotonergic
response after 1 h of nasal administration of test samples, and scores were
based on the presence or absence of behavioral symptoms.

5. Results
5.1. PLGA nanospheres
5.1.1. Particle size (PS), ZP and PDI
PS of PLGA nanospheres (MNEs) studied using Malvern Zetasizer UK,

system with zeta potential of −20.98 ± 5.12 mV that demonstrated good
colloidal stability. See Fig. 1.
The two formulations, MNE-02 and MNE-03, were evaluated for
entrapment efficiency (%) testing whereas MNE-01 was not taken into
consideration for future research based on the data from the particle
size, zeta potential, and PDI measurements.

5.1.4. Morphology of PLGA nanospheres
SEM results in Fig. 4 revealed that the PLGA nanospheres exhibited
spherical dark nanoparticles within narrow range of 280.34 ± 6.61 to
416.0 ± 9.67 nm. The spherical shape of nanospheres was maintained
by PLGA and the dark particles indicated drug entrapment. The narrow
range of particle size attributed to PVA that acted as a stabilizer inter­
acting at the interfacial layer of organic and aqueous phase that reduced
the interfacial tension and prevented coalescence of nanoparticles. PVA
formed a protective cover on the PLGA droplets and prevented aggre­
gation of PLGA nanoparticles stabilizing the emulsion droplets. The
organic phase comprised of PLGA and water immiscible solvent like
DCM with high homogenization speed (800–1200 rpm) for formation of
emulsion led to decreased particle size.
6. Microneedles
6.1. Characterization of microneedles
6.1.1. Morphological analysis of microneedles
The morphological analysis of MN-03 conducted using SEM as shown
in Fig. 5 (a) and (b). The microneedle image showed height of 2.179 mm
and the polymeric MN arrays depicted smooth external surface with
cracks seen on the bulging ends. Cracks were developed due to
desorption of microneedles during SEM analysis owing to the beam of
electrons. The polymer matrix of PVA and Povidone K-90 offered
intermolecular rigidity with better mechanical strength to the MN array.
PVA is a water-soluble synthetic polymer that acts as an emulsifier to
PLGA nanospheres covering the surface of nanospheres and mainte­
nance of smaller droplet size in nanometer range. PVP K-90 is also a
water-miscible synthetic polymer with higher viscosity and acts as a
solubilizer to many poorly soluble drugs like ritonavir, ciprofloxacin and
carbamazepine. The higher viscosity (≈90,000 cps) of both polymers
resulted in slow erosion and controlled delivery of drugs.
6.1.2. % In-vitro release from microneedles
Franz diffusion cell was used for the in-vitro release studies per­
formed on dialysis membrane, and the findings for MN-03 are shown in
Fig. 6. The results indicated controlled release profile for both drugs
ERM and CFE when studied for 48 h with in-vitro release of 52.01 ± 5.71
% for ERM and 87.04 ± 2.44 % for CFE. The controlled release rate was
attributed to polymeric matrix combination like PLGA, PVA and PVP K-
90. PLGA nanospheres underwent slow hydrolytic degradation and
resulted in controlled delivery for 48 h. Further the combination of
polymeric matrix of PVA and PVP K-90 is hydrophilic and exhibited
higher viscosity but released the entrapped drugs slowly in a controlled
manner.
6.1.3. Ex-vivo drug release study

7.4. Pharmacokinetics studies
The pharmacokinetics profile for ERM post-intranasal administration
of MNs are presented in Fig. 13 and the pharmacokinetics parameters
were calculated using PK Solver 2 software (Excel add-ins) and reported
in Table 5. The pharmacokinetics studies revealed concentration for
ERM in brain. According to the graph in Fig. 4, brain absorption
increased from 1.27 g/mL after 30 min to 3.34 g/mL after 60 min before
dropping to the starting level of 1.33 g/mL after 90 min. These findings
showed that the Cmax for ERM was attained within 60 min, and that the
concentration thereafter quickly decreased within 90 min, which was
consistent with previously published studies of ERM (Arnold et al.,
2014). The increased brain uptake (3.64-fold increase) for MNs as
compared to plain ERM was confirmed by MNs, which showed a
consistent rise in the ERM concentration from 1.34 g/mL at 30 min to
2.26 g/mL at 60 min and 4.84 g/mL at 90 min. These results verified that
the encapsulation of ERM in PLGA nanospheres and further fabrication
in polymeric matrix of PVA and PVP K-90 exhibited controlled release of
ERM with enhancement in absorption through the olfactory pathway via
nose-to-brain route. As shown in Table 2, the pharmacokinetics pa­
rameters of Tmax and Cmax and AUC0-t revealed higher values 90 min,
4.843 ± 0.44 µg/mL and 201.045 ± 1.92 µg/mL* min, respectively for
MN-03 as compared to ERM. The increase in values for pharmacoki­
netics parameters attributed to the encapsulation of ERM in PLGA
nanospheres (% EE of 86.21 ± 4.52 %) and to higher viscosity (approx.
1,00,000 cps) of polymeric matrix of PLGA, PVA and PVP K-90. PLGA
resulted in slow hydrolytic degradation coupled with hydrophilic
polymers like PVA and PVP K-90 that showed swelling behavior in
presence of water with slow erosion of the contents.
8. Discussion
Migraine is the 3rd prevalent illness in the world affecting 50 %
adults worldwide resulting in disability and reduced quality life. Hence
an effective migraine treatment is a dire need to mitigate the risk of
disability caused due to migraine attacks. ERM-loaded dissolving MNs
with all these properties were created in order to provide a rapid onset of
action, easy self-administration and increased therapeutic efficacy. The
MNs were fabricated using w/o/w double emulsion method to form

PLGA nanospheres in a base of PVA and PVP K-90 polymeric matrix. The
dissolving MNs constituted of combinational drugs ERM and CFE having
nanoparticle size of 280.34 ± 6.61 nm with good colloidal stability of
−20.98 ± 5.12 mV. The higher entrapment efficiency was attributed to
lipophilic nature of ERM wherein increased concentration of PLGA and
presence of water-immiscible solvent like DCM with PVA as a stabilizer
for primary emulsion prevented particle aggregation and increased %
EE. The controlled release rate of ERM and CFE-loaded MNs was due to
higher viscosity of polymeric matrix of PLGA, PVA and PVP K-90. PLGA
resulted in slow hydrolytic degradation coupled with PVA and PVP K-90
hydrophilic polymers that swell in presence of water leading to slow
erosion of the contents. Because of hydrophilicity of these polymers the
MNs so formed reduce the possibility of biohazardous sharp waste
accumulating in the nasal mucosa after application. These hydrophilic
polymers combine to create swellable hydrogels, which when in contact
with biofluids result in the needle tips being deliberately cracked,
extending their duration within the body and enable controlled drug
delivery up to 48 h. The slow release for ERM from MNs was due to the
entrapment in strong polymeric network of biodegradable PLGA and
PVA that lead to in slow erosion of entrapped drugs. Mechanical
strength and insertion properties were satisfactory for the MNs to result
in good penetration of nasal mucosa for therapeutic efficacy. ERM
exhibited its anti-migraine potential by acting on receptors like 5-HT1B,
5-HT1D, dopamine receptors present on intracranial nerves that induce
constriction of nerves and relief from migraine. The strong hydrophilic
polymeric matrix encapsulating ERM in the MNs demonstrated the
controlled release rate (52.01 ± 5.71 % for ERM) and the reduced
number of abdominal contrictions confirmed anti-hyperalgesic activity
of MNs. Pharmacokinetic studies revealed higher values for the pa­
rameters Tmax, Cmax and AUC0-t as 90 min, 4.843 ± 0.44 µg/mL and
201.045 ± 1.92 µg/mL* min, respectively studied that confirmed
constant presence in brain with 3.64-fold increase in brain uptake. The
serotonin toxicity results demonstrated the absence of response to
behavioural symptoms and hence considered to safe for intranasal
administration. Drugs that exhibit serotonergic symptoms work by
various mechanisms like.
1. Increased serotonin synthesis – dietary supplements L-trytophan
2. Increased serotonin release – Opiods and Antidepressants
3. Activation of serotonergic receptors – anti-migraine drugs, Opiods
and Antidepressants
4. Serotonin reuptake inhibitors – SNRIs, SSRIs and Tricyclic
antidepressants
5. Inhibitors of serotonin metabolism – MAOIs, Triptans, Anxiolytics
Based on the above-listed drugs, this study was conducted for
ergotamine fabricated MNs to evaluate the impact of formulation on the
animals. Research findings concluded the similarity in manifestations
for rodents and humans and hence this animal study results can be
extrapolated to humans in order to mitigate the potential risk of sero­
tonin syndrome in humans. The serotonin toxicity studies demonstrated
for MN-treated group elicited absence of response and the results were
similar to control group that indicated MNs constituting ERM do not
exhibit any serotonin-induced toxicity. ERM falls under the category to
activate the serotonergic receptors eliciting the responses like neuro­
mucular excitations but the studies for fabricated MNs illustrated
absence of response. When compared to control and ERM-treated
groups, the histological examination of brain tissues indicated normal
nucleated cells with no abnormalities and no toxicity. Hence the fabri­
cated ERM and CFE-loaded MNs were found to be efficacious alternative
treatment for migraine with use of 3D applicator for effective delivery of
MNs for intranasal administration.

applicator for better management of migraine owing to the painless,
effective and safe route for drug delivery. These pain-free systems
fabricated as dissolving MNs entrapped in polymeric matrices of PVA
and PVP offered synergistic action of ERM and CFE coupled with
