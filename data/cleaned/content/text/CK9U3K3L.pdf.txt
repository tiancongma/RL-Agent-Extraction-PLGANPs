a b s t r a c t
Topical administration of minoxidil (MXD) on the skin is a widely used method for the treatment of hair
loss. To enhance the efﬁcacy of MXD in human skin, the appendageal route through the hair follicle could
be promising. Therefore, we developed MXD-loaded double emulsion nanoparticles (MXD-DE-NPs) using
poly (D,L-lactide-co-glycolide; PLGA) and surfactants. The size of MXD-DE-NPs was 137.8 ± 0.814 nm and
their encapsulation efﬁciency of MXD was approximately 43%. The MXD-DE-NPs showed no aggregation
in aqueous conditions at 1% w/v MXD concentration without ethanol and propylene glycol. In human fol-
licular dermal papilla cells, the NPs showed fast cellular uptake and no signiﬁcant toxicity. After treat-
ment on the skin of shaved mice, efﬁcient skin penetration of the NPs could be observed on
ﬂuorescence imaging. NP treatment for three weeks resulted in efﬁcient regrowth of hair in mice without
ethanol and propylene glycol similar to the traditional MXD formula. These results demonstrated that
MXD-DE-NPs are a promising carrier for MXD in the treatment of hair loss.

Introduction
Alopecia is abnormal hair loss due to various reasons including
severe stress, genetic disorders, and nutritional imbalance [1]. The
frequency of alopecia is increasing worldwide; thus, the relevant
market is growing every year [2], and in particular, the recent
interest regarding appearance further contributes to this concern.
The only two medical treatments approved by the FDA for alopecia
proven to improve hair loss are ﬁnasteride and minoxidil (MXD).
Finasteride is a 5-alpha reductase inhibitor and mainly used in
tablet form for oral administration. The drug is effective; however,
side effects, such as impotence, were reported in some patients [3].
Conversely, MXD is an antihypertensive vasodilator and generally
used as a topical treatment on the skin [4].
Recently, a clinical study in which the effect of applying 5%
MXD to the skin was compared with oral administration of 1 mg
MXD for female-pattern hair loss patients was published [5]. When
comparing the hair density 24 weeks after drug treatment, the
orally administered MXD group showed a better outcome than
topical administration, and side effects were not observed except
for hypertrichosis. The result is notable considering the efﬁcacy
of local delivery is generally superior to systemic delivery. In topi-
cal delivery through the skin, human skin is thicker than mouse
skin used in disease animal models and skin permeation experi-
ments. Because human skin is very thick, the results of mouse
experiments may not represent the results of clinical trials [6]. In
human skin, the hair follicles are actively generated and localized
in deep areas from the epidermis to the subcutaneous tissue.
Therefore, to efﬁciently deliver drugs past the thick epidermal
layer of human skin, an alternative strategy would be to optimize
the appendageal route through the hair follicle rather than the
transcellular route through the existing tonic solution of ethanol
and glycerol [7].
Recently, nanoparticles (NPs) have attracted the attention of
biomedical researchers as potential drug carriers [8]. Various NPs
have been developed for drug delivery in numerous diseases [9].
In particular, NPs showed promising data in transdermal drug
delivery resulting in efﬁcient skin penetration mainly through hair

Herein, we developed double emulsion (DE) type NPs with poly

glycol. The formula was optimized and the size and shape of the
resulting MXD-loaded DE-NPs (MXD-DE-NPs) were analyzed. Flu-
orescence microscopy was used to observe the cellular uptake in
human follicular dermal papilla cells. Next, the skin penetration
of the NPs was observed using real-time imaging and histology
after application on the skin of shaved mice. Finally, the effect of
the DE-NPs on hair growth was tested in the same mice models
and compared with the MXD formula with ethanol and propylene
glycol.

Preparation of MXD-DE-NPs
First, 30 mg Pluronic F-127 and 30 mg PLGA were dissolved in
1 mL DCM (oil phase). Then, 70 mg MXD was dissolved in 1 mL
aqueous solution (distilled water, DW:ethanol = 1:1 containing
3% acetic acid (v/v)) as the ﬁrst water phase. The second water
phase (5 ml) used 2% glycerol (v/v in DW) containing PVA 2.5%
(w/v), NaCl 0.5% (w/v), and emulsan 0.24% (w/v). The ﬁrst water
phase was introduced to the oil phase and emulsiﬁed using a probe
sonicator (VC505, Sonics and Materials, Newtown, CT, USA) at 100
watts for 90 seconds in an ice bath. Then, the water-in-oil (W/O)
emulsion was added into the second water phase and emulsiﬁed
by sonication at 200 watts for 90 seconds in an ice bath. The DE
solution was stirred at 65 C for one hour in a fume hood for
DCM evaporation. Next, the solution was subjected to dialysis with
a 14-kDa dialysis bag in DW for one hour to remove the unloaded
drugs and obtain the MXD-DE-NPs. For the control, empty NPs
were similarly prepared without MXD. For imaging, NPs were pre-
pared using 1 mg sulforhodamine B (Rho B), a hydrophilic dye,
instead of MXD. NPs were lyophilized using a freeze dryer from
ilSinBioBase (Korea) and resuspended when used.

Cellular uptake imaging
Human hair follicle dermal papilla cells (Promocell, Heidelberg,
Germany, used at passage 3) were seeded at 3  104 cells/well in
confocal dishes with DMEM high glucose serum medium contain-
ing 10% FBS and 1% antibiotic–antimycotic solution. After 3 days,
the cells were washed with DPBS and then treated with free Rho
B or Rho B-DE-NPs, both at 8 ug/mL Rho B. The treatment time
and temperature applied varied among samples. Then, after wash-
ing with DPBS, the cell nuclei were stained with Hoechst 33342 for
20 minutes and washed again with DPBS. Finally, cells in phenol
red-free medium containing 2% FBS were observed using confocal
laser scanning microscope (LSM800, Carl Zeiss, Germany).
Transdermal uptake in C57BL/6 mice
All mouse experiments in this study were approved by the Sam-
sung Medical Center Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee
(Approval code: 20210617001) and conducted according to the
AAALAC International Guidelines and the United States Animal
Welfare Act.
Male C57BL/6 mice (7-week-old, 20–22 g) purchased from Ori-
ent BIO (Seoul, Korea) were treated after acclimatization to labora-
tory conditions for 1 week. Mice were fed with standard mouse
food and water and stayed in a controlled environment with 12-
hour light/dark cycles, temperature, and 35–60% humidity. After
one week adaptive period, 8-week-old mice were used for
experiments.
Using isoﬂurane to maintain respiratory anesthesia, the dorsal
area (approximately 2 cm in width and 4 cm in length) of mice
was shaved with hair clippers.
RHO solution (25 ug in 200 uL) was applied to the shaved
mouse dorsal area 1 hour and 5 hours before IVIS measurement.
IVIS was observed for each group and the related optical index
(ROI) value of the 1-hour image was adjusted to 1. Thereafter, mice
were euthanized using CO2 asphyxiation and the skin was excised,
embedded in OCT, and frozen for histological study.
Anagen induction assay in C57BL/6 mice
The mice were randomly divided into four groups (9 male mice
each). All mice were anesthetized with isoﬂurane. The dorsal area
(approximately 2 cm in width and 4 cm in length) of mice was
shaved with hair clippers. Animals in the sham group did not
receive any treatment except for hair removal. To prepare the solu-
tion for the positive control group, MXD was dissolved in a mixture
of propylene glycol (10%, v/v) and ethanol (30%, v/v) to a content of
1% (w/w). For the 1% MXD-DE-NP group, MXD was dissolved by
adding DW to obtain 1% MXD. For the empty group, the same
amount of NPs was used as in the MXD-DE-NP group except
MXD was excluded. Equal quantities of prepared solutions (200
uL) were topically applied to the shaved area of mice once a day
for 21 days. Then, mice were euthanized using CO2 asphyxiation
and the skin was excised and ﬁxed in 10% formalin solution for his-
tological study.
Ex vivo porcine skin permeability test
The whole skin of a pig with fat removed was purchased in size
of 1 cm  1 cm (Apures co., Gyeonggi-do, South Korea). We put
1 ml of 1xPBS and paper in a 60 mm cell culture dish, put the epi-
dermis of porcine skin on it. We applied RHO solution (5 ug in 50
ul) to it, and wrapped it with foil while separating each sample.
They were placed in an incubator at 37 C for 1, 5, and 24 hours.
After that, the treated tissues were cut and embedded in OCT so
that the middle part was facing down to make a frozen block. After
cryo-sectioning, the tissue slide was observed using a confocal
microscope image.
Results
Development and characterization of MXD-DE-NPs
MXD-DE-NPs were developed using water-in-oil-in-water (W/
O/W) solvent evaporation method (Scheme 1b) [14]. The ﬁrst
water phase containing MXD was added to an oil phase containing
Pluronic F-127 and PLGA, and then emulsiﬁed to prepare a W/O
emulsion. Then, the emulsion was added into the outer second
water phase containing PVA, emulsan, and NaCl, resulting in W/
O/W DE-NPs after sonication. The ratio of each component was
experimentally optimized as shown in Table 1 to achieve small
size, narrow size distribution, and EE. The sizes of empty DE-NPs
and MXD-DE-NPs were 124.1 ± 1.343 and 137.8 ± 0.814 nm,
respectively, showing similar sizes after drug loading (Fig. 1a). Both
NPs showed spherical morphology in TEM images. In particular,
similar to a previous report, acetic acid was used to prevent aggre-
gation and increase EE of MXD [15] (Table 2 and Fig. 1b). When
MXD was dissolved with a solvent including acetic acid during for-
mulation, the EE was 43%; however, without acetic acid, the EE was
only 25% and large aggregates of the unloaded MXD could be
observed with the naked eye. These data showed that MXD could
be stably prepared in water without using ethanol and propylene
glycol, and the MXD-DE-NPs did not aggregate at 1% w/v MXD con-
centration. After loading, MXD was gradually released from MXD-
DE-NPs over time in aqueous conditions (Fig. 1c). In addition,
MXD-DE-NPs showed similar sizes after freeze-drying and resus-
pension, indicating possibility of stable storage in the dried form
(Fig. 1d).
Cellular uptake and viability test of MXD-DE-NPs in dermal papilla
cells
Human hair follicle dermal papilla cells were used to test the
cellular uptake of the NPs. For ﬂuorescence imaging, sulforho-
damine B (Rho B) was loaded in DE-NPs instead of MXD resulting
in Rho B-DE-NPs, and their cellular uptake was observed using con-
focal microscopy. Rho B-DE-NPs showed more efﬁcient cellular
uptake than free Rho B without NPs (Fig. 2a). The mean ﬂuores-
cence intensity of Rho B-DE-NP-treated cells was approximately
1.76-fold higher than in free Rho B-treated cells (Fig. 2b). Notably,
the cellular uptake mainly decreased at 4 C showing the uptake of
NPs was based on an energy-dependent process such as endocyto-
sis [16]. In time-dependent studies, Rho B-DE-NPs showed high
ﬂuorescence intensity from 1 hour, and the intensity gap between
the cells treated with free Rho B and Rho B-DE-NPs increased over
time (Fig. 2c and 2d). The viability of dermal papilla cells was eval-
uated using the CCK-8 assay after treatment in the three groups
(empty DE-NPs, MXD, and MXD-DE-NPs) for 48 hours (Fig. 2e).
The cell viability in all groups did not decrease compared with
untreated control cells showing the DE-NPs had negligible toxicity
which may be because materials reported and regarded as non-
toxic were used during formulation. Furthermore, the viability of
MXD-DE-NP-treated cells increased more than of control cells
and the difference was statistically signiﬁcant. The results showed
that MXD-DE-NPs may have enhanced cell proliferation.

and anagen phase in mouse hair follicles and the growth rate of
hair, C57BL/6 mice experiments were performed. Statistically sig-

group and the untreated sham group. However, the 1% MXD dis-

Ex vivo penetration test of Rho B-DE-NP in porcine skin
In order to evaluate the skin penetration behavior of DE-NPs in
thicker skin than mice, an ex vivo pig skin permeability study was
performed (Fig. 5a). Samples were collected 1, 5, and 24 hours after
skin application. Porcine skin is a tissue with a poor distribution of
hair follicles, and we obtained images around hair follicles. In the
case of free Rho B dissolved in 30% ethanol and 10% propylene gly-
col, there was no image of the ﬂuorescent dye moving through the
appendageal route or dyeing the hair follicles at any time (Fig. 5b,
upper panel). On the other hand, the Rho B-DE-NP-treated group
showed weak ﬂuorescence around the hair follicles located in the
epidermis from 5 hours after treatment, and it could be clearly
observed that the sample entered the hair shaft via appendageal
route at 24 hours (Fig. 5b, lower panel). We could observe many
hair follicles in the dermis were stained (Fig. 5c).
Discussion
In hydrophilic ﬂuorescent dyes, water cannot pass through the
skin barrier but when incorporated into NPs, the dyes can effec-
tively penetrate the skin barrier. However, NPs with incorporated
hydrophilic dye were inferior in permeability to ethanol or propy-
lene glycol, which physically open the skin barrier [17,18]. NPs are
a method of delivering drugs by widening the gap between cells or
directly opening the cell membrane. Conversely, in the case of NPs,
the skin penetration mainly ﬂows into the hair shaft and delivers
the drug directly to the hair follicles [15,19]. Although the perme-
ability is lower than with ethanol and propylene glycol combina-
tion, the anagen ratio, number of hair follicles, and hair growth
level appear to be similar.
Based on observation of hair growth in the animal model, 1%
MXD-DE-NPs showed 3.3-fold difference in hair growth score, 4-
fold change in anagen ratio, and 2-fold greater number of hair fol-
licles compared with the sham and empty groups; however, the 1%
MXD group did not show statistical difference from the tonic solu-
tion dissolved in 30% ethanol and 10% propylene glycol group.
The MXD-DE-NPs we designed enabled a stable formulation of
MXD and efﬁcient delivery without ethanol and propylene glycol.

and hair growth effect was only similar to but did not outperform
commercialized formulas. The NPs can be further improved
because they showed more efﬁcient penetration through the skin
than the free form of drugs. Reportedly, the skin penetration of
NPs varies based on their components [20]. The MXD-DE-NPs in
the present study were optimized to have a small size and high
EE; however, the procedure was based on in vitro data. Therefore,
we postulate that deeper penetration of NPs can be achieved in
future studies if further optimization is performed based on animal
data.
The mouse skin used in the skin permeability study was about
300 lm thick from the epidermis to the dermis, and the thickness
of the porcine skin was 2–3 mm, about ten folds thicker. It might
be the reason that DE-NP showed intense ﬂuorescent signals in
hair follicles after 5 hours in mice experiments, but it was delayed
in porcine skin. Even though there is a difference between ex vivo
and in vivo experiments, we could observe that the arrival time of
the NPs to hair follicles has a strong relationship with the thickness
of the skin. The porcine skin test showed delivery through hair fol-
licles is important particularly in thick skins of pig and maybe
human because extensive penetration of thick skin is very difﬁcult.
In addition, our NP showed an excellent penetration effect using
the appendageal route but cannot penetrate the skin other than
hair follicles. Therefore, for effective drug delivery through the skin
using NPs, it will be important to rationally control the delivery
and drug release pattern in consideration of the thickness of the
skin and the density of hair follicles in the target area. Furthermore,
additional studies using other NPs such as ethosomes and transfer-
somes that were reported for efﬁcient penetration will be also
helpful [20,21].
Conclusion
In summary, MXD-DE-NPs were developed for efﬁcient delivery
of MXD for the treatment of hair loss. The MXD-DE-NPs were com-
posed of PLGA, PVA, Pluronic F-127, and emulsan, and formulated
