Enhancing Ocular Drug Delivery: The Effect of Physicochemical
Properties of Nanoparticles on the Mechanism of Their Uptake by
Human Cornea Epithelial Cells

ABSTRACT: This study investigates the effect of nanoparticle size and surface
chemistry on interactions of the nanoparticles with human cornea epithelial cells
(HCECs). Poly(lactic-co-glycolic) acid (PLGA) nanoparticles were synthesized
using the emulsion-solvent evaporation method and surface modified with
mucoadhesive (alginate [ALG] and chitosan [CHS]) and mucopenetrative
(polyethylene glycol [PEG]) polymers. Particles were found to be monodisperse
(polydispersity index (PDI) below 0.2), spherical, and with size and zeta potential
ranging from 100 to 250 nm and from −25 to +15 mV, respectively. Evaluation of
cytotoxicity with the 3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-diphenyl-2H-tetrazolium
bromide (MTT) assay indicated that incubating cells with nanoparticles for 24 h
at concentrations up to 100 μg/mL caused only mild toxicity (70−100% cell
viability). Cellular uptake studies were conducted using an in vitro model
developed with a monolayer of HCECs integrated with simulated mucosal
solution. Evaluation of nanoparticle uptake revealed that energy-dependent
endocytosis is the primary uptake mechanism. Among the different nanoparticles studied, 100 nm PLGA NPs and PEG-PLGA-150
NPs showed the highest levels of uptake by HCECs. Additionally, uptake studies in the presence of various inhibitors suggested that
macropinocytosis and caveolae-mediated endocytosis are the dominant pathways. While clathrin-mediated endocytosis was found to
also be partially responsible for nanoparticle uptake, phagocytosis did not play a role within the studied ranges of size and surface
chemistries. These important findings could lead to improved nanoparticle-based formulations that could improve therapies for
ocular diseases.
KEYWORDS: ocular drug delivery, ocular barriers, in vitro cornea model, polymeric nanoparticles, PLGA nanoparticles, cellular uptake,
endocytosis
■INTRODUCTION
Increased suffering from various eye diseases is a major
concern for ophthalmologists and ocular drug-delivery
scientists.1 While topically applied ophthalmic formulations
are popular and makeup the majority of marketed products,2,3
they suffer from low bioavailability due to a short residence
time on the surface of the eye and poor penetration into ocular
tissues. As shown in Figure 1, significant barriers that prevent
entry of topical formulations into the eye include the tear film
and cornea.4,5
The tear film is a complex fluid consisting of a superficial
lipid layer and a mucoaqueous layer that has increasing mucin
concentration down toward the epithelial tissue.6 Its barrier
properties arise from the heterogeneous mesh of mucus,
physicochemical entrapment of foreign entities by functional
groups on the mucin, and dynamic clearance by continuous
secretion and shedding of mucus components at the mucosal
surface.7−9 The cornea tissue is also a significant barrier
consisting of five tissue layers with varying physiochemical
properties.10 Although the epithelium layer is only 10% of the
total cornea thickness, it accounts for 90% of the corneal
barrier function. The stratified epithelial layer itself is
composed of arrays of different types of epithelial cells that
are tightly packed using junctional complexes at different
depths.11−13
Efforts to enhance bioavailability of topical formulations
include applying viscosity enhancers,14 penetration en-
hancers,15 ointments,16 prodrugs,17 and in situ gels18 that
increase the drug bioavailability by improving solubility and
precorneal residence time. However, these formulations can

tems have shown the ability to overcome ocular barriers by
prolonging retention time on the ocular surface, improving
ocular penetration, sustaining drug release, enhancing target-
ing, lowering ocular irritation, reducing degradation of unstable
drugs, and improving bioavailability.21−23 Over the past
decades, various types of polymeric nanoparticles have
attracted interest for ocular drug-delivery applications24 with
polylactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA) nanoparticles among the
most extensively explored. The unique physicochemical
properties of PLGA allow formation of nanoparticles with
wide ranges of size, surface properties, and shape25,26 and serve
as a promising platform for treatment of ocular disease.25
Studies support that they are well tolerated by the eye,27 have
high uptake and permeability in exterior ocular tissues,28 and
provide controlled and sustained release of various pay-
loads.29−33
Although several studies have explored nanoparticle
formulations for topical ocular drug delivery and reported
that nanoparticles with wide ranges of physicochemical
properties can enter the cornea cells, there is still a knowledge
gap in our understanding of how their physicochemical
properties, including size and surface properties, affect their
interactions with the corneal barrier. Understanding the
mechanisms of nanoparticle uptake by cornea cells may
allow researchers to tune the physicochemical properties of
nanoparticles for specific applications. The aim of this work
was to address this gap.
Nanoparticle permeation through cellular barriers occurs
through three different pathways: paracellular, intracellular,
and transcellular pathways. This study focused specifically on
the transcellular pathway or endocytosis. We developed an in
vitro model of the ocular surface barrier by integrating a tear
film-mucosal barrier with human corneal epithelial cells for
evaluation of nanoparticle uptake. The in vitro model was
employed to explore the uptake and mechanism of uptake of
PLGA NPs as a function of the temperature and time. In
addition, pharmacological endocytosis inhibitors were applied
to probe the dominant pathways for uptake of nanoparticles.

Preparation of PLGA Nanoparticles. Various sizes of
Coumarin-6 loaded PLGA nanoparticles (NP) were synthesized
using the oil-in-water emulsion-solvent evaporation method.34 To
prepare the oil phase, predetermined amounts of PLGA were
dissolved in 0.5 mL of organic solvent containing 5 μg of C6 per 1
mg of PLGA polymer. This oil solution was then added to an aqueous
PVA solution (the surfactant) and sonicated with a probe tip
sonicator (Qsonica, USA) to form a nanoemulsion. An ice bath was
used to keep the solution below the PLGA glass transition
temperature while sonication was continued until the desired energy
input to the system was reached. The nanoemulsion was then poured
into a round-bottom flask containing PVA aqueous solution, and the
system stirred at 600 rpm at room temperature for 3 h to effect
evaporation of the organic solvent and hardening of PLGA
nanoparticles. Nanoparticles were then collected by centrifuging for
30 min at 14,800 rpm and then washed with DI water (3 times) for
removal of excess PVA. The nanoparticles were then dispersed in
1.5% w/v aqueous solution of trehalose, a cryoprotectant, and
lyophilized before storing at −80 °C for further use.
Surface Modification of Nanoparticles. The effect of surface
chemistry was determined using 150 nm nanoparticles modified with
alginate (ALG), chitosan (CHS), or polyethylene glycol (PEG).
Modification with ALG and CHS was performed during particle
synthesis with addition of 3 mL of aqueous solution of the surface
modifier (3% w/v) after partial hardening of the C6 loaded PLGA
NPs. The system was given 2 h for coating of the PLGA nanoparticles
with the shell polymers and full hardening of the NPs. Nanoparticle
collection, washing, and lyophilization were done according to steps
described for the bare PLGA nanoparticles.
Surface modification with PEG was performed using EDC/NHS
coupling chemistry on previously made 150 nm C6 loaded PLGA
nanoparticles. Briefly, 1 mg of lyophilized nanoparticles were washed
by suspension in deionized water and then centrifuged at 15,200 rpm
for 30 min to remove trehalose. The particles were then suspended in
an aqueous solution of EDC/NHS at a molar ratio of 1:1 (50 mg/mL
EDC, 37 mg/mL NHS) by sonication followed by 30 min rotation on
an Argos RotoFlex system. The particles were then centrifuged and
washed with deionized water for removal of any excess EDC and
NHS. Activated NPs were resuspended and reacted with an aqueous
solution of mPEG-NH2 at a concentration of 1 mg/mL for 1 h. NPs
were centrifuged and washed with deionized water to remove
unreacted PEG and then suspended in a trehalose solution,
lyophilized, and stored at −80 °C for further use.
Characterization of Nanoparticles. Nanoparticle hydrodynamic
diameter (HD), polydispersity index (PDI), and zeta potential (ZP)
were determined by dynamic light scattering (DLS) and laser Doppler
electrophoresis (LDE) using a Zetasizer Nano ZS (Malvern
Instruments, Malvern, U.K.). Measurements were taken for dispersion
of the nanoparticles in purified deionized water (ELGA Purelab Flex).
Immediately after synthesis or surface modification of nanoparticles,
10 μL samples were taken from washed nanoparticles and suspended
in 1 mL of purified deionized water and sonicated for 20 s at 20%
amplitude prior to DLS measurement. Reported values of HD, PDI,
and ZP are the mean ± SD of three independent samples.
Morphological examination of nanoparticles was performed using
scanning electron microscopy (SEM, Zeiss EVO 50, Carl Zeiss
Meditec, Oberkochen, Germany) operating at a voltage of 20 kV. To
prepare SEM samples, a freshly synthesized nanoparticle suspension
was diluted (1:100) and 10 μL of the diluted suspension pipetted on a
glass coverslip which was attached with double-sided carbon tape to
an aluminum stub. Samples were air-dried overnight and then gold-
coated with a sputter coating device (EMS 550x, Carl Zeiss Meditec,
Oberkochen, Germany) to improve electrical conductivity.
Evaluation of NP Stability in Simulated Ocular Solution.
Colloidal stability of NPs in simulated ocular solution was evaluated
by monitoring changes in nanoparticle HD and PDI using DLS. The
simulated ocular solution consisted of cornea cell-culture media
(KSFM) supplemented with BPE, rEGF, insulin, and hydrocortisone
to make the complete growth medium and type II porcine stomach
mucin (MUC) at a concentration of 100 μg/mL to mimic the
mucosal component of the tear film. Lyophilized nanoparticles were
washed with deionized water and centrifuged to remove trehalose
prior to being added to the simulated ocular solution at a
concentration of 100 μg/mL. Particles were sonicated at 20%
amplitude for 1−2 min for complete suspension in the ocular
biological solution, and they were incubated at 37 °C for 24 h.
Nanoparticle suspensions were analyzed with DLS at various time
intervals of up to 24 h to observe changes in HD and PDI.
Cell Culture. Immortalized human cornea epithelial cells
(HCECs; CRL-11516, ATCC, USA) were used to develop an in
vitro cell-based model. Cells were cultivated according to protocol
provided by ATCC. Briefly, cells were cultured in keratinocyte-serum
free medium (KSFM) supplemented with 5 ng/mL human
recombinant EGF (rEGF), 0.05 mg/mL bovine pituitary extract
(BPE), 0.005 mg/mL insulin, and 500 ng/mL hydrocortisone. A
humidified incubator with 5% CO2 at 37 °C was used, with the
culture medium renewed every 2−3 days until the cells reached 80%
confluency. To improve cell adherence, all cell culture flasks and well
plates were coated with a mixture of fibronectin (0.01 mg/mL),
bovine serum albumin (0.01 mg/mL), and bovine collagen type I
(0.03 mg/mL) in EMEM, incubated at 37 °C, and kept at 4 °C until
use.
Nanoparticle Cytotoxicity. In vitro toxicity of NP suspension, in
simulated ocular solution (MUC/KSFM) using HCECs, was
determined using the colorimetric 3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-
diphenyltetrazolium bromide (MTT) assay.35 Cells in KSFM were
seeded into precoated 96-well plates (Greiner Bio-One, 655180) at a
seeding density of 2 × 104 cells/well, and the plates were then
incubated at 37 °C for 24 h. Next, the culture media (KSFM) in each
well was replaced with 200 μL MUC/KSFM containing 100 μg/mL
of porcine stomach mucin and different concentrations of each group
of nanoparticles: 0, 0.1, 0.5, 1, 5, 10, 50, 100, 500, and 1000 μg/mL.
Positive controls (100% viability) consisted of wells containing cells
and only 200 μL MUC/KSFM (untreated condition). The plates
were incubated at 37 °C for 24 h, after which the supernatant was
removed, cells were washed twice with PBS, followed by addition of
200 μL culture media in each well. To perform the assay, 50 μL filter
sterilized MTT reagent (2 mg/mL in PBS) was added to each well,
and the plates were then incubated at 37 °C for 4 h. At the end of the
incubation period, the culture media was removed from each well, and
the purple crystals of formazan remaining in the wells solubilized by
adding 100 μL DMSO. Following mixing on a plate shaker for 5 min
to complete dissolution of formazan crystals, the absorbance was
measured spectrophotometrically using a SpectraMax i3 spectropho-
tometer (Molecular Devices, USA) plate reader at 570 nm.
Percentage of cell viability was calculated as the absorbance of
samples relative to the absorbance of untreated cells. The experiment
was performed in triplicate for each condition.
Quantitative Uptake of Nanoparticles by HCECs. Quantita-
tive uptake of nanoparticles by HCECs was evaluated using
fluorescence spectroscopy. Cells were seeded into clear 96-well plates
(Greiner Bio-One, 655180) at a seeding density of 3 × 104 cells/well
and the plates were incubated at 37 °C for 24 h. The culture media in
each well was replaced with MUC/KSFM containing 100 μg/mL
mucin and 100 μg/mL NPs, and the plates were incubated at 37 °C
for 20, 40, and 60 min. At the end of the incubation time, NP
suspensions were removed and cells were washed three times with
PBS and then lysed with Triton X-100/PBS solution (0.25% v/v).
The lysate was analyzed to quantify NP uptake by fluorescent
measurement at λex = 475 and λem = 515 nm using a SpectraMax i3
(Molecular Devices, USA) plate reader. The experiments were
performed in triplicate wells under each condition.

uptake of bare and surface-modified PLGA NPs by HCECs was
performed by cell staining and visualization of the uptake by
fluorescence microscopy following a short exposure time. Cells were

Determination of Mechanisms of Nanoparticle Uptake. To
determine the endocytic pathways for uptake of NPs by HCECs,
different pharmacological inhibitors were employed individually and
in combination to block single or multiple endocytic pathways. Cells
were seeded into precoated clear 96-well plates (Greiner Bio-One,
655180) at a seeding density of 5 × 104 cells/well, and plates were
incubated at 37 °C for 24 h to allow cell equilibration and attachment
to the bottom of wells. Prior to exposure to NPs, cells were
preincubated with optimized concentrations of nystatin (324 μM),
dynasore (120 μM), rottlerin (12 μM), and/or cytochalasin D (60
μM) for 5 min. Then, in the presence of the inhibitors, 100 μg/mL
bare PLGA NPs (100, 150, 200, and 250 nm) or surface-modified
PLGA NPs (PLGA/ALG, PLGA/CHS, and PLGA/PEG NPs) were
added to the cells, and the plates were incubated at 37 °C for 20 min.
Following the incubation, MUC/KSFM containing nanoparticles and
inhibitors were removed, and cells were washed thrice with PBS, and
lysed with Triton X-100/PBS solution (0.1% v/v) for protein analysis.
NP uptake was measured by fluorescence measurement at λex = 475
nm and λem = 515 nm using a SpectraMax i3 (Molecular Devices,
USA) plate reader. In the control group, NPs were applied to cells
without inhibitor treatment. The uptake percentages of samples with
inhibitor treatments were denoted relative to the control. After
fluorescence measurement, protein content of the lysates was
quantified by a Pierce detergent compatible Bradford assay kit. The
experiment was performed in triplicate wells for each condition, and
the uptake percentage was normalized based on the overall protein
amount per well.
■RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
Characterization of Nanoparticles. HD, PDI, and ZP.
Emulsion-solvent evaporation is a well-established method for
preparation of PLGA NPs,34 and several approaches have been
developed to control nanoparticle size and surface charge.35,36
To facilitate the detection of nanoparticles in this study, C6
fluorescent dye was loaded into the nanoparticles with
encapsulation efficiencies of 97.6 ± 0.3, 98.4 ± 0.3, 98.9 ±
0.9, and 99.9 ± 0.1% for 100, 150, 200, and 250 nm PLGA
NPs, respectively. In this work, nanoparticle size was
controlled by varying the sonication energy used for emulsion
formation, the surfactant concentration, and the used organic
solvent, while zeta potential was tuned by addition of alginate,
chitosan, or PEG. Nanoparticles used in this study ranged in
size from 100 to 250 nm, zeta potential from −25 to +15 mV,
and all PDI values were at 0.2 or smaller, as shown in Table 1
and Figure 2. The differences in sizes and zeta potentials
between 100, 150, 200, and 250 nm PLGA NPs were all
statistically significant (p-value < 0.05). Additionally, the zeta
potentials of PLGA-150, ALG-PLGA-150, CHS-PLGA 150,
and PEG-PLGA-150 were also significantly different (p-value <
0.05). As shown in Table 1, bare PLGA NPs are negatively
charged; likely due to the presence of terminal carboxyl groups
in the PLGA polymer. Surface modification of 150 nm bare
PLGA NPs with alginate, chitosan, and polyethylene glycol,
shifted the zeta potential toward more negative, positive, and
neutral values, respectively, due to the polymers respective
anionic, cationic, and non-ionic nature.
The rationale behind the selection of this size and zeta
potential range was to fabricate nanoparticles with phys-
icochemical properties that are applicable for topical ocular
drug delivery. The majority of nanoparticles studied for topical
ocular drug delivery have sizes between 200 and 250 nm.32 In
addition, mucoadhesion and mucopenetration are notable
strategies that have been explored to overcome the ocular
surface barrier.37 Therefore, ALG38 and CHS39 as mucoadhe-
sive polymers and PEG37 as mucopenetrative polymer were
selected for surface modification of bare PLGA NPs to create a
wide range of zeta potential.
Nanoparticle Morphology. SEM was used to characterize
the morphology of the bare PLGA NPs. As seen in
representative SEM images in Figure 3, the PLGA NPs
showed a spherical shape with smooth surface morphology.
The nanoparticles were also observed to have good size
uniformity, in agreement with PDI values determined by DLS.
Colloidal Stability of Nanoparticles in Simulated Ocular
Solution. Stability of the nanoparticles within the ocular
environment is an important factor in determining the practical
applicability of the formulation. Upon contact with physio-
logical fluids, nanoparticles may interact with a variety of
biomolecules and undergo aggregation,40 which can increase
particle size and alter diffusion and sedimentation velocities
affecting the results of in vitro studies. The colloidal stability of
nanoparticles suspended in MUC-KSFM was studied by
recording the size and PDI at various time intervals over a
period of 24 h. As shown in Figure 4, all nanoparticles retained
their initial size for the first 8 h and then particle aggregation
led to some increase in size and PDI. Particles with negative
and neutral surface charge showed a relatively low percentage
increase in size (4.7 ± 5.0%) over 24 h. Positively charged
CHS-PLGA-150 nanoparticles, however, showed a 52.4 ±
3.0% increase in size, which is assumed to be due to
electrostatic interactions with negatively charged proteins in
the simulated ocular solution.
Cytotoxicity Studies. In vitro toxicity studies were
conducted using a confluent monolayer of HCECs supple-
mented with a simulated mucosal solution to resemble the
ocular condition. Supplementation of cell-based models with
artificial mucus solution is a well-established method in the
literature to mimic epithelial surfaces with a mucosal layer.41
MUC5AC has been measured to be an abundant mucin in the
tear film, with concentrations of 0−200 μg/mL porcine
stomach mucin equivalent in tears of healthy eyes.42 In this
study, type II mucin from the porcine stomach was utilized for
supplementation of the in vitro HCEC model. As commercial

medium.48 Additional studies are required to determine the
exact mechanism of this induced toxicity. Since all groups
showed greater than 70% viability at NP concentrations up to

Quantitative Uptake of Nanoparticles by HCECs. To
investigate the effect of physicochemical properties on cellular
uptake of NPs, HCECs were incubated with nanoparticles for
20, 40, and 60 min at 37 °C. Results shown in Figure 6 are
calculated as the ratio of the uptake fluorescent intensity
relative to the total fluorescent intensity of nanoparticles
initially added to the cells. According to Figure 6A, the
majority of nanoparticle uptake occurred during the first 20
min of incubation with the plateau possibly occurring due to
uptake saturation of the cells.49 According to 20 min results
shown in Figure 6B, the 100 nm PLGA NPs had significantly
higher uptake than the other bare NPs and there is no
significant difference between the uptake of 150, 200, and 250
nm particles (p-value > 0.05). Furthermore, among the ∼150
nm particles with different surface chemistries, PEG-PLGA

was increased for particles that were more neutral in charge,
regardless of the surface chemistry. The observed behavior
could be due to the influences of surface charge on cell
membrane interactions and particle stability. The more
negatively charged particles were observed to be stable in the
simulated ocular fluid but likely had unfavorable interactions
with the negatively charged cell membranesreducing overall
uptake compared to more neutral particles. While greater
cellular interactions would be expected of the highly positive
CHS-PLGA-150 NPs, these showed significant aggregation in
the simulated ocular fluid, which would lead to increase in
particle size and decrease in total surface area availableboth
of which could negatively impact cellular uptake.

specific endocytosis pathways. It has been reported that
blocking one uptake pathway can result in activation of other
endocytic mechanisms which can yield misleading outcomes.60
Therefore, to ensure experimental accuracy, inhibitors were
used in combination to better understand the contribution of
each pathway. A series of toxicity experiments were first
conducted to find a balance between sufficient inhibitory
concentrations/exposure time and minimal effects on cell
viability. The cells were exposed to inhibitors for a total period
of 30 min (10 min preincubation with inhibitors followed by
20 min of incubation with NPs). In this study, 15 different
mixtures of inhibitors were used, as listed in Table 3 with their
respective blocked pathways. Regarding the activity of each
inhibitor, it should be mentioned that caveolae-mediated
endocytosis (CVME) is a cholesterol-dependent process and
nystatin can decompose cholesterol, thus inhibit caveolae/
lipid-mediated endocytosis.61 Dynamin is a GTPase protein
that is essential for membrane fission during clathrin-mediated
endocytosis (CME) in eukaryotic cells. Dynasore is a GTPase
inhibitor that rapidly and reversibly inhibits dynamin activity
and therefore prevents endocytosis.62 Rottlerin modifies actin
distribution, which leads to inhibition of macropinocytosis.63
Finally, cytochalasin D inhibits the endocytosis process
through inhibition of actin polymerization which is necessary
for phagocytosis.64
Figure 9 shows the uptake results as a percentage of the
control group for which no inhibitor was used. According to
the results, rottlerin (ROT) and nystatin (NYS) had the
greatest effects on nanoparticle uptake. Compared to the
control group where cells were not exposed to any inhibitor,
treatment of the cells with ROT reduced the uptake level to
7.1 ± 2.6, 10.7 ± 1.2, 4.1 ± 1.7, 5.5 ± 1.5, 7.8 ± 0.7, 10.7 ±
1.2, and 13.7 ± 7.3 for PLGA-100, PLGA-150, PLGA-200,
PLGA-250, ALG-PLGA-150, CHS-PLGA-150, and PEG-

decreased the uptake level to 6.0 ± 3.1, 9.8 ± 1.5, 7.9 ± 3.2,
19.5 ± 21.1, 16.0 ± 8.3, 13.4 ± 2.4, and 34.3 ± 28.1 for PLGA-
100, PLGA-150, PLGA-200, PLGA-250, ALG-PLGA-150,
CHS-PLGA-150, and PEG-PLGA-150 NPs, respectively.
These findings suggest that macropinocytosis (MPS) and
CVME are the two dominant mechanisms responsible for
uptake of PLGA NPs in this study and blocking these two
pathways has a significant reduction in the uptake level (p-
value < 0.05). The results also indicate that CME is partially
responsible for the uptake of NPs as treatment with dynasore
(DYN) decreases the uptake level to 39.7 ± 8.3, 72.0 ± 13.9,
39.3 ± 11.4, 58.2 ± 6.7, 76.2 ± 26.7, 54.8 ± 11.4, and 55.3 ±
35.6 for PLGA-100, PLGA-150, PLGA-200, PLGA-250, ALG-
PLGA-150, CHS-PLGA-150, and PEG-PLGA-150 NPs,
respectively, compared to the control groups. Although the
effect of DYN treatment on uptake inhibition is less than the
effect of ROT and NYS treatment, but the reduction in the
uptake level after treatment with DYN is also statistically
significant (p-value <0.05). Treatment of the cells with
cytochalasin D (CYT) showed no decrease in uptake,
indicating that phagocytosis (PHG) does not play a role in
the uptake of nanoparticles within the range of physicochem-
ical properties that was studied. Previous studies also suggested
that indomethacin nanoparticles are taken up by HCECs
through CVME, CME, and MP,59 and liposomal formulation is
taken up by CVME and CME.60
When cells were treated with a combination of inhibitors, a
size-dependent trend on the uptake inhibition in CME and MP
pathways was observed, suggesting that these two pathways are
more favorable for larger NPs. In addition, by comparing the
condition where only PHG is activated with the last group
where all pathways are blocked and assuming PHG does not
affect the uptake, a similar size-dependent trend was also
observed, which suggests the possibility of other size-
dependent mechanisms that was not considered in our studies.
To understand the effect of surface charge on uptake
mechanisms, cellular uptake with only one active pathway (i.e.,
use of 3 inhibitors) as a function of zeta potential was studied.
As shown in Figure 10, a peak in the uptake by macro-
pinocytosis (MP) was observed close to neutral zeta potential
which suggests that this pathway, which is a nonspecific
pathway, favors neutral NPs. Uptake of nanoparticles through
CVME, on the other hand, does not seem to be charge-
dependent, possibly due to the caveolae receptor being located
toward the interior surface of the cellular membrane.65,66
Therefore, it is hypothesized that particle−receptor electro-
static interactions do not govern the interactions, and other
types of interactions may be involved; further investigations are
needed to evaluate this hypothesis. Uptake through CME
seems to have a peak close to −20 mV, which may indicate
that electrostatic interactions are somehow involved with
clathrin, which is a positively charged receptor.67 It should be
noted, however, that ALG-PLGA NPs showed lower uptake
than less negatively charged bare PLGA, possibly indicating the
role of other interactions as well. The other two conditions,
with PHG active and with all four pathways blocked, showed
maximum uptake at the −22 mV zeta potential.

that the optimal size for increased uptake by human corneal
endothelial cells is about 100 nm. However, it was observed
