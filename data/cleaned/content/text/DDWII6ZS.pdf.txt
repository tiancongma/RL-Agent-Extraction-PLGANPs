Folate-decorated PLGA nanoparticles as a rationally
designed vehicle for the oral delivery of insulin
Diabetes mellitus represents a group of syn­
dromes, typified by hyperglycemia, resulting
from impairment of insulin secretion, defects in
insulin action or both. Insulin is a protein hor­
mone, which plays a key role in regulating the
carbohydrate and fat metabolism in our body. As
insulin deficiency is a major cause of diabetes mel­
litus, exogenous insulin is used medically to treat
patients with certain forms of this disease. The
current regimen of insulin therapy involves mul­
tiple, daily subcutaneous (sc.) injections, which
not only causes uncontrolled hypoglycemia but
also a threatening punishment to diabetic patients
with poor compliance. Moreover, the peripheral
hyperinsulinemia caused by subcutaneously
administered insulin, further aggravates patients’
conditions in the form of certain renal and cardio­
vascular complications [1]. In order to circumvent
the problems and side effects associated with sc.
insulin, several alternative delivery routes such as
pulmonary, nasal, transdermal, buccal and oral
routes have been investigated. However, none of
these delivery routes are free from limitations. For
instance, the pulmonary route of delivery is chal­
lenged with problems related to poor bioavailabil­
ity (BA), high dose and lower cost–effectiveness
[2,3]. Similarly, transdermally delivered insulin
fails to transfer an adequate concentration of the
drug across the skin barrier [1].
The oral route is considered to be the most
convenient and patient-compliant route of drug
administration [4]. Oral delivery stands to be an
attractive approach for the mitigation of pain
caused by sc. administration of insulin and
thereby, offers an effective means of circum­
venting many of the problems associated with
conventional insulin therapy. Oral delivery of
insulin can radically improve the glucose homeo­
stasis by mimicking the physiological pattern of
insulin release, which involves initial passage
and utilization of 50% of insulin through liver
(a major organ involved in glucose homeosta­
sis), followed by peripheral action [5–7]. However,
the oral delivery of insulin seems to be beyond
reach because of its high propensity to undergo
chemoenzymatic degradation under the hostile
environment of the GI tract and poor intesti­
nal permeability [1,2,7]. An array of nontargeted
novel nanocarrier-based approaches have been
investigated for the oral delivery of insulin so as
to evade the enzymatic and permeability barri­
ers of the GI tract. These include, and are not
limited to, liposomes [8,9], polymeric nanopar­
ticles (NPs) [7,10–16] and solid lipid NPs [17]. It is
well documented that polylactide-co-glycolide
(PLGA) is a biocompatible, bio­degradable, US
FDA approved and safely administered polymer
employed for in vivo applications. The PLGA
Aims: The present study reports a novel approach for enhancing the oral absorption and hypoglycemic
activity of insulin via encapsulation in folate-(FA) coupled polyethylene glycol (PEG)ylated polylactide-co-
glycolide (PLGA) nanoparticles (NPs; FA-PEG-PLGA NPs). Materials & methods: Insulin-loaded FA-PEG-PLGA
NPs (size ~260 nm; insulin loading ~6.5% [w/w]; encapsulation efficiency: 87.0 ± 1.92%) were prepared
by double-emulsion solvent evaporation method. The bioavailability and hypoglycemic activity of orally
administered FA–insulin NPs were studied in diabetic rats. Results & conclusion: FA-PEG-PLGA NPs (50  U/kg)
exhibited a twofold increase in the oral bioavailability (double hypoglycemia) without any hypoglycemic
shock as compared to subcutaneously administered standard insulin solution. Insulin NPs maintained a
continual blood glucose level for 24 h, which, however, was transient (<8 h) in the case of subcutaneous
insulin and associated with severe hypoglycemic shock. Overall, we have developed a patient-compliant,
oral nanoformulation of insulin, once-daily administration of which would be sufficient to control diabetes
for at least 24 h.

Ligand-mediated active targeting has emerged
as a novel armamentarium for improving the
site specificity of many therapeutic agents to
their pathological site. As a targeting moiety,
folic acid (FA) has shown immense promise in
the tumor-specific delivery of a variety of anti­
cancer agents [21]. However, the strategy has
seldom been explored for the oral delivery of
bioactives. As for the oral delivery of insulin, it
would be particularly useful if the protein could
be encapsulated within a biocompatible nano­
system, surface modified with ligands, whose
corresponding receptors are expressed on the
GI tract. Such ligand-decorated nanocarriers
were thought to improve the oral absorption
of insulin through some specialized, receptor-
mediated endocytic pathway. A similar attempt
was made by Chalasani and coworkers, wherein
vitamin B12-conjugated dextran NPs were used
for enhancing the oral absorption of insulin via
a cobalamin (vitamin B12)-mediated pathway
in the rat intestine [22,23]. Furthermore, some
recent reports have embarked on the feasibility of
using folate (FA) conjugates for improved uptake
and transport of certain bioactives or vesicular
systems across the GI tract [24,25]. According to
these reports, reckonable levels of FA receptors
are also expressed by the GI tract so that intesti­
nal absorption of FA is mediated through a spe­
cialized, acidic pH-dependent carrier-mediated
process, which involves the reduced FA carrier
as well as proton-coupled FA transporter/HCP1.
Encouraged by these reports, we sought to
explore the feasibility of using FA-decorated
PEGylated PLGA NPs for enhancing the oral
absorption of insulin, while avoiding premature
degradation of the protein under the hostile GI
tract environment [26]. In line with that ratio­
nale, insulin-loaded FA-conjugated PEG-PLGA
NPs (FA–insulin NPs) were prepared via double-
emulsion, solvent evaporation method and freeze-
dried. In addition to extensive physicochemical
characterizations, the synthesized NPs were also
analyzed for their chemical and conformational
stability, as well as long-term storage stability.
Finally, in the course of extensive pharmacody­
namic (PD) and pharmacokinetic (PK) studies
in diabetic rats, we demonstrated the successful
oral delivery of insulin through folic acid con­
jugated nano­particles. A meaningful PK–PD
correlation was also established.
Materials & methods

 Materials
PLGA 50:50 (inherent viscosity 0.4  dl/g
in chloro­form at 25°C) was procured from
Boehringer (Ingelheim, Germany). FA was
purchased from Sisco Research Lab. Pvt. Ltd.,
(Mumbai, India). Bovine pancreas insulin,
N-hydroxysuccinimide, PEG–bis-amine (molec­
ular weight: 3000 Da), polyvinyl alcohol (molec­
ular weight: 30,000–70,000 Da), sodium azide,
streptozotocine, trehalose and triethyl amine
were purchased from Sigma Aldrich (MO, USA).
Trifluoroacetic acid, pancreatin and pepsin were
obtained from Loba Chemie (Mumbai, India).
Ethyl acetate (lab research grade), acetonitrile
(HPLC grade) and methanol (HPLC grade) were
purchased from JT Baker, (NJ, USA). Ultrapure
water (SG water purification system, Barsbuttel,
Germany) was used for all the experiments. All
other reagents used were of analytical grade.

 Synthesis of polymers & their
characterization
Amine-terminated PEGylated PLGA (NH2-
PEG-PLGA) and FA-conjugated PEGylated
PLGA were synthesized using carbodiimide
chemistry as previously described in the literature
with necessary modifications [21,27–30]. Figure 1 is
a schematic representation of the different steps
involved in the synthesis of polymers.

 Synthesis of the amine-terminated
diblock copolymer
For the preparation of the amine-terminated
diblock copolymer (NH2-PEG-PLGA), PLGA
was activated using standard carbodiimide
chemistry, as described in earlier reports [21,27–30].
Thereafter, activated PLGA (1 g, 0.0294 mmol),
dissolved in anhydrous di­chloromethane
(DCM; 8 ml) was added dropwise to five-times
molar excess of PEG–bis-amine dissolved in
2 ml of anhydrous DCM. A catalytic amount
(100 µl) of anhydrous triethylamine was added
to the mixture with gentle stirring. The reac­
tion was carried out at room temperature (RT)
for 8 h under N2 atmosphere. The reaction was

 Characterization of synthesized
polymers
The conjugation of FA on PLGA was preliminar­
ily studied using UV and further authenticated via
Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) and nuclear
magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy.

 UV analysis
The extent of FA conjugation (percentage
molar conjugation) on NH2-PEG-PLGA was
evaluated by UV spectroscopic analysis (BioTek
Instruments, VT, USA). A standard calibration
curve was prepared by recording the absorbance
of serially diluted standard FA samples in DMSO
at 358 nm (FA e = 8643.5 M−1cm−1). The amount
of FA conjugated in a polymer was determined
by recording the absorbance of PLGA-PEG-FA
at 358 nm.

 FTIR analysis
For FTIR analysis, PLGA, NH2-PEG-PLGA
and FA-PEG-PLGA were dissolved in DCM.
Subsequently, neat samples were analyzed upon
evaporation of DCM by FTIR (Nicolet Impact
410, Perkin Elmer Instrument, MA, USA).

 NMR analysis
For NMR analysis, PLGA, NH2-PEG-PLGA
and FA-PEG-PLGA were dissolved in deuterated
DMSO. 1H-NMR analyses were carried out in
a Bruker DRX 400 MHz NMR spectrometer
(Billerica, MA, USA).

 Preparation of insulin-loaded
PLGA NPs
Insulin-loaded PLGA NPs were prepared by
the previously reported double emulsion solvent
evaporation method, with slight modification [31].
Briefly, the stock solution (8 mg/ml) of insulin
was prepared by dissolving in 0.01 M HCl con­
taining 0.1% weight/volume (w/v) sodium azide.
Insulin solution (0.5 ml; according to 5, 7.5 or
10% weight/weight [w/w] theoretical loading) was
added dropwise to PLGA solution in ethyl acetate
(5 ml) over a period of 2 min under magnetic stir­
ring at 1200 rpm. The mixture was then homog­
enized (PT 4000, Polytron, Lucerne, Switzerland)
at 10,000 rpm for 2 min to obtain the primary
emulsion which was then added dropwise to 2%
(w/w) polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) solution (10 ml)
over a period of 10 min under continuous stirring
followed by homogenization at 12,500 rpm for
5 min to obtain a double emulsion.. The entire
process was carried out in an ice bath. The second­
ary emulsion was poured into 30 ml water and
kept under magnetic stirring at RT until com­
plete evaporation of ethyl acetate. Finally, NPs
were separated and washed by centrifugation at
12,500 rpm for 10 min. The supernatant obtained
was analyzed by a validated reverse phase (RP)-
HPLC method [32,33] to determine the amount of
unentrapped insulin and the pellet was redispersed
in water and centrifuged again. The same washing
cycle was repeated twice.

 Optimization of process variables
Effect of homogenization speed
Selection of an optimal homogenization speed
was necessary to achieve optimum particle size,
polydispersity index (PDI) and encapsulation
efficiency. PLGA NPs were prepared at differ­
ent homogenization speeds (10,000, 12,500
and 15,000  rpm), keeping the other experi­
mental parameters, such as theoretical load­
ing 5%, homogenization time 5 min and 2%
PVA concentration, constant. Particle size and
the PDI of PLGA NP dispersion was measured
using z-sizer (Nano ZS, Malvern Instruments,
Worcestershire, UK).
Effect of homogenization time
For optimization of homogenization time,
PLGA NPs were prepared at previously opti­
mized homogenization speed (i.e., 12,500 rpm
for 4, 5 and 6 min) and its effect on particle size,
PDI and encapsulation efficiency was studied.
Effect of theoretical drug loading
Finally, PLGA NPs were prepared at differ­
ent insulin loading (i.e., 5, 7.5 or 10% w/w
of polymer) and its effect on particle size and
encapsulation efficiency was studied. The other
experimental parameters such as homogeniza­
tion speed (12,500 rpm), homogenization time
(5 min) and stabilizer concentration (2% PVA)
were kept constant.

NH2-PEG-PLGA & FA-PEG-PLGA NPs
Insulin-loaded NH2-PEG-PLGA and FA-PEG-
PLGA NPs (insulin NPs) were prepared using
the same protocol and with the same optimized
parameters as described above for the preparation
of insulin-loaded PLGA NPs.


 Freeze drying studies
All insulin NPs were freeze-dried using previ­
ously developed and patented stepwise freeze
drying process [101]. Briefly, 2 ml of the NP
suspension was filled into glass vials. Trehalose
was used as a cryoprotectant at different con­
centration (2.5, 5, 7.5 and 10% w/v) and its
concentration was optimized in terms of size,
PDI, reconstitution time and Sf:Si ratio. The
freeze-dried samples were resuspended in water
and evaluated for size and PDI.

 In vitro release studies
All nanoformulations were characterized for
their in vitro release profile under different pH
conditions that prevail in different body fluids
(pH 1.2 in stomach, pH 6.8 in intestine and
pH 7.4 in blood) [34]. Briefly, 20 mg of freeze-
dried NP formulations were dispersed in 10 ml
of acidic media (pH 1.2; USP XXVII) and
incubated at 37 ± 1°C for 2 h under in a shaker
water bath operated at 100 rpm. At the prede­
termined time intervals, 0.5 ml of sample was
withdrawn and NPs were pelletized by centrifu­
gation at 12,500 rpm for 10 min. The pellet was
dispersed in 0.5 ml fresh medium and added
back in the release medium. The clear superna­
tant was employed for determination of insulin
concentration. After 2 h the NPs were trans­
ferred to a 20 ml phosphate buffer of pH 6.8
and incubated at 37 ± 1°C for a further 4 h
under continuous shaking at 100 rpm. Samples
were collected at regular intervals and replaced
by fresh media, as described above. Finally, after
4 h, NPs were transferred to phosphate buffer
pH 7.4 and periodic sampling was carried for
the next 18 h. All the collected samples were
analyzed by the validated RP-HPLC method
to determine the amount of insulin released.

the different polymeric NPs. Briefly, equal

into individual wells of 4–20% polyacryl­

Conformational stability
The conformational integrity of insulin encap­
sulated in various nanoformulations was deter­
mined using far UV circular dichroism (CD)
spectroscopy (J-815, JASCO, Tokyo, Japan).
Briefly, standard insulin solution and insulin

software.
Storage stability of freeze-dried
formulations
Freeze-dried NPs were assessed for their stabil­
ity at 4°C and at RT over a period of 3 months
according to the protocols reported in the litera­
ture [36]. Briefly, insulin-loaded freeze-dried NPs
were transferred to 5 ml glass vials, sealed with
plastic caps and were kept in a stability cham­
ber at 4°C and RT (25 ± 2°C, relative humid­
ity 50 ± 5%). The different formulations were
monitored for change in particle size, PDI and
physical appearance.
Stability in simulated gastric fluid &
simulated intestinal fluid
The stability of different insulin NPs was
measured in simulated gastric fluid (SGF) and
simulated intestinal fluid (SIF) was determined
according to the protocols reported in the litera­
ture [4]. Briefly, freeze-dried insulin-loaded NP
formulations and standard insulin solution were
incubated with SGF (pH 1.2) USP XXVIII (with
enzymes) and SIF (pH 6.8) USP XXVIII (with
enzymes). After 2 h, pepsin in SGF was inacti­
vated by adding 0.5 M NaOH while pancreatin
in SIF was inactivated by adding 0.5 M HCl.
Nanoparticle dispersions were then centrifuged
and the supernatant was analyzed for insulin
content by RP-HPLC, as described earlier. The
insulin was also extracted from the pellet by dis­
solving the pellet in 1 ml acetonitrile:0.5 M HCl
(9:1 v/v) mixture followed by extracting insu­
lin into the 2 ml 0.5 M HCl. Subsequently, the
mixture was centrifuged to remove precipitated
PLGA and insulin concentration in the super­
natant was analyzed by the RP-HPLC method.
The amount of insulin recovered (percentage)
upon treatment with SGF and SIF was obtained
by comparing these with the insulin recovered
from the NPs untreated with SGF and SIF.

 In vivo studies
Animals
Adult male Sprague–Dawley rats (250 ± 30 g)
procured from the central animal facility of the
National Institute of Pharmaceutical Education
& Research (Punjab, India), were used for PD
and PK evaluation of insulin NPs. Animals were
housed in plastic cages under standard labora­
tory conditions with 12 h light/12 h dark cycle
and free access to standard rodent dry diet and
water ad libitum. All experimental protocols
were duly approved by Institutional Animal
Ethics Committee (IAEC) of the National
Institute of Pharmaceutical Education &
Research (Approval no. IAEC/09/63). All the
experiments were performed in accordance with
guidelines of the Committee for the Purpose of
Control and Supervision of Experiments on
Animals, India.
Induction of diabetes
Diabetes was induced in animals by intra­
peritoneal administration of streptozotocin
(55 mg/kg) dissolved in ice cold 10 mmol citrate
buffer at pH 4.4 (for preserving its conforma­
tional integrity) just before administration.
Subsequently, animals were housed for 1 week
under standard laboratory conditions. Glucose
levels in the animals were estimated and only
the animals with blood glucose levels above
250 mg/dl were considered as diabetic and used
in this study [37].
Antidiabetic activity

24 h during the experiment; but were allowed
water ad libitum and fed thereafter. Animals
were divided inro eight different groups.

OCH3-PEG-PLGA and FA-PEG-PLGA NPs
were orally administered to the first four
groups of animals in doses equivalent to 50
IU/kg of insulin The fifth group was orally
administered with standard insulin solution

the structure of FA (Figure 3C).

 In vitro release study
As already stated, insulin is highly prone to deg­
radation in the presence of gastric and intestinal
enzymes. Hence, for a complete understanding
of the in vitro release profile of insulin from
NPs, avoiding enzymatic intervention was one
of the essential prerequisites. To mimic the
ideal physio­logical situation, insulin release
from NPs over different time points was deter­
mined at various pH conditions (gastric pH
1.2 from 0–2 h, intestinal pH 6.8 from 2–6 h
and plasma pH 7.4 from 6–24 h); however,
keeping this in mind, the intrinsic proclivity
of insulin towards chemo­enzymatic degrada­
tion, enzyme-free release media was used. As
evident from Figure 7, all three nanoformulations
(i.e., plain PLGA NPs, NH2-PEG-PLGA NPs
and FA-PEG-PLGA NPs), presented a similar
type of release profile, that is, an initial burst
release, followed by a sustained release pattern.
Quantification of drug release showed that
plain PLGA, PEGylated PLGA and FA-PEG-
PLGA NPs presented an initial burst release
of 43 ± 2.65%, 37 ± 2.86% and 40 ± 2.46%,
respectively, followed by a cumulative sus­
tained release of 60 ± 2.85%, 52 ± 3.51% and
56 ± 3.17%, respectively at the end of 24 h.
After 24 h, surface-modified NPs (NH2-PEG-
PLGA and FA-PEG-PLGA NPs) showed less
insulin release compared with the insulin-
loaded PLGA NPs. The cumulative drug release
was fitted into different release models namely
zero order, first order, Higuchi’s square root
plot and Hixson Crowell cube root plot and
the model giving a correlation coefficient close
to unity was considered as order of release. As
illustrated in Table 6, after initial burst release,
NPs followed Higuchi’s square root pattern of
release which involved diffusion alone during
the initial phase, followed by combined diffu­
sion and degradation in the later phases. To
further check the accuracy of the drug release
data, the release profile was cross-checked
using the dialysis method. Not surprisingly, the
results were comparable with those obtained via
centrifugation method.

All insulin NPs were evaluated for their oral

Insulin NPs (e.g., PLGA, NH2-PEG-PLGA,
FA-PEG-PLGA) were prepared by a double
emulsion solvent methods which is consid­
ered to be one of the most suitable method for
encapsulation of hydrophilic drugs, proteins
and peptides within nanopolymeric matrices
[39,40]. During NP preparation, ethyl acetate,
rather than DCM, was employed as a solvent
for polymer. The lower hydrophobicity of ethyl
acetate relative to DCM was thought to exert
a lesser impact on the chemical and conforma­
tional stability of the protein during nanoen­
capsulation [41]. The ratio of the internal aque­
ous phase to external organic phase was kept at
1:10 v/v so as to obtain very small size droplets
of primary emulsion which finally resulted in
NPs with smaller size and higher encapsula­
tion efficiency. PVA, used as a surfactant in
the external aqueous phase, was found to form
a layer at the interface, between organic and
external second aqueous phase thus prevent­
ing aggregation of NPs during evaporation of
ethyl acetate [34]. The nanoparticle prepara­
tion process was optimized in terms of dif­
ferent process variables such as homogeniza­
tion speed, homogenization time, droplet size
and percentage of theoretical drug loading.
Homogenization was carried out at different
speeds (10,000, 12,500 and 15,000 rpm) for
5  min. Reduction in the particles size was
observed when homogenization speed was
increased from 10,000 to 12,500  rpm due
to the input of high shear, which led to the
formation of smaller size droplets. On fur­
ther increasing the homogenization speed to
15,000 rpm, the size of NPs decreased but
the encapsulation efficiency declined due to
the formation of smaller droplets, which may
have resulted in increased interfacial surface
area and subsequent outward diffusion of
insulin from the inner aqueous phase to the
outer aqueous phase. Considering these facts, a
homogenization speed of 12,500 rpm was con­
sidered to be optimum and chosen for further
studies. The homogenization period for the
formation of NPs is also a critical parameter
for controlling the particle size, encapsulation
efficiency and insulin stability [4]. In gen­
eral, an increase in homogenization period is
accompanied with a reduction in particle size;

To further comprehend how the surface
modification of PLGA NPs influenced their
oral absorption, all NPs were evaluated for their
oral hypoglycemic activity. Oral administration
of pure insulin solution revealed no hypoglyce­
mic activity because of its intrinsic proclivity
towards enzymatic degradation as well as poor
permeability across the GI tract [7]. An increase
in the blood glucose level was observed in the
animals belonging to the diabetic control group
(Figure  11). Interestingly, orally administered
insulin NP formulations exhibited significant
hypoglycemic response. Encapsulation of insulin
within a nano-PLGA matrix not only prevented
premature degradation of the protein/drug but
concomitantly enhanced its absorption in the
GI tract via specialized mechanisms. It is well
reported by different research groups, including
ours, that polymeric NPs are absorbed via a spe­
cialized region in the GI tract, namely M-cells
in Peyer’s patches [4,39,46]. The increased hypo­
glycemic response of insulin NPs with respect
to standard insulin was thus attributed to their
specific absorption via the M-cells. In this con­
nection, it is noteworthy to mention that hypo­
glycemic activity of NH2-PEG-PLGA NPs was
significantly lower when compared with their
pristine PLGA counterpart. The observed differ­
ence was seemingly a consequence of aggregation
of NH2-PEG-PLGA NPs in the GI tract and/or
nonspecific targeting of amine groups, associated
with the PLGA core. As evident from particle
characterization data, the z-potential of NH2-
PEG-PLGA NPs (-3.37 ± 3.42 mV) was too low
to afford any electrostatic stabilization to the
synthesized NPs. As a result, NH2-PEG-PLGA
NPs showed severe agglomeration and sub­
sequent deterioration in particle size, when
exposed to gastrointestinal fluids, namely SGF
and SIF. In addition to the aggregation problem,
it is also feasible that the highly reactive surface
amine group binds nonspecifically at any site
other than the GI tract and gets stuck there.
To revalidate our concept, a control formulation
based on PLGA-PEG-OCH3 (molecular weight
~39,000 D) NPs was synthesized and admin­
istered orally in diabetic rats. Similar to NH2-
PEG-PLGA NPs, CCH3-PEG-insulin NPs also
exhibited a z-potential near to neutral and aggre­
gation in SGF and SIF. Nevertheless, hypoglyce­
mic activity of rats treated with insulin-loaded
PLGA-PEG-OCH3 NPs was considerably higher
than their amine-terminated counterparts sug­
gesting that oral absorption of NP-encapsulated
drugs critically depend on the nature of their
surface functionality. Although NH2-PEG-
PLGA NPs showed lower activity compared with
its CCH3-terminated counterpart, amine func­
tionalization of PLGA was crucial to realize the
successful introduction of FA into the polymeric
backbone. Among all the treatment groups, rats
orally administered with FA–insulin NPs exhib­
ited the highest hypoglycemic response. The
results clearly indicate that integration of FA
with PEG-PLGA played a key role in augment­
ing the oral absorption of nanocarriers in the
GI tract through a FA receptor-mediated endo­
cytic pathway. To prove the concept, the same
FA–insulin NPs were orally administered into a
separate group of animals, pretreated with excess
of folate ,and antidiabetic activity was tested as
described previously. As shown in Figure 11, hypo­
glycemic response of FA–insulin NPs in animals
pretreated with free FA was almost comparable
to those treated with plain PLGA NPs (Figure 11).
These results clearly corroborate that FA–insulin
NPs developed during the course of the study
are absorbed in the GI tract via FA receptor-
mediated endocytosis. FA-pretreatment blocked
the endocytic pathway that was originally
involved in the absorption of FA–insulin NPs
by the GI tract and thus negatively influenced
the NP-induced hypoglycemia.

The blood glucose level, as depicted in Figure 11,
often falls below the basal level, thereby leading
to undesired nephrotoxicity as well as cardio­
toxicity. Furthermore, hypoglycemia induced by
sc. administered insulin is only transient and is

To further establish a correlation between the
hypoglycemic activity of insulin NPs and actual
plasma concentration of insulin, the plasma
insulin levels of the various treated groups were
determined. Due to their site-specific absorp­
tion in the GI tract, polymeric NPs showed a
steady increase followed by a decrease in con­
centration of insulin over a period of 24  h
(Figure  12). Among all the nanoformulations,
FA–insulin NPs demonstrated maximum Cmax
(26.78 ± 3.66 µIU/ml) and subsequently, the
highest oral BA (19.62 ± 1.68%). The results
were encouraging, when compared with previ­
ously reported literature values. For example,
hypromellose phthalate-coated PLGA NPs
(20 IU/kg) exhibited a BAR of 6.27 ± 0.42%
[47]; self-assembled NPs of polyglutamic acid
and chitosan (30  IU/kg) demonstrated a
BAR of 15.1 ± 0.9% [13]. Similarly, a BAR of
13.21 ± 1.42% was demonstrated with a blend
of NPs made of polycaprolactone and Eudragit®
(100 IU/kg; Evonik Industries, Essen, Germany)
[15]. Thus, orally administered FA-PEG-PLGA
NPs demonstrates two-times higher AUC and
cumulative hypoglycemia, when compared
with the sc. administered free insulin solution.
Subsequently, a meaningful correlation could
be established between the PK and PD activ­
ity of FA–insulin NPs. As illustrated by PD as
well as PK studies, FA-PEG-PLGA NPs revealed
the highest efficacy among all the formulations.
These results unambiguously corroborate that
FA conjugation significantly impact the oral
absorption of NPs. Considering all results, we
propose a dual mechanism of absorption of
NPs that involves absorption through M-cells
as well as FA transporters in the intestinal wall.

illustrate the mechanism of NP absorption.
Conclusion
In conclusion, we have successfully exploited
FA-coupled PEGylated PLGA NPs for enhanc­
ing the oral BA of insulin via synergistic absorp­
tion through M-cells as well as FA transporters
in the GI tract. Integration of FA with PEG-
PLGA enhances the oral absorption of nanocar­
riers through FA receptor-mediated endocytosis.
The formulation exhibited prolonged, controlled
hypoglycemia for at least 24 h and could out­
wit many of the drawbacks (e.g., transient
hypoglycemia with severe hypoglycemic shock)
that are prevalent with the currently available
therapy of sc. administered insulin injection.
Furthermore, the freeze-dried FA-PEG-PLGA
NPs can be formulated as capsules, a single
dose of which would be sufficient to control the
blood glucose level for at least 24 h. Currently,
no oral formulation of insulin is available in the
market. Only the sc. injections are available for
the treatment of diabetes mellitus. Repetitive sc.
injections, however, result in patient incompli­
ance. Under such a scenario, encapsulation of
insulin within FA-PEG-PLGA NPs pave the way
towards an easy-to-administer, patient-friendly
oral formulation that can combat diabetes with
improved therapeutic profile as compared with
the commercially available sc. insulin injection.
Future perspective
In the current scientific panorama, as oral deliv­
ery of insulin is beyond reach to the scientific
community, the current findings may inaugu­
rate a new direction for delivering insulin orally
while enhancing its BA. Although the present
work is preliminary, yet the same offers a plat­
form technology for making feasible the oral
delivery of insulin exploiting the carrier property
of NPs (in terms of protecting insulin in harsh
GI condition) and their FA-mediated transport
of NP across the gastrointestinal membrane.
The technology can also be extended to other
delicate protein- and peptide-based biomolecules
(e.g., vaccine antigens). The polymer used in the
present formulation (PLGA) is very costly and
can be replaced with some cheaper alternatives
to further reduce the cost of formulation.
