## Materials Methods
2.1. Bacterial cultures and molecular confirmation
Aeromonas hydrophila strain (A. hydrophila Aero B COFM), a diseased fish isolate, was retrieved from the institute repository maintained at -80 degC in an ultra-low freezer (PHCbi, Japan). The culture was activated by inoculating in nutrient broth and incubating at 30 degC for 24 h, then subculturing on nutrient agar and incubating at 30 degC for 24 h. Genus-specific confirmation was done by PCR for target genes, namely the lip gene , aer gene , and ompW gene  (Table 1). ATCC 49140 was used as the reference strain of A. hydrophila in this study.
2.2. PCR amplification of the omp23 gene
The genomic data of A. hydrophila bearing GenBank accession number CP027804.1 was retrieved to identify and target the omp23 gene sequence and to design three primers: F1, F2, and R (Table 1). Primers F1 and R facilitated full-length gene amplification, cloning, and DNA sequencing. At the same time, F2-R combination, was used to exclude the signal peptide-encoding area and for cloning and expressing only the omp23 gene region. PCR reactions were conducted in 30 mL volumes with 1X Taq amplification buffer (HiMedia, India), 10 mM dNTPs (2.5 mM each), 20 pmol of each primer, and 1 U of Taq polymerase (HiMedia). The template, ranging from 200 to 400 ng, and nuclease-free water served as the negative control. PCR included an initial denaturation at 95 degC for 5 min, followed by 30 cycles of denaturation at 95 degC for 1 min, annealing at 52 degC for 1 min, extension at 72 degC for 1 min, and a final extension at 72 degC for 10 min. Resultant PCR products were electrophoresed on 1.5-2 % agarose gels, followed by gel staining with ethidium bromide (5 mg/mL) and imaging using a Bio-Rad gel documentation system (Bio-Rad, USA).
2.3. Cloning and sequencing of the omp23 gene
Following amplification using F1 and R primers, the PCR product of the full-length open reading frame (ORF) of the omp23 gene was purified using a Qiagen PCR purification kit (Qiagen, Germany). The purified PCR product was cloned using the Qiagen PCR cloning kit, following the manufacturer's protocol (Qiagen, Germany). Positive colonies were identified using blue-white screening on Luria Bertani (LB)-ampicillin-X-gal plates containing 100 mg/mL ampicillin and 2 % X-gal . Selected white colonies were confirmed by PCR using the F1 and R primers and sequenced for inserts in PCR-positive recombinant plasmids, (M/S Eurofins Genomics India Pvt Ltd, Bengaluru, India). The obtained nucleotide sequences were subsequently analyzed for similarity using the basic local alignment search tool (BLAST-NCBI).
2.4. Cloning and over-expression of recombinant Omp23
The segment of the omp23 gene, excluding the signal peptide, was amplified using F2 and R primers. The resultant PCR product was purified and ligated to the pBAD expression system (Thermo Fisher, USA) at 37 degC for 2 h. The ligated product was introduced into chemically competent Escherichia coli BL21 cells following the heat shock method. The transformants were selected by plating the cells on LB agar plates supplemented with ampicillin (100 mg/mL). Confirming clones carrying the recombinant plasmid was done by PCR using F2 and R primers. The isolated recombinant clones of Omp23 were cultured in LB broth supplemented with ampicillin (100 mg/mL) at 37 degC with continuous agitation. Upon reaching an OD600 of 0.5-0.8, induction of protein expression was initiated by adding 0.2 % arabinose. Whole-cell lysates were used to analyze recombinant protein expression for protein extraction. The expression of the recombinant protein was studied by electrophoresis in 12 % sodium dodecyl sulfate-polyacrylamide gel (SDS-PAGE) .
2.5. Purification of recombinant Omp23
The purification of rOmp23 protein was done using nickel-nitrilotriacetic acid (Ni-NTA) affinity chromatography (Qiagen), having six histidine tags (His6) . The purity was assessed in 12 % SDS-PAGE gel, and protein concentration was determined using the bicinchoninic acid (BCA) protein assay kit (Sigma-Aldrich, USA) using a spectrophotometer (Multiskan SkyHigh microplate spectrophotometer, ThermoFisher, USA). For further characterization, Western blotting was performed according to the method established by Towbin et al. . The target protein was identified using a mouse anti-His antibody (diluted 1:1000 in PBS, Biolegend, USA). Interaction of the protein bands with the antibody on a nitrocellulose membrane (Pall Corporation, USA) was visualized by subsequent incubation with an anti-mouse horseradish peroxidase conjugate (diluted 1:2500 in PBS, Promega, USA). Chemiluminescent signals were generated using enhanced chemiluminescence substrates (ECL Western blotting substrates, Bio-Rad, USA), and the resulting bands were visualized utilizing a chemiluminescence documentation system (MINI HD9, UVITEC, UK).
2.6. Preparation of nanoparticle and characterization
The encapsulation of rOmp23 in PLGA NPs (PLGA Mn: 7000-17000 MW, Sigma-Aldrich) involved the W1/O/W2 double emulsion solvent evaporation technique as described previously . In this process, 10 mg of the protein was dissolved in a buffer solution (100 mM NaH2PO4, 10 mM Tris-Cl, 8 M Urea, pH 4.5) and emulsified in the organic phase, which consisted of 0.9 mL of dichloromethane (DCM), 100 mg/mL PLGA, and 0.5 mg/mL Pluronic F-127 in 3 mL of polyvinyl alcohol (25 mg/mL PVA). The primary emulsion was created by sonication for 20 min on ice using a sonicator (Qsonica, USA). The outer aqueous phase was then added, and sonication was continued for an additional 20 min in ice chill condition to form the secondary emulsion. added to a 40 mL solution of 25 mg/mL PVA and stirred for 20 h at ambient temperature (27degc) using a magnetic stirrer until DCM completely evaporated. Protein-loaded PLGA NPs were isolated and washed twice with ultracentrifugation. Finally, the obtained pellets were dispersed in 1X PBS and lyophilized (Zirbus Technology, Germany). The hydrodynamic diameter and polydispersity index of the NPs was estimated using a zeta sizer (SZ-100V2, Horiba Instruments Ltd, Japan). The lyophilized samples were appropriately diluted with PBS (pH 7.4), and aliquots from each batch were examined in dynamic light scattering (DLS) cuvettes. Analysis was performed in triplicate for each preparation, for size distribution and polydispersity index using DLS at a scattering angle of 90deg at a temperature of 25 degC, and standard deviations were calculated for each measurement. Additionally, scanning electron microscopy (SEM) (ZEISS Gemini, Australia) was done at the National Institute of Technology Karnataka, India, to study the morphology of the NPs.
2.7. Encapsulation efficiency measurement and in vitro protein release study
To evaluate the integrity of the protein and to ascertain its encapsulation efficiency, the rOmp23-containing particles were dissolved in a solution of 0.5 % w/v SDS (pH 7.4), prepared by dissolving SDS in a 0.1 M NaOH solution . The protein amount encapsulated within the nanoparticles was calculated by subtracting the concentration of free protein in the supernatant from the total protein loaded during NP preparation, by the formula of Rauta et al. : Encapsulation efficiency (%) = (Total amount of protein - Free protein)/(Total amount of protein) x 100. To study the release kinetics of the protein from the nanoparticles, the protein-loaded nanoparticles were incubated at 28 degC in 1X PBS, and samples were drawn at specified intervals (0, 3, 6, 9, up to 48 h). Following centrifugation at 12,000 rpm for 10 min, 500 mL of the supernatant was collected, and an equal volume of PBS was added (Dubey et al., 2016 a). The released rOmp23 protein in the collected samples was quantified using the BCA method (Pierce(tm) BCA protein assay kit, ThermoFisher).
2.8. Fish and bacterial strains
Tilapia fish, Oreochromis niloticus, weighing approximately 9-11 g, were obtained from the College of Fisheries, Mangalore, India, and carefully transported in oxygenated bags to the experimental site. They were acclimatized for a month before commencing the experiments, and were confirmed for the absence of Aeromonas, Edwardsiella, and Vibrio infections. Skin samples from five randomly selected fish were collected for bacterial isolation, and genotypic identification by PCR. The fish were kept in aerated tanks, and commercial feed was given during the period. The tank water was cleaned twice weekly to maintain a healthy environment and minimize the risk of disease outbreaks.
2.9. Vaccine preparations for oral delivery
The Omp23 encapsulated nanoparticle was meticulously blended with finely ground and sieved commercial fish feed with a dose of 10 mg/g of fish body weight to form a uniform dough. This moist vaccine-feed mixture was extruded using a hand extruder and allowed to air-dry at room temperature (32 degC). Subsequently, the prepared dry pellets were stored at 4 degC until ready for use. Fish were fed with vaccine once in a day that accounted for 1 % of the fish's body weight for 21 days.
2.10. Determination of lethal dose (LD50) of A. hydrophila
The LD50 value of A. hydrophila on tilapia was determined using thirty fish evenly distributed among three separate tanks, each containing ten fish. A control tank that contained ten fish was also maintained. Viable counts of A. hydrophila were determined using the surface spread method on tryptone soya agar by plating ten-fold serial dilutions of an overnight culture. The culture was centrifuged, resuspended in sterile phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), and diluted to achieve three consecutive dilutions with viable counts of 1.6 x 108, 1.6 x 107, 1.6 x 106, and 1.6 x 105 cells per 0.1 mL. These dilutions were injected intraperitoneally into the fish, and mortality was observed for up to one week. The obtained LD50 value was subsequently calculated for experiments assessing protection against challenge.
2.11. Vaccination, challenge, and relative percentage of analysis
The health assessment of O. niloticus was based on clinical observations, including surface morphology, pathological signs, and monitoring abnormal swimming behaviour. Four fish were sampled for external and detailed pathological examinations of internal organs. The fish were divided into four groups, each comprising 45 fish. Group 1 served as the control group and received commercial feed. Group 2 was fed empty NPs devoid of rOmp23 protein. Group 3, denoted as the vaccine group, received NPs coated with rOmp23 protein (10 mg/g of fish body weight). Group 4, named the protein group, received rOmp23 protein. Each group was carried out in triplicates (15 fish in each group). Throughout the 21-day experimental trial, the fish were fed twice daily. Following two weeks of immune stimulation, fish were challenged using a pathogenic strain of A. hydrophila (1.6 x 107 CFU/mL) intraperitoneal injection at 0.1 mL/fish. Fourteen days post-challenge, mortality, and clinical signs were documented, and the level of protection was estimated in different groups i.e., nanoparticle feed group (NPFG), Protein feed group (PFG), Empty nanoparticle feed group (ENFG) and Commercial feed group (CFG) using Kaplan-Meier survival analysis. The relative percent survival (RPS) was calculated over 14 consecutive days using the formula outlined by Kole et al. : RPS = {1 - (mortality in the immunized group/mortality in the control group)} x 100 %.
2.12. Non-specific immune response studies
2.12.1. Collection of serum
Following vaccination, blood samples were drawn from protein feed group (rOmp23), empty nanoparticle feed group, rOmp23-NP, and commercial feed group (control), at multiple time points: 7, 14, 21, and 49 days of post-vaccination (dpv), according to the protocol of Siriyappagouder et al. .
2.12.2. Myeloperoxidase activity
2.12.3. Anti-protease activity
2.12.4. Bacterial agglutination activity
The agglutination test was performed in U-shaped microtitre plates . The titre was expressed as the reciprocal of the highest serum dilution, showing agglutination of the bacterial cells.
2.13. RT-PCR detection of the expression of immune-related genes in the vaccinated fish
To study the expression patterns of immune-related genes in vaccinated fish, intestine and kidney tissue samples were collected at various time intervals (0, 7, 14, and 21 days). Total RNA extraction was done using the TRIzol (Invitrogen, USA) method. The obtained RNA from samples were diluted in RNase-free water, and their concentration and purity were determined using a spectrophotometer (NanoPhotometer(r), Implen, USA). Quantitative polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR) was performed to quantify TNF-a, CD4, IFN- g, IL-10, and IL-1b gene expression levels in the intestine and kidney. The one-step TB Green PrimeScript RT-PCR kit (Takara, Japan) was used for RT-qPCR analysis on a CFX Opus 96 Real-Time PCR platform (Bio-Rad, Singapore). Gene-specific primers used are listed in Table 1, with the ELF-1a gene serving as the internal control. Relative gene expression of each group was calculated using the formula: Relative expression = 2^(-DDCt), where DCt represents the difference between the sample Ct value, the ELF-1a Ct value, and DDCt indicates the difference between the DCt value of the vaccinated and the control group . Triplicate data points were used for analysis.
2.14. Statistical analysis
The RT-qPCR results were subjected to two-way ANOVA for statistical significance. GraphPad Prism, version 8.4.3. (GraphPad, CA, USA), was utilized for the data analysis. For statistical analysis, the RT-qPCR results were examined by two-way ANOVA to determine statistical significance. GraphPad Prism, version 8.4.3. ## Results
3.1. PCR identification of omp23 of A. hydrophila
The gene omp23 was confirmed by amplifying the target sequence using F1 and R primers, resulting in a distinct single band of 627 bp. Confirmation of the exclusion of the signal peptide region was achieved by visualizing a PCR-amplified product of 567 bp (Fig. 1).
3.2. Cloning and expression of rOmp23
Upon analysis, the DNA sequence corresponding to the omp23 gene product was deposited in GenBank and assigned accession number: MZ044977. Optimal expression of rOmp23 was observed 4 h post-induction with 0.2 % arabinose. The molecular weight of the resulting Omp23 deduced sequence was observed to be 37 kDa on 12 % SDS-PAGE (Fig. 2). Following purification, the recombinant protein exhibited high purity on SDS-PAGE, and Western blot analysis showed a distinct band, thus confirming it as Omp23.
3.3. Encapsulation and characterization
Size distribution analysis revealed the empty PLGA NPs to be in the size range of 209 +- 36.1 nm (Fig. 3A), while the rOmp23-encapsulated NPs were larger with a size range of 346.5 +- 15.1 nm (Fig. 3B). The polydispersity index was calculated to be 0.308 +- 0.02. The SEM images depicted the spherical structure of the formulated NPs (Fig. 3C). The encapsulation efficiency of rOmp23 in PLGA NPs was found to be 74.45 %. In vitro release showed approximately 63 % of the encapsulated protein released within 48-h (Fig. 3D).
3.4. Non-specific immune response studies
After vaccination, the non-specific immune response parameters in tilapia fish were assessed in the different groups (rOmp23, Empty NP, rOmp23-NP, and Control) at various post-immunization time intervals. Fig. 4A shows myeloperoxidase activity, and Fig. 4B the antiprotease activity. Across all treated groups and control (rOmp23, Empty NP, rOmp23-NP, and Control), parameters such as bacterial agglutination assay, myeloperoxidase, and antiprotease levels exhibited significantly higher values (p < 0.05) compared to the control group. The higher values obtained in the NPG treated group were statistically significant (p < 0.05) than those other groups (rOmp23, Empty NP and Control). These parameters did not differ much in all the treated groups at 21 and 49-days post-immunization. Myeloperoxidase titers were notably higher (p < 0.05) in all nanoparticle-vaccinated groups compared to the control. The anti-protease activity in nanoparticle-vaccinated fish ranged from 60 % to 80 % across different days, with the highest inhibition (80 %) observed on the 14th day post-immunization. In Fig. 5, bacterial agglutination titre was significantly higher (p < 0.05) in the treated groups than in control.
3.5. Expression analysis of immune genes
Following vaccination, RT-qPCR analysis was performed to examine the expression levels of immune genes in both the intestine and kidney tissue of the fish. 21 dpv, the nanovaccine group showed substantial upregulation of the TNF-a, CD4, IL-10 expression in the intestine compared to the ENP group. Fig. 6 shows an initial increase in gene expression for IFN-g and, IL-1b which gradually decreased over time. During the initial stages in the nano-vaccine administered group, high levels of IL-10 and CD4 gene expression were observed in the kidney. In contrast, IFN-g, IL-1b, TNF-a gene expression gradually increased, with a decrease observed at certain time points.
3.6. Challenge study
The RPS in vaccinated and control groups was evaluated in the challenge experiment. The day following the challenge, clinical signs that presented as darkened body colour and skin ulcers leading to gradual mortality were observed in both the control and the group fed with empty NP. Mortality rates gradually increased, becoming pronounced four days post-challenge. Fig. 7 illustrates the survival rates in all the groups, and the corresponding RPS values, which were 73 % for the NPG, 46.42 % for the PG, and 10 % for the ENPG groups.

## Discussion
Vaccination is one of the best and most affordable prevention strategies for infectious diseases in humans and other animals, including fish. Most of the molecules in the recently developed subunit vaccines are pure proteins derived through recombinant DNA technology; however, they are ineffective due to a lack of suitable adjuvants or vaccine carriers. Hence, safe, and useful delivery vehicles are critical for vaccination. The ability of PLGA particles to gradually release peptides and proteins sustainably over one to four months makes them useful for various commercial items, including the medical and pharmaceutical industries . OMPs on the surface of Gram-negative bacteria have sought after molecules to develop fish vaccines because of their strong immunogenicity coupled with protective immunity . The effectiveness and characteristics of the outer membrane protein of A. hydrophila encapsulated in PLGA nanoparticle have been examined in this study as a viable vaccine candidate against motile aeromonad infections of fish. This study used the DLS method to analyze the nanoparticle for the size and polydispersity index. The size of the Omp23 encapsulated in PLGA was 346.5 nm. The increased nanoparticle size shows that the protein has been encapsulated with PLGA , where the size of PLGA OMP-NPs from 98.6 nm to 166.4 nm was observed. The polydispersity index of PLGA-Omp23 NPs was 0.313. It was nearly similar to the observation by Rauta et al. , who obtained the PDI value of 0.308 thus for their PLGA-OMP NPs, confirming the monodisperse nature. The in vitro study to examine the release of Omp23 in PBS for 48 h showed an exponential rise in the antigen release after 25 h. The release was 45 % in 48 h, higher than the 34 % of rOmpW release obtained by Dubey et al., . The kinetics of rOmpW in vitro release follows a typical pattern, beginning with a rapid initial release of antigens within 24 h followed by a continual release over an extended period. A follow-up on the release test, if continued after 48 h, could have revealed a sustained continuous release of the rOmpW antigen from encapsulated NPs . This demonstrates the ability of PLGA to release the antigen slowly over time, which is necessary to strengthen the immunological response. The SEM image of the nanoparticle showed it to be spherical in shape and uniform, similar to the observations by Rauta et al. . The 74.45 % encapsulation efficiency for PLGA NPs obtained in this study was higher than the 53.56 % shown by Dubey et al. . High encapsulation efficiencies enable NPs to convey the antigens effectively in vivo . The formulation of the nanovaccine was optimized in this study based on particle size, polydispersity index, encapsulation efficiency, and in vitro release kinetics. Moreover, these conditions provided a high encapsulation efficiency (74.45 %) and sustained release profile over 24 h, which are essential for effective antigen delivery and prolonged immune stimulation. The optimized conditions balanced both physical stability and biological efficacy, as evidenced by the elevated cytokine and antibody responses in tilapia.
Post-vaccination response at different time intervals was studied to evaluate non-specific immune parameters in the four experimental groups, (rOmp23, Empty NP, rOmp23-NP and Control), each aimed at eliciting varying levels of immune response compared to the control. The group treated with NP showed heightened immune response compared to the others. In fish, innate immunity serves as a critical first line of defence for protection. The pivotal role of innate immunity in fish is paramount for initial pathogen defense, guiding subsequent adaptive immune responses . Myeloperoxidase (MPO), primarily produced by neutrophils, constitutes a crucial innate immune system molecule. It plays a significant role in combating bacterial threats by leveraging its antibacterial effects. MPO's action on halide ions in the presence of H2O2 generates hypohalites, thus effectively eliminating bacteria . This study observed an early increase in MPO levels, consistent with previous findings, which was evident at ten days post-immunization (dpi) in the immunized group. This observation suggests that the antigenic preparations in this study stimulate both specific and non-specific components of the immune system, working synergistically to confer protection. Fish serum typically contains an array of protease inhibitors and antiproteases aimed at curtailing pathogen invasion . Bacterial pathogens often secrete diverse proteases that play a role as virulence factors. These enzymes boost the pathogen's virulence, simultaneously breaking down host proteins and supplying crucial precursor amino acids for bacterial growth and replication. The sera derived from BF-immunized fish displayed varying degrees of anti-protease activity, ranging from 60 % to 80 % during different days of analysis. Our study showed the highest level of protease inhibitory activity (around 80 %) on day 14 post-immunization. The notable suppression of protease activity observed underscores the potential of vaccine to combat bacterial virulence and averting the degradation of host proteins. The PLGA-OMP treated group demonstrated significantly higher immune response compared to other groups at both 21- and 42-days post-immunization. Previous studies have shown that PLGA micro-particles enhance phagocytosis by macrophages . and neutrophils in mice and humans. Comparable results were also observed with the use of PLGA alone in mammals  and in fish, specifically rainbow trout .
In this study, changes in the immune defence system upon vaccination against A. hydrophila resulted in the upregulation and downregulation of specific immune genes. The investigation of immune-related gene expression patterns in fish is relatively recent, leading to a better understanding in future of immune response to pathogens. TNF-a and IL-10, identified as crucial components of innate immunity , play significant roles. The activation of innate immunity mediated by these cytokines likely influences the initiation of specific adaptive immune responses . IL-10 is recognized as an anti-inflammatory cytokine that mitigates inflammation by blocking chemokine receptors and impeding the effects of pro-inflammatory cytokines, thus reducing potential host damage . It is also associated with B cell-triggered immunoglobulins . In this study there was a gradual increase in IL-10 and TNF-a expression levels in the intestine of the vaccinated group. Upregulation of IL-10 was initially observed in the kidney during the early post-vaccination period in the nano vaccine administered group. This upregulation potentially helps maintain a delicate balance between pro- and anti-inflammatory responses, as Jiang et al.  suggested. Moreover, IL-10 contributes to synthesizing a multifunctional cytokine inhibitory factor, which aids in immune-suppressive functions, significantly hinders proinflammatory cytokine responses such as IL-1b, TNF-a, and IFN-g, preventing tissue damage. Studies in fish have reported increased IL-10 mRNA expression levels due to various immunostimulants, linked to transcriptional changes in innate immune cells, like macrophages . TNF-a and IL-10 are crucial components of innate immunity . Activation of these cytokines potentially primes the onset of specific adaptive immune response . TNF-a gene upregulation in the intestine on the 21st-day post-vaccination in the nanovaccine-administered group. On the other hand, TNF-a gene upregulation occurred in the kidney before vaccination, followed by a gradual decrease in gene expression. Previous reports have highlighted a similar pattern wherein an initial rise in IL-1b and TNF-a expression occurred shortly after bacterial infection, followed by decline . IL-10 functions as an anti-inflammatory cytokine, pivotal in reducing inflammation . Its role in minimizing host damage involves blocking chemokine receptors and curbing the impact of pro-inflammatory cytokines. Additionally, IL-10 has links to B cell-induced immunoglobulins . Our study showed a gradual increase in IFN-g, an important immunoregulatory molecule in innate and adaptive immunity. An upregulation of IFN-g expression in both the intestine and kidney of the vaccinated group was observed on 14th day, thus suggesting that the nanovaccine probably stimulates both innate and adaptive responses, potentially fostering the activation of immune-related macrophages . In a study by Lindenstrom et al., , IL-10 and IL-1b expression levels in the intestine and kidney exhibited variation being significantly low between weeks 2 and 4 and relatively elevated during weeks 6 and 8. Saurabh and Sahoo , observed the upregulation of IL-1b in trout skin as a response to the parasite Gyrodactylus derjavini infection. In the Indian major carp, Catla catla infected with A. hydrophila, IL-1b expression significantly decreased, suggesting potential pathogen toxin release during different phases of attachment. This process seems to play a significant role in regulating inflammation, affecting the down-regulation and upregulation of IL-1b and IL-10 expression. Savan et al.  surmised the involvement of IL-10 in synthesis of a multifunctional cytokine inhibitory factor and promoting immune-suppression. Its pivotal role in counteracting proinflammatory cytokine response like IL-1b, TNF-a, and IFN-g, while simultaneously preventing tissue damage, has been studied . Some researchers have reported increased IL-10 mRNA expression levels in fish due to various immunostimulants, and studied the associated transcriptional changes in innate immune cells like macrophages . The early-response pro-inflammatory cytokine, IL-1b, plays a crucial role in stimulating immune response by activating lymphocytes or triggering the release of other cytokines that subsequently activate macrophages, natural killer cells, and lymphocytes . In this study, we noted an upregulation of the IL-1b gene in the intestine on the 7th day, followed by a downregulation in the nanovaccine-administered group on the 14th and 21st day post-vaccination. On the contrary, upregulation of the IL-1b gene was observed in the kidney on the 14th and 21st days post-vaccination in the nanovaccine-administered group. Higher CD4 and MHC-II expression levels offer significant advantages regarding exogenous antigen presentation, reflecting enhanced antigen presentation . In our study, upregulation of the CD4 gene was observed in the intestine of the nanovaccine-administered group on the 21st day. In the kidney, CD4 upregulation occurred before vaccination, followed by a gradual decrease in expression in the control nonvaccinated fish group. This suggests that activated CD4 T cells, producing the IL-2 cytokine, facilitate the differentiation of CD4 T cells into various T cell subsets, particularly Th cells, thereby augmenting immune response processes . The highlight of the study was the high RPS of 73 obtained in the group vaccinated with the rOmp23 nanovaccine 14 days after the challenge. In contrast, the control groups displayed signs of darkened body colour and loss of balance starting 2nd day post-challenge, eventually leading to mortality. The clinical signs were likely due to individual differences and were not considered significant in calculating the final survival. The cardinal findings of this study are a pointer to the critical role of innate immunity in fish through oral vaccination. Zhang et al.  observed similar results in their study, underscoring the immune regulatory mechanism activated by oral vaccines.
While the current study demonstrates the feasibility of expressing and purifying the target protein, a potential limitation lies in the scalability of the procedure. For practical or industrial applications, large quantities of high-purity protein are required, which can have challenges in terms of cost, time, and technical efficiency. The yields obtained under laboratory conditions may not readily translate to large-scale production due to constraints in expression efficiency, solubility, or purification throughput. Therefore, additional optimization strategies, such as enhancing expression systems or employing bioprocess engineering techniques, may be necessary to overcome these limitations and make the process viable for broader application.
This research provides essential insights into immune factors associated with nano-oral vaccines in fish, confirming the efficacy of incorporating protective antigens into nanoparticles to elicit long-lasting innate and adaptive immune responses. Interestingly, the rOmp23-PLGA vaccine developed in this study was superior in providing a high degree of protection.

## Conclusion
The findings of this study underscore the potential of utilizing PLGA encapsulated with outer membrane protein Omp23 as a promising vaccination strategy against aeromonad infections of fish. The encapsulation process demonstrated efficient antigen release kinetics, sustained release over time, and protection against acidic conditions, all of which are essential for preserving the integrity of the antigen. The ability of the nanoparticle to trigger a heightened immune response, particularly the non-specific immune parameters such as myeloperoxidase and anti-protease activity, signifies its capability to bolster the host's defense mechanisms. The upregulation of crucial immune-related genes like IL-10, TNF-a, IFN-g, and IL-1b in vaccinated fish suggests a robust activation of both innate and adaptive immunity, critical for combating pathogen invasion and establishment. The enhanced expression of genes associated with antigen presentation, such as CD4 and MHC-II, further supports the notion of improved immune response elicited by the vaccination. The RPS of 73 % post-challenges in the group administered with the rOmp23 nanovaccine highlights its effectiveness in conferring protection compared to the control groups, which displayed signs of infection and subsequent mortality. These outcomes emphasize the pivotal role of innate immunity, especially in the context of oral vaccination in fish. In conclusion, the study demonstrates that the PLGA-encapsulated Omp23 nanoparticle vaccine possessed promising attributes: efficient antigen delivery, innate and adaptive immunity activation, and significant protection against fish aeromonad infection. These findings pave the way for developing effective and long-lasting vaccination strategies in aquaculture for other infectious agents and promote blue transformation goals advocated by FAO.
