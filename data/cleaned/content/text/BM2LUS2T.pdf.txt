Accepted: 24 July 2023 / Published online: 7 August 2023

Abstract
Purpose  The purpose of this study was to develop inhalable pirfenidone (PFD) nanoparticles for the delivery of the medicine
to specific alveolar epithelial cells (AECs) in the idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF) treatment. PFD has major problems
like low oral route absorption due to the presence of food, faster elimination half-life, and potent phototoxicity. PFD-loaded
poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) nanoparticles provide enhanced release of drug and reduce its toxicity, dose, and dos-
ing frequency by targeting the AECs.
Methods  The PFD-loaded PLGA NPs were prepared by a single emulsion solvent evaporation method. The preliminary
studies were carried out with process parameters like the selection of organic phase, effect of organic phase volume, effect of
stabilizer (PVA) concentration, drug:polymer ratio, and sonication time. The effects of two formulation variables including
the drug:polymer ratio and surfactant concentration were examined by using ­32 full factorial design applied to get optimized
formulation. The final product was lyophilized and made free-flowing powder formulation for pulmonary administration.
Results  Optimized PFD-loaded PLGA NPs showed initial burst release followed by sustained up to 71.19 ± 5.39% at
24 h. The particle size, polydispersity index, zeta potential, and % entrapment efficiency were found to be 523 ± 6.42 nm,
0.183 ± 0.011, 1.62 ± 0.22 mV, and 44.14 ± 3.74% respectively.
Conclusions  There was no significant aggregation of PFD-loaded PLGA NPs during 6 months of stability study and spheri-
cal shape nanoparticle with smooth surface as per SEM study. Hence, PFD-loaded PLGA NPs can be a suitable alternative
to the currently available therapy.
Keywords  Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis · Pirfenidone · PLGA nanoparticles · Single emulsion solvent evaporation · 32
Full factorial design
Introduction
The presence of cellular proliferation, interstitial inflam-
mation, fibrosis, or a combination of these traits inside the
alveolar wall that is not caused by infection or cancer is what
defines the interstitial lung disease family. IPF is the most
prevalent kind of idiopathic interstitial lung disease. IPF is
a fatal disease in which the uncontrolled deposition of the
extracellular matrix leads to progressive loss of lung func-
tion [1]. It is a chronic, progressive, fibrotic interstitial lung
disease with specific imaging and histologic characteristics
that mostly affects older persons. The prevalence of IPF has
been reported to range between 10 and 60 cases per 100,000
in the USA, while one study found that the incidence was
494 instances per 100,000 in 2011 among persons over the
age of 65, more than double the frequency documented

10 years earlier [2]. Increased hospitalizations and mortal-

PFD (Fig. S1) was the first antifibrotic medication
approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA)
in 2014 for the treatment of IPF. PFD has been shown to
decrease the evolution of IPF by reducing the creation of
pro-inflammatory and pro-fibrotic cytokines such as tumor
necrosis factor-α (TNF-α) and transforming growth factor-β
(TGF-β). However, oral conventional delivery of PFD has
been associated with some side effects like cough, head-
ache, diarrhea, fever, abnormality of hepatic function, diz-
ziness, and facial paralysis. Moreover, it has a short half-
life (2.4 h) with faster elimination rate from the body. The
PFD gets absorbed slowly after oral administration due to
presence of food in the stomach, and for this reason, it is
administered orally with three tablets (267 mg) three times
a day, and for a chronic disease like IPF, it may result in
patient incompliance [3].
Non-oral delivery of PFD has gained interest in recent
times. Abnoos et al. prepared PFD nanoparticles for trans-
dermal delivery system with 94% entrapment efficiency
and observed enhanced permeation from chitosan alginate
nanoparticles [4]. Jose et al. prepared liposomal hydrogel
formulation to improve permeation of PFD through skin
membrane; the studies revealed that transdermal route can
be a potential drug delivery system for PFD [5]. Pulmo-
nary drug delivery offers a vast variety of advantages over
conventional oral drug delivery. By improving the physical
characteristics of the particles, nanoparticle-mediated drug
delivery systems bring up new opportunities [6]. The large
surface area with faster absorption of drug molecules has
been because of high vascularization and the elimination
by the first-pass effect. This targeted drug delivery reduces
the total daily dose quantity, hence reducing the side effects.
Pardeshi et al. prepared sustained release microspheres for
pulmonary delivery of PFD and concluded that depositing
the drug loaded microspheres in the central and peripheral
lung region maximizes therapeutic concentration with mini-
mal side effects. The developed formulation may also sustain
the drug release and reduce dosing frequency and improve
patient compliance [7]. Biodegradable polymeric nanopar-
ticles are widely used for targeting pulmonary delivery and
may exhibit sustained release effects. Polymers offer a wide
variety of advantages, including improved surface qualities,
excellent drug encapsulation, longer drug delivery, protec-
tion from degradation, and long shelf life. FDA-approved
synthetic biodegradable polymer PLGA is used widely
nowadays. However, non-toxic glycolic acid and lactic acid
are formed due to degradation through natural pathways of
PLGA. It is biodegradable, biocompatible, stable, non-toxic,
controlled release, and non-immunogenic [8, 9].
However, very few literatures are available discussing the
use of biodegradable polymer such as PLGA for formulating
PFD nanoparticles for treatment of IPF [10]. Considering
this, the present investigation was carried out to prepare PFD
nanoparticles for the treatment of IPF. The nanoparticles
were prepared using a single emulsion solvent evaporation
method, and the influence of independent factors on depend-
ent variables was investigated using a ­32 full factorial design.
The following objectives were decided to be achieved:
1.	 Mean particle size of the optimized formulation should
be less than 1 µm.
2.	 Mass median aerodynamic diameter less than 5 µm.
3.	 Sustain the drug release for 24 h.
Materials
Pirfenidone was gifted by ZCL Chemicals Ltd., Gujarat,
India. Poly(D,L-lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA 50:50,
inherent viscosity 0.25 dL/gm, average molecular weight
24,000–38,000) was purchased from Nomisma Healthcare,
Gujarat, India. Low molecular weight chitosan was gifted
by Meck Pharmaceuticals & Chemicals Private Limited,
Gujarat, India. Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) (90% hydrolyzed,
average Mol wt. 45,000) was supplied by Sigma Aldric
Chemicals Private Limited, Karnataka, India. Inhalable
grade lactose was procured from Molychem, Mumbai, India.
Phosphate buffer saline (PBS) pH 7.4 was obtained from
Sigma-Aldrich, India. Dichloromethane and acetic acid (gla-
cial) chloroform, ethyl acetate, ethanol, and acetone were
purchased from Chemdyes Corporation, Gujarat, India. All
the solvents used were of HPLC grade and other chemicals
used were of analytical grade.
Methods
Formulation of Pirfenidone‑Loaded
PLGA Nanoparticles
The preliminary studies were carried out to evaluate the
effect of various formulation and process variables on the
formation of nanoparticles. Various assorted organic sol-
vents were employed, namely dichloromethane, acetone, eth-
anol, ethyl acetate, and chloroform, to prepare nanoparticles.
Further studies were carried out to find out suitable volume
of organic solvent, sonication time, and stabilizer concen-
tration for preparation of PFD-loaded PLGA nanoparticles.
The PFD-loaded PLGA NPs were prepared by a single
emulsion solvent evaporation method. Briefly, drug (PFD,
100 mg) and polymer (PLGA 50:50) were taken in the dif-
ferent ratios and dissolved in the specified amount of organic
solvent dichloromethane (3 mL). Concurrently, an aque-
ous PVA solution was prepared of varying concentration.

Further organic phase content was added dropwise slowly
into a continuous stirred aqueous phase containing PVA and
homogenized at 8000 rpm (High-Speed Homogenizer, Remi
Industries Limited, Mumbai, India) for 5 min. The O/W
emulsion formed was sonicated in a probe sonicator (Delta,
Ahmedabad, Gujarat, India) at 98% amplitude and 20 Hz for
5 min over ice bath immediately. The prepared emulsion was
kept on a magnetic stirrer under the atmospheric conditions
at 400 rpm for 3 to 4 h to complete evaporation of dichlo-
romethane and form polymeric nanoparticles (as per Fig. 1).
The prepared suspension of nanoparticles was centrifuged
(Remi Cooling Centrifuge, Remi Industries Limited, Mum-
bai, India) at 15,000 × g for 30 min, and the nanoparticle
pellet was collected and washed twice with phosphate buffer
to remove the unentrapped drug. The free PFD amount in the
supernatant was measured by UV–visible spectrophotometer
at 311 nm [11]. The nanoparticles were pre-frozen in a gly-
col bath and then lyophilized at − 79 °C for 24 h (primary
drying was carried out at − 40 °C for 24 h and secondary
drying was carried out at − 15 °C for 48 h) with mannitol
(5% w/v) as a cryoprotectant using a Lyophilizer (Delvac
Pumps Pvt. Ltd., Chennai, India) and stored in vials until
further use [12, 13]. From it, entrapment efficiency % (EE%)
and cumulative drug release % were determined. Mean parti-
cle size (MPS), zeta potential (ZP), and polydispersity index
(PI) were determined using Zetasizer. The effect of different
experimental parameters such as a polymer (PLGA) to drug
(PFD) ratio, stabilizer concentration, selection of organic
phase, and sonication time on the particle size and encapsu-
lation efficacy of PFD nanoparticles was determined.

Characterization of PFD‑Loaded PLGA Nanoparticles
Drug‑Excipient Compatibility Studies
Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) and differ-
ential scanning calorimetry (DSC) were carried out by using
the drug-excipient compatibility studies.
Sample Preparation and Analysis by FTIR  The FTIR spectra
(Thermo Scientific, USA) of pure PFD, PFD with PLGA,
and the optimized formulation were recorded with KBr and
compressing the mixed to generate self-supporting discs. The
KBr press compression strength was set at 5 tons for 5 min
between 4000 and 400 ­cm−1; the scanning range was retained.
Sample Preparation and Analysis by DSC  DSC thermograms
of pure PFD and PFD:PLGA were generated by DSC 60
(Shimadzu, Japan). The samples (PFD and PFD:PLGA)
were heated in an airtight aluminum pan at a scanning rate
of 10 °C from room temperature to 300 °C with a 1-min
hold period. To keep up in an inactive environment, a rate
of 20-mL/min nitrogen gas was purged.
Mean Particle Size and Polydispersity Index Measurement
The nanoparticle size analyzer Zetatrac (Microtrac Inc.,
USA) was utilized to examine the particle size and polydis-
persity index of PFD-loaded PLGA NPs. The charged parti-
cles are oscillated using a high-frequency AC electric field.
The modulated power spectrum (MPS) technique is used to
examine the Brownian motion power spectrum, which is a
component of the power spectrum originating from oscillat-
ing particles. The freshly prepared sample of NP dispersion
(containing 1% w/v NPs) was diluted with 1.0-mL water.
The PFD-loaded PLGA NPs dispersed sufficiently, and that
sample was used after being sonicated for 2 min [14]. The
evaluations showed no sample disintegration or aggregation,
and each sample measurement was performed in triplicate.

In Vitro Drug Release Study
The release of PFD was evaluated by previously calculated
and weighed amount of dried PFD-loaded PLGA NPs (20 mg)
in 100 mL of phosphate buffer saline (PBS) solution pH 7.4.
Dialysis membrane of molecular weight cut-off of 12 kDa was
used for drug release study. The nanoparticles were poured
into a dialysis bag and kept in a basket of USP type I dissolu-
tion apparatus (shown in Fig. S2). The system was stirred at
100 rpm and was kept at 37 ± 0.5 °C [15]. Five milliliters of
sample was collected from the medium at time intervals of
0, 0.5, 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, 12, and 24 h, and that was replenished by
fresh PBS solution. Using a UV–visible spectrophotometer
set to 311 nm, the collected samples were passed through
a 0.22-µm filter membrane and evaluated for PFD content.
Morphology by Scanning Electron Microscopy
Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) analysis of the opti-
mized formulation was carried out for the outer surface
morphology of nanoparticles detected with SEM. The NPs
were lyophilized and fixed on metal stubs using double-
faced adhesive tape, then coated with gold to a thickness of
16 mm (Emitech K550 equipment), and viewed with a Zeiss
DSM 940. A microscope with a digital imaging captures
system (DISS of Point Electronic GmbH, Halle, Germany).
In a SEM examination, electrons are transferred from sample
particle surfaces [15].
Mathematical Modeling of Drug Release Kinetics
To understand better the mechanism of drug release from
formed nanoparticles, data from in vitro release studies were
fitted to multiple kinetic equations. The kinetic models used
were zero order, first order, Higuchi, and Korsmeyer-Peppas
model. Statistical comparisons were conducted using Micro-
soft Excel and one-way ANOVA. The criteria for signifi-

Accelerated Stability Study
An accelerated stability study was done according to
International Council for Harmonization guidelines.
The freshly prepared and optimized batch of polymeric
nanoparticles of PFD was filled in a screw-capped glass
container. The stability chamber was maintained with
a temperature of 25 ± 2  °C and relative humidity of
60 ± 5%. Vials filled with nanoparticles were kept in the
stability chamber. The NPs from the chamber were ana-
lyzed for different time intervals of each month of the
first 3 months and then on the 6th month directly [17,
18]. From every time interval, PFD-loaded PLGA nano-
particles were evaluated to check stability for different
responses including MPS, zeta potential, PI, drug entrap-
ment, and drug release.
Formulation of Dry Powder Inhaler
To ensure pulmonary delivery acceptability, the optimized
formulation of PFD-loaded PLGA NPs was transformed into
a dry powder inhaler (DPI). To convert the nanoparticles in
the form of a dry powder inhaler, freeze-dried nanoparti-
cles and anhydrous inhalable grade lactose were manually
blended with a geometrical dilution technique [17]. The bulk
density and tapped density were carried out after anhydrous
lactose mixed with optimized PFD NPs in 1:1 ratio. The
good flow feature of the dry powder inhaler was used to
optimize it. To check the modifications of nanoparticles
in the form of DPI, the optimized formulation was further
evaluated for MPS and ZP.
Aerodynamic Properties by Andersen Cascade Impactor (ACI)
A nonviable eight-stage Anderson cascade impactor (ACI,
Thermo Fisher Scientific Inc.) was used to measure the mass
median aerodynamic diameter (MMAD) of the optimized
PFD nanoparticles as a DPI. The nanoparticles were aero-
solized as a DPI using Rotahaler device with a flow rate of
23.7 L/min through the cascade impactor. After deposition
of DPI in each chamber, the content of PFD was determined
by dissolving it in methanol and examined with a UV–visible
spectrophotometer set at λmax 311 nm. The MMAD, geo-
metric standard diameter (GSD), and % fine particle frac-
tion (FPF) were calculated using “MMAD Calculator.” Fine
particle fraction (particles < 5 μm) represents particles with
a high possibility of entering deep into the lungs. Particles
that are extra fine (< 1.0 μm) enhance peripheral lung deposi-
tion, and inhalers with a fine particle fraction that is flow rate
independent deliver a more uniform dosage to the lungs [17].
Statistical Analysis
Interactive multiple regression analysis and F-statistics were
utilized to evaluate the response. The models were used to
plot two-dimensional contour plots and three-dimensional
response surface methodology using STATISTICA software
at the values of X1 and X2 between − 1 and + 1. At predeter-
mined value of responses, MPS, PI, ZP, EE%, drug release
% (12 h), and drug release % (24 h) were investigated as
response parameter.
Results and Discussion
Drug‑Excipient Compatibility Study
Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FTIR) Study
The FTIR spectra of pure drug PFD and physical mixture of
PFD and PLGA 50:50 are shown in Fig. S3. The major peaks
shown to the drug PFD confirm the presence of different
groups. O = C stretch (α, β unsaturated ketone) at 1612 ­cm−1
in pure PFD that was obtained at the same 1612 ­cm−1 in the
physical mixture of PFD and PLGA. C-N aromatic stretch
vibration (3° amine) in pure PFD, and physical mixture
was 1274 ­cm−1 for both. All other peaks of C–C aromatic
stretching vibration, C-H aliphatic stretching vibration, and
C = C phenyl stretching vibration are obtained 1530 ­cm−1,
3054 ­cm−1, and 825.6 ­cm−1, respectively, in the narrow
range with similar intensity [4, 19]. Between the spectra
of PFD and PLGA, there was no significant shifting or loss
of functional peaks. Hence, FTIR confirmed no chemical
interaction between PFD and polymer.
Differential Scanning Calorimetric (DSC) Studies
DSC thermograms were generated for pure drug PFD,
physical mixture of PFD-PLGA 50:50, PLGA NPs, and
PFD-loaded PLGA NPs. PFD sample and physical mixture
showed an endothermic peak of PFD at 109 °C. The PLGA
NPs and PFD-loaded PLGA NPs had PFD peaks observed
at 109.81 °C and 108.11 °C. PLGA gives no peak as it is
amorphous in nature. The DSC thermogram of both pure
drug and physical mixture displayed the endothermic peak
at 109 ℃ which is indicative of drug in crystalline form [14].
PLGA presents only one step of weight loss between 170
and 245 °C. The DSC curve of 50:50 PLGA indicated ther-
mal transitions related to the glass transition of the polymer
at a temperature between 45.7 and 74.6 °C, with a midpoint
of 59.8 °C. The results show a broad endothermic peak
for PLGA around 59.8 °C, which is related to the evapo-
ration of residual water or solvent from the PLGA. Since

Preliminary Studies
Selection of Organic Phase
It is evident from the results that the physical characteristics
of organic solvents have a significant impact on NP prepa-
rations. The interfacial tension and viscosity of the solvent
were shown to be the key factors of particle size. PFD-loaded
PLGA NPs were prepared by single emulsion solvent evapo-
ration method. For this emulsion, organic phase and aqueous
phase were required together to aid the formulation of NPs
[20]. NPs are created by colliding and coalescing emulsion
droplets following organic solvent diffusion; their sizes are
dependent on stability of the emulsion droplets. The MPS and
EE% of PFD-loaded PLGA NPs made with different organic
solvents are depicted in Fig. S5 when stabilizer (PVA) is used
at a constant concentration of 1% w/v in water. The small par-
ticle size was obtained with a range of 301 ± 5 to 354 ± 5 nm
of ethyl acetate, acetonitrile, and ethyl alcohol (with partially
water soluble to fully water-soluble solvents). While DCM and
chloroform generated nanoparticles, particle size was higher
in 402 ± 7 to 552 ± 9 nm, respectively (water-immiscible to
partially water-soluble solvents). Table S1 in supplementary
material enlists the various physical properties of the organic
solvents studies before selection of organic phase.
The low interfacial tension between the aqueous and
organic phases, arising from their partially water-soluble
nature, was attributed to both the sufficiency of the stabi-
lizer’s protection against coalescence and the small parti-
cle sizes for ethyl acetate. DCM is immiscible with water;
almost every nanoparticulate formulation resulted in sub-
stantial aggregation when it was employed as the organic
phase solvent and formed a large mean particle size. DCM
formulations had greater EE and were most preferable to
form PLGA nanoparticles. Despite the presence of a sta-
bilizer, acetone and ethanol are fully miscible with water
and do not form stable emulsions between the organic and
aqueous phases. When the two phases are mixed, the PLGA
forms sub-micron particles, resulting in a high particle size
due to coalescence of droplets obtained in emulsion contain-
ing the acetone or ethanol as an organic phase [21].
Effect of Organic Solvent Volume
From the results shown in Fig S6, the volume of dichlo-
romethane in the organic phase of emulsion had appreciable
effect on the MPS. This change in particle size may be due to
the change in viscosity [22]. As the volume of dichlorometh-
ane increases, it leads to decreased viscosity of the organic
(internal) phase. That change in viscosity could affect the
emulsion droplet size in aqueous phase. During the solvent
evaporation, larger droplets break down into smaller parti-
cles which are solidified and form nanoparticles. The EE%
was not affected by the volume of dichloromethane.
Sonication Time
After the observation with different sonication time, the par-
ticle size of PFD NPs and EE% of PFD into NPs are shown
in Fig. S7. There were no significant changes observed
in EE% as the sonication time from 1, 5, 10, and 20 min,
respectively. The particle size of PFD NPs was influenced
with sonication time from 1 to 20 min. As the sonication
time increases, the particle size reduces from 698.7 ± 12.2 to
188.9 ± 7.2 nm and EE% from 46.77 ± 2.49%, 55.43 ± 1.22%,
68.89 ± 3.04%, and 61.34 ± 2.54% respectively. The nan-
oparticle size decreased when applied sonication time
increased. It can be attributed to the high released energy
in emulsification process, resulting in the formation of
smaller droplets which affect the size of polymeric nano-
particles. This increase may be because of de-emulsification
process or agglomeration [23]. The shape was also affected
by sonication time as the 1 min had irregular shape with
lump formation and 5, 10, and 20 min had spherical shape
of nanoparticles. The 20-min sonication time had very fine
particles, but they were extremely aggregated [22, 23].

Effect of X1 and X2 on Mean Particle Size
As mentioned in the “Materials” and “Methods” sections,
the nanoparticles were prepared using a single emulsion
solvent evaporation method. The response surface plot
(Fig. 2A) and contour plot (Fig. 2B) were generated to
understand the effect of X1 and X2 on MPS. The obtained
p-value for both independent variables was < 0.05 which
implied a significant effect of both independent variables
on the particle size, as per Table 3. The X1 variable's positive
coefficient suggested a positive correlation between both,
which suggests that when PLGA concentration increased, so
did particle size. Contrary to that, negative correlation was
observed between X2 and particle size suggesting decrease
in the particle size upon increasing the concentration of
PVA. This could be related to the dispersed phase viscos-
ity increasing in the single emulsion technique, resulting in
reduced net shear stress and larger nanodroplets. Further-
more, when the viscosity of the PLGA solution increases, it
becomes more difficult to disperse it into the aqueous phase,
resulting in larger nanoparticles [24].
Effect of X1 and X2 on Zeta Potential
The NPs having PLGA concentration allowed modification

Effect of X1 and X2 on % Entrapment Efficiency
As revealed from Fig. 5A and B, increasing the concentra-
tion of PLGA would increase entrapment efficiency. This
might be because increased polymer concentrations resulted
in more viscous organic solutions. As previously stated in
this study, the higher the viscosity, the lower the net shear
stress, resulting in bigger particle sizes. Larger nanopar-
ticles have enough surface area to entrap PFD molecules.
Increased viscosity, on the other hand, could prevent the
drug from diffusing from the organic to the aqueous phase,
promoting PFD entrapment. Smaller droplets develop as the
stirring speed increases, increasing the overall surface area
of the nanoparticles [24]. This provides the polymer matrix
with more room to accept more PFD molecules, resulting in
increased encapsulation efficiency.

Effect of Formulation Factors on In Vitro Release Studies
The drug release study of all the batches was carried out
including optimized batch as it showed the maximum EE.
The dissolution profile showed biphasic behavior consist-
ing of initial burst release followed by a slow-release phase
(Fig. 7). The presence of surface-free drugs can be attributed
to the initial burst release in the external phase and drug
adsorbed on the surface of particles while the slow release
was due to drug encapsulated within the PLGA matrix [28].
According to the release pattern, a loading dosage of around
40% of the drug might be released after 2–3 h of adminis-
tration as a burst release, while the rest (up to 80%) would
sustain the therapeutic dose for a long time (24 h), perhaps
preventing significant variations in the drug plasma level.
The release kinetics followed the Higuchi model
(r2 = 0.997). The drug release behavior from the polymer
matrix followed a biphasic pattern, with an initial burst fol-
lowed by a slower sustained release. The release of PFD
from PLGA nanoparticles may be majorly due to the diffu-
sion of PFD from the pores on the particle surface.

Accelerated Stability Studies
The freshly prepared and optimized batch of polymeric NPs
of PFD remained stable for more than 6 months, accord-
ing to the results. During the stability testing, the particle
size was significantly increased from 366 ± 9 to 370 ± 5 nm.
The change in particle size was negligible, showing that the
system was stable over 6-month duration within acceler-
ated conditions. Even though a low zeta potential suggests
an unstable colloidal formulation, the polymeric nano-
particulate formulation used in this study with a low zeta
potential was found to be stable for 6 months, indicating that
the zeta potential played no role in the formulated batches
stability. In this case, the protective feature and viscosity
delivered by the polymer (PLGA 50:50)-PVA may be a
contributing element for the system’s stability [31]. After
6 months, the entrapment efficiency of the optimized batch
was found to be 81.47 ± 1.00% which indicated that the drug
was retained within the nanoparticles during the stability
period. As shown in Table 7 the in vitro % cumulative drug
release at 12 h and 24 h was found to be 79.92 ± 3.01% and
83.47 ± 2.64%, respectively, after 6-month stability study
which indicates that there were no major changes observed.

Aerodynamic Characterization
The PFD-loaded nanoparticles (optimized batch) were taken
for the in vitro aerodynamic study. The deposition of PFD
nanoparticle-loaded DPI in the different chambers based on
particle size of the cascade impactor was evaluated. The
deposition of particles in various sections of the trachea
and lungs is determined by factors such as particle size and
shape. Particle size in the range of < 1 µm can reach deeper
to alveoli. The PFD-loaded PLGA nanoparticles were loaded
to cascade impactor. Fine particle fraction and extra-fine
particle fraction were found to be 71.39% and 28.83%. The
emitted dose from DPI was found to be 91.09%. FPF is
directly proportional to the in vivo drug deposition into the
lung. With a geometric standard deviation (GSD) of 1.92,
the optimized DPI revealed MMAD of the nanoparticles
particles with a size of 3.52 μm. An MMAD of less than
5 µm indicates targeted delivery to the lungs, whereas a GSD
of less than 3 indicates a narrow aerodynamic diameter dis-
tribution. Hence, prepared DPI has good potential to reach
deep into the lungs.
Conclusion
The PFD-loaded PLGA nanoparticles were successfully
prepared using the single emulsion technique. Overall, the
results show that drug:polymer concentration was the pri-
mary factor affecting particle size, whereas stabilizer (PVA)
concentration was primarily affecting entrapment efficiency.
For the formulation of nanoparticles with defined particle
size, entrapment efficiency, and drug release at 12 h and
24 h, a ­32 full factorial design was used as statistically
systematic approach. The sustained release behavior was
obtained during 24 h of PFD-loaded polymeric PLGA-NPs.
We conclude that as the concentration of PLGA is increased,
the mean diameter of the particle increases, which could be
due to an increase in the internal phase viscosity. The ideal
conditions for the preparation of PFD-loaded PLGA NPs
include a high PLGA concentration and PVA concentration,
as well as a higher organic solvent removal rate. PFD-loaded
PLGA NPs may be considered a promising effect for pul-
monary delivery of PFD for idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis
with possible clinical application. PFD-loaded optimized
PLGA-NPs were spherical as per the SEM study. The results
showed that increasing drug, polymer, and PVA concentra-
tions increased nanoparticle diameter; however, the organic
to aqueous phase volume ratio and sonication duration were
necessary for size optimization. The formulation of PFD-
