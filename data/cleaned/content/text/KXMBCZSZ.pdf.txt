Here,
 a well-controlled
 method  to  prepare
 magnetic/cisplatin-loaded
 nanocapsules
 with  different
 mor-
phologies
 has  been  developed
 by  using  a W/O/W
 double
 emulsion-solvent
 evaporation
 technique.
 This
study
 focuses
 on  the  inﬂuences
 of  solvent
 removal
 method
 and  existence
 of  stabilizers
 on  the character-
istics
 of  nanocapsules
 in  a double  emulsion
 procedure.
 Biodegradable
 poly(lactic-co-glycolic
 acid)
 [PLGA]
nanocapsules
 were
 used  for  encapsulation
 of  cisplatin
 and  magnetic
 nanoparticles.
 The
 effect
 of prepara-
tion
 conditions
 on  the size,  morphology
 and  surface
 properties
 of  PLGA  nanocapsules
 containing
 cisplatin
and
 magnetic
 nanoparticle
 were  investigated
 too.  Solvent
 removal
 from  the  double  emulsion
 was
 carried
out by using
 two  methods;
 slow  evaporation
 at  ambient
 condition
 and  fast  evaporation
 under
 reduced
pressure.
 PLGA
 nanocapsules
 were  prepared
 in  the
 presence
 of  stabilizers
 during
 the  ﬁrst
 and
 second
emulsiﬁcation
 steps  and  also  without  any  stabilizer.
 Changing
 in  the  morphology
 and  decrease
 in  mean
diameter
 of  magnetic
 nanocapsules
 were  observed
 when  slow  evaporation
 was
 exploited
 in comparison
with
 evaporation
 at  reduced
 pressure.
 It was found  that  the  loading
 efﬁciency
 of cisplatin  was
 dramat-
ically
 dependent
 on  the solvent
 removal
 method.
 This  step  had  a signiﬁcant
 effect
 on  morphology
 and
size.
 DLS  data  showed
 that
 the  mean  diameter
 of  magnetic
 nanocapsules
 was
 ranging  between
 142  and
384
 nm  with  polydispersity
 index  of  0.071–0.111
 with  zeta  potential
 from  −10.5  to−16.5  mV.  On  the
other
 hand,
 the  loading
 efﬁciency
 of cisplatin
 was  related
 to the  preparation
 conditions
 and
 was  between
41
 and  62%  (w/w).  The
 prepared
 nanocapsules
 were  characterized
 by  SEM  and  AFM  to observe
 surface
topography.
 These  micrographs
 reveal  a spherical
 shape
 of  magnetic
 nanocapsules
 with  smooth
 surface
and
 almost
 no  aggregation
 was  observed.
 TEM  analysis
 was  used  to  investigate
 the  internal
 morphology
of
 magnetic/cisplatin-loaded
 nanocapsules,
 too.

1.
 Introduction
Cisplatin
 (cis-diamine
 dichloro
 platinum
 II,
 CP)
 is
 a
 platinum-
based
 chemotherapy
 drug
 used
 to
 treat
 various
 types
 of
 cancers,
such
 as
 sarcomas,
 some
 carcinomas,
 lymphomas
 and
 germ
 cell
tumors,
 but
 dose
 limiting
 toxicities
 or
 intrinsic
 and
 acquired
 resis-
tances
 limit
 its
 application
 in
 many
 types
 of
 cancer
 including
prostate.
 A
 magnetic
 drug
 targeting
 is
 highly
 desirable
 for
 reduc-
tion
 of
 side
 effect,
 toxicity
 and
 prolongation
 of
 the
 pharmaceutical
drug
 efﬁcacy
 [1–3].
A promising
 way
 of
 optimizing
 action
 of
 cisplatin
 is
 to
 target
it
 to
 tissue
 sites
 via
 polymeric
 nanocapsules
 with
 a
 magnetic
 core.
The
 magnetic
 core
 can
 make
 it
 possible
 to
 direct
 the
 nanocapsules
to
 the
 desired
 target
 area
 by
 means
 of
 external
 magnetic
 ﬁelds,
 ﬁx-
ing
 them
 at
 the
 local
 site
 while
 the
 drug
 is
 released
 and
 acts
 locally
[4,5].
 The
 polymeric
 shell
 would
 have
 the
 role
 of
 transporting
 the
cisplatin
 and
 releasing
 it
 during
 biodegradation
 or
 diffusion
 [6,7].
Therefore,
 the
 dosage
 of
 the
 drug
 can
 be
 reduced
 and
 its
 side
 effect
is
 kept
 at
 minimum
 level.
 The
 use
 of
 magnetic
 nanoparticles
 for
drug
 delivery
 systems
 must
 address
 issues
 such
 as
 desired
 release
proﬁle,
 drug
 loading
 capacity,
 aqueous
 dispersion
 stability,
 biocom-
patibility
 with
 cells
 and
 tissue,
 and
 retention
 of
 magnetic
 properties
after
 modiﬁcation
 with
 polymers.
Morphology,
 particle
 size
 and
 release
 properties
 of
 nanocap-
sules
 are
 the
 key
 considerations
 to
 design
 nanoparticulate
 delivery
systems.
 Since
 the
 release
 proﬁle
 of
 drug
 dominantly
 depends
 on
polymer
 nature
 and
 morphology
 of
 nanocapsules,
 fundamental
understanding
 of
 the
 relationship
 among
 these
 key
 characteristics
and
 release
 mechanisms
 is
 essential
 to
 yield
 useful
 products
 [8].
It
 has
 been
 indicated
 that
 the
 morphology
 of
 nanocapsules
depends
 on
 the
 rate
 of
 polymer
 solidiﬁcation
 and
 solvent
 removal
at
 the
 interface
 [9]. The
 surface
 is
 smooth
 when
 the
 polymer
 pre-
cipitates
 slowly
 due
 to
 slow
 removal
 of
 the
 organic
 solvent
 [10].
However,
 there
 are
 few
 reports
 regarding
 the
 effect
 of
 processing
parameters
 on
 the
 internal
 morphology
 of
 nanocapsules
 prepared
by
 W/O/W
 double
 emulsion
 technique
 [10,11]. This
 study
 on
 mor-
phology
 seems
 necessary
 as
 a
 crucial
 factor
 affecting
 the
 drug
release
 from
 nanocapsules
 especially
 during
 the
 diffusion
 process.
Morphology
 of
 nanocapsules
 is
 dominantly
 determined
 by
 the
 pro-
cess
 conditions.
 Porous
 nanocapsules
 have
 a
 large
 surface
 area
and
 hence
 have
 a
 high
 initial
 burst
 [12].
 Method
 and
 rate
 of
 sol-
vent
 removal,
 dispersed-phase/continuous-phase
 ratio,
 polymer
concentration
 and
 stabilizer
 concentration
 in
 inner
 water
 phase
are
 critical
 factors
 in
 determining
 morphology
 and
 surface
 area
 of
nanocapsules
 [12].
Poly(lactic-co-glycolic
 acid)
 [PLGA],
 from
 the
 ester
 family,
 has
been
 widely
 used
 in
 the
 biomedical
 technology.
 PLGA
 is
 an
 FDA-
approved
 polymer
 for
 certain
 clinical
 uses,
 which
 have
 been
utilized
 widely
 in
 drug
 delivery
 systems
 as
 an
 effective
 encapsu-
lating
 material
 [13].
 A
 double
 emulsion
 method,
 adapted
 to
 the
particularities
 of
 drug
 to
 be
 encapsulated
 [11], was
 applied
 for
 the
preparation
 of
 the
 magnetic/cisplatin-loaded
 nanocapsules
 (MCPs).
This
 method
 was
 selected
 based
 on
 its
 simplicity
 and
 because
 it
can
 lead
 to
 nanocapsules
 having
 size
 suitable
 for
 drug
 targeting
applications
 [12]. The
 formation
 of
 PLGA
 nanocapsules
 from
 dou-
ble
 emulsion
 can
 be
 divided
 into
 two
 stages:
 (a)
 droplet
 formation
and
 (b)
 solvent
 removal
 and
 solidiﬁcation.
 Either
 of
 these
 stages
may  be
 the
 dominant
 one
 to
 determine
 morphology
 and
 release
characteristics
 of
 PLGA
 nanocapsules
 [14].
 Surfactants
 play
 a
 key
role
 in
 separation
 of
 the
 two
 phases
 (oil/water)
 to
 form
 the
 emul-
sion.
 The
 surfactants
 stabilize
 the
 dispersed
 phase
 droplets
 formed
during
 emulsiﬁcation,
 inhibit
 coalescence
 of
 droplets
 and
 deter-
mine
 particle
 size,
 morphological
 properties,
 surface
 composition
and
 release
 property
 of
 the
 nanocapsules
 [15].
Alex
 and
 Bodmeier
 [16]
 studied
 mechanisms
 of
 particle
 forma-
tion
 from
 a
 double
 emulsion.
 They
 found
 that
 some
 microparticle
characteristics
 such
 as
 drug
 loading
 efﬁciency,
 porosity,
 and
 sur-
face
 morphology
 are
 strongly
 related
 to
 the
 way
 of
 precipitation
of
 polymer
 layer.
 Particle
 morphology
 is
 dependent
 on
 the
 relative
rates
 of
 oil
 phase
 evaporation
 and
 diffusion
 of
 the
 inner
 aqueous
phase
 into
 the
 outer
 one;
 if
 the
 oil
 phase
 evaporation
 is
 faster,
 then
porous
 particles
 are
 obtained
 and
 vice
 versa
 [17].
 Jeyanthi
 et
 al.
 [18]
reported
 that
 the
 dilution
 solvent
 removal
 technique
 yields
 spheres
with
 a
 uniform
 honeycomb-like
 internal
 structure
 with
 pore
 sizes
depending
 on
 the
 extent
 of
 aqueous
 phase
 dilution.
 At
 higher
 dilu-
tions,
 pores
 were
 larger
 since
 removal
 of
 organic
 solvent
 was
 faster
and
 polymer
 precipitation
 became
 more
 rapid.
 However,
 they
 did
not
 report
 the
 effect
 of
 aqueous
 phase
 addition
 rate
 on
 the
 release
proﬁles.
In

our

previous

studies

[14,19],
we

prepared

polymeric
nanocapsules

by

using

a

double

emulsion

solvent

diffu-
sion/evaporation

technique.

However,

the

effect

of

process
parameters
 on
 the
 morphology
 and
 surface
 properties
 of
 nanocap-
sules
 was
 not
 investigated
 previously.
 First,
 we
 attempted
 to
encapsulate
 cisplatin
 and
 magnetite
 nanoparticles
 into
 PLGA
nanocapsules
 through
 double
 emulsion
 method
 by
 using
 of
different
 concentrations
 of
 two
 types
 of
 surfactants
 (Span
 and
Tween).
 The
 nanocapsules
 were
 ﬁnally
 prepared
 via
 evaporation
at
 reduced
 pressure
 and
 also
 at
 ambient
 conditions.
 Then
 they
were
 fullycharacterized
 by
 DLS,
 SEM,
 AFM
 and
 TEM
 and
 the
 effect
of
 preparation
 conditions
 of
 magnetic
 nanocapsule
 on
 the
 particle
size
 and
 morphology
 were
 studied.
 Furthermore,
 in
 vitro
 release
proﬁles
 and
 drug
 loading
 efﬁciency
 of
 magnetic/CP-loaded
 PLGA
nanocapsules
 were
 analyzed
 for
 better
 understanding
 of
 the
 effects
of
 preparation
 method
 on
 loading
 efﬁciency
 and
 release
 behavior.

4.
 Conclusions
The
 objective
 of
 present
 study
 is
 to
 develop
 a
 nanoparticulate
drug
 delivery
 system
 of
 anticancer
 drug
 “cisplatin”
 with
 higher
therapeutic
 efﬁcacy
 and
 fewer
 side
 effects
 to
 maintain
 its
 structural

 and
 suitability
 for
 magnetic
 drug
 targeting
 in
 targeted

 Also,

double

 (Span
and

 on
 size,
 morphology
 and
 surface
 property
 of
 nanocap-
sules
 was
 performed.
The
 mean
 diameter
 of
 the
 prepared
 nanocapsules
 ranged
 from
142
 to
 384
 nm.
 Fast
 solvent
 removal
 led
 to
 a
 loss
 of
 morpholog-
ical
 control
 and
 the
 nanocapsules
 turned
 into
 irregular
 shapes.
 It
is
 hypothesized
 that
 as
 the
 rate
 of
 solvent
 removal
 is
 increased;
more
 components
 are
 likely
 to
 disperse
 in
 the
 continuous
 phase
and

 disturbing

 droplet
 formation
 and
 stability.
 The
 fast

 (evaporation
 in
 reduced
 pressure)
 results
 in
 both

increase
 in
 surface
 roughness
 and
 mean
 diameter
 of
 the
 magnetic
nanocapsules.
 The
 increase
 in
 surface
 roughness
 is
 mainly
 due
 to
the
 fast
 solvent
 removal.
 As
 a
 conclusion,
 the
 results
 suggest
 that
the
 method
 of
 solvent
 evaporation
 from
 the
 oil
 phase
 (DCM)
 gen-
erates
 nanocapsules
 with
 a
 diverse
 structure,
 roughness
 and
 size.
The
 drug
 loading
 efﬁciency
 was
 between
 41
 and
 62%,
 relating
 to
the
 preparation
 conditions
 and
 the
 in
 vitro
 release
 proﬁles
 were
found
 to
 be
 dependent
 on
 the
 size
 and
 porosity
 of
 the
 magnetic
nanocapsules.
 The
 release
 rate
 increases
 with
 increasing
 in
 poros-
ity
 and
 size.
 Finally,
 it
 is
 believed
 that
 the
 prepared
 nanocapsules
are
 good
 candidate
 for
 cisplatin
 delivery
 system.
