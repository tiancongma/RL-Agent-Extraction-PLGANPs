## Materials Methods
PLGA (Resomer RG 50:50 H; inherent viscosity 0.41 dl/g) and cyclosporine were gift samples from Boehringer Ingelheim (Ingelheim, Germany) and Panacea Biotech (Mumbai, India), respectively. Sandimmune Neoral(r) (Novartis) was purchased from a local pharmacy store. Naproxen and Cremophor EL(r) were purchased from Sigma (St. Louis, MO, USA). Didodecyldimethylammonium bromide (DMAB) was purchased from Aldrich (St. Louis, MO, USA). Ethyl acetate and acetonitrile (HPLC grade) were purchased from Rankem Fine Chemicals (New Delhi, India). Thiobarbituric acid (GR grade), sodium lauryl sulphate (LR grade), sodium hydroxide (LR grade) and acetic acid (LR grade) were purchased from Loba chemie, India. Ultrapure water (SG Water Purification System, Barsbuttel, Germany) was used for all the experiments.

NPs were prepared by emulsion-diffusion-evaporation technique  with slight modifications. In brief, 10 mg of cyclosporine and 50 mg of PLGA were dissolved in 2 ml of ethyl acetate at 1000 rpm for 1 h. The organic phase was added dropwise, with constant stirring to 3 ml of 0.1% DMAB solution. The resulting primary emulsion was sonicated for 1 min with the help of a probe sonicator (UP200S Ultrasonic processor, Germany). To this emulsion, 30 ml of water was added with constant stirring at 1000 rpm resulting in nanoprecipitation. The resultant preparation was kept overnight on moderate stirring to evaporate the organic solvent.
2.3. Particle size and zeta potential of NPs
The particle size was measured by dynamic light scattering (Nano ZS, Malvern Instruments, Malvern, UK) and zeta potential was estimated on the basis of electrophoretic mobility under an electric field.
2.4. Entrapment efficiency
Entrapment efficiency was determined by centrifuging the drug-loaded NPs at 30,000 rcf for 30 min and separating the supernatant. The pellet was dissolved in acetonitrile and analyzed by reversed-phase (RP) HPLC system consisting of 717 plus auto sampler, pump controller and a Waters 996 PDA detector which are configured to Millennium32 software version 3.05.01. All separations were achieved on the Symmetry C18, 4.6 x 250 mm, 5 mm analytical column (Waters, USA) maintained at 70 degC. Acetonitrile:methanol:0.05 M phosphoric acid (45:40:15) was used as mobile phase at flow rate of 1 ml/min. The detection wavelength was 210 nm.
2.5. Effect of solvent on the particle size and entrapment efficiency
The NPs were prepared using various solvents and their combinations and the effect of solvent on the particle size, polydispersity index (PDI) and entrapment efficiency was studied.
2.6. Solubility study of cyclosporine in DMAB solutions
To evaluate the role of stabilizer in solubilization of the drug in aqueous phase, solubility of cyclosporine at various concentrations of DMAB was measured by shake flask method. Briefly, aqueous solutions containing different concentration of DMAB (0.05%, 0.1%, 0.25%, 0.5% and 1% w/v of aqueous phase) were taken into the vials to which 5 mg of cyclosporine was added. The vials were kept in the shaker bath maintained at 37 degC and 75 rpm for 5 days. The samples were withdrawn at an interval of 24 h until equilibrated solubility is reached. The withdrawn samples were centrifuged at 30,000 rcf and supernant were collected, diluted appropriately with the mobile phase and analyzed by validated RP-HPLC method.
2.7. Effect of stabilizer concentration on particle properties
The NPs were made with five different concentrations of DMAB, viz. 0.05%, 0.1%, 0.25%, 0.5%, and 1% w/v of the aqueous phase and its effect on the particle size and entrapment efficiency was evaluated.
2.8. Initial drug loading studies
The effect of initial drug loading on the particle size and entrapment efficiency was studied by preparing the particles with initial drug loading from 5 to 20% w/w of polymer.
2.9. In vitro drug release study
The prepared NP formulation was evaluated for in vitro drug release by dialysis membrane method. NPs containing 1 g of entrapped cyclosporine were redispersed in 1 ml of phosphate buffer containing 0.05% w/v of Tween 80 and placed in the dialysis bags with a molecular mass cutoff of 12,000 Da. The bags were suspended in 10 ml of 10 mM phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) and placed in shaker bath at speed of 100 rpm at 37 degC. The release medium was completely replaced with fresh medium at 6, 12 and 24 h on the first day followed by sampling at a 24 h interval. The amount of cyclosporine released in the medium was analyzed using validated HPLC method.
2.10. Hemocompatibility study of NPs
Fresh heparinized blood was obtained from the Sprague Dawley (SD) rats. Blood was mixed at 1:1 (v/v) ratio with water (positive control); with normal saline (NS) (negative control); 5 mg/ml of Cremophor EL(r) in NS; 2 mg/ml of Sandimmune Neoral(r) in NS; blank NPs equivalent to 10 mg/ml of drug-loaded NPs in NS; 0.1% DMAB in NS and cyclosporine NPs equivalent to 2 mg/ml, 10 mg/ml and 100 mg/ml of cyclosporine in NS. Samples were incubated at 37 degC for 12 h in a water bath. At predetermined time points (30 min, 6 h and 12 h), the samples were withdrawn and intact red blood cells (RBC) were separated from plasma by centrifugation at 6000 rcf for 5 min. Supernatant was collected and incubated for 30 min at room temperature to allow for hemoglobin oxidation. Oxyhemoglobin absorbance was measured spectrophotometrically at 540 nm. The percentage of RBC lysis was calculated based on the assumption that 100% RBC lysis resulted from a 1:1 (v/v) mixing of blood with distilled water.
2.11. In situ permeation studies
The in situ uptake studies were carried out by closed loop method . Male SD rats weighing between 200 and 240 g were used for the study. The overnight fasted rats were anesthetized by injecting Thiopental (50 mg/kg body weight). Midline incision was made on the abdomen and the intestinal segment of jejunum (length about 15 cm) was separated from the rest of the intestine by tying the distal end with thread. The drug suspension was made by dispersing the drug in sodium carboxy methyl cellulose solution followed by homogenization to get the drug concentration of 1 mg/ml. The above made cyclosporine suspension, Sandimmune Neoral(r) and drug-loaded nanoparticles each containing 1 mg drug were injected into the proximal portion of the intestine with the help of a syringe followed by ligation with the thread. The segment of jejunum was excised after 2 h and the intestinal content was washed with water. The intestinal contents were dissolved in acetonitrile and centrifuged. The clear supernatant was evaluated for drug content by validated RP-HPLC. The uptake was calculated indirectly by analyzing the % of drug, which remained unabsorbed in the intestinal lumen.
2.12. Bioavailability studies
For in vivo studies, male SD rats weighing 200-240 g were used. Sandimmune Neoral(r) and cyclosporine nanoparticulate formulations were administered to the overnight fasted rats by oral gavage tube at a dose of 15 mg/kg body weight of the animal. The blood samples (0.5 ml) were collected from the retro-orbital plexus under mild ether anesthesia at 2, 6, 12, 24 and then every 24 h for 7 days into heparinized microcentrifuge tubes (containing 50 U heparin/ml of blood). After each sampling, 1 ml of dextrose-normal saline was administered to prevent changes in the central compartment volume and electrolytes. Plasma was separated by centrifuging the blood samples at 6000 rcf for 5 min at 4 degC. To 250 ml of plasma, 750 ml of acetonitrile was added to precipitate proteins and 50 ml of 2 mg/ml of internal standard (Naproxen) was added. The samples were vortexed and centrifuged at 20,000 rcf for 15 min. The supernatants were separated and vacuum-dried in Maxi Dry Plus (Heto Lab Equipment, Germany). The residues were reconstituted in 120 ml of acetonitrile/water (50:50) and vortexed for 10 min, after which they were washed twice with 1 ml of n-hexane followed by centrifugation at 20,000 rcf for 15 min. The clear supernatants were analyzed for drug content by validated RP-HPLC.
2.13. Evaluation of the nephrotoxicity profile
Nephrotoxicity studies were performed on male SD rats weighing 200-240 g. The animals were housed in a temperature-controlled room (22 +- 2 degC) on a 14/10 h light/dark cycle. Standard pellet diet and water were available. The animals were randomly divided into four groups. Group-I was kept as control without any treatment; Group-II was treated with Sandimmune Neoral(r) (15 mg/kg/day); Group-III received blank NPs equivalent to that of drug-loaded NPs and Group-IV was administered with drug-loaded NPs equivalent to 15 mg/kg/day cyclosporine. All treatments were given perorally by gastric gavage through a metallic tube. The animals were fasted for 6 h before all the treatments. The treatment was continued for 30 days. After 24 h of last dose, the animals were sacrificed and blood samples were collected into heparinized micro centrifuge tubes and analyzed for biochemical parameters.
2.14. Estimation of BUN and PC levels
BUN and PC were determined colorimetrically using commercially available kits (Accurex Biomedical Pvt. Ltd, Mumbai, India).
2.15. Estimation of pro-oxidant/anti-oxidant balance
The MDA, a measure of lipid peroxidation, was assayed in the form of thiobarbituric acid reacting substances (TBARS) . The method works on the principle of formation of TBARS upon the reaction of MDA with thiobarbituric acid, which is a pink colored complex that has a peak absorbance at 532 nm. In brief, the reaction mixture consisted of 0.1 ml of 8.1% sodium lauryl sulphate, 0.75 ml of 20% acetic acid solution adjusted to pH 3.5 with sodium hydroxide and 0.75 ml of 0.8% aqueous solution of thiobarbituric acid was added to 0.1 ml of plasma sample or tissue homogenate. The volume of the mixture was made up to 2 ml with distilled water and heated at 95 degC for 60 min. After cooling with tap water, the mixture was centrifuged and the supernatant was analyzed at 532 nm spectrophotometrically. The amount of TBARS formed was calculated against the calibration curve prepared using MDA as standard. The renal MDA contents were expressed as nanomoles of malondialdehyde per milligram of protein.
2.16. Plasma levels of cyclosporine after last treatment
C2 (blood sample withdrawn 2 h post-dose) and trough cyclosporine levels (C0; blood samples drawn before the next oral dose) have been conventionally used to predict risk of toxicity or organ rejection. So, the plasma blood samples were withdrawn at 2 and 24 h after the last treatment. The plasma was separated and cyclosporine levels were analyzed by the RP-HPLC method as described previously.
2.17. Histopathological evaluation
The organs including kidneys, liver, spleen, jejunum, ileum and colon were isolated immediately after sacrificing the animal. The tissues were washed with ice-cold saline and fixed in a 10% neutral buffered formalin solution. The tissues were then embedded in paraffin blocks and used for histopathological examination. Five-micrometer thick sections were cut, deparaffinized, hydrated and stained with hematoxylin and eosin. The histological sections were examined under microscope (Leitz, Wetzlar, Germany) for the morphological changes. The morphometric analysis of the histological sections of kidneys were carried out for all the treatment groups. The diameters of the glomerular capillary tuft (CD) and the Bowman's capsule (BD) were measured with the help of an internal micrometer and the ratio CD/BD was calculated as an index of glomerular collapse .

## Results
PLGA NPs containing cyclosporine were prepared using DMAB as stabilizer. Use of DMAB led to smaller sized particles with narrow size distribution. The dispersion of the organic phase due to stirring resulted in irregular-sized globules in equilibrium with continuous phase. Sonication led to reduction in size of globules and addition of water destabilizes the equilibrium to force the organic solvent to diffuse to the continuous phase. This process led to nanoprecipitation resulting in the formation of NPs. Overnight stirring at room temperature ensured complete evaporation of organic phase.
The above-mentioned process parameters led to smaller particles with narrow distribution; however, entrapment efficiency was very low. Literature reports and the findings in our laboratory suggest that the choice of the solvent, stabilizer concentration, initial drug loading, method of preparation along with the physicochemical properties of drug can significantly alter the entrapment efficiency. Therefore, the process optimization of the cyclosporine-loaded NPs was taken up.
The influence of organic solvents on particle size and entrapment efficiency was studied using various solvents and their combinations as outlined in Table 1. The stabilizer concentration used was 1% w/v of aqueous phase. Ethyl acetate was found to be the ideal solvent for making the PLGA NPs with narrow size distribution; however, the entrapment was very low. The particles were also made with dichloromethane (DCM), chloroform and DCM:ethyl acetate (80:20) with not much success in entrapment, on the other hand these solvents also lead to larger particles with wide size distribution as shown in Table 1. The physicochemical properties of solvent like viscosity, vapor pressure and surface tension could play a crucial role in the overall particle properties. None of the solvents and their combinations tested could influence the entrapment efficiency suggesting the least possible role of organic solvent on cyclosporine entrapment under the studied conditions.

The stabilizer is another important factor, which can affect the entrapment efficiency of lipophilic drug as they have a tendency to form molecular aggregates known as micelles. The micellar arrangement is such that the hydrophobic portions of the stabilizer molecules form the hydrophobic core and the polar head groups remain in the periphery of the micelles. The micelles have a tendency to solubilize the lipophilic drug molecules inside the hydrophobic core resulting in an increased solubility of the lipophilic drugs. Solubility of cyclosporine was increased with an increased concentration of DMAB as shown in Table 2. The highest solubility of cyclosporine (644.2 +- 26.8 mg/ml) was found in 1% w/v DMAB solution.
All values reported are mean +- S.D. (n = 3).

Considering the concentration-dependent solubility of cyclosporine in DMAB solutions, drug-loaded NPs were prepared at varied DMAB concentrations (0.05%, 0.1%, 0.25%, 0.5% and 1% w/v). As DMAB concentration was decreased from 1% to 0.05%, increase in the particle size was observed, with the proportional increase in the entrapment efficiency (Table 2). The lowest particle size was obtained with the highest concentration of DMAB (1% w/v of aqueous phase), while the highest entrapment efficiency was obtained with the lowest DMAB concentration (0.05% w/v of aqueous phase). The concentration-dependent solubility of cyclosporine in the DMAB solutions was thought to be due to the formation of micellar structures resulting in poor entrapment at 1% DMAB. Considering the size and entrapment efficiency, 0.1% DMAB concentration was found to be optimal which resulted in the formation of monodispersed particles with the size of 143.3 +- 8.7 nm, zeta potential of 75.7 +- 3.1 mV (at pH 4.4) and entrapment efficiency of 71.9 +- 1.7% at initial drug loading of 20% w/w of polymer. The initial drug loading was also found to influence the particle size and entrapment efficiency. With increase in the initial drug loading from 5 to 20% w/w of polymer, the entrapment efficiency was increased (from 43.0 +- 3.7% to 71.9 +- 1.7%).
Several mechanisms are responsible for the drug release from the NPs including surface and bulk erosion, diffusion and desorption . The initial drug release from the PLGA matrix has been found to occur by the diffusion of drug from polymer matrix, whereas during the later phases the release is mediated through both diffusion of drug as well as the degradation of the polymer matrix itself. PLGA degradation occurs through a process of autocatalytic hydrolysis of ester bonds. The acidic monomers and the oligomers formed catalyze the further degradation of the polymer. Thus, any factor that influences the formation or retention of the acidic monomers in the particles could affect the polymer degradation rate and the release of the entrapped drug .
For conducting an in vitro drug release study for the formulation containing lipophilic drug, maintenance of the sink condition during the release study is the most important aspect. Since cyclosporine is a highly lipophilic drug; the sink condition has to be maintained during the release study, which was achieved by the addition of 0.05% w/v of Tween 80 in the phosphate buffer. Most of the drug-loaded nano- and microparticulate formulations show a biphasic release pattern wherein there is an initial burst release followed by a sustained release of the drug. However, low molecular weight PLGA NPs have also been reported to exhibit zero order release profile, particularly with lipophilic drug molecules . Similarly, the cyclosporine NPs also exhibited zero order release profile and showed sustained drug release for 23 days at a nearly constant rate (37 mg/day). The cumulative release was fitted into different release models: zero order, first order, Higuchi's square root plot and Hixson Crowell cube root plot. Zero order release pattern was best fitted with release profile of NPs with r2 of 0.9905.
As NPs are expected to remain in the blood circulation for longer time, their hemocompatibility was evaluated in vitro with the rat blood. Incubation of blood with an equal volume of water (as positive control) resulted in instantaneous RBC lysis. Incubation with 5 mg/ml of Cremophor EL(r) (which is one of the main components of the commercial formulation) and 2 mg/ml of Sandimmune Neoral(r) led to significant RBC lysis compared to NS (negative control). The RBC lysis in the presence of stabilizer was concentration- and time-dependent. The presence of DMAB at concentration of 1 mg/ml (0.1% w/v), resulted in 15.4, 30.9 and 65.8% of RBC lysis after 30 min, 6 h and 12 h incubation, respectively (Fig. 1). In contrast, blank as well as drug-loaded NPs (up to 10 mg/ml) did not cause significant hemoglobin release after 12 h incubation with blood, which was comparable to the percentage of RBC lysed in the normal saline group. However, cyclosporine NPs at concentration of 100 mg/ml enhanced the hemolysis after 12 h (Fig. 1).
The PLGA NPs used in the present studies were made with DMAB, which is a surface-active agent, and literature reports suggest that surface-active agents can compromise the membrane integrity . Thus, it is necessary to determine the effect of NPs on RBC lysis. Due to the presence of the DMAB stabilizer on the particle surface, the NPs should increase the hemolysis. However, it was hypothesized that because of NP formation from oil-in-water (o/w) microemulsion droplets, the hydrophobic portion of the stabilizer was immobilized within the NP matrix and hydrophilic head was localized on the surface of the NPs . This unique arrangement implies that DMAB no longer retained its surface-active properties and therefore, should not have caused RBC lysis. Incubation of blood with Cremophor EL(r) at a concentration present in the Sandimmune Neoral(r) has significantly enhanced the hemolysis, as it has been reported to possess inherent hemolytic activity. Sandimmune Neoral(r) group showed even more hemolysis as compared to the Cremophor EL(r) group. Cyclosporine also reported to have hemolytic activity, thus synergistic effect of Cremophor EL(r) and cyclosporine could result in the higher RBC lysis (Fig. 1).
Since our interest is in exploring nanoparticles for the oral delivery, in situ uptake studies were carried out to confirm the uptake of nanoparticles from the intestine, which is a prerequisite for orally administered colloidal delivery systems. The % intestinal uptake of cyclosporine as sodium CMC suspension, Sandimmune Neoral(r) and cyclosporine-loaded nanoparticles were compared. The cyclosporine nanoparticles showed significant higher intestinal uptake of around 90% as compared to Sandimmune Neoral(r), which showed around 55% uptake. The least uptake of only 10% was observed for the cyclosporine suspension (Fig. 2).
It has been observed that enhancement of electrostatic interaction between the mucosal surfaces and particulate carriers has a significant effect on their uptake and overall bioavailability . The epithelial cells in various tissues including gastro-intestinal tract carry negative charge on their surface due to the presence of negatively charged residues of proteins in the outer membrane of the cell. It has been anticipated that positively charged colloidal systems could interact strongly with negatively charged cells leading to better permeability and overall bioavailability. By virtue of their cationic nature, DMAB stabilized particles showed higher uptake; however, the role of particle size on the intestinal uptake cannot be ignored. Sandimmune Neoral(r) contains cyclosporine in the form of microemulsion, which can be readily solubilized and absorbed depending on its permeability from the intestinal wall. However, in case of the suspension, cyclosporine was present in powder form, which must be dissolved for subsequent absorption, which is a rate-limiting step for cyclosporine absorption due to its very low aqueous solubility. As a result, cyclosporine uptake from the suspension formulation was quite low (Fig. 2).
The incorporation of cyclosporine into PLGA NPs was explored as a method to increase the oral bioavailability of this drug. The mean cyclosporine concentration in the plasma after oral administration of cyclosporine NPs and Sandimmune Neoral(r) (as a reference) at 15 mg/kg single dose in rats are illustrated in Fig. 3. The relevant pharmacokinetic parameters including Cmax, Tmax and AUC0-inf are listed in Table 3.

A wide variability in the absorption phase was observed between Sandimmune Neoral(r) and cyclosporine NPs. Sandimmune Neoral(r) upon oral administration resulted in sharp Cmax within 2 h; however, the plasma concentration of cyclosporine decreased rapidly. Whereas, relatively slow increase and sustained plasma concentration of cyclosporine for a longer time were observed after administration of cyclosporine NPs, with significantly delayed Cmax occurring at 24 h, suggesting an obvious controlled release of cyclosporine from the NPs. The commercial formulation is a premicroemulsion concentrate containing cyclosporine. It rapidly releases cyclosporine upon exposure to the GIT fluids following oral administration leading to overshooting of the plasma levels of cyclosporine (Fig. 3). Since cyclosporine is a critical dose drug with narrow therapeutic window, the overshooting of plasma levels is undesirable, which leads to nephrotoxicity. On the other hand, the overshooting of plasma levels was absent in the cyclosporine NPs treated group as it showed controlled in vivo release of cyclosporine.
There was a marked difference in the AUC0-inf between Sandimmune Neoral(r) and cyclosporine nanoparticulate formulation. The AUC0-inf was higher in the NPs treated group with a relative bioavailability of 119.2% as compared to Sandimmune Neoral(r), indicating improved bioavailability of cyclosporine as a nanoparticulate formulation. The NPs are preferentially taken up intact from the GIT by the M cells in the Peyer's patches of gut associated lymphoid tissue (GALT) followed by their shuttling into systematic circulation and drug release in the blood. The uptake involves the process of mucoadhesion and transcellular or paracellular transit through the intestinal membranes. These processes along with the controlled release of cyclosporine from the nanoparticulate carriers could be responsible for the lower values of Cmax and delay in appearance of maximum concentration in NPs treated group.
Dai et al.  reported an overall increase in relative bioavailability of cyclosporine by 32.5% with the nanoparticulate formulations of Eudragit(r) as compared to Sandimmune Neoral(r). However, these formulations were not efficient in controlling the drug release and cyclosporine was detectable in plasma only for 3 days. On the other hand, the PLGA nanoparticulate formulation was very efficient in controlling the release of cyclosporine and showed controlled release of cyclosporine over a period of 5 days.
It is well known that nephrotoxicity is one of the major drawbacks associated with the cyclosporine therapy. It has been reported that by controlling the drug release and modifying tissue distribution of drugs using colloidal carriers, important adverse effects may be minimized or even abolished . The preparation of cyclosporine-loaded NPs have previously been addressed by different authors in order to increase the therapeutic efficacy of the drug; however, no reports on chronic nephrotoxic potential of such formulation were available. Therefore, considering the potential of PLGA NPs for controlling drug release, the present experiments were conducted to determine their in vivo nephrotoxic potential.
In spite of great therapeutic potential of cyclosporine as an immunosuppressive agent, the adverse effects, most important of which is the nephrotoxicity, limits its clinical use. Cyclosporine-induced chronic nephrotoxicity is characterized by increased BUN, PC and MDA levels in kidneys and plasma, in addition to the reversible and irreversible morphological changes in the kidneys. BUN is an important biochemical marker, which is routinely evaluated as an indicator of clinical renal function. Abnormal rise in the BUN levels indicates the dysfunction or damage to the kidney. Similarly, PC is another reliable marker of the clinical renal function. Creatinine, the end product of muscle metabolism, is also filtered and excreted by the kidneys. The elevated PC levels are also important indicative of renal dysfunction. The results of the study revealed that administration of Sandimmune Neoral(r) (15 mg/kg/day, orally for 30 days) caused an abnormal renal function in all rats, which was indicated by significantly (***P < 0.001) higher BUN and PC levels in that group as compared to control rats (Table 4). However, significantly (***P < 0.001) lower BUN and PC levels in the cyclosporine NPs treated group as compared to Sandimmune Neoral(r) revealed lower nephrotoxicity in that group. There were no significant changes in BUN and PC levels in the blank NP treated rats indicating that particles itself do not exert any nephrotoxic effects (Table 4).

Many reports have confirmed that oxidative stress is one of the major mechanisms involved in the cyclosporine nephrotoxicity. A close relationship between oxidative stress and CyA-induced nephrotoxicity has been confirmed in many experimental models , . Cyclosporine has been reported to enhance the MDA, an end product of the lipid peroxdation which serves as an index of the intensity of the oxidative stress, in the mesangial cells . Chronic cyclosporine treatment caused significant (***P < 0.001) increase in MDA levels in plasma as well as kidneys compared to control and blank NPs treated rats. However, significantly (***P < 0.001) lower levels of MDA have been observed in the cyclosporine NPs treatment group as compared to the Sandimmune Neoral(r) treated group, again indicating lower toxicity in that group (Table 4).
As shown in Fig. 4, the nanoparticulate formulation showed lower fluctuations in plasma levels of cyclosporine as compared to commercial formulation, which could account for lower nephrotoxicity of the nanoparticulate formulation. However, the role of modified tissue distribution of the drug due to the carrier system can not be ignored. Additionally, cyclosporine undergoes significant first pass metabolism following oral administration leading to the formation of large amount of potentially toxic metabolites, which could also account for the nephrotoxicity of the Sandimmune Neoral(r). Since nanoparticulate carriers bypass the first pass metabolism, it leads to a lower rate of the metabolite formation, which could be the other possible reason for lower nephrotoxicity of cyclosporine NPs.
The cyclosporine-induced nephrotoxicity is generally reversible; however, it may lead to irreversible morphological changes including glomerular collapse, tubular atrophy and interstitial fibrosis upon chronic treatment. , . These morphological changes lead to progressive loss of renal functions. The kidney section from the control animal (without treatment) showed normal proximal and distal tubules, glomerular apparatus and Henle's loops (Fig. 5A). The glomerula of Sandimmune Neoral(r) treated rats showed significantly (***P < 0.001) higher glomerular collapse and widespread tubular atrophy (Fig. 5B and Table 5). The blank NPs treated rat kidneys showed no sign of structural changes indicating that NPs themselves do not exert the toxic effects (Fig. 5C). Interestingly, cyclosporine nanoparticulate formulation treated rats showed significantly (**P < 0.01) lower glomerular collapse and only mild tubular atrophy as compared to Sandimmune Neoral(r) treated rats (Fig. 5D and Table 5). No sign of isometric vacuolation was observed in any of the rat kidneys.

Histopathological examination of the primary metabolizing organ (liver), spleen and the three intestinal segments viz. jejunum, ileum and colon were also carried out to study the presence of any inflammation after the administration of NPs. The control rats showed normal cytoarchitecture of hepatic parenchyma including Kupffer cells, hepatocytes and sinusoids (Fig. 6A). On the contrary, Sandimmune Neoral(r) treatment caused morphological alterations including disorganization of hepatic parenchyma with widespread cell swelling and congestion of sinusoids (Fig. 6B). However, no sign of morphological alterations was observed in the blank as well as cyclosporine NPs treated rat livers (Fig. 6C and D).
The spleen histology of animals treated with Sandimmune Neoral(r) and blank as well as cyclosporine NPs was not different from normal, untreated ones in terms of the lymphoid tissue mass, the sinusoids and the appearance of RBCs. The inflammatory cells (lymphocytes) are inherently present in the normal intestinal segments of jejunum, ileum and colon since GIT is constantly challenged by the ingested xenobiotics, food and microorganisms. Inflammatory response in the intestine is normally evidenced by the appearance of inflammatory cells in the muscle layer encircling the mucosa. In the present experiment none of the intestinal segments showed any inflammatory cells in the muscle layer (Fig. 7A-D). The mucosa showed no evidence of degeneration, suggesting that the NPs did not induce damage or initiate any inflammatory response in these tissues. A comparison of healthy tissue from untreated animals with that of animals treated with the Sandimmune Neoral(r) and blank as well as cyclosporine NPs showed no sign of inflammation after 1 month treatment (Fig. 8).

## Conclusion
The current study revealed the fact that cyclosporine-loaded PLGA NPs can be prepared with high entrapment efficiency using low stabilizer concentration. These particles were efficient in controlling the drug release in vitro and in vivo. The in vitro hemocompatibility studies revealed no significant hemolysis exerted by the NPs upon the incubation with the blood, indicating very good hemocompatibility. The nanoparticulate formulation showed significantly improved intestinal uptake and oral bioavailability as compared to Sandimmune Neoral(r). Most importantly, the nanoparticulate formulation exhibited lower nephrotoxicity upon the chronic administration in rats as compared to the commercial formulation. Together these results indicate that PLGA NPs could be a potential alternative to the existing cyclosporine treatment.
