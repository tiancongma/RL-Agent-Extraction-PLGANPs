## Materials Methods
An effective delivery system for P. gingivalis antigens should preserve
antigen integrity, enhance its own permeation across the mucosal bar
rier, and ensure optimal uptake by antigen-presenting cells (APCs) in the
GALT. In this study, biodegradable/biocompatible polymers such as
poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) and chitosan (CS) were used to
design the intended nanovaccine. PLGA is an aliphatic polyester char
acterized by its hydrolytic instability, which promotes timely degrada
tion upon contact with biological fluids (Frizzell and Woodrow, 2020;
Amin and Boateng, 2023). PLGA nanoparticles (NPs) can be coated with
CS, a naturally occurring cationic polysaccharide with hydrophilic,
mucoadhesive, and permeation-enhancing properties , to promote enhanced adhesion and penetration through the
negatively charged, hydrophilic mucus layer lining the intestinal
epithelium, thereby resisting removal by the continuous mucus flow in
the gastrointestinal tract . These properties allow
CS-coated PLGA (PLGA-CS) NPs to access the M cells of the GALT and be
translocated from the gut lumen to the underlying APCs (Borges and
Borchard, 2013). PLGA and CS degrade into lactic and glycolic acid, and
glucosamine, respectively, which are naturally metabolized by the body,
minimizing long-term toxicity concerns (Makadia and Siegel, 2011;
Kean and Thanou, 2010). Biodegradation ensures controlled release and
eventual clearance of the delivery system, enhancing safety and

Therefore, a new framework for the prevention and treatment of AD
has emerged, underscoring the importance of managing periodontitis to
mitigate the onset and progression of AD (Panza et al., 2019; Harding
et al., 2017). In this context, we hypothesize that vaccination against
P. gingivalis may be an innovative, feasible approach to break the link
between periodontitis and AD. While all vaccines proposed to date
against P. gingivalis have exclusively targeted periodontitis (Jong and
van der Reijden, 2014; O'Brien-Simpson et al., 2016), the suggested
mechanisms for P. gingivalis penetration in the systemic circulation and
the brain are an opportunity for developing a prophylactic vaccine
against AD. Our proposal focuses on developing an oral nanovaccine
containing P. gingivalis extract antigens for mucosal delivery exploiting
the mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue (MALT) components, particu
larly the gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT), for eliciting protective
mucosal and systemic immune responses . With this
purpose, we will trigger immunity by oral vaccination attempting to
break not only P. gingivalis ability to cross the BBB (systemic humoral
and cellular response), but also to avoid the crossing of the weakened
oral/intestinal barriers (mucosal immunity).

The present study aimed at the optimization, characterization, and
preliminary in vitro evaluation of a candidate PLGA-CS nanovaccine for
oral delivery of P. gingivalis extract antigens to the GALT, combining the
protective and controlled release properties of PLGA with the mucosal
targeting ability of CS. The proposed nanocarrier was prepared by

formula parameters following a Design-of-Experiment (DoE) approach
to target NPs with suitable physicochemical characteristics for oral de
livery and subsequent antigen encapsulation. Physicochemical and in
vitro characterization studies were performed, including mucoadhe

PLGA-CS NPs containing encapsulated PAP (PAP-loaded PLGA-CS
NPs), were prepared using a modification of previously described dou

2020; Vila et al., 2002). Briefly, PLGA polymer was dissolved in DCM to

2.1.7. In vitro release study of P. gingivalis extract antigens

The in vitro release studies were performed in triplicate by incubating
accurately weighed 5 mg of NPs (from batches with 1000 ug antigens
initially added within the internal phase) in 1 mL of 10 mM PBS pH 7.4,
under continuous shaking at 37 *C. At predetermined time intervals,
individual samples were centrifuged at 10,000 x g, 4 *C for 10 min
(Allegra 64R High Speed Centrifuge). The amount of protein released
was dosed in the supernatants by the MicroBCA(tm) Protein Assay Kit
(Pierce, USA), measuring the absorbances by spectrophotometry in a
microplate reader (FLUOstar Omega, BMGLabtech, Germany) at 562 nm
(n = 3). The supernatant from empty PLGA-CS NPs was used for baseline
correction.

2.1.5. Preparation of P. gingivalis extract-loaded chitosan-coated PLGA
NPs

PLGA-CS NPs containing encapsulated P. gingivalis extract antigens
(extract-loaded PLGA-CS NPs) at different concentrations were prepared
by adjusting the double emulsion (w1/o/w2) solvent evaporation

PAP (detailed in Section 2.1.3.) to the final optimized conditions derived
from the DoE-based experiments: PLGA amount 30 mg, PVA concen
tration 1.25 % (w/v), w1/o phase ratio 0.3. NPs were prepared across
PLGA:antigen ratios ranging from 120:1 to 30:1 (w/w), adding four
different initial amounts of extract antigens (250, 500, 750, and 1000
ug) within the internal aqueous phase. Following NPs preparation and
harvesting by centrifugation, the supernatant was preserved for quan
tification of the non-encapsulated extract antigens. NPs in the pellet
were resuspended with filtered purified water and freeze-dried. Each
formulation was prepared in triplicate. Controls consisted of empty
PLGA-CS NPs produced under identical conditions.

2.1.8. Stability and integrity studies

The stability of extract-loaded PLGA-CS NPs was assessed under
simulated gastrointestinal conditions in terms of particle size and PdI as
previously detailed . Briefly, NPs were first incubated
1:1 with simulated gastric fluid (SGF, 2.0 g/L NaCl adjusted with HCl 0.1
N at pH 1.2) for 2 h under gentle agitation (100 rpm, 37 *C), and then
incubated for further 4 h under the same conditions in simulated in
testinal fluid (SIF, 6.80 g/L KH2PO4, NaOH 0.89 g/L, pH 6.8). DLS
measurements were performed as described above (see Section 2.1.6.)
before and after incubation with SGF and SIF. Each experiment was
performed in triplicate and the average values were calculated.

in RPMI 1640 culture medium, supplemented with 10 % fetal bovine
serum (FBS), 100 units/mL of penicillin, 100 ug/mL of streptomycin

cells/mL. Four days prior to exposure to NPs, cells were seeded both in

this period, adherent M0 macrophages were gently washed with pre-

entiation and the plates were incubated for 24 h (37 *C, 5 % CO2). At the
end of the incubation period, plates symmetrically divided for M0 and
M1 THP-1 macrophages were used to evaluate the cytotoxicity and
cellular uptake of empty and extract-loaded PLGA-CS NPs in both phe
notypes as described in the following sections.

3.1.1.3. Prediction and validation of optimal formula parameters

optimal mucosal delivery, particle size emerges as a critical attribute
determining their ability to penetrate mucosal barriers and interact with

for colloidal stability. Surface charge can affect mucoadhesion, with

of CS coating may, however, be advantageous for preventing premature
antigen release in the gastrointestinal tract, ensuring that a higher
amount of antigen is retained within the NPs until they interact with the
APCs in the GALT. Therefore, the sustained protein release profile is
suitable for mucosal vaccination, including a prolonged stimulation of
the immune system.

PLGA-CS NPs did not confer any discernible variation in the uptake
when compared with empty NPs at equivalent concentrations, suggest
ing the inherent characteristics of the PLGA-CS nanocarrier, rather than
the payload, predominantly dictate the interaction and subsequent up
take by the THP-1 cells. Indeed, previous literature indicates that the
presence of positively charged CS molecules at the surface of PLGA NPs
induces a stronger interaction with cell membranes, leading to a higher
internalization (Dur'an-Lobato et al., 2015). While this is the case for
non-phagocytic cells in general, the same does not necessarily applies to
differentiated THP-1 phagocytic cells, which preferentially take up
highly positively or negatively charged NPs . This
premise was confirmed in this study, where non-purified NPs, presenting
a pronounced z-potential value (ca. +50 mV), demonstrated a clearly
superior internalization by THP-1 cells than their purified, less charged
