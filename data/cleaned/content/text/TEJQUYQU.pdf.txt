1.
 Introduction
Inorganic

luminescent

nanoparticles

(NPs)

have

recently
become
 interesting
 candidates
 for
 optically
 active
 labels
 in
 biosens-
ing
 and
 bioimaging
 [1]. Those
 NPs
 provide
 stable
 in
 time
 optical
response,
 with
 signiﬁcantly
 decreased
 photobleaching
 when
 com-
pared
 to
 e.g.
 organic
 dyes
 [2,3].
 Additionally,
 due
 to
 the
 large
two-photon
 absorption
 and
 action
 cross-sections
 for
 e.g.
 colloidal
semiconducting
 quantum
 dots
 [4,5], it
 is
 possible
 to
 excite
 visi-
ble
 emission
 under
 nearinfrared
 (NIR)
 excitation.
 This
 effectively
increase
 the
 signal
 to
 noise
 ratio
 by
 the
 suppression
 of
 autoﬂuo-
rescence
 background
 signal
 and
 allows
 for
 deeper
 penetration
 of
incident
 light
 through
 the
 cells
 and
 tissues.
 The
 nonlinear
 opti-
cal
 processes
 require,
 however,
 the
 use
 of
 expensive
 high
 power
femtosecond
 lasers
 sources,
 the
 issue
 which
 could
 be
 overcome
 by
the
 use
 of
 up-converting,
 lanthanide
 doped
 NPs
 as
 labels
 [6]. Due
to
 the
 ladder-like
 energy
 levels
 in
 lanthanide
 ions
 and
 possibility
of
 energy
 transfer
 processes
 between
 neighbor
 ions,
 the
 intense
visible
 luminescence
 after
 low
 power
 laser
 diode
 excitation
 from
NIR
 region
 of
 light
 can
 be
 observed
 [7]. In
 particular,
 NaYF4 NPs
co-doped
 with
 Er3+/Yb3+ or
 Tm3+/Yb3+ ions
 are
 considered
 as
 the
most
 efﬁcient
 up-converting
 materials
 for
 bio-related
 applications
[8]. The
 low
 phonon
 energy
 in
 NaYF4 matrix
 assures
 lack
 of
 non-
radiative
 depopulation
 of
 emissive
 energy
 levels
 [9], while
 good
matching
 of
 the
 energy
 levels
 position
 between
 Er3+ (or
 Tm3+)
 and
Yb3+ ions
 ensure
 effective
 energy
 transfer.
 In
 contrast
 to
 simul-
taneous
 two-photon
 absorption
 –
 process
 which
 includes
 virtual
states,
 the
 up-conversion
 process
 involves
 absorption
 of
 NIR
 light
(980
 nm)
 by
 Yb3+ ions
 through
 real
 metastable
 energy
 level,
 and
sequential
 energy
 transfer
 to
 emissive
 Er3+ or
 Tm3+ ions
 [10]. Up
 to
now
 several
 synthesis
 techniques
 have
 been
 developed
 and
 opti-
mized,
 and
 monodispersed
 up-converting
 NaYF4 NPs
 with
 various
sizes
 and
 shapes
 can
 be
 obtained
 [11]. Nevertheless,
 as
 the
 most
commonly
 used
 for
 colloidal
 NaYF4 NPs
 synthesis
 wet
 chemistry
techniques
 usually
 yield
 hydrophobic
 NPs,
 with
 oleic
 acid,
 oley-
lamine
 or
 trioctylophosphine
 oxide
 ligands
 stabilizing
 their
 surface,
the
 efﬁcient
 and
 straightforward
 NPs
 surface
 hydrophilization
 for
any
 of
 bio-related
 applications
 remains
 the
 main
 challenge
 [12].
The
 general
 surface
 functionalization
 strategies
 for
 up-converting
NaYF4 NPs
 include:
 ligand
 exchange
 or
 oxidation,
 covering
 the
NPs
 with
 hydrophilic
 silica
 shell
 or
 layer-by-layer
 assembly
 [13].
Very
 recently
 novel
 approach
 for
 up-converting
 NPs
 aqueous
 phase
transfer
 have
 been
 proposed
 by
 our
 group
 [14], which
 was
 based
on
 the
 encapsulation
 of
 multiple
 NaYF4 NPs
 within
 the
 multi-
layer
 nanocarriers
 with
 sizes
 below
 200
 nm.
 Rocha
 et
 al.
 [15]
 have
shown
 the
 possibility
 to
 co-encapsulate
 Nd3+ ions
 doped
 LaF3 NPs
and
 gold
 nanorods
 inside
 biocompatible
 polymeric
 nanosystems
for
 efﬁcient
 photothermal
 therapy
 applications.
 The
 encapsulation
of
 lanthanide
 doped
 NPs
 seems
 to
 be
 beneﬁcial
 for
 bio-related
applications
 because
 of
 the
 improved
 solubility,
 high
 long-term
 col-
loidal
 stability
 and
 biocompatibility
 of
 the
 obtained
 vehicles,
 with
simultaneous
 possibility
 of
 co-encapsulation
 with
 other
 photoac-
tive
 compound
 (i.e.
 organic
 photosensitizers)
 while
 simultaneous
preservation
 of
 the
 optical
 properties
 of
 the
 hybrid
 cargo
 loaded
inside
 the
 liquid
 core.
Nevertheless
 the
 precise
 choice
 of
 an
 appropriate
 nanocarrier
preparation
 technique
 is
 largely
 dependent
 on
 the
 physicochemi-
cal
 properties
 of
 the
 active
 cargo
 and
 the
 nanocarrier
 components,
i.e.,
 surfactants,
 oils
 and
 polymers
 [16]. Recently
 the
 most
 effective
approaches
 for
 inorganic
 or
 theranostic
 cargo
 encapsulation
 are
the
 nanoprecipitation
 technique
 [17], the
 layer-by-layer
 adsorp-
tion
 of
 polyelectrolytes
 on
 oppositely
 charged
 matrices
 [14,18], the
solvent
 diffusion
 method
 [19,20]
 and
 the
 single
 or
 double
 emul-
sion
 solvent
 evaporation
 approach
 [21,22]. Among
 the
 reported
techniques,
 increasing
 attention
 has
 been
 devoted
 to
 the
 double
emulsion
 solvent
 evaporation
 process,
 since
 it
 leads
 to
 the
 simul-
taneous
 hydrophilic
 and
 hydrophobic
 compounds
 encapsulation
in
 micro-
 or
 nano-scale
 form
 [23]. Thus,
 these
 systems
 seems
 to
 be
the
 most
 favorable
 in
 cosmetics,
 pharmaceutic
 and
 especially
 ther-
anostic
 application
 with
 the
 aim
 of
 taking
 advantage
 of
 their
 double
(or
 multiple)
 compartment
 structure
 where
 active
 compounds
 (i.e.
hybrid
 ﬂuorophores
 as
 inorganic
 NPs
 and
 organic
 photosensitiz-
ers)
 may
 also
 migrate
 from
 outer
 to
 inner
 phase,
 providing
 a
kind
 of
 reservoir
 suitable,
 i.e.
 to
 enhance
 their
 spectroscopic
 and
functional
 properties
 for
 targeted
 NIR-induced
 photodynamic
 ther-
apy
 (PDT)
 of
 some
 cancers
 [21,23]. The
 main
 issue
 when
 using
double
 emulsion
 process
 is
 the
 nanocarrier
 production
 in
 a
 well-
controlled
 manner,
 with
 homogeneous
 droplet
 size
 by
 optimizing
different
 process
 variables
 and
 by
 using
 the
 appropriate
 biocompat-
ible
 and
 biodegradable
 substrates
 [23].
 As
 it
 has
 been
 proved
 before
an
 application
 of
 biocompatible
 polymers
 from
 polyester
 family,
approved
 by
 the
 US
 Food
 and
 Drug
 Administration
 for
 use
 in
 human
therapeutic
 and
 clinical
 devices,
 can
 enhance
 the
 active
 molecules
encapsulation
 as
 well
 as
 improve
 the
 nanocarrier
 stability
 and
 bio-
compatibility
 [17,24]. In
 the
 last
 decades
 poly(lactic-co-glycolic
acid)
 (PLGA)
 became
 the
 most
 attractive
 representative
 of
 the
aliphatic
 polyester
 family,
 since
 it
 degrades
 slowly
 via
 hydrolysis
in
 aqueous
 environments
 into
 the
 biocompatible
 products
 of
 lactic
and
 glycolic
 acid,
 releasing
 the
 encapsulated
 agents
 over
 a
 period
of
 weeks
 to
 months
 [25]. Besides
 the
 wide
 range
 of
 erosion
 times,
PLGA
 possess
 also
 tuneable
 mechanical
 properties,
 thus
 it
 has
 been
extensively
 studied
 for
 the
 development
 of
 novel
 nanosystems
for
 controlled
 delivery
 of
 active
 molecules
 as
 drugs,
 proteins
 and
other
 macromolecules
 in
 commercial
 use
 and
 in
 research
 [24,25].
Although
 PLGA
 is
 a
 material
 that
 possesses
 many
 advantages
 for
drug
 delivery
 and
 therapeutic
 applications,
 reproducible
 formation
of
 PLGA-origin
 nanocarriers
 for
 effective
 encapsulation
 of
 inor-
ganic
 diagnostic
 materials
 such
 as
 up-converting
 lanthanide-doped
NaYF4 NPs
 by
 means
 of
 double
 emulsion
 method
 for
 future
 thera-
nostic
 application,
 can
 be
 challenging.
The
 work
 reported
 here,
 extends
 our
 recent
 studies
 on
 encapsu-
lation
 of
 different
 types
 of
 organic
 and
 inorganic
 ﬂuorescent
 agents
designed
 for
 theranostic
 applications
 via
 new
 emulsion-templated
approaches
 [14,17,18,26]. The
 main
 goal
 of
 the
 present
 contribu-
tion
 focus
 on
 synthesis,
 evaluating
 long-term
 colloidal
 stability
 and
optical
 properties
 of
 newly
 designed
 nanostructure:
 small
 (∼5
 nm
in
 size)
 up-converting
 NaYF4:Er3+,Yb3+ NPs
 encapsulated
 via
 dou-
ble
 emulsion
 evaporation
 process
 stabilized
 by
 a
 biocompatible
polyester
 (PLGA)
 and
 nonionic
 surfactants
 (Span
 80
 and
 Crempohor
A25)
 with
 various
 hydrophobicity.
 We  showed,
 that
 the
 rationally
designed
 nanocontainers,
 for
 luminescence
 NaYF4:Er3+,Yb3+ NPs,
with
 double
 (or
 multiple)
 compartment
 structure
 can
 enhance
 the
properties
 of
 colloidal
 polymeric
 nanocarriers,
 enrich
 their
 multi-
functionality,
 and
 further
 open
 new
 perspectives
 in
 applications
 by
co-encapsulation
 of
 hybrid
 cargo
 with
 both
 diagnostic
 and
 thera-
peutic
 activity.
