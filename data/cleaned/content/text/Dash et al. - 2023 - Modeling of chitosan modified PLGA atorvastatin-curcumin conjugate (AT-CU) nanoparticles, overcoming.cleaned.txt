4.1. Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR), mass spectroscopy, high
performance liquid chromatography (HPTLC), and melting point analysis

Using NMR spectroscopy (Bruker BioSpin GmbH, SPECT), a mag
netic field was applied to the atomic nucleus to identify the chemical
composition and molecular structure of the AT-CU molecule. The tem
perature was set at 298 *C with DMSO as the solvent, and the frequency
of the spectrometer was 700.18 Hz . The molecular
mass of the AT-CU conjugate was determined using mass spectroscopy
(XEVO-G2XSQTOF#YFA1829). The high-performance thin layer chro
matography (HPTLC) (the instrument used: CAMAG) was done using
chloroform: methanol (7:3) as mobile phase to estimate the purity of the
AT-CU. All three methanolic samples (AT, CU, and AT-CU) of 7 ul and
10 ul were applied to a 10 x 10 cm precoated silicagel aluminium plate
(60 F254; Merck Pvt. Ltd) with a thickness of 0.5 mm. The mobile phase
was prepared by mixing chloroform and methanol (7:3 v/v). WinCAT
software (CAMAG) was used to generate the HPTLC data. Furthermore,
the melting point (G LAB melting point apparatus) was checked for AT,
CU, and AT-CU.

3.2. In silico molecular docking study

Molecular docking studies were performed to verify the binding re
lations between AT, CU and AT-CU with potential target proteins related
to atherosclerosis at the molecular level. The linking between them in
terms of energy calculation aims docking score which declares the
predicted interaction between them. Six potential target proteins were
selected for in silico docking analysis and the minimum binding energy
indicates a strong binding score. In this study we investigated the
atherosclerotic potential of AT, CU and AT-CU using a guided molecular
docking approach with a view of their affinity towards the types of
proteins, CASP3 (PDB ID-2XYG), MAPK3 (PDB ID-4QTB), MAPK1 (PDB
ID-6SLG), AKT1 (PDB ID-6CCY), NOS2 (PDB ID-3E7G), and PTGS2 (PDB
ID-5IKR). The best potential target was scrutinized basing on the highest
negative docking score. The x ray crystal structures of the proteins were
retrieved from the Research Collaboratory for Structural Bioinformatics
Protein Data Bank (RCSB PDB). BIOVIA discovery studio 2021 client
software was used to optimize the PDB coordinates of the target protein
and the ligand molecule prior to docking analysis. The coordinates were
stable in conformation and had the least energy state. The binding
pockets of the target protein were investigated using CAST p 3.0. In
order to perform the docking analysis, autodock tools like Autodock vina
1.1.2 was used (Trott and Olson, 2010). Other software used here in
cludes MGL tool 1.5.6, Marvin sketch, and Pymol for its interaction and
visualization study. The parameters used for docking are default
parameters.

4.2. Fourier transforms infrared spectroscopy (FTIR spectroscopy)

The therapeutic effectiveness of the drug may fluctuate due to po
tential chemical interactions between different components. The
chemical stability of the synthesized AT-CU conjugate was verified using
FTIR spectral data. The sample was molded in to pellets by using po
tassium bromide (KBr) and analysed by placing the pellets in the sample
holder (FT/IR-4600, Jasco) .

4.3. UV spectrophotometer

The wavelength at which the AT-CU conjugate had maximum ab
sorption was checked by using ultraviolet spectroscopy (Thermo scien
tific, Evolution 201-UV-Visible Spectrophotometer).

3.3. Molecular dynamic simulation

In order to verify the structural stability and correlate our experi
mental findings MD simulation studies were performed. The Desmond
Schrodinger package, version 2022-1 with OPLSforce field was used in
molecular dynamic (MD) simulation to assess and interpret the docked
calculation of the highest binding affinity docked complex. For MD
simulation, the Apo-PTGS2, AT with PTGS2, CU with PTGS2, and AT-CU
withPTGS2 docked complex conformers were selected. All the structures
were solvated individually in the triclinic box using water molecules as
solvent and were neutralizing the system using Na+ and Cl-ions. Each
system was then minimized to avoid steric hindrance and atomic
clashes. The system was then processed through NVT ensemble and
finally 50 ns of production run were performed with default parameters
.

4.4. Saturation solubility study

Sufficiency of AT, CU, and AT-CU (5 mg) were dissolved in 25 ml of
distilled water, 0.1 N HCL, and 6.8 phosphate buffer sequentially over
the course of 72 h in a magnetic stirrer set to 500 rpm at 37 *C. The
resulting solution was then sonicated for 30 min to investigate the sol
ubility enhancement of the conjugate. Finally the solution was centri
fuged at 10000 rpm for 10 min and it was stabilized for 5 h before being
filtered (0.45 um syringe) and subjected to UV spectrophotometric
analysis . A molecular docking study was conducted
to experimentally demonstrate the precise binding interaction that
contributes to improving the solubility of AT-CU in water using auto
dock tools like Autodock Vina 1.1.2.

An attractive direction to explore in order to strengthen the con

the application of nanotechnology. Particularly in the context of car

3.1. Conjugate synthesis

The conjugate was synthesized following Dash, 2023, in brief prior to
AT-CU conjugate synthesis the atorvastatin was subjected to esterifica
tion, lactone prevention, and finally substitution of the hydroxyl group
with methyl group (Fig. 1). Following these procedures led to the initial
esterification of atorvastatin: Five drops of sulphuric acid were added to
a 100 ml methanolic solution of atorvastatin (2.465 mmol) at 0 *C for 10
min. 10% sodium bicarbonate aqueous solution was then added to the
resulting solution after stirring for 6 h at room temperature. Next
washing the organic layer of the mixture with brine and extracting it
with ethyl acetate, it was dried on magnesium sulphate. After that the
prevention of lactone formation was achieved by taking the esterified
atorvastatin (3.483 mmol) in adequate amount of dichloromethane and
to that solution, 17 mg of para-toluene sulphonic acid was added, the
resulting solution was allowed to stir for about 4 h at reflux. 20 ml of
10% sodium bicarbonate solution was introduced to quench the reaction
then the product was extracted with dichloromethane followed by
washing with brine and finally dried with magnesium sulphate. The
replacement of the methyl group with a hydroxy group was achieved by
introducing 30 ml of tetrahydrofuran into the previous lactone protected
structure of atorvastatin (1.436 mmol). Then 7.8 ml of 1 M sodium bi
carbonate solution was added to that above solution and stirred for 72 h
at room temperature. It was then mixed with 10 ml of 1 M aqueous
hydrochloric acid solution. The solution was extracted by diethyl ether,
organic layer was washed with brine and dried with magnesium sul
phate, and then the organic layer was evaporated under reduced pres
sure to get the product. The crude product was refined by using silica gel
chromatography (ethyl acetate/n-hexane) to obtain the pure product.
The synthesis of AT-CU was achieved using an equimolar ratio of

with dichloromethane, the solution was again rinsed, and the combined

For the study, the animal house at the School of Pharmaceutical
Sciences, Siksha 'O' Anusandhan (Deemed to be University), Bhuba
neswar, India, provided thirty male, 8-week-old Swiss albino mice
weighing 20-25 gm. Each animal was housed in a conventional, clean
poly-acrylic cage, and the room was kept at a temperature of 23 *C with
a relative humidity of 65-85% and a light-dark cycle of 12:12 h. The
animals had unrestricted access to food and water. For the induction of
atherosclerosis, the mice were provided with high fat diet i.e. western
diet for period of 12 weeks. The western diet was enriched in saturated
fat, cholesterol and cholate . The mice were randomly assigned
in to the following six groups (n = 5): group I was treated as normal
control i.e without high fat diet (HFD), group II did not receive any
treatment (i.e only HFD), group III and group IV was administered with
AT (2.05 mg/kg) and CU (102.3 mg/kg) respectively along with HFD,
group V and group VI was given lower (50 mg/kg) and higher dose
(104.3 mg/kg) respectively of AT-CU with HFD. Each group was
administered with corresponding formulations once in a day for 10

8.6. In vitro release study

The cumulative release curves in Fig. 9 represent the drug release
behaviour of drug AT-CU from the nanoparticles as well as pure AT-CU.
Further readings for pure AT-CU were not taken after 24 h because the
release was found to be 38.78% and 38.52% at 18 h and 24 h respec
tively. The dissolution of pure AT, CU along with AT-CU has been con
ducted and given in the supplementary files (Fig. S-8). At 18 hrs the %
drug release was found to be 38.78% for AT-CU, whereas for AT the %
drug release was 17.36%, and for CU it was 14.71%. Beyond that time no
significant drug release was observed up to 36 hrs. In terms of nano
particles, they are a component of a controlled drug delivery system in
which the drug is released in a controlled manner and have proven to be
extremely effective in modifying drug release behavior. The prepared
polymeric nanoparticles had a better drug release profile than the pure
drug AT-CU. One possible explanation for this behavior is the presence
of the drug AT-CU in the colloidal range in the prepared polymeric
nanoparticles, which facilitates drug release from the nanoparticles. On
the contrary, pure drug particles are coarse, which impedes their release
(Almehmady et al., 2023; Pourseif et al., 2023). Due to the burst release,
the cumulative AT-CU release from PLGA-AT-CU NPs (F1) hit 71.5%
after 18 h. Because a strong burst release might weaken the effectiveness
of a drug and possibly have negative consequences on the body, hence it
should be avoided (Yoo and Won, 2020; Beig et al., 2022). After 18 h,
the cumulative AT-CU release from CS-PLGA-AT-CU NPs was 50.32%,
46.92%, 43.27%, 37.12%, and 24.63% for the F2, F3, F4, F5, and F6
formulations, respectively. Better sustained release was demonstrated
by CS-PLGA-AT-CU NPs (F2-F6), but since the sustained release of F5
and F6 was significantly less, F4 was determined to be the optimum
formulation. The primary release rate of CS-PLGA-AT-CU NPs was much
slower than that of PLGA-AT-CU NPs which signifies the drugs adsorp
tion to chitosan on the surface of nanoparticles. Furthermore, the release
rate of AT-CU is gradually decreased by increasing the amount of chi
tosan in the nanoparticles. This finding demonstrated that the burst
release issue of PLGA-AT-CU NPs could be successfully resolved by CS-
PLGA-AT-CU NPs, leading to more regulated and persistent release of
AT-CU. As ester is very susceptible to degradation, an analysis of the
degradation of the AT-CU conjugate in phosphate buffer (pH 6.8) for up
to 54 h and in 0.1 N HCl (pH 1.2) for 2 h was done using UV visible
