Investigating the structural characteristics of nanoencapsulation
white tea extract using polylactic glycolic acid (PLGA)

Received: 11 April 2024 / Accepted: 8 September 2024 / Published online: 28 September 2024

Abstract
The objective of the present study is to optimize the extraction of white tea extract (WTE) containing the highest concen-
tration of polyphenolic compounds (TPC) under ultrasound intensity, ultrasound time, and different solvents, and then
to investigate the effect of nanoencapsulation using PLGA on the structural characteristics of the optimized extract. The
results of the investigation showed the effect of the time and intensity of ultrasound as well as the type of solvent used on the
extraction performance of the extract. Thus, the optimal conditions were obtained by using methanol solvent and applying
15 min of time and 70% ultrasound intensity to extract the extract with the highest antioxidant capacity and maximum TPC,
respectively, at the rate of 77.65% and 68.38 mg/g. The preparation of white tea extract nanocapsules (WTE-NCs) using
PLGA was based on solvent-emulsion evaporation and ultrasound. WTE-NCs were investigated in terms of efficiency and
release of WTE micro-coating and structural properties through particle size determination, morphological analysis, and
FT-IR. The micro-coating efficiency was 91.89% on the first day, which decreased to 76.16% after 22 days. The particle size
analysis results showed that WTE-NCs with a particle size of 68.36 nm increased to 447.23 nm after six months. In terms
of morphology, WTE-NCs had a spherical structure. The present study’s findings showed that micro-coating using PLGA
can effectively protect and cover WTE.
Keywords  Polyphenol · White tea · Ultrasound extraction · Microencapsulation · Polylactic glycolic acid
Introduction
Tea is an agricultural product and an export commodity
worldwide. Different types of tea are produced in other
countries based on people's tastes, habits, and cultures. Due
to the special aroma and taste produced from the shoots of
this plant [Camellia sinensis (L.) O. Kuntze], tea is one of
the most popular drinks among all people in most countries
of the world. White tea is produced from the young shoots
of the Camellia sinensis plant and is not oxidized. White
tea is mainly harvested from the buds or very young leaves
of the plant before they fully open and are processed by air
drying; This has caused the special taste and smell of white
tea and its popularity (Zohora and Arefin 2022). White tea
is made from very young tea leaves and/or buds that are
picked before they completely open and then dried in the
air. Despite the fact that the quality of commercial tea is
affected by various processing techniques, catechins, caf-
feine, and amino acids found in fresh tea leaves are three
crucial factors. The most important catechin compounds in
tea are epicatechin (EC), epicatechin gallate (ECG), gallo-
catechin (GC), epi-gallocatechin (EGC), epi-gallocatechin
gallate (EGCG12), and gallocatechin gallate (GCG), in addi-
tion, another abundant compound in tea can be called amino
acids, including theanine (Hinojosa-Nogueira et al. 2021;
Wen et al. 2020; Williams et al. 2019).
When it comes to phenolic chemicals, every plant mate-
rial is different. As a result, research into the most efficient
extraction method and the possible applications of various
processing techniques in making good use of solvents is
crucial. Also, there are technological parameters affecting

vent and its combination with other solvents, solid to solvent
ratio, pressure, and time used in extraction (Şahin and Şamlı
2013). Therefore, it is crucial for practical uses that the
extraction method be run under ideal conditions. Based on
studies to extract bioactive compounds from different plant
organs, especially for heat-sensitive compounds, ultrasound
technology is known as a new and successful method with
high performance and minimal solvent and time consump-
tion. This method is accepted as a green extraction method
due to no damage to bioactive compounds. The recovery of
bioactive chemicals from plant materials and the improve-
ment of food industry processing are both aided by ultra-
sound-assisted extraction (Yusoff et al. 2022).
Taste is the most important factor affecting the sensory
quality of white tea, which depends on several compounds
such as amino acids, catechins, and various oxides of this
compound, as well as soluble sugars present in tea leaves. To
put it simply, catechins are what give tea its signature bitter
flavor and astringent quality (Zhou.et al. 2022). Therefore,
micro-coating technology can be used to cover unpleas-
ant taste, smell, and color and prevent its adverse effect on
the quality of food (Suhag and Nanda 2015; Ahmadi et al.
2022). Another reason for using micro-coating is its delayed
release and ease of use. By using a simple method, liquid
food composition can be converted into a solid form (with
the ability to flow freely). Microencapsulation is also used
to prevent adverse reactions between active compounds and
food components and control active compounds' release.
Microencapsulation is the process by which live cells are
encased in a shell material, shielding them from the damag-
ing effects of their surroundings while still allowing nutri-
ents to diffuse into and out of the matrix and promote their
survival (Vivek 2013). Using emulsion systems is one of
the most important methods of producing food compounds
that contain bioactive compounds. Different emulsions and
methods of double emulsion production are used in making
practical microparticles and nanoparticles and for micro-
coating and controlled release. Considerations in selecting
a polymer include: the intended nanoparticle size range,
surface properties, biocompatibility, and biodegradability.
Furthermore, the physicochemical properties of the carrier,
such as water solubility, are determined by the structure of
the polymer (Elsabahy and Wooley 2012; Yoo et al. 2010).
In the pharmaceutical and food industries, for instance,
micro-coating systems based on glycolic acid and lactic acid
copolymer particles (polylactic glycolic acid) are used to
treat a wide range of conditions, from cancer to tissue engi-
neering (Chen et al. 2014; Zakaria et al. 2022). Considering
the importance of the bioactive compounds of plants, the
present study aims to optimize the extraction conditions of
white tea extract based on the highest total phenol content
and antioxidant capacity using the response surface method
and then to investigate the effect of nanoencapsulation of the
optimized extract using polylactic glycolic acid (PLGA) on
the structural properties through particle size analysis, con-
trolled release rate, morphological structure determination
by SEM and TEM, interactions using FT-IR.
Materials and methods
Materials
The city of Babolsar was the source of the white tea that
was purchased. Chemicals, including ethanol, metha-
nol, folinic acid reagent, gallic acid, sodium carbonate,
2,2-diphenyl-1-picrylhydrazyl reagent (DPPH), polyvinyl
alcohol, and poly (lactic-co-glycolic acid), were obtained
from Sigma, Germany.
Ultrasonic extraction
Under a magnetic stirrer (Big squid white, IKA, Germany)
at 200 rpm, 0.5 g of WTE powder was mixed with 1:10
volumes of various solvents (ethanol, methanol, and etha-
nol/methanol combination). Ultrasonic probe processor
(UP-200H, Hielscher, Germany) was used to blend the
dispersions for 5, 10, and 15 min at varying sonication
intensities (40, 70, 100%). After that, the samples were
centrifuged at 4000 rpm (Universal, Sigma 2-16kL type,
Germany) for 10 min at room temperature. The extracts
were smear-free with Whatman No. 1 paper before being
concentrated in a rotary evaporator (Heidolph model lab-
orota 4001) at 40 °C in a vacuum until all of the solvent
was gone.
Physicochemical tests
Total phenolic content
The test tube included 500 μL of WTE and 2.5 mL of
Folin–Ciocalteu reagent that had been diluted 10:1. A 7.5%
sodium bicarbonate solution was added after 5 min, and the
mixture was left at room temperature for an additional hour.
After 60 min of dark storage, the absorbance of the solu-
tions was measured at 765 nm using a spectrophotometer
(Ultraspec 2000, Pharmacia Biotech, England). Sheikhole-
slami et al. (2021) defined TPC as the amount of gallic acid
in milligrams per gram of dry weight sample.

Experiments design
Extraction conditions of WTE rich in polyphenols, antioxi-
dant capabilities, and extraction efficiency were optimized
using the response surface approach and Design Expert ver-
sion 10 software's Box–Behnken design. During this study,
the effect of three independent variables including solvent
(ethanol, methanol, and ethanol/methanol), intensity (40,
70, and 100%), and time (5, 10, and 15 min) of ultrasound
on TPC, DPPH radical inhibition rate, and WTE efficiency
was evaluated. Experiments were analyzed with 13 levels
with five repetitions at the central point, and Design Expert
software version 10 was used to draw optimization diagrams.
Optimum extract concentration
From the best extract, different ratios of 20, 40, 60, 80, and
100 mg/mL were made. Then, the amount of phenolic com-
pounds and the percentage of inhibition of free radicals in
each concentration were measured, as in the previous experi-
ments, and the effect of the extract concentration on TPC,
the DPPH radical inhibition rate was investigated.

an appropriate surfactant. Twenty mg of PLGA, 2 mL of

Fourier transform infrared (FT‑IR) analysis
After preparation, a Fourier transform infrared spectrom-
eter (IR solution, Shimadzu Corporation, Japan) was used to
investigate the interaction between the compounds in WTE
and lyophilized nanocapsules. This was done by subject-
ing WTE powder, lyophilized WTE-NCs, and PLGA mixed
with KBr powder to a wavelength range of 4000–400 ­cm−1
(Shashidhar and Manohar 2018). Also, in order to evalu-
ate the stability of lyophilized WTE-NCs, they were stored
at room temperature for six months. The storage stability
was evaluated by observing how little change there was in
the chemical compositions of the WTE-NCs over that time
period.
Statistical analysis
Minitab software (version 17) was used to conduct a sta-
tistical analysis of WTE-NCs in order to assess particle
properties, micro-coating effectiveness, and release. Data
are reported as mean ± standard deviation (SD) and analysis
was done in triplicate.
Results and discussion
Total phenolic content (TPC) of WTE
The results of the investigation according to Fig.  1
showed that with the increase in the intensity and time
of ultrasound, the total phenol content of WTE increased
significantly (P < 0.05). Ultrasound can disrupt the cell
membrane by causing cavitation and thus increase TPC.
By producing cavitation, ultrasound can damage cell
membranes, increasing the amount of phenol in the final
product. The optimal solutions for the extraction perfor-
mance of polyphenols were determined to be an ultrasound
intensity of 70% and an ultrasound duration of 15 min.
The effectiveness and yield of ultrasonic extraction are
directly influenced by factors including the manner of
applying ultrasound radiation (direct or indirect), extrac-
tion parameters, such as duration, temperature, type of
solvent employed, and concentration (Tiwari 2015). As
a result of the potential for phenolic compounds to be
degraded and/or oxidized and the subsequent production
of extremely reactive hydroxyl radicals when utilizing high
frequencies or high powers, applying a long-term, continu-
ous treatment during extraction is not advised in practice
(d’Alessandro et al. 2012). It is important to remember

that utilizing greater ultrasonic time and power destroys
phenolic components and is a loss of time and energy
(Luo et al. 2020). The results of the present study showed
that the highest amount of TPC was found in methanolic
extracts (68.4 mg GAE/g), ethanol/methanol combination
(55/1 mg GAE/g), and ethanol (37.30 mg GAE/g), respec-
tively. Obviously, the total phenolic content extracted by
ultrasound and methanol was significantly higher than
that of ethanol solvent and ethanol/methanol combination.
According to this result, it can be stated that ultrasonic
waves propagate more easily through the liquid medium,
especially in the methanol solvent. Bindes et al. (2019)
reported that a higher amount of TPC (12,318 mg/L) from
the extract of green tea leaves due to the abundant extrac-
tion of polyphenolic compounds including catechin and
its compounds such as EC, EGC and EGCG12, as well as
other components such as Caffeine and gallic acid were
obtained under optimal conditions of extraction by ultra-
sound (77 °C, 73 g/liter ratio of tea to water and 77%
ultrasound intensity). In addition, the TPC of green tea,
oolong tea, and black tea extracts was 192.76, 180.99, and
163.13 mg GAE/g, respectively (Islam et al. 2018).
Antioxidant properties
The antioxidant capacity of WTE is depicted in Fig. 2 as a
function of solvent extraction and sonication. According to
Fig. 2, 15 min of ultrasound at 70% ultrasonic intensity and
methanol resulted in the maximum antioxidant activity. The
reason for this is the high release of bioactive compounds
present in green tea leaves as a result of the rupture of cell
structures by ultrasound treatment, which with the high
emission of ultrasound waves in the methanol solvent has
caused the proper penetration of this solvent into the white
tea leaves. In fact, by combining methanol in the solutions
before applying ultrasound, the most bioactive compounds
were released based on their solubility in the solvent during
the extraction process.
Maximum free radical scavenging activities (DPPH)
were 31.3, 77.7, and 53.3 μg/mL for ethanol, methanol, and
ethanol/methanol, respectively. It can be concluded that

solvents, such as ethyl acetate and n-hexane, revealed that

activity compared to the other extracts (Faramayuda et al.

cells is the result of their solubility, which is caused by the
polarity of the compounds. Therefore, in order to extract
plant extracts, it is better to choose the solvents used accord-
ing to the solubility of bioactive compounds. For this reason,
hydroalcoholic mixtures are the most suitable solvent sys-
tems for extraction due to the different polarities of bioactive
compounds including phenolic compounds and the safety of
this system for human consumption (Muñiz-Márquez et al.
2013).
Extraction efficiency
Based on the results obtained in Fig. 3, increasing the time
and intensity of ultrasound has led to a gradual increase
in extraction efficiency. As sonication duration and inten-
sity increased, the extraction yield gradually increased.
Increasing the ultrasound intensity from 40 to 80% led to
an increase in the rate of sonochemical reaction, which was
reduced by applying 80% ultrasound intensity. Increasing
the extraction efficiency by increasing the intensity of the
ultrasound helps to increase the solubility and decrease
the viscosity, thereby accelerating the mass transfer. Its
extraction efficiency is explicitly defined based on the time
required for extraction. Nevertheless, protracted ultrasound
exposure can reduce extraction efficiency due to oxidation.

ultrasound improves extraction efficiency compared to con-

refer to the report of Vázquez and colleagues that extrac-

ciency in the recovery of anthraquinones from the stems of

the extraction efficiency obtained using the Soxhlet method
(34 mg/g), and which also involved cutting down on solvent
usage and cutting down on extraction time (from 16 to 2 h)
(Vázquez et al. 2014).
Figure 3 illustrates how the extraction efficiency is drasti-
cally altered when solvent extraction and sonication inten-
sity are applied together. Because ultrasound shrinks the
particles, the contact area between them expands, allowing
more of the solvent to permeate the plant tissue. Figure 3
displays the results, showing that methanol has the largest
impact on extraction success. Nawaz et al. (2020) found
that an increase in solvent polarity improved extraction per-
formance. Since methanol is more polar than ethanol, it is
much more effective as an extraction medium. Solubilization
occurs when a solvent generates sufficient interactions with
a solute to overcome the solute's inherent molecular attrac-
tion to itself.
Extraction optimization
The phenolic components and antioxidant qualities were
used to determine the optimal circumstances, which included
using an ultrasound application time of 15 min and an ultra-
sound intensity of 70%. Under optimal conditions, the data
obtained (at a confidence level of 0.95 percent) with R2
higher than 0.95 were 68.4 mg GAE/g and 77.7% for TPC
and DPPH radical inhibitory capacity, respectively.

Characterization of WTE‑NCs particles
One of the crucial properties of nanoparticles that can be
manipulated is their size (Jong and Borm 2008; Kulkarni
and Feng 2013). This property can be changed by altering
the test circumstances, such as molecular weight or copoly-
mer concentration. According to the study's findings (Fig. 4),
the characteristics of a particle's size are greatly influenced
by time, so as time went on, the particle's size increased. For
example, the particle's size, M/z, Span, and zeta potential
went from 68.36, 76.80, 1.096, and 10.2 nm on the first day
of production to 447.23, 5868, 1.660, and 57.5 nm after six
months (Table 3). In the current study, the dispersion index
(PDI) is about 0.1, which proves the creation of a homoge-
neous system. The polydispersity index (PDI) is a dimen-
sionless measure of particle size distribution, and it should
be minimized that it is an essential factor for developing a
proposed system for the delivery of effective compounds.

The PDI index, which ranges from zero for uniform, high-
quality samples to one for nonuniform samples, measures
the uniformity of the emulsion particles. The nanoencapsu-
lation made in this study exhibited homogeneous particles,
according to the results. In a previous study, PDI values of
guabiroba fruit extract coated with PLGA ranged from 0.24
to 0.43, indicating polydisperse polymer systems for all
nanoparticles studied; they reported that the micelles made
with guabiroba fruit extract and PVA (surfactant) during the
emulsification process significantly increased the PDI values
of the loaded particles (Pereira et al. 2018). On the first day,
WTE-NCs exhibited the smallest span, indicating a more
uniform nano-colloidal system and a narrower size distri-
bution. The mass transfer of solute molecules (both WTE
and PLGA) from the organic phase to the aqueous phase
is accelerated throughout the mixing process, especially at
higher speeds, leading to the production of smaller parti-
cles and a more uniform supersaturation level throughout
the final mixture.
On the other hand, the molecular size (or weight) of the
copolymer used to make micro-coatings can considerably
affect the particle size, resulting in the smallest particle size
being produced by the copolymer with the lowest molecular
weight (Chow et al. 2015). However, this problem causes
nanoparticles to grow in a liquid medium, which results in
the loss of these particles' nano properties because nanopar-
ticles are unstable to agglomeration and bulking. It grows
over time by gathering together, and the size of the nanopar-
ticles increases quickly. Particle sizes for PLGA nanoparti-
cles loaded with cinnamon bark extract have been reported
to range between 140 and 250 nm in one study (Hill et al.
2013), which is larger than the particle size in the present
investigation on the first day. The particle size of saffron
extract coated with whey protein concentrate (WPC) and
pectin has also been studied. In this study, it was found that
the W/O/W emulsions made by simultaneous (simultane-
ous method) and sequential (sequential method) absorption
of WPC-pectin have significant droplet sizes of 536.3 and
482.3 nm, respectively. According to this study's research-
ers, the usage of two biological polymers (WPC and pec-
tin) may be to blame for the increase in particle size and
thickening of the outer layer of multiple W/O/W emulsions
(Esfanjani et al. 2015). Finally, it can be said that various
operating conditions, such as the external/internal phase
ratio, the speed at which the emulsion is stirred, the volume
and temperature of the water used for dilution, the amount
of polymer, and the concentration of stabilizer, all affect the
properties of particles (Armendáriz-Barragán et al. 2016;
Mora-Huertas et al. 2010).
Release and efficiency of WTE‑NCs
The amount of white tea extract released into the outer aque-
ous phase of the double emulsion (W/O/W) was examined,
and the effectiveness findings are shown in Table 4. White
tea extract was coated with PLGA copolymer as a double
emulsion. The release percentage after 22 days of storage at
30 degrees Celsius for the emulsion stabilized with PLGA
copolymer and WTE carrier (dispersed phase of primary
water-in-oil emulsion) was 15.73%. The micro-coating effi-
ciency, as shown in Table 4, was 91.89% on the first day and
fell to 76.16% after 22 days. Jiménez-Alvarado and Beristain
(2009) used a protein–polysaccharide-based water-in-oil-
in-water double emulsion to study the iron's capacity for
micro-coating. They demonstrated that the release of iron
that has been trapped in the phase is considerably affected by
strengthening the wall around the double emulsion oil drop-
lets (outer wall). Curcumin was successfully encapsulated by
PLGA and PDDA in a different study, with an efficiency of
91%. The increase in encapsulation efficiency was attributed
to the double emulsion's increased resistance; in fact, emul-
sions stabilized with two layers have a high ability to pre-
serve microencapsulated compounds due to their high wall
strength (Zakaria et al. 2022). Similar to the current study,
Gao et al. (2020) reported that 95.64% of curcumin was suc-
cessfully microencapsulated by PLGA (Gao et al. 2020).
Additionally, higher EE values reported by Pereira et al.
(2015) for guabiroba fruit extract equal to 98.5% (Pereira
et al. 2015) support the findings of the current study.

Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FT‑IR)
Different functional groups and bonds in the chemical struc-
tures of the samples absorb specific frequencies of IR rays.
Infrared spectroscopy is a suitable tool for studying struc-
tural changes in the micro-coating process. Therefore, in
the present study, the structural changes of WTE, WTE-
NCs samples after preparation and after six months, and
the structure of PLGA are investigated and shown in Fig. 6.
In WTE spectroscopy (-WTE6), a long peak was observed
at 3398 ­cm−1 wave number; in addition, there were several
peaks in the wave numbers 2923, 2368, 1699, 1650, 1456,
1141, and 1035 ­cm−1 on the WTE spectroscopy. According
to previous works, the peaks at 3438 and 2926 ­cm−1 are asso-
ciated with the stretching vibration of hydroxyl compounds,
the peak at 1699 cm1 in the range of 1820–1660 ­cm−1 is
associated with the asymmetric stretching vibration of car-
bonyl groups C=O, and the peak at 1650 ­cm−1 is associated
with C=C alkene bond groups (Nandiyanto et al. 2019). In
the spectrum of PLGA polymer, a broad peak at the wave-
number of 3454.27 ­cm−1 corresponding to the hydroxyl
group was observed. Also, PLGA has peaks related to ali-
phatic CH stretch (2921.95 ­cm−1), CO ester (1737.74 ­cm−1),
and C–O ether group (1103.20 ­cm−1), which has been
reported in studies of wavenumbers similar to the present
study in PLGA copolymer (Gaspar et al. 2018; Nandakumar
et al. 2013). According to the results, several peaks were
observed in the WTE sample, some of which were removed
or shifted by micro-coating it with PLGA, so that the peaks
in wave numbers 3398, 2923, 1141, and 1035 ­cm−1 have
shifted to wave numbers 3463, 2948, 1164, and 1093 ­cm−1,
respectively. An increase in the intensity of some peaks was
observed in the WTE-NCs spectrum (1757 ­cm−1) com-
pared to the PLGA spectrum (1737/74 ­cm−1). However, a
change in the intensity of the peaks was observed in the
range of 1000–1500 ­cm−1 and there was a chemical inter-
action between WTE and PLGA nanoparticles. Based on
previous research, the peaks at 3364 ­cm−1 and 3431 ­cm−1 in
the spectra of PLGA nanoparticles and PLGA micro-coated
farnesol, respectively, are associated with environmental
humidity (Gürsu 2020). In the present study, a broad peak
at 3463 ­cm−1 wave number was observed in two spectra of
WTE-NCs after preparation and WTE-NCs/6 months after
six months, which can be in line with the mentioned study.
Finally, based on the results, it can be stated that the micro-
coating of WTE by PLGA caused changes in the intensity
and position of the absorption peaks; these changes can be
attributed to the chemical and physical interaction between
the hydroxyl, carbonyl, and alkene groups of the extract and
the ether, ester, and aliphatic groups of the PLGA copoly-
mer. After 6 months, no new peaks or any obvious changes
were observed in the spectrum of WTE-NCs/6 months,
which indicates that WTE-NCs/6 months maintain their
normal state; accordingly, WTE can be successfully micro-
coated by PLGA copolymer.
Conclusion
New studies confirm that ultrasonic and solvent extractions
are viable options for white tea. Maximum phenolic compo-
nent content, DPPH free radical inhibition percentage, and
extraction efficiency by methanol solvent, 70% ultrasound
intensity, and 15 min of ultrasound application yielded the
best results for extracting white tea. In addition, white tea
extract's antioxidant activity boosts as its concentration
rises. After being optimized with PLGA copolymer, white
tea extract microencapsulation efficiency was 91.89%. PDI
values near to 0 indicated that the resulting nanoencapsula-
tion contained homogenous particles, and examination of
the nanocapsules revealed a particle size of 68.36 nm. Both
morphological analysis and FT-IR spectroscopy indicated
that white tea extract and PLGA copolymer might undergo
partial reactions. According to these findings, PLGA copoly-
mer can be used to create nanoencapsulation as a delivery
vehicle for phenolic compounds found in white tea and other
beneficial chemicals.
