## Abstract
Background

Lung cancer remains a leading cancer type, but current
chemotherapy is limited by issues including poor drug delivery,
toxicity, and resistance. To address these challenges, we developed
a novel PLGA-PEG-LHRH (PPL) nanoconjugate system for improved
drug delivery. Curcumin, known for its anticancer and P-gp
inhibition properties, was co-loaded with bcl2siRNA (bclsR) to
inhibit the bcl2 protein, thus overcoming both resistance
mechanisms.

Background

Lung cancer is the most frequent subtype of cancer, causing death
in both men and women. It is predicted that by 2035, 3 million
cases of lung cancer will be prevalent globally [1, 2]. The World
Health Organization (WHO) reports that lung cancer is greatly
increasing the disease burden worldwide with 1,350,000 new
instances (12.4% of all new cancer cases) resulting in 17.6% of all
cancer-related deaths in 2020 . Tobacco use, occupational
asbestos exposure, cannabis or marijuana use, radon exposure, air

pollution, exposure to the group 1 carcinogen arsenic, inflammation
and cellular damage during respiratory infections, inflammation
and scarring associated with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease,
and a family history of lung cancer are the common etiological
factors for the development of lung cancer . According to
histology, there are two types of lung cancer: small cell lung cancer
(SCLC, 13%) and non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC, 85%).
Additionally, NSCLC is further classified as big cell carcinoma (510%), squamous cell carcinoma (25-30%), and lung
adenocarcinoma (40%) . Treatment for lung cancer involves
the combined use of radiation therapy, chemotherapy, and/or
surgery depending on the stage and condition. Most lung
malignancies are identified at advanced stages, with local tumor
invasion or distant metastases, and are thus not amenable to
surgery because of the lack of early-stage diagnostics, resulting in
poor 5 year survival rate. Therefore, the only options for treating
lung cancer currently are chemotherapy and radiation therapy [5,
6]. However, chemotherapy encounters several difficulties, such as
poor bioavailability of the drug, systemic cytotoxicity, rapid
elimination, non-specific drug distribution, and the emergence of
drug resistance which is a major concern [1, 7]. The major causes
of resistance to drugs are pump and non-pump resistance. The
pump resistance is caused by the action of proteins termed ABC
transporters, such as P-Glycoprotein (p-gp), which are responsible
for drug efflux and therefore lowering intracellular concentration of
the drug [8, 9]. There are reports on the suppression of this pump
resistance with p-gp inhibitors. However, despite the suppression of
drug efflux pumps, cells can still survive because of activated nonpump resistance. Activation of cell apoptosis mostly via Bcl-2
protein is the primary mechanism of non-pump resistance .
Formulations based on short-interfering RNA (siRNA) have
the potential to treat a variety of genetic targets by silencing them
effectively and specifically [8, 11]. The majority of anticancer
medications concurrently activate the cellular pump and non-pump
defense, preventing cell death. Therefore, it is important to
concurrently block both pump-dependent and non-pump
mechanisms of resistance in cells to successfully reduce the overall
resistance to chemotherapy . The current strategy for addressing
issues related to drug resistance is focused on combination
treatment targeting both the resistance-causing mechanisms. The

Curcumin, a natural polyphenol produced from the rhizome of
Curcuma longa (turmeric), is a potential anticancer drug 
because of its capacity to trigger cancer cells to undergo apoptosis
without having harmful effects on healthy cells [13, 14].
Curcumin's potential to inhibit nuclear factor-kappa B (NF kappa B),
which controls apoptosis, cell proliferation, and inflammation in
healthy cells, is the primary cause of its anticancer properties
. According to recent studies, curcumin also has a
potential inhibitory effect on the overexpressed p-gp of tumor cells
[13, 15, 18]. However, despite its promising properties, its clinical
use has been constrained because of its poor water solubility
(0.125 mg/L), poor bioavailability, severe metabolism, degradation,
and quick excretion . To circumvent these
restrictions, numerous techniques have been utilized with Nano
Drug Delivery Systems, such as liposomes, inorganic carriers,
protein-based nanocarriers, and polymeric nanoparticles for
improved local retention and safer systemic delivery [14, 16].

Polymeric nanoparticles (PNPs) are sub-micron carriers consisting
of an oily core encapsulated within a polymeric-based shell, with
surfactants present at the interface . Due to the existence of a
polymeric wall, PNPs have a high drug-loading capacity, are
physicochemically stable, and are resistant to enzymatic
destruction. Additionally, PNPs may improve the pharmacokinetic
profile of hydrophobic medicines and reduce their toxicity [21, 22].
PNPs can easily deliver curcumin and offer longer circulation,
greater permeability, and resistance to metabolic processes .
Among the different polymers utilized in the cancer drug-based
formulation, the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) recognized
the poly (DL-lactide co-glycolide acid) (PLGA)-based

to its relatively low toxicity, biocompatibility, biodegradability,
non-immunogenicity, and sustained release capabilities [23, 24].
Moreover, PLGA is widely used to transport proteins, peptides,
small-molecular-weight substances, and nucleic acids such as small
interfering RNA (siRNA) [25, 26]. One major limitation of curcumin
is its short circulation time, moreover, nanoparticles also suffer

similar problems due to RES (reticuloendothelial system) uptake
[23, 27]. Hence, the stealth coating of the nanovesicle is essential to
have prolonged circulation times. The PEGylation of PLGA NP can
be used to prevent the opsonin binding, extend the nanoparticles'
duration of circulation, and reduce RES uptake [23, 28, 29].

organs, reducing the risk of systemic toxicity and subsequent
harmful side effects. Targeting the receptor with a high-affinity
targeting ligand will selectively recognize the overexpressed
receptors on the cancer cell surfaces, and could enhance the

PPL conjugation

The LHRH ligand was conjugated with the PLGA-PEG copolymer by
dissolving 100 mg of prepared PPCH and 10 mg of activated LHRHNH2 in anhydrous methylene chloride solution and stirred for 1 h
under nitrogen at room temperature. Three milligrams of EDC and
1 mg of DMAP were added to the dispersion, which was then stirred
at room temperature under nitrogen for for48 h. The product was
subsequently obtained by precipitating in ice-cold diethyl ether;
filtered, purified with dialysis, lyophilized (Coolsafe 110-4,
LABOGENE, Germany), and stored at - 20 degC [7, 23].

Evaluation of Conjugates

The formation of PPL conjugate was confirmed by Fourier transform
infrared (FTIR), X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), nuclear
magnetic resonance (NMR), and bicinchoninic acid (BCA) studies.

FTIR analysis

The FTIR spectra of the conjugated and unconjugated PLGA were
compared to check for structural change due to conjugation by
scanning from 400 cm-1 to 4000 cm-1 in FTIR spectrometer
(PerkinElmer Model: L1600400 Spectrum Two Ft-IR/DTGS).

NMR study

The conjugation of PLGA-PEG and PPL was confirmed by the proton
NMR spectrum (ECX400 -Jeol 400 MHz, Japan). The standard 1H
NMR spectra were analyzed to verify the conjugation at 400 MHz
by using DMSO as solvent.

XPS study

XPS is one of the most promising instruments for
identifying chemical entities on a surface. The conjugation of
(DLys6) LHRH to nanoparticles was confirmed based upon the
presence of nitrogen peak in XPS. Nitrogen is a constituent of the
various bonds (peptide and amide) formed between the NPs and

peptides through NHS activation. The samples were dispersed in
water, processed, and analyzed using a Thermo Fisher Scientific Xray photoelectron spectrometer (Escalab Xi +) equipped with a
monochromatic AI k alpha source. 50 eV pass energy was employed
for the analysis with a 200 eV survey.

BCA Protein Assay

The quantity of LHRH peptide in PPL conjugation was quantified
utilizing the BCA Protein Assay Kit (Abcam), following the

concentration determination. It employs a copper salt, reducible by
protein(s), forming a colored complex with bicinchoninic acid, with
a strong absorbance at 562 nm. The complex intensity correlates
with protein amount.

Formulation of curcumin- and siRNA-loaded PPL
nanoconjugate system

The curcumin- and siRNA-loaded PPL nanoparticle (CUR-SiRNAPPL NP) was formulated using the method of double emulsion
solvent evaporation (w1/o/w2) . Briefly, 20 mg of PPL and
10 mg of curcumin were dissolved completely in a mixture of
acetone and methanol (3 mL, 7:3 v/v) to form the organic phase.
Fifty micrograms of bclsR was mixed with 50 mg of poly-L-lysine
(PLL) in 200mL of nuclease-free water, and the resulting solution
was added gradually to the organic solution. The mixture was then
sonicated (Labman Scientific Instruments) at 4 degC for 15 min to
produce w1/o primary emulsion. This primary emulsion was then
added gradually to deionized water containing Polyvinyl Alcohol
(PVA) and tween 80 solutions, with continuous stirring, and
sonicated at 4 degC for 15 min to form w1/o/w2 double emulsion.
This emulsion was then stirred in room condition until the organic
solvent was completely evaporated. Then, the CUR-SiRNA-PPL NPs
were obtained by centrifuging at 15,000 rpm for 50 min at 4 degC and
freeze-dried.

Evaluation of various acetonitrile/water ratios (with 2% acetic acid)

developed using a solvent system of acetonitrile/water (2% acetic

the peak area against concentrations with the determination of R2

value [35, 36].

Pharmacokinetic study

The pharmacokinetics study was performed by using Albino Wistar
rats, 8-10 weeks old having 150-200 gm body weight. The study
was conducted in accordance with the CPCSEA guidelines, after
obtaining approval from the Institutional Animal Ethical Committee
(IAEC) (Ref no: GIPS/IAEC/PhD/PRO/01/2023). Animals were
provided a standard diet and water ad libitum and maintained in a
12:12 h light-dark cycle with constant ambient conditions. All rats
were fasted overnight before the experiment but had free access to
water. A total of 18 rats either male or female were taken and
divided into 3 groups (6 in each group). The groups I received CURSiRNA-PPL NP, group II received CUR-PLGA NPs and group III
received curcumin suspension. The above-mentioned treatments
were administered intravenously through the tail vein in a dose

equivalent to 50 mg/kg body weight to different groups of animals.
Then 0.5 mL of blood was collected from each animal at
predetermined time intervals (0.5 h, 1 h, 2 h, 4 h, 6 h, 10 h, and
24 h) by retro-orbital puncture and transferred to the K2 EDTA vials
with immediate vortexing. Blood samples were centrifuged at
5000 rpm for 20 min at 4 degC. The supernatant (200mL) was
collected as plasma samples and preserved at -800 C. Then 1.2 mL
of a mixture of ethyl acetate and methanol (9:1) was added to
plasma as extracting solvent, vortexed for 5 min, and centrifuged at
8000 RPM for 20 min at 4 degC. From the supernatant, 1 mL of the
organic layer was collected, and evaporated, and the residues were
reconstituted in acetonitrile [42, 43]. Then the CUR concentration
was determined by HPLC (Arc Waters RPHPLC) using mobile phase
acetonitrile/water (2% acetic acid) (85:15 v/v) at a flow rate of
0.5 mL/min and at a wavelength of 425 nm.

In vivo anticancer study and biodistribution study

Animal study design

For performing in vivo anticancer study and biodistribution study,
Benzo(a)pyrene [B(a)P] induced lung cancer model was followed
using Balb/c mice. B(a)P, a polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon
present in tobacco, is a crucial agent for lung cancer pathogenesis
. For inducing lung cancer, adult Balb/c mice (20-25 g) of
either sex were used. The protocol was approved by the IAEC of the
University (GCU/IAEC/SPON/PRO/4/2023), ensuring all

CPCSEA and Animal Ethical Committee guidelines. Mice were
housed in Individually Ventilated Caging (IVC) (Orchid Mumbai),
provided with a standard diet and free access to water, and
maintained with a 12:12 h light-dark cycle at 25 degC.

In vivo anticancer study

For the in vivo anticancer study, 36 Balb/c mice were taken, and
they were divided into six groups. Mice of Group-1 (Positive
Control) were kept in the normal laboratory without any cancer
induction. Other mice were treated with B(a)P dissolved in corn oil

at a dose of 50 mg/kg body weight orally, twice a week, for
16 weeks to induce lung cancer . Mice were divided into
different groups; Group 2 (treatment with saline), Group 3
(treatment with PPL Blank NPs) (PPL blank NP), Group 4
(treatment with PPL NPs with curcumin) (CUR-PPL NP), Group 5
(treatment with CUR-SiRNA-PPL NPs) and Group 6 (treatment with
CUR suspension). After 16 weeks, the groups were treated with
the required dose (dosing volume 100 ul) by intravenous (IV)
administration through tail-vein for 12 days (every 3rd day, leading
to a total of 5 dosing/mice). On the 13th day after treatment, the
mice were euthanized by cervical dislocation, and lungs were
collected and observed for the growth of the tumor. The tumor size
was assessed by measuring two perpendicular dimensions using
Vernier calipers. Tumor volume was determined using the formula:

$${\text{V}} = \left( {{\text{W}}^{{2}} \times {\text{L}}}
\right)/{2},$$

where "L" is the tumor length at the longest point, and "W" at
the widest point .

The tumor inhibition ratio (TIR %) was calculated using the
formula:

$${\text{TIR}}\left( \% \right) = \left( {{\text{Wc}} -
{\text{Wt}}} \right)/{\text{Wc}} \times {1}00\% .$$

where Wc and Wt are the mean weight of the tumor of the saline
group and treatment group respectively .

In vivo biodistribution study

The in vivo biodistribution study was performed on 18 B(a)P
induced balb/c mice by inducing lung cancer as described by Velli
et al. ; Kasala et al. ; Magesh et al.  for up to 16 weeks.
After 16 weeks, the mice were divided into 3 groups, and the
treatments (SiRNA-CUR-PPL NP, CUR-PPL NP, and CUR-Susp) were
given to the three groups with the required dose (dosing volume
100 ul) respectively, by intravenous (IV) administration through
tail-vein. The mice were then euthanized by cervical dislocation,
and the major organs like the heart, liver, spleen, kidney, and lung

were excised, washed, and weighed. The separated organs were
then homogenized (IKA T25 digital Ultra-Turrax) with PBS,
centrifuged at 5000 rpm for 20 min at 4 degC and the supernatant was
collected. Acetonitrile was added to the resulting solution for
deproteinization. Ethyl acetate (1.2 mL) and methanol (9:1) mixture
was added to the solution, vortexed for 5 min, and centrifuged at
8000 RPM for 20 min at 4 degC. From that, 1 mL organic layer was
collected, evaporated, and reconstituted in acetonitrile [42, 43].
CUR concentration was determined by HPLC using acetonitrile (2%
acetic acid) (85:15 v/v) at 0.5 mL/min and 425 nm wavelength.

TNF-a and p53 marker estimation

Plasma from each mouse group was collected for analysis. TNFalpha and p53 levels were determined using an ELISA kit
(Elabscience) and FineTest TP53 ELISA kit, respectively, using the
standard protocol and absorbance was read at 450 nm.

Histopathology study

A histopathological study was conducted by collecting the lung

## Results
The PPL conjugate was successfully synthesized and characterized
using FTIR, 1H NMR, XRD, XPS and BCA assay. Curcumin and
bclsR-loaded PLGA nanoemulsions were prepared by double

targeting in lung cancer drug delivery, luteinizing-hormone
releasing hormone (LHRH) peptide which is specific to extracellular
overexpressed receptors in lung cancer cells can be targeted [23,
30, 33]. In healthy lung cells, LHRH expression is quite low, but it
is increased in the malignant condition . LHRH peptides are
extensively investigated for their targeting capabilities and delivery
of anticancer drugs to LHRHR-overexpressing sites. Shitole et al.
 formulated PPL nanocapsules encapsulating both docetaxel
and quercetin, demonstrating their efficacy against prostate cancer
. Similarly, Cao et al.  formulated PPL polymer micelles
encapsulating docetaxel, reporting improved anticancer activity
against prostate cancer due to LHRH targeting . In a separate
study, Li et al.  developed LHRH-targeted polysaccharide
nanoparticles for cisplatin delivery in breast cancer, achieving
higher cellular internalization in MCF-7 cells and improved tumor
accumulation and suppression compared to non-targeted
nanoparticles . Hence the nanoparticles can be specifically
targeted to tumor cells by conjugating with ligands, siRNAs, or
antibodies .

Despite all the developments in the use of nanoformulations for
better therapy of lung cancer, its successful translation to clinical
application is still lacking. Heterogenicity of tumors, insufficient
delivery, toxicity, and resistance are some of the major issues to be
addressed. The design of multifunctional nanoformulations with a

more combined approach is therefore necessary for better delivery
against these cells. Hence, in the present study, a multifunctional
approach has been designed for better site-specific delivery to lung
cancer cells by employing curcumin as a powerful anticancer drug
and a possible p-gp inhibitor downregulating the efflux pumps, and
siRNA targeting the BCL2 mRNA to downregulate its apoptosis

uptake by cancer cells, enhancing the effectiveness of anticancer
medications and decreasing the related systemic toxicity. Many
efforts have been made to reduce drug resistance focusing on
downregulating any one mechanism. However, very limited
attempts have been made till now to inhibit both pump and nonpump mechanisms simultaneously along with an active targeting
mechanism. This study proposes using curcumin as a potential P-gp
inhibitor to downregulate efflux pump-mediated resistance and
bclsR to downregulate bcl2 mRNA-regulated non-pump resistance
along with active targeting of cancer cells by using LHRH ligand.
Hence, the development of a multifunctional nanoconjugate with
more site-specific delivery along with down regulators of both
mechanisms shall be an innovative approach for designing therapy
addressing both problems of systemic toxicity and drug resistance
involved in chemotherapy of lung Cancer.

The confirmation of the formation of PPL nanoconjugate was
performed by FTIR, 1H NMR, XPS, and BCA analyses of the free
polymer and the conjugates. FTIR analysis was used to confirm the
conjugation of LHRH to PPCH. The FTIR spectra for acid-terminated
PLGA displayed characteristic peaks at 1743 cm-1 for the C = O
stretch and 1161 cm-1 and 1080 cm-1 corresponding to the C - O

stretch of PLGA. In addition to the peaks for PLGA, the spectra of
the PPCH conjugate (Fig. 1 A) exhibited additional peaks at
2945 cm-1of C - H stretch of CH2, 1566 cm-1 for N-H amide,
1359 cm-1 for C-H bending of alkane group, 1277 cm-1 for C-O
stretching ether and 961 cm-1 for C-H stretching of alkane group,
thereby, confirming the covalent conjugation of acid-terminated
PLGA with NH2-PEG-COOH and thus, synthesis of diblock
copolymer PPCH. The FTIR spectra for PPL (Fig. 1 A) revealed
additional peaks, in addition to those observed for PPCH, at
3302.82 cm-1 for the NH stretch of amide, 1560 cm-1 for the -NH
amide bands, and an increased intensity at 1650 cm-1

corresponding to the C = O amide/peptide bond. These
observations confirm the formation of the PPL conjugate.

## Conclusion
From the study, it may be concluded that the PPL nanoconjugate
system, loaded with curcumin and bcsR, can be potentially
effective, multifunctional targeted approach for lung cancer
therapy.

The study developed a combination therapy for lung cancer using
curcumin and siRNA targeting BCL2 mRNA, delivered via PPLNPs.
This system targets cancer cells through the LHRH ligand, reducing
systemic toxicity and increasing drug accumulation at the tumor
site. The PPL copolymer was synthesized and confirmed through
various analyses, and the NPs showed efficient drug and siRNA
entrapment with sustained release properties. In vitro studies
revealed significant cytotoxicity and enhanced uptake of the NPs in
A549 lung cancer cells. The combination therapy effectively
downregulated P-gp and BCL2 mRNA, addressing both pump and
non-pump resistance mechanisms. In vivo, pharmacokinetic analysis
in rats showed sustained drug release and increased bioavailability.
Biodistribution studies confirmed preferential drug accumulation in
the lungs. In vivo, anticancer studies in mice demonstrated superior
tumor inhibition with the combination therapy, supported by
histopathological observations indicating reduced tumor
development and inflammation. These findings suggest that the
proposed targeted delivery system holds promise for more effective
lung cancer treatment by enhancing therapeutic efficacy and
minimizing adverse effects. Future studies investigating the
potential for combining this therapy with other agents could
enhance its therapeutic impact and broaden its applicability to
other resistant cancer types. The effectiveness of conjugating LHRH
with siRNA may potentially be investigated in other malignancies
where LHRHR is overexpressed. Additionally, more studies may be
encouraged to further assess biodistribution and systemic toxicity
and validate the system's translational potential.
