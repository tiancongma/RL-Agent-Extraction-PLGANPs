Lupeol exhibits anti-inﬂammatory effects; unfortunately it shows low water solubility. An alternative to
overcome this is the development of nanomaterials. Several methods for nanomaterial production are
available. One of them is emulsiﬁcation/solvent-evaporation. The objective of the present work was to
evaluate physical properties, transport and in vitro modulator effects on NF-kB of poly (lactide-co-gly-
colide) (PLGA) nanoparticles loaded with lupeol. Nanonutraceuticals were prepared with 16% (w/v) of
lupeol. Size distribution and morphology were measured by particle size analyzer and TEM. In vitro
release of lupeol was studied by three different models: Higuchi, Siepmann & Peppas, and Power law.
Transport of nanonutraceutical was studied in a Caco-2 cell model and by GCeMS. Modulator effect on
NK-kB was studied by western blot analysis. Nanonutraceuticals were 10% larger than the nanoparticles
without lupeol (372 vs 337 nm) and presented a broader size distribution (0.28 vs 0.22). TEM results
displayed spherical structures with a broader size distribution. Entrapment efﬁciency of lupeol was
64.54% and it in vitro release data ﬁtted well to the Power law and Higuchi equation (R > 0.84e0.84).
Strong regulation of NF-kB of nanonutraceutical was observed. It was not observed any transport across
the Caco-2 cell model at the different experimental conditions.

1. Introduction
Several natural compounds show biological effects, as the pen-
tacyclic triterpenes. They are based on a 30-carbon skeleton
comprising 5 six-membered rings (ursanes and lanostanes) or 4
six-membered rings and 1 ﬁve-membered ring (lupanes and
hopanes) (Wal et al., 2011). They can be found in the balsam, and
plant resins (Mufﬂer et al., 2011); and usually in the diet, where a
consumption of 250 mg per day is estimated for this compound
(Saleem, 2009). One of them is lupeol, a lupane-type pentacyclic
triterpene present in diverse plants such as Japanese pear, aloe leaf,
mango pulp extract, ginseng oil, etc. (Siddique and Saleem, 2011).
Regarding to its anti-inﬂammatory effect, lupeol has shown
inhibitory activities on pro-inﬂammatory cytokines such as IL-2,
IFN-g and TNF-a (Bani et al., 2006; Ahmad et al., 2010), IL-4, IL-5,
eosinophils reduction (Vasconcelos et al., 2008) and effect against
pro-inﬂammatory enzymes like iNOS and COX-2 (Saleem et al.,
2004; Sanchez-Burgos et al., 2013).

Unfortunately, lupeol as all triterpenes shows very low water
solubility (0.02 mg/mL) at 20 C (Jager et al., 2007). This fact is a
problem when the compound is orally delivered. An alternative to
overcome low oral bioavailability is by the use of nanotechnology.
Nanotechnology is a multidisciplinary discipline that uses physical
and chemical processes to investigate and develop materials with
speciﬁc functions and new properties. Thus, nanonutraceutical is
considered as the use of nanotechnology in the nutraceutical sci-
ences as a fundamental tool in the development of nano-
nutraceuticals that can be used to enhance the bioavailability of
lipophilic compounds, control the kinetics release, change bio-
distribution, provide stability and minimize side effects (Huang
et al., 2010; Kuang et al., 2010; Sensoy, 2014).
Few reports about nanoencapsulation of lupeol have been
published, from our best knowledge only one (Redes et al., 2011),
attempted the ﬁrst effort to nanoencapsulate lupeol by the use of
interfacial polycondensation and spontaneous emulsiﬁcation pro-
cesses, obtaining particles sizes over 600 nm. Unfortunately, large
size nanoparticles are stuck in the liver and may not reach the
desired target site (Singh et al., 2008). However, there are several
methods to obtain small size nanoparticles; the most common is
the emulsion solvent-evaporation, which is commonly used to
formulate
nanoparticles
with
polymers
and
an
emulsiﬁer
commonly PVA. The formulated nanomaterials using PVA are
usually uniform in size and are easy to disperse in an aqueous
medium. Typically, the oil phase is an organic solvent containing a
polymer and the bioactive principle of interest, which are emulsi-
ﬁed in an aqueous phase containing PVA to form an oil-in-water (o/
w) emulsion (Bhave et al., 2011).
The most used polymer for the emulsion solvent-evaporation
method is the poly (D,L e lactide) (PLA) alone or with copolymers
as glycolide that has been widely used for low water solubility
payload particles, and reported as very useful for increasing the
release rate (Wang et al., 2003). PLGA nanoparticles has been
widely used because they are biodegradables, did not show toxic
effects, and their degradation products are the innocous lactic and
glycolic acid (Panyam et al., 2002; Kim et al., 2006), reason why FDA
has approved it for clinical trials. The knowledge about the active
release of bioactive compounds from nanoparticles can be very
useful to decide if the material can be suited for short or long term
applications (Gupta et al., 2012). There are three main rate con-
trolling mechanisms in the active release: i) Diffusion, ii) swelling,
and iii) erosion (Langer and Peppas, 1983). PLGA can generally be
regarded
as
bulk
eroding
dosage
forms
(Burkersroda
and
Goepferich, 1998). However, Faisant et al. (2002), indicates that
most often polymer erosion is not the only process that contributes
to the drug release. After water imbibition into PLGA particles
active compound dissolution and cleavage of unstable ester bonds
occurs. Simultaneously active compound diffuses out due to con-
centration gradients (Faisant et al., 2002). Most of the models used
for describing the controlled release of substances have been based
on solutions of the Fick diffusion equation (Siepmann and Peppas,
2001) or the empirical Power law. From our best knowledge,
there are no reports about release of lupeol from nanoparticles.
The aim of this study was to prepare and evaluate physical
properties, transport and modulator effects on NK-kB of lupeol and
nanoparticles loaded with lupeol (nanonutraceuticals).

used as the surfactant for the emulsiﬁcation (98%e99% hydrolysis
degree and average MW 30,000e50,000 g/mol, SigmaeAldrich, St.
Louis, Mo, USA). Lupeol, N,O-Bis(trimethylsilyl)triﬂuoroacetamide
(BSTFA), dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO), Minimal essential medium
(MEM),
(3-(4,5-Dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-diphenyltetrazolium

streptomycin,
5-bromo-4-chloro-3-

Nuaille, France) and D(þ) trehalose, 98% was obtained from VWR

2.2. Biological material
Caco-2 (colorectal adenocarcinoma) cells were obtained from
the American Type Culture Collection (ATCC number HTB-39™)
(Rockville, MD, USA).
2.3. Synthesis of unloaded nanoparticles and nanonutraceuticals
with entrapped lupeol (PLGL)
Nanoparticles were made following the method of solvent
evaporation proposed by Gomes et al. (2011). Nanonutraceutical
was developed with 50 mg of PLGA (65:35) added to 2 mL of
dichloromethane in a ﬂask, and then adding lupeol (16% w/v; rate
PLGA:lupeol was 14.28:1). The aqueous phase comprised 20 mL of
PVA (0.5%) solution, which was homogenized by an Ultra-turrax®
disperser (13,000 RPM) and the organic phase added drop to drop.
The emulsion was sonicated for 20 min at 2 C (70 W). Organic
solvent was separated by rotaevaporation. The residue was ultra-
ﬁltered (membrane of 50 kDa, 40 psi in and 10 psi output pres-
sures). After ultraﬁltration, nanoparticles and nanonutraceuticals
were kept for 2 h at 80 C and freeze-dried at 50 C under
5 mtorr (9.67  105 psi) vacuum for 48 h in a Labconco freeze-
dryer 5 (Labconco, Kansas City, Mo., USA). D(þ) trehalose was
added (1:1) (w/w) relative to the amount of nanoparticles or
nanonutraceuticals. Finally, the lyophilized samples were stored in
a desiccator and placed in the freezer (20 C) until further use
(Astete and Sabliov, 2006). Unloaded nanoparticles (PLGA) were
also prepared and used as control.
2.4. Particle size and size distribution
The size distribution of nanonutraceuticals was determined in
nanopure water at 25 C, using a particle size analyzer (90 Plus
particle size analyzer, Brookhaven Instruments Co., Holtsville, NY.,
USA). For the measurements, 200 mL of the nanonutraceutical
suspension were dispersed in 2 mL of water. The water was pre-
viously ﬁltered using a 0.02 mm ﬁlter (Anotop 10, Whatman, Pis-
cataway, NJ, USA). The analysis was performed at a scattering angle
of 90, refractive index of 1.590, and 25 C. A standard of 100 nm
(nanosphere size standards, Duke Scientiﬁc Co., Palo Alto, CA, USA)
of a polymer microsphere in water (polymer density of 1.05 g/cm3,
refractive index of 1.590 at 589 nm) was used to verify equipment
calibration. Measurements of size and polydispersity index were
made for freshly synthesized nanonutraceuticals (prior ultraﬁltra-
tion), ultra-ﬁltered nanonutraceuticals, and re-suspended nano-
nutraceuticals (after freeze-drying). For each sample, the mean
diameter and the standard deviation of 9 determinations were
calculated.

The morphology of nanonutraceuticals was examined by

2.7. Cell viability assay and transport of nanonutraceutical across a
Caco-2 culture
Prior to the identiﬁcation of nanonutraceutical transport, au-
thors aimed to investigate the intrinsic cytotoxicity of PLGA nano-
nutraceuticals and lupeol on the cell viability by the MTT assay
(Youm et al., 2014).
Caco-2 cells were grown in MEM supplemented with 10% FBS,
2.2 g/L of sodium bicarbonate, 100 U/mL penicillin and 100 mg/mL
streptomycin at a ﬁnal pH 7.2e7.4 and maintained at 37 C under a
5% CO2 and 95% air atmosphere at constant humidity. Cells were
seeded at a density of 5  104 cells/cm2 on a 6 transwell plate of
0.4 mm pore size (Transwell® Permeable Supports, CORNING Inc,
NY, USA), and the medium was changed every 3 d along 21 d to
obtain conﬂuent, differentiated cell monolayers. Before the Caco-2
uptake experiment, the growth medium was removed from each
culture well and the monolayer was washed twice with Hank's
balanced salt solution (HBSS). The treatments at equivalent con-
centration of lupeol (20 mM) were then diluted with MEM. The
apical side of the cell monolayers received 1.5 mL of the treatment
and the basolateral side, 2.6 mL of FBS-free MEM. The media from
the apical and basolateral sides was collected and stored in an ultra-
low temperature freezer until use.
Samples were re-suspended in 200 mL of methanol and
shaken mechanically. The samples were then dried using N2 before
derivatization

(Agilent Technologies, Wilmington, DE, USA), equipped with a

2.8. Modulator effect on NF-kB in Caco-2 cell system stimulated
with TNF-a
Caco-2 cells were seeded at 2.4  106 cells/plate on a 100 mm
plate and treated on day 3 after seeding as follows: DMSO control,
TNF-a control, all treatments were carried with co-incubation with
10 ng/mL of TNF-a during 24 h except DMSO control for model of
inﬂammation. Concentrations of 5, 10, and 20 mM of lupeol, and
PLGA nanoparticles or nanonutraceuticals were used; DMSO was
used as vehicle in all treatments.
Cells were washed with PBS 1X and EDTA-Trypsin 0.25% was
added; then cells were re-suspended in PBS 1X, centrifuged at
3000 rpm (4 C, 5 min), and the supernatant, discarded. The cells
were re-suspended in PBS (pH 7.8 with a 0.01% protease inhibitor
cocktail) (SigmaeAldrich, St. Louis, MO, USA). Cells were homoge-
nized and centrifuged at 8500 rpm during 15 min at 4 C. The su-
pernatant was collected and stored at 80 C until use.
Cytosolic and nuclear extracts were prepared by lysing the cells
using a cytoplasmic extraction buffer (HMKEE) containing 20 mM
Hepes (pH 7.9), 10 mM KCl, 1.5 mM MgCl2, 1 mM EDTA, 1 mM EGTA,
1 mM DDT, 250 mM sucrose and the protease inhibitor cocktail.
Cells were homogenized by ultraturrax during 10e15 s at slow
motion and centrifuged at 900 g (5 min, 4 C). The supernatant was
collected, representing the cytosolic fraction. The pellet was added
with lysis buffer containing 25 mM TriseHCl (pH 7.6),150 mM NaCl,
2% Triton X-100 and 0.1% SDS.
It was used 60 mg of protein extracted mixed with protein
loading buffer. Equal protein amounts were separated by 12% SDS-
PAGE under reducing conditions and electroblotted onto nitrocel-
lulose membranes. The membranes were blocked in 5% non-fat
milk for 4 h, overnight incubated with the appropriate primary

2.9. Statistical analysis
Data were expressed as mean ± SE and analyzed by analysis of
variance (ANOVA). Tukey's test was used to test for differences
among means for which ANOVA indicated a signiﬁcant value
(P < 0.05). All experiments were done by triplicate. The experi-
mental data (controlled release experiment) were ﬁtted to the
different proposed models using Statistica software for Windows,
version 7.0 (Stat Soft, Tulsa, OK).

3.2. Morphology
In order to investigate detailed structure of the particles, TEM
images of nanoparticles (unloaded particles) (Fig. 1a) and loaded
PLGL (nanonutraceuticals) (Fig. 1b) particles were obtained and
shown in Fig. 1. The diameter of nanoparticles (unloaded) and
nanonutraceuticals loaded (not in solution) ranged from 25 to
150 nm, the spherical structure and smooth surfaces showed a
broad size distribution and a tendency to form clusters. The ag-
gregation between particles could be related to insufﬁcient steric
stabilization by the PVA, which is a non-ionic surfactant. The PVA
chain is formed from alternating hydrophilic and hydrophobic
segments. The poly (vinyl acetate) portion of the PVA molecule
anchors at the oil/water interface during emulsiﬁcation and in-
tegrates with the polymer matrix once the organic solvent evapo-
rates. Therefore, a residual PVA remains associated with the
particles, even after the repeated washing with water (Bhave et al.,
2011). This arrangement lead to the formation of the PVA “sun
brightside” observed by TEM (Derakhshandeh et al., 2011), called
“corona” (Gomes et al., 2011).
3.3. Entrapment efﬁciency
The entrapment efﬁciency for lupeol was 64.54 ± 0.02%. This
value was lower than other encapsulated hydrophobic components
in PLGA obtained by the emulsiﬁcation/evaporation method
(Zigoneanu et al., 2008). However, several reports about encapsu-
lation of highly hydrophobic/non-polar compounds and PLGA show
efﬁciencies around 50e80% (Were et al., 2003; Cortesi et al., 2002;
Jain et al., 2010), values, which are in agreement with the ones
found in the present work.
3.4. In-vitro release lupeol behavior
The release of bioactive compounds is related to several factors
such as wall materials, physicochemical properties, morphology,
interactions between the particle components, etc. (Huang and
Brazel, 2001). There are only three possible ways for bioactive
molecules to be released from a PLGA system: (1) by transport
through water-ﬁlled pores, (2) transport through the polymer, and
(3) transport due to dissolution of the encapsulated polymer
(which does not require bioactive transport) (Fredenberg et al.,
2011). The system used in the present work was lupeol and PLGA.
The lupeol is a hydrophobic compound with a relatively low mo-
lecular weight, thus the transport may occur through the polymer
(Pahl, 1999). However, the bioactive compound must enter the
water phase, either at the surface or into the nanoparticles pores,
before being released (Fredenberg et al., 2011). Also, in this sense
lupeol is prone to be localized in the hydrophobic core formed by
polylactide-co-glicolide (PLGA) or in the interface between PVA and
PLGA. Results about modeling release of lupeol employing Equa-
tions (2) and (4) yield same results in terms of R (Higuchi model,

3.6. Effects of lupeol and nanonutraceutical treatments on the
modulation of expression of NF-kB
It is well known that NF-kB pathway is the most important route
in the development, progress and support of many chronic dis-
eases. NF-kB controls the expression of a large number of genes
involved in the physiological response associated with immune-
inﬂammatory aspects, oxidative stress, cell adhesion, differentia-
tion and apoptosis (Pahl, 1999). Multiple evidences indicate that
NF-kB is related to the progress of inﬂammation and cancer, making
it an important target for the control of multiple pathologies
This research explored the effects of nanonutraceutical devel-
opment in the modulation of NF-kB, compared against lupeol
(Table 1). Lupeol keeps NF-kB at cytosol and prevents its trans-
location to the nucleus, while interestingly the nanonutraceutical
shows the opposite behavior because NF-kB level at cytosol is
decreased and NF-kB at the nucleus is increased as the dose of
treatment is higher. Our best results with nanonutraceuticals were
obtained at the lowest dose (5 mM). These results suggest that
biological effects of nanonutraceuticals should be taken carefully
and equivalent doses of the active compound and the lupeol in the
nanonutraceuticals could lead to enhanced effects (Danhier et al.,
2009).
Multiple evidences indicate that NF-kB is related to the pro-
gression of inﬂammation and cancer, making it an important target
for the control of multiple pathologies. Treatment with lupeol in
cells has shown to reduce in a signiﬁcant way the expression of
oncogenic proteins such as Ras. Thus, it modulates the expression
of several molecules involved in signaling pathways of protein ki-
nase C alpha/ornithine decarboxylase gene expression (PKCa)/ODC,
PI3K/Akt and therefore reducing activation of the NF-kB pathway
(Saleem et al., 2005). This effect has also been found in animals as
CD-1 mice treated with 12-O-tetradecanoyl-phorbol-13-acetate
(TPA), produces an activation of NF-kB and PI3K/Akt; lupeol topi-
cally applied (1e2 mg/mouse) causes a decrease in NF-KB activa-
tion and IKKa, phosphorylation and degradation of IkBa (Saleem
et al., 2004). These results are consistent with a good anti-
inﬂammatory activity by this compound. Overall, the results
show that the best anti-inﬂammatory effects are obtained at higher
dose of pure lupeol and at lower doses with the nanonutraceutical,
this possibly caused by an increased bioavailability in the cell
interior. Thus the nanonutraceutical may be an alternative for the
treatment of inﬂammatory bowel disease (IBD), However, more
research is required to provide a broader overview of its real
potential.

nanonutraceuticals treatments
Retention time of lupeol was found to be 38.2 min (Fig. 3). Major

trimethylsilyl sample, the lupeol fragmentation patterns were
similar to those found in the samples analyzed from the culture

From the results of transport at 8 h of incubation (Fig. 3), only a
small fraction of lupeol (traces) was able to transport through the
apical to the basolateral side. This result may be beneﬁcial in order
to demonstrate that encapsulation can improve the stability of
lupeol in an aqueous solution such as the culture medium. Several
reports indicate that endocytosis of PLGA nanoparticles by Caco-2 is
clathrin-mediated (Reix et al., 2012). In this sense, it has also been
found that the intracellular fate of the nanoparticles is generally
lysosomes, where the rupture and release of the active principle
from nanoparticles occurs. This causes that transcytosis be small
and therefore having poor signiﬁcance (He et al., 2013). Addition-
ally, the absence of a lupeol signal in PLGL treatments at the apical
and basolateral sides could be explained by the fact that nano-
particles have a higher stability in aqueous environments even at
longer times, protecting the bioactive components and providing
stability (Wang et al., 2009).
4. Conclusion
The release of lupeol from nanoparticles was well described by
power law and Higuchi equation. It was not observed transport
across Caco-2 cell model at different experimental times. Best anti-
inﬂammatory effects were observed at highest dose of pure lupeol
and lowest dose of nanonutraceutical compound, this different
behavior could be related to an increased bioavailability of encap-
sulated lupeol in the cell interior.
