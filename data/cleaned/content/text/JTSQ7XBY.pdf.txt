1. Introduction
In recent decades, nanocarrier-based drug delivery systems
continue to gain considerable attention due to their potential to
improve a drug’s bioavailability, efﬁcacy, water solubility, drug
encapsulation efﬁciency, sustained drug release, and to minimize
undesirable toxicity [1]. Current nanocarriers such as biologically
erodable microspheres [2], liposomes and lipid nanoparticle [3],
micelles [4], gold nanoparticles [5] and quantum dots [6] have
been investigated as potential oral drug delivery systems. Co-
polymers containing polyethylene glycol (PEG) and PLGA blocks
(now approved by the FDA for human use) are attracting great
interest both in academia and industry for biomedical applications
due to their biodegradable nature and biocompatible properties
[7,8]. Moreover, PEG and PLGA have been employed as a drug
delivery system to improve the pharmacokinetic proﬁles of small
molecules. Gref et al. [9] concluded PEG-coated particles as a con-
trolled intravenous delivery for hydrophobic drugs, which could
increase blood circulation times and reduced liver accumulation.
Song et al. [10] assessed the potential of PLGA-PEG-PLGA triblock
copolymeric micelles to ameliorate the pharmacokinetics and tis-
sue distribution of Curcumin after intravenous injection. Li et al.
[11] demonstrated improved efﬁcacy of curcumin in SD rats after
intravenous
administration
of
PLGA-lecithin-curcumin-PEG
nanoparticles. However, except from intravenous administration,
no other administration methods such as chronic oral gavage
administration have been reported as an extended delivery system
for PLGA-PEG-PLGA micelles.
Ursolic acid (UA, 3b-hydroxy-urs-12-en-28-oic acid), a well
known triterpenoid photochemical isolated from the skin of vari-
ous fruits including apples, pears, olives, prunes and cranberries
[12,13] has been shown to exhibit considerable biological effects
including immunomodulatory [14], anti-inﬂammatory [15], antiul-
cer [16], antibacterial [17], hepatoprotective [18] and attractive
multifunctional anticancer activities [19–24]. Due to its abundant
supply in nature, UA has been studied as an attractive starting
point for further structural modiﬁcations. Over the past ﬁve years,
many derivatives of UA have been reported by us to improve its
efﬁcacy against cancer cells [25–29]. Recently, our study demon-
strated that the 3b-acetoxy-urs-12-en-28-oic acid hexamethylene-
diamine (US597) has great potentials in the prevention and
treatment of cancer [26,30]. The anti-cancer mechanisms of
US597 include cell cycle arrest, mitochondrial inhibition and apop-
tosis/necrosis induction [26]. Furthermore, we also demonstrated
that US597 could inhibit the adhesion and migration of cancer cells
by suppressing cell surface adhesive molecules [30]. More impor-
tantly, the side effect of US597 to hosts appeared to be minimal.
While UA and US597 are promising anti-metastasis drugs, their
translation to clinical application are limited by poor water solubil-
ity and low bioavailability. Since US597 was proven to be more
effective to cancer treatment than UA, but its low bioavailability
by oral administration limits further application [30]. Up to now,
nano-formulation including PEG and PLGA co-polymers has never
been used to help UA derivatives overcome these translation barri-
ers. In a recent study of our group, polyamidoamine (PAMAM) and
mesoporous silica nanoparticles (MSN) were taken advantage to
improve the water solubility of UA [31,32]. Thus, we herein
hypothesized that the triblock polymeric PLGA-PEG-PLGA micelles
formulated with US597 as a chemopreventive and chronic oral
dosage form can provide higher bioavailability and increased
anti-cancer efﬁcacy than free drug.
In this study, for the purpose of developing a novel drug deliv-
ery system as a sustained oral dosage form for cancer treatment,
the formulation of triblock co-polymer PLGA-PEG-PLGA micelles
loading US597 was synthesized and determined. The morphology,
diameter, zeta potential, drug loading and encapsulation efﬁciency
of US597@micelles were fully characterized. Moreover, the drug
release of US597@micelles in vitro was determined. The cytotoxic-
ity of US597@micelles over the US597 alone was demonstrated in
various cell lines. Particularly, to conﬁrm that US597@micelles has
better bioavailability than free US597, the in vivo pharmacokinetic
studies in Sprague–Dawley rats (SD rats) by ultra-performance liq-
uid chromatography tandem mass-spectrometry (UPLC/MS/MS)
analysis method were performed. To the best of our knowledge,
this is the ﬁrst time PLGA-PEG-PLGA micelles has been applied as
an oral dosage, and the formulation of PLGA-PEG-PLGA micelles
loaded US597 has been ﬁrstly applied for sustained delivery sys-
tem as a chronic oral dosage form. PLGA-PEG-PLGA micelles might
also be applied to other similar terpenoid analogue as a soluble
oral dosage form for clinical application.
2. Experimental methods
2.1. Materials and reagents
US597 was synthesized by our lab. Polyether diamine with a
predominant polyethylene glycol (PEG) backbone (NH2-PEG-NH2,
Mw: 4000) was purchased from Jenkem technology Co., Ltd
(China), poly (lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) (lactide: glycol-
ide = 75: 25, Mw: 35,000) was purchased from Jinan Daigang Bio-
material Co., Ltd (China). The solvent of methyl tertbutyl ether
(MTBE) and internal standard Finasteride was a generous gift from
the University of Traditional Chinese Medicine in Fujian. Cell lines
HepG2 (Hepatocellular carcinoma cells), B16F10 (Melanoma cells),
and SW620 (Colon cancer cells) were preserved by our lab. Other
reagents, at either analytical or chemical purity standards, were
obtained from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd (China).
2.2. Synthesis of PLGA-PEG-PLGA and US597@micelles
PLGA (0.1 mmol) was dissolved in 5 mL anhydrous dichloro-
methane (DCM), after that, N,N0-dicyclohexylcarbodiimide (DCC)
(0.8 mmol) and N-Hydroxysuccinimide (NHS) (0.8 mmol) were
added in the solution. The mixture was then stirred at room tem-
perature for 16 h under N2, and ﬁltered through a 0.45 lm PTFE
syringe ﬁlter to remove the dicyclohexylurea (DCU) from product.
The precipitate was then added to cold anhydrous ether and stirred
at 0 C for 30 min. The solution was ﬁltered through a PTFE syringe
ﬁlter and freeze-dried under vacuum overnight to obtain the solid-
iﬁed activated PLGA.
The triblock polymer PLGA-PEG-PLGA micelles were prepared
from activated PLGA, DCM, and PEG-bis-amine. Brieﬂy, Activated
PLGA (0.2 mmol) was dissolved in 8 mL anhydrous DCM in a round

at 1400 rpm for 5 h to remove DCM. The solution was then ﬁltered
by 0.45 lm microporous membranes to remove the non-entrapped
drugs, and lyophilized to obtain the US597@micelles (Fig. 1).
2.3. Surface morphology, FTIR analysis and particle size
To determine the morphology of the PLGA–PEG–PLGA micelles
structure, the samples were diluted to an appropriate concentra-
tion with the physiological saline. The copper mesh was clamped
with forceps to pick up a small sample; 2% of phosphotungstic acid
solution was dropped on the copper mesh and stained for 20 s.
Filter paper was used to absorb the extra dye liquor. The copper
mesh was then dried naturally at room temperature. Particle
morphology was observed by a Nova NanoSEM230 transmission
electron microscope at an accelerating voltage of 100KV [10].
The characterization of the PLGA-PEG-PLGA micelles was car-
ried out by FTIR analysis using a FTIR spectroscopy (Nicolet 410,
USA). The PLGA-PEG-PLGA sample was dried thoroughly and
spread on the potassium bromide tablet, and scanned at the range
of 4000–400 cm1.
To determine the particle diameter and zeta potential of
US597@micelles, we employed a photon correlation spectroscopy.
US597@micelles samples were diluted by the physiological saline
solution. The particle diameter was measured by a dynamic light
scattering instrument, Zetasizer Nano ZS90 (Malver, USA) with
the He-Ne laser beam, at a 633 nm, 25 C, 90 scattering angle. Zeta
potential was measured by Nanotrac Wave (Microtrac Inc.) at
25 C. Particle size and zeta potential data was repeated three
times by capacity distribution and averaged [33].

concentration through UV spectrophotometry. This method was
used to determine the content of US597 in the micelles solution.

2.6. Cellular viability assay
The cell viability of the blank micelles, free US597 and
US597@micelles were determined by MTT assay, in which the
mitochondrial converse to MTT (3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-
diphenyltetrazolium bromide) was detected by the change of opti-
cal density at 570 nm [34]. For cells to be in the best growth phase,
104 HepG2, B16F10, and SW620 suspended cells were placed in 96-
well tissue culture dishes (100 lL per well) at 37 C, 5% CO2 for
24 h. HepG2 cells were exposed to blank micelles, free US597, or
US597@micelles for 24 h at different US597 concentrations (1, 3,
6, 15 or 25 mg/L). For comparison, B16F10 and SW620 were
exposed to blank micelles, free US597, or US597@micelles for
24 h at a 15 mg/L concentration of US597. The medium was then
removed and replaced with a 100 lL MTT reagent (10 lL 5 mg/
mL MTT and 90 lL serum-free and phenol red-free medium); the
96-well plate was incubated for another 4 h at 37 C. The medium
was removed and replaced with 100 lL of dimethyl sulfoxide
(DMSO) to dissolve the blue formazan crystals produced by living
cells. The plates were read on an ELISA reader (Thermo Labsystems,
Shanghai Co. Ltd., Vantaa, Finland) to detect the absorbency of each
well (OD), cell proliferation inhibition rate was calculated: inhibi-
tion rate (%) = (1  mean OD value of the drug group/Mean OD val-
ues of the control group)  100%, the data was processed on the
software application GraphPad Prism.
2.7. Cell apoptosis measurement
Cell apoptosis measurement was measured by using Annexin
V-FITC/PI staining and analyzed by a ﬂow cytometry (BD Bio-
sciences). HepG2 cells were seeded in a 6-well plate (5  105 cells
per well) and incubated for 24 h at 37 C with 5% CO2. Blank
PLGA-PEG-PLGA micelles, free US597 or US597@micelles at the
US597 concentration of 15 mg/L was added to the well. Untreated
cells were also used as controls. After incubated for 24 h at 37 C
with 5% CO2, cells in the well were washed twice with PBS fol-
lowed by carefully trypsinized to avoid mechanical damage of
the membrane. Dead cells and alive cells were merged, washed
twice with PBS and suspended in binding buffer. 5 lL Annexin
V-FITC and 5 lL PI were added in the suspension respectively,
and the cells were incubated for 15 min. Then ﬂow cytometry
analysis was performed. FITC and PI ﬂuorescent intensity and
the percentage of cell population within each plot quadrant rep-
resented the distribution of cells in four different stages. The data
were analyzed with FlowJo software.
2.8. Fluorescence confocal microscopy measurement
HepG2 cells were seeded in a 12-well plate (200,000 per well)
at 37 C, 5% CO2 for 24 h to form a monolayer. The medium was
then removed and the cells were treated with blank PLGA-PEG-
PLGA micelles, free US597 and US597@micelles with US597 con-
centration of 15 mg/L. After another 12 h of incubation at 37 C
and 5% CO2, the medium with dead cells was discarded and cells
were washed three times with PBS. Then cells were stained with
agent MitoTracker Red CMXRos (0.25 lM) for 30 min to stain
the mitochondria. Followed by washing cells twice with PBS, and
ﬁxing with 4% paraformaldehyde for 10 min. After another twice
wash with PBS, the nuclei of HepG2 cells were stained with agent
40,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole
(DAPI)
(10 lg/mL)
for
10 min.
Finally, the cells were imaged under a Leica confocal microscope
SP8 with excitation at 405 nm and 504 nm.

3. Results and discussions
3.1. Preparation and characterization of PLGA-PEG-PLGA micelles
loaded US597
Drug vehicles prepared by a one-step nano-precipitation or self-
assembly are particularly promising since they would be favorable
for large-scale manufacturing. The synthetic scheme of triblock
co-polymer PLGA-PEG-PLGA that was ﬁrst synthesized by Zentner
et al. [7]. In this study, we determined the effect of the drug to
polymer ratio in drug loaded PLGA-PEG-PLGA micelles. As shown
in Tables 1 and 2, increasing the amount of US597 has minimal
inﬂuence on the diameter and zeta potential of micelles, which
exhibited from 76.7 nm to 85.1 nm and 5.76 mV to 10.65 mV. Fur-
thermore, we found that increasing the concentration of US597
from 3 mg/mL to 30 mg/mL has minimal impact on the encapsula-
tion and drug loading efﬁciencies of US597 which stayed constant
at approximately 80% and 27%, respectively. These results indi-
cated that the capacity of triblock polymer needed to encase drugs
inﬂuenced drug loading and encapsulation efﬁciency with only
slight increases when drug concentrations increased. Such phe-
nomenon could be explained by the efﬁcient packing of high and
low molecular weight polymers, which could result in better
US597 entrapment than individual polymers [35]. Moreover,
Fig. 2(A) showed that US597@micelles observed under SEM has a
relatively homogeneous size, spherical shape and smooth surface.
As shown in Fig. 2(C), the Fourier transform infrared spectrum of
PLGA-PEG-PLGA copolymer was obtained to conﬁrm the functional
groups, which was consistent with the results from previous
reports about PLGA-PEG-PLGA copolymers [10,36]. The peaks at
2875, 1350 and 980 cm1 showed the methylene group near an
oxygen atom and methyl groups in PLGAPEG-PLGA. The strong
peak at 3560 and 1620 cm1 were indicative of the terminal
hydroxyl groups
in
the copolymeric micelles. These results
demonstrated that PLGA-PEG-PLGA copolymers was successfully
synthesized by us.

To synthesize a drug-encapsulated nanoparticle with high efﬁ-
ciency, we need to select the correct polymer composition, a reli-
able solvent to dissolve drugs, and a solid technique [37].
Therefore, we chose a classical method based on PLGA-PEG-
PLGA [7,10,35] as a reference. By following the synthetic scheme
of triblock co-polymer PLGA-PEG-PLGA, we obtained the micelle
with a mean diameter lower than 100 nm (approx. 77–85 nm),
which is favorable to its speciﬁc extravasation of tumor microvas-
culature and tumor accumulation according to previous reports
[11,38]. We also found that increasing the concentration of
US597 from 3 mg/mL to 30 mg/mL has minimal impact on encap-
sulation and drug loading efﬁciencies of US597. The high drug
loading
and
encapsulation
efﬁciency
of
PLGA-PEG-PLGA
we
obtained indicated that this micelle could provide US597 a large
space for drug loading. The higher ratio of hydrophobic groups
(PLGA) in the core of PLGA-PEG-PLGA micelles made US597 more
easily loaded into the core of PLGA-PEG-PLGA micelles by interac-
tion between the lipophilic part of US597 (rigid triterpenoids ring
structure) and hydrophobic group of PLGA-PEG-PLGA micelles. On
the other hand, the hydrophilic group (PEG) in the surface of
PLGA-PEG-PLGA micelles well interact with the polar head group
of US597 (ANH2). So, this high EE and DL were considered as a
result of the synergistic effect of the unique micellar and drug
interaction and encapsulation effect. This data is far higher than
previous reports of PLGA-PEG-PLGA like Zhang’s study in which
drug loading efﬁciency is 8.7% [36].

3.2. Study of in vitro US597 release from US597@micelles
The drug releasing from PLGA nanoparticles is a complicated
procedure which
can
be
affected by
nanoparticle
diffusion,
degradation speed, and physicochemical drug properties [39]. We
determined the time course of US597 release from the triblock
polymer PLGA-PEG-PLGA and PBS. The in vitro release proﬁle of
both free US597 and US597@micelles were shown in Fig. 2(B),
which presented the regular pattern of drug release from PLGA-
PEG-PLGA micelles. Obviously, free US597 released fast from PBS
and continued for around 20 h before fully release. As for
US597@micelles, during the ﬁrst 40 h, US597 exhibited a relatively
quick release from PLGA-PEG-PLGA micelles, which could be
ascribed to the dissociation of surface-bound US597. Subsequently,
the drug release began to slow down by a steady continuing

Pharmacokinetic studies play an important role in developing
new medicines. By probing into the drug disposition process
(absorption, distribution, metabolism and elimination), the most
appropriate route of administration and the best dose regimen
can be determined [45]. Many attempts to determine the pharma-
cokinetics of UA have been made by LC-MS [46], HPLC-MS [47], and
UPLC/MS/MS [48]. But the pharmacokinetic studies of US597 and
US597-loaded PLGA-PEG-PLGA micelles haven’t been reported
until now. Traditional HPLC–UV and HPLC/MS/MS lacks sensitivity
and accuracy, requires large volumes of plasma for analysis
(approx. 500 lL) as well as complicated and costly sample-
preparation processes [49]. By contrast, the ultra-high perfor-
mance LC with MS/MS spectrometry method has developed in
recent years and is attracting intense interest for pharmacokinetic
study due to its high sensitivity, accuracy, and precision [50,51].
There are few studies for PLGA or PEG nanoparticles loading drugs
as a sustained oral delivery system. Khalil et al. [52] provided an
oral delivery of PLGA–PEG blend nanoparticles for curcumin. Jain
et al. [53] developed an FA-PEG-PLGA NPs oral nanoformulation
of insulin to control diabetic patients’ diabetes. We ﬁrstly provided
an chronic oral dosage for the formulation of PLGA-PEG-PLGA
micelles encapsulate drugs, in this paper, US597. As we can see
in this study, US597 encapsulated in the tri-block polymeric
PLGA-PEG-PLGA micelles may contribute to a higher bioavailability
than that of free US597.
4. Conclusions
In the present study, a US597-loaded triblock polymeric PLGA-
PEG-PLGA system (US597@micelles) was ﬁrstly successfully devel-
oped for anti-cancer therapy by oral administration. The system of
US597@micelles was proven to be spherical with a well uniformed
diameter around 80–90 nm, indicating a better extravasation of
tumor microvasculature and tumor accumulation. The drug load-
ing efﬁciency was much higher than conventional PLGA-PEG-
PLGA micelles carriers. US597@micelles demonstrated enhanced
rapid cell uptake and growth inhibition and induction of apoptosis
on HepG2 cancer cells. Moreover, a fast and suitable UPLC/MS/MS
method was ﬁrstly built to study the in vivo pharmacokinetics of
US597 and US597@micelles in rat plasma after oral administration.
The improvements in absorption, metabolism and clearance of
US597@micelles indicate that US597@micelles possesses better
bioavailability,
pharmacokinetic
proﬁles
and
enhanced
anti-
cancer properties in SD rats. Therefore, US597@micelles could
serve as a chronic oral administration form which is suitable for
future clinical application. The system of triblock polymeric
PLGA-PEG-PLGA micelles also helps further hurdle the critical bar-
riers encountered by UA and its analogues (including low solubility
and undesirable bioavailability) by oral administration.
