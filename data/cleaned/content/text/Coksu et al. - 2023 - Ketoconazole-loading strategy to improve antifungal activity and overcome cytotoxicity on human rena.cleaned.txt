## Abstract
Ketoconazole (KTZ), an antifungal agent used to treat localized or systemic fungal infections by
inhibiting ergosterol synthesis, exhibits restricted efficacy within eukaryotic cells owing to its
elevated toxicity and limited solubility in water. This study aims to improve the biological
activity and overcome cytotoxic effects in the renal system of the hydrophobic KTZ by
incorporating it into poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) nanoparticles (NPs) utilizing
biomaterial nano-engineering techniques. KTZ-loaded PLGA NPs (KTZ-NPs) were prepared by
single emulsion solvent evaporation method and characterized by using dynamic light scattering
(DLS), electrophoretic light scattering (ELS), Fourier transform-infrared (FT-IR) spectroscopy
and scanning light microscopy (SEM). Particle size and zeta potential of KTZ-NPs were
determined as 182.0 +- 3.27 nm and -27.4 +- 0.56 mV, respectively. Antifungal activity was
analyzed with the time-kill and top agar dilution methods on Candida albicans (C. albicans) and
Aspergillus flavus (A. flavus). Both KTZ and KTZ-NPs caused a significant decrease in A. flavus
cell growth; however, the same effect was only observed in time-killing analysis on C. albicans,
indicating a methodological difference in the antifungal analysis. According to the top agar
method, the MIC value of KTZ-NPs against A. flavus was 9.1 mg ml-1, while the minimum
inhibition concentration (MIC) value of KTZ was 18.2 mg ml-1. The twofold increased
antifungal activity indicates that nanoparticular drug delivery systems enhance the water
solubility of hydrophobic drugs. In addition, KTZ-NPs were not cytotoxic on human renal
proximal tubular epithelial cells (HRPTEpCs) at fungistatic concentration, thus reducing fungal
colonization without cytotoxic on renal excretion system cells.

Keywords: antifungal activity, biocompatibility, controlled release systems, ketoconazole,
nanoparticles, PLGA

2.2. Preparation of KTZ-loaded PLGA NPs

KTZ-loaded PLGA NPs were synthesized using the oil-in-water

described . Firstly, KTZ and PLGA were dissolved in ethanol-DCM and DCM, respectively. The KTZ and PLGA solutions
were mixed and vortexed to ensure to obtain organic phase
homogeneously. This organic phase was emulsified with aqueous
PVA solution (3% w/v) by sonication using a microtip probe
sonicator (Bandelin Sonopuls, Germany) over an ice bath. The
obtained emulsion was added dropwise to 0.1% w/v PVA
solution and stirred for 4 h on a magnetic stirrer at room temperature where organic solvents were removed under constant
stirring. The prepared NPs were precipitated by centrifugation
(40 min., 9000 rpm), washed three times with ultrapure water,
and then freeze-dried. Free NPs were prepared by the same

-20 degC until used for further analysis.

2.3.3. FT-IR analysis.
FT-IR spectra of free KTZ, PLGA, and
freeze-dried nanoparticles were obtained in the attenuation total
reflectance (ATR) mode using an FT-IR spectrophotometer
(Shimadzu, Japan) for chemical analysis of functional groups
present in NPs and to confirm encapsulation of KTZ into NPs
. The spectra were obtained from 4000 to 600 cm-1 and
4 cm-1 resolution with 16 scans per sample.

2.3.4. SEM analysis.
The surface morphology, shape, and

electron microscopy . Freeze-dried powdered NPs were

then coated with gold under vacuum and analyzed with a

Considering the obtained MIC values, it was observed
that KTZ-NPs exhibited greater antifungal efficacy against A.
flavus than KTZ. The limited water solubility of antifungal
drugs represents a significant impediment to their biological
activity. The twofold increase in antifungal activity observed
with KTZ-NPs can be attributed to the enhanced water
solubility of KTZ facilitated by nanoparticles. The sustained
and controlled release of the drug from KTZ-NPs likely
extended the interaction time with fungal spores in the aqueous medium, thereby contributing to the observed decrease
in the MIC value relative to the free drug. In various studies,
it has been reported that antifungal agent/drug-loaded polymeric nanoparticles increase antifungal activity compared to
free drugs . Peng et al produced voriconazole-PLGA
nanoparticles and showed that they have a more permanent
and more effective antifungal activity than free voriconazole
. Patel et al produced nanoparticles of different sizes to
examine a mechanism of action of the antifungal activity of
itraconazole-loaded PLGA nanoparticles against fungal cells.
It was also shown by Patel et al that 200 nm particles showed
consistent inhibitory activity at all concentrations compared to
free itraconazole, while particles with 630 and 1060 nm sizes
showed less antifungal activity at the lowest drug concentration. Patel et al suggested that other mechanisms may
play a role in facilitating itraconazole penetration into fungal

cells, but that particle size is a crucial parameter for drug
delivery efficiency . The particle sizes we obtained below
200 nm may influence the antifungal activity. Considering the
size of the produced nanoparticles, an increase in efficiency in

KTZ-NPs was 9.1 mg ml-1, while the MIC value of KTZ was
18.2 mg ml-1 against A. flavus. Antifungal activity against A.
flavus twofold increased with the long-term release of ketoconazole (approximately 96 h) despite low-dose exposure.
Nephrotoxicity evaluation showed that KTZ NPs resulted in
higher cell viability compared to free KTZ treatment. This
indicates that the encapsulation of KTZ into nanostructures
improves its biocompatibility. It is thought that these outputs
will provide important benefits in future therapeutic applications. In addition, in further studies, the surface of NPs produced
