Optimizing the Process Parameters for Encapsulation of Linamarin into PLGA Nanoparticles Using Double Emulsion Solvent Evaporation Technique
Abstract
Linamarin-loaded poly(lactide- co -glycolide) (PLGA) nanoparticles were prepared by using a double emulsion solvent evaporation method using polyvinyl alcohol as a surfactant. A two-factor full factorial design, Box–Behnken design and surface response methodology, were employed to improve the nanoparticle yield, particle size, and drug encapsulation efficiency by determining the optimum levels of the process parameters, namely the polymer concentration, surfactant concentration, and homogenization speed. Both linear and quadratic empirical models were developed to express each response parameter as a function of the studied factors. A total of 12 and 17 experimental runs were necessary to obtain adequate empirical models ( p < 0.05) for both factorial and Box–Behnken designs, respectively. The final optimum values for the polymer concentration, surfactant concentration, and homogenization speed were determined at 9.8 mg/mL, 50 mg/mL, and 23,000 rpm, respectively. Formulations obtained at this optimum preparing condition resulted on average 96% yield, 61.2% drug encapsulation efficiency, and 144.6 nm particle size. However, all prepared nanoparticles showed some kind of controlled drug release; most likely the final optimized nanoparticles exhibited the most biphasic controlled drug release profile with a minimal burst release and overall drug release of <30% in 120 h of incubation. © 2012 Wiley Periodicals, Inc. Introduction
Polymeric and gold nanoparticles have been investigated as drug delivery systems for different biomedical applications such as in vitro and in vitro assay, in vivo imaging, and cancer therapy. 1 - 3 Targeting cancer cells is a major goal for most researchers who aim to convey a sufficient and controlled dose of drug to the tumor. 4 Linamarin (Fig. 1 ), a cyanogenic glycoside (2-β- d -glucopyranosyloxy-2-methylpropanenitrile), is found in cassava ( Manihot esculenta Crantz) root cortex and parenchyma and has good water solubility. The bitter taste of fresh cassava roots is associated with high levels (>90%) of cyanogenic glycosides, mainly linamarin. 5 , 6 Cytotoxicity effects of pure linamarin as well as aqueous and methanol extract of cassava linamarin on Caov3, MCF-7, HT-29, and HL-60 cells had proved to have a bright future as an alternative anticancer. 7 , 8 This anticancer activity of linamarin is attributed to the release of hydrogen cyanide (CN) upon hydrolysis of linamarin. It is important to highlight that HCN is toxic and can subsequently kill both cancerous as well as normal cells. Therefore, because of both the high water solubility of linamarin and toxicity of HCN, it is crucial to formulate well the drug delivery carrier to ensure that only the correct amount of linamarin is released in the human system at the right time and area to enhance drug efficacy and avoid any side effects. Linamarin's high water solubility represents a major challenge for its nanoencapsulation using an aqueous medium. A biodegradable poly( d,l -lactide- co -glycolide) (PLGA) polymer has been used in the current research as a carrier polymer to nanoencapsulated linamarin.

To improve product or process quality, it is crucial to understand the process behavior as well as amount of variability and its impact on the process. Most of the experimental designs involve changing one variable at a time while maintaining the others at a fixed level. The limitation of this type of experimentation is that it is unreliable, time consuming, requires a large number of runs and resources, and may also give false data because it ignores the interaction effects between the investigated process parameters. To overcome this problem, highly automated statistical tools such as design of experiments and response surface methodology (RSM) are highly recommended. The advantages of these statistical tools for empirical modeling, analysis, and optimization of any process are that all the parameters can be varied simultaneously over a set of experimental runs. Moreover, valid, reliable, and sound conclusions can be drawn effectively and economically. Experimental deigns such as factorial design and RSM have been widely used to find an approximating function for the prediction of future responses and to determine the factor values that could optimize the response function. 9 - 12 These are highly empirical models and treat the system as a black box. They relate the inputs and outputs by empirical correlations that fit reasonably well with the experimental results and are reliable only within the region of input–output of these experimental results. Because of the complexity of the physical and chemical phenomena involved, each drug delivery system should be first characterized precisely before carrying out the above-mentioned empirical modeling. Therefore, the best physicochemical characteristics of nanoparticles can be obtained by optimizing their encapsulation process. The aim of this work is to optimize the process parameters for preparation of linamarin-loaded PLGA nanoparticles.
Materials and Methods
MATERIALS
PLGA, with lactide:glycolide (L/G) ratios; (50:50), and average molecular weights of 40,000–75,000, 98% hydrolyzed polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) with an average molecular weight of 16,000, and dichloromethane (DCM) assay (≥99.8%) were purchased from Sigma–Aldrich (Poole, UK). Other analytical-grade chemicals, such as dipotassium hydrogen phosphate and potassium dihydrogen phosphate, disodium hydrogen phosphate, sodium hydroxide, sodium chloride, and potassium chloride were also supplied by Sigma–Aldrich. Linamarin with a purity of >97% was purchased from AG Scientific, Inc. (San Diego, CA).
EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN
The experimental design for the formulation of linamarin-loaded PLGA nanoparticles was carried out in two stages. PLGA nanoparticles prepared by using the emulsification solvent evaporation method have been found to be influenced by multiple parameters such as a PLGA (lactide/glycolide) ratio, polymer concentration, surfactant type and concentration, solvent nature, stirring rate and time, and formulation temperature. 13 - 18 A preliminary scouting study (screening process) was carried out by preparing some batches to determine the most significant process parameters that greatly affect the characteristics of the nanoparticles (NPs). After the screening process, the effect of only two factors, namely PVA concentration ( A ) and polymer concentration ( B ), were investigated using a 2 2 full factorial design. 19 , 20 The selected high and low levels were 30 and 50 mg/mL for PVA and 2.5 and 10 mg/mL for PLGA. The design was carried out in triplicate, two center points with a total of 14 completely randomized experiments. In the second stage, a new process parameter, namely homogenization speed ( C ), which ranged from 15,000 to 23,000 rpm, was introduced to the best values of the PVA concentration ( A ) and PLGA concentration ( B ) selected from the first stage. Both Box–Behnken design 21 - 23 combined with RSM 19 , 20 have been employed in the second stage of optimization. Meanwhile, in both stages the studied responses are nanoparticle yield, drug encapsulation efficiency, and particle size. Design Expert (6.0.4, Stat-Ease Inc., Minneapolis, MN) software was employed to design and analyze the data. The data collected using both designs were analyzed using ANOVA. 22
PREPARATION OF THE NANOPARTICLES
Linamarin-loaded nanoparticles were prepared using a double emulsion solvent evaporation, sometimes referred to as the water-in-oil-in-water (W/O/W) technique. The W/O/W technique employed in the current research was according to the one used by researchers. 24 - 27 Meanwhile, the preparation process was based on the use of a homogenizer (Heidolph DIAX 900, Germany) in a two-step emulsification process.
MORPHOLOGY OF THE NANOPARTICLES
To examine the surface morphology, the freeze-dried nanoparticles were adhered to a double-sided adhesive carbon tape placed on an aluminum stub and then coated with gold–palladium (Baltec SC030 Sputter Coater, Germany). The morphological characteristics of the nanoparticles were evaluated using a scanning electron microscope (Philips XL30 ESEM, Eindhoven, Netherlands).

PARTICLE SIZE
The average particle size was investigated using the photon correlation spectroscopy. The measurements of the particle size were performed as follows: 10 μL of the nanoparticle fresh emulsion was well suspended in 1 mL of distilled water to ensure a complete homogeneity. The average particle size of the suspension was then measured using a particle-size analyzer (Nanophox, Sympatec Gmball, System-Partikel-Technik, Königsbrunn, Germany).
IN VITRO DRUG RELEASE
A weighed quantity of the NPs was suspended in a closed conical flask containing a specific volume of 0.1 M phosphate-buffered saline pH 7.4. The NP suspensions were then incubated at 37°C, under a constant shaking rate at 150 rpm. At predetermined time intervals, 1.5 mL liquor of each sample was removed and centrifuged at 10,000 rpm to separate nanoparticles. One milliliter of sample supernatant was placed in a cuvette, and the absorbance of linamarin was measured at a wavelength of 545 nm (Spectronic Genesys TM and PC model 336003; Spectronic Instruments). A fresh 1.5-mL buffer solution was placed in the centrifuge tube and vigorously shaken to release the nanoparticles that adhered to the walls and bottom of the tube. Then, the liquor was returned to the incubation flask.
Results and Discussion
This research benefits from the advantage of biodegradable PLGA polymer to formulate PLGA nanoparticles loaded with linamarin as a controlled release anticancer candidate. Nanoparticulate drug delivery systems have been chosen as the convenient carriers to be prepared because of their multiple advantages such as high stability and drug loading capacity and good penetration into the cells. At the preliminary factor screening process, two copolymers, namely PLGA (50:50) and PLGA (85:15), were tested for the production of nanoparticles. Nanoparticles produced using PLGA (50:50) were better in terms of their physicochemical characteristics compared to nanoparticles prepared using PLGA (85:15); therefore, PLGA (50:50) was employed throughout this work. It was also observed that increasing the volume of an internal aqueous drug solution increases the particle size and decreases the DEE. The same observation was also previously reported by other researchers. 18 , 31 Therefore, the volume of the internal aqueous solution was kept at 1 mL throughout all experiments. The internal aqueous solution of volume <1 mL was excluded due to difficulty in dispersing the concentrated and viscous drug solution into the organic phase. It is also expected that drug loss on container walls will increase during its transfer to the organic solution.
Successful formulations of linamarin-loaded PLGA nanoparticles were carried out by employing the double emulsion solvent evaporation technique. The best PVA concentration limits were selected as 30–50 mg/mL because a concentration of <30 mg/mL yielded microparticles rather than nanoparticles, whereas those of >50 mg/mL yielded nanoparticles with high surface residual PVA that was difficult to remove by washing. The screening process revealed that homogenization has an advantage over sonication as a method of emulsification because the latter produced particles with a size of >50 μm. The best-selected total homogenization time for the double emulsification process was selected at 6 min (3 min/each). This selectivity was due to the fact that at the homogenization time of >6 min, no obvious differences in particle sizes were observed; instead, some distortions were observed on the particle surfaces (broken particles). After the screening process, the surfactant (PVA) and polymer (PLGA) were first selected as two important factors to be studied and their concentration limits were determined. The effect of these selected factors on characteristics of the nanoparticles was first investigated using the factorial design. Nanoparticle Yield
Preparation techniques with high yields avoid considerably material loss, enhance production, and reduce manufacturing cost. Interestingly, nearly all the formulated batches gave yields that ranged between 40% and 87%. Variations observed with all formulations indicate that the responses are strongly affected by the process factors. From the ANOVA table, it was found that the factors A , B , C , A 2 , and AB are statistically significant ( p < 0.05). The model has good correlation coefficients ( R 2 = 0.9641 and adjusted R 2 = 0.9180), indicating that the empirical model is suitable for the prediction of the response with respect to the process factors. The data normality was evaluated using the normal probability plot of residuals (Fig. 2 ).

The yield was changed from 40% to >80% with an increase in the polymer (PLGA) concentration from 2.5 mg/mL (coded −1.00) to 10 mg/mL (coded +1.00), indicating that the higher the polymer concentration used in the formulation, the greater the yield was. Three-dimensional response surface and contour plots were also used to estimate the effects of the independent process factors on the yield. The greater increase in the yield (from 49.3% to >85%) was observed with higher values in both the PLGA concentration (10 mg/mL) and homogenization speed (23,000 rpm). Meanwhile, increasing homogenization speed, from 15,000 to 23,000 rpm, was also associated with an increase in the yield from 50% to 62% and from 60% to >85% at 6.25 and 10 mg/mL of PLGA concentration, respectively (Fig. 5 b).
The curvature observed on the surface response and the contour plots explain a nonlinear relationship between the yield and the different process parameters, and it also indicates the interaction effect. In fact, it is clearly seen that the yield ranging between 68% and 73% could only be obtained at high levels of the different process parameters. The increase in the yield with increasing PLGA and PVA concentrations could be explained by the greater stabilizing property of PVA during the emulsification process, which will enhance stabilization of the globules and formation of more particles. 35 , 36 The high PLGA concentration means viscous solutions, leading to larger and denser particles being produced, and this will subsequently increase the yield. The increase in the yield with PVA, PLGA concentrations, and homogenization speed observed in this research work correlates with those reported by Mao and colleagues, who reported an increase in the yield from 77% to 83% with an increasing PLGA concentration from 8% to 32%. The author also reported an increase in the yield from 80% to 83% when the PVA concentration increased from 0.1% to 1%. 30 , 37
Drug Encapsulation Efficiency
It is desirable to produce a drug delivery system with a high drug encapsulation efficiency to reduce the quantity and frequency of the administration of the drug. Efforts and precautions have been made to enhance encapsulation efficiencies (EE) because the double emulsion method (W/O/W) generally suffers from low EE because of the long time required for solvent removal and washings. 27,38−41 The data observed for EE were analyzed, and from ANOVA results, the empirical model has a significant F value ( p < 0.0001), insignificant lack of fit ( p < 0.6369), and R 2 (0.9830) and adjusted R 2 (0.9612). It was found that A , B , C , A 2 , B 2 , C 2 , and AC are statistically significant model terms ( p < 0.05), whereas AB and BC are not significant. The fitness of the quadratic empirical model was also estimated from the normal plot of the residuals, as shown in Fig. 6 .

Figure 8 shows a considerable increase in the DEE (i.e., from 31% to 62%) with the increase in both the PVA concentration and homogenization speed to their maximum values. However, the DEE increased from 31% to 56.7% with an increasing homogenization speed from 15,000 to 23,000 rpm at 40 mg/mL PVA, and from 52.9% to 59.4% at 50 mg/mL PVA concentration. This indicates that beyond 50 mg/mL PVA the effect of the homogenization speed on the DEE tends to be small. These results could also be easily depicted by the contour plots (Fig. 8 b).

The limited effect of the homogenization speed at a high PVA concentration could be due to the limited dispersion of the highly viscous PVA solution. Mao and colleagues reported an increase in drug DEE from 83% to 90% with increasing PVA concentration from 0.1% to 1%. 37 , 44 The authors also reported an increase in the DEE from 81% to 91% with an increasing homogenization speed from 13,500 to 22,500 rpm. 44 However, our results correlate with those found by Mao and colleagues; they contradict those reported by Manchanda and colleagues, who reported a decrease in the DEE from 70% to 5% for indocyanine green and from 40% to 10% for doxorubicin when they increased the PVA concentration from 1% to 5%. 45
From Figs. 9 a and 9 b, it is also apparent that there is a sharp increase in the DEE with the increase in both PLGA and homogenization speed. The curvilinear lines shown in contour plots (Figs. 7b, 8b, and 9b) indicate a nonlinear relationship between the process parameters, the PLGA concentration and homogenization speed, and the DEE. The DEE was observed to increase, that is, from the range of (31.2–40.8%) to the range of (47.3–55%), with an increase in the PLGA concentration (i.e., from 6.25 to 10 mg/mL), and homogenization speed (from 15,000 to 23,000 rpm). The great effect of the homogenization speed on the DEE might be attributed to the energy exerted, which could have enhanced the dispersion of liquids, as well as the formation of the globules that accommodated the drug. 46 Generally, the change in DEE with respect to the PLGA concentration was greater than that observed with changing PVA concentration. The lowest DEE was 19.7%, whereas the highest was around 62.0%. The highest values of the DEE (i.e., from 53.0% to 62.0%) were obtained with high PLGA concentrations (8.13–10 mg/mL).

The increase in the DEE related to the polymer concentration might be related to the increasing viscosity of the organic phase (emulsion droplet) of W/O/W emulsion, which lies in between two aqueous phases (internal drug solution and external surfactant solution). Any increase in the viscosity this organic phase could reduce the drug diffusion, that is, from the inner aqueous phase to the outer aqueous one. As a result, more drug molecules will be incorporated into the nanoparticles; hence an enhanced DEE will be expected. A twofold increase in DEE, as a result of the increasing polymer concentration (i.e., from 8% to 32%) was also reported by Mao and colleagues. 44 Manchanda and colleagues also reported an increase in DEE from 15% to 22% for indocyanine green (ICG) and to 38% for doxorubicin when they increased the PLGA concentration from 13.3 to 66.7 mg/mL. 45 Meanwhile, an increase in the DEE with the increase in the polymer concentration was also reported by other authors. 16,17,47−49
The increase in the DEE, associated with the hike in the PVA concentrations, could also be explained by the greater stabilizing property of the higher surfactant (PVA) concentration during the emulsification process. 43 The rise in the DEE with the PVA concentration was also observed by Feng and Huang. 50 The smaller the particle sizes produced at the high PVA concentration, the larger surface areas will be, and this may increase the chance for drug molecules to be attached to the nanoparticle surfaces; thus the DEE will be increased. The result of high DEE with increasing the homogenization speed was also found by several authors. 51 , 52 Most of the nanoparticles produced in this study exhibited a DEE greater than 45%, which are considered good values for water-soluble drugs since they suffer from low EE due to their rapid partitioning to the external aqueous phase. 27,53−55 From the contour plots, it is clear that the process parameters should be set at one of three different parameter combinations to produce nanoparticles with high DEE (>50%). The combinations are (a) about 45–50 mg/mL PVA with >9 mg/mL PLGA (Fig. 7 ), (b) about >45 mg/mL PVA with >20,000 rpm homogenization speed (Fig. 8 ), or (c) at a value of >8.13 mg/mL PLGA with >19,000 rpm homogenization speed (Fig. 9 ).
Nanoparticle Size and Size Distribution
Nanoparticle size was investigated because it affects the physical stability, cellular uptake, biodistribution, drug release, and polymer degradation. Generally, all measurements of particle size and size distribution using photon correlation spectroscopy gave a slightly different interpretation and more accurate results than those observed using the SEM micrographs. Particle size measurements were analyzed, and the model F value was found to be significant ( p < 0.0001). The process parameters A , B , C , A 2 , B 2 , C 2 , AC , and BC were statistically significant ( p < 0.05), whereas AB was insignificant, and the model has an insignificant lack of fit ( p = 0.8111), high R 2 (0.9897), and adjusted R 2 (0.9765). The model fitness was also evaluated using the normal plot of residuals (Fig. 10 ).

Normal plot of the residuals for the particle size.
The average particle sizes of the produced nanoparticles ranged from about 150 up to 1500 nm. The small-sized nanoparticles ensure efficient adhesions to and interactions with biological cells. 56 , 57 Large particles (>1.5 μm) were also observed in the samples after being freeze dried. Since measurements were carried out on fresh nanoparticles emulsions and resuspended freeze-dried nanoparticles, the formation of large particles might be a result of the aggregation or fusion of small-sized particles during solvent evaporation or the freeze-drying process. The tendency of nanoparticles to aggregate to form larger particles during the freeze-drying process was also previously reported. 58 , 59 To obtain a better performance of the NPs, the size was reduced as small as possible by optimizing the process parameters. Effects of the process parameters, polymer (PLGA) concentration, surfactant (PVA) concentration, and homogenization speed on the size of the particles were evaluated. Our results indicate that increasing both PVA concentration and homogenization speed reduces the particle size. On the other hand, increasing PLGA concentration increases the particle size.
Response surface and contour plots display the effects of the process parameters on the particle size. Fig. 11 a shows that to obtain smaller particles it is favorable to formulate nanoparticles using high PVA and relatively low PLGA concentration. Nanoparticles with an average size <350 nm were produced at high PVA concentrations (>48 mg/mL) and low PLGA concentrations (<6.5 mg/mL). Formulations at <45 mg/mL PVA and >8.13 mg/mL PLGA yielded nanoparticles with an average size >747 nm (Fig. 11 b).

The response surface and contour plots (Figs. 12 a and 12 b) show the effects of the PVA concentration and homogenization speed on the particle size. It is obvious that small nanoparticles, with an average size of 203 nm, are likely to be produced when both the PVA concentration and homogenization speed are set at high levels at 50 mg/mL and 23,000 rpm, respectively. The decrease in the particle size with PVA is gradual, whereas there is a sharp decrease in the particle size with respect to an increase in the homogenization speed.

Figures 13a and 13b show that high homogenization speed and relatively low PLGA concentration are favorable formulation conditions for the production of smaller nanoparticles. The smallest nanoparticles (188.6 nm) were produced at the 6.25-mg/mL PLGA concentration and 23,000 rpm. The change in the particle size, as a result of increasing PLGA concentration (6–10 mg/mL), was large (453–1198 nm). An increase in the particle size with increasing PLGA concentration was also previously reported by Bilati and collegues. 60 On the other hand, the size decreased from 453 to 203.4 nm with an increasing homogenization speed from 15,000 to 23,000 rpm. The contour plot shows a nonlinear relationship between the particle size and PLGA concentration and homogenization speed, which signifies strong interactions between these process parameters, specifically at high values. Although all contour plots showed some degree of interaction, the effect was more predominant in the PLGA concentration with homogenization speed contour plot (Fig. 13 b).

Thus, it could be generally concluded that the particle size decreased and the granulometric distribution became narrower when the PVA concentration was increased. This PVA concentration effect could be explained by stabilization of the globules during emulsification, which is the most crucial step in the double emulsion solvent evaporation method. Meanwhile, the amount of surfactant plays a key role in the emulsification process as well as protection of the droplets, which avoids the coalescence of the globules. 61 , 62 In particular, a very low surfactant concentration usually leads to the aggregation of polymer droplets and production of large particles; consequently, little or even no nanoparticle will be produced. Thus, the surfactant concentration should be between the “limits” to control the nanoparticle size and other properties such as DEE. 63 , 64 Manchanda and coworkers reported a decrease in the particle size from 159 to 113 nm with an increasing PVA concentration from 1% to 5%, whereas the particle size increased from 137 to 164 nm with an increasing PLGA concentration from 13.3 to 66.7 mg/mL. 65 The decrease in the particle size with an increasing PVA concentration has been widely reported by other researchers. 58 , 60 , 66 - 69
The increase in the particle size with respect to the PLGA concentration could probably be due to the enhanced viscosity of the organic phase, and consequently this will reduce dispersion of the aqueous phase containing a drug into the polymer organic phase. Because the final size of the particles is dependent upon the first globule size generated throughout the emulsification process, coarse emulsions or a greater aggregation number may be obtained as a result of a high PLGA concentration and this will lead to larger particles with wider size distributions. 66 , 70 - 72 Our findings correlate with those found by Le-sheng and colleagues, who reported an increase in both particle size (i.e., from 34.3% to 70%) and DEE (i.e., from 55% to 76%) when they increased the PLGA concentration from 15% to 40%. The authors also reported a decrease in the size with the homogenization speed. 73 A similar observation was also found by Budhian and coworkers, who reported an increase in the particle size from 200 to 300 nm when the polymer concentration was varied from 5 to 66.6 mg/mL. 74 Meanwhile, the increase in the particle size with an increasing polymer concentration for the PLA and PLGA polymers has been widely reported. 3 , 18 , 37 , 61 , 66 , 75 , 76
After investigating the effects of the PVA and PLGA concentration and homogenization speed on the yield, DEE, and particle size, the best values of these parameters for semifinal optimization were determined as follows: (1) about >48 mg/mL PVA concentration and <6.5 mg/mL PLGA concentration; (2) about >47 mg/mL PVA concentration and >22,000 rpm homogenization speed; and (3) about <7.19 mg/mL PLGA concentration and >20,000 rpm homogenization speed. Based on these selected values, a numerical optimization using the desirability approach was applied to locate the optimal settings of the formulation parameters to obtain the desired responses. Based on the desired nanoparticle properties, numerous formulations were proposed by setting constraints on the independent process parameters and responses.
DRUG RELEASE FROM THE OPTIMIZED NANOPARTICLES
The drug release profiles of the optimized, nonoptimized nanoparticles compared to that of free drug, are shown in Fig. 14 . From the figure, it is clear that the free drug (linamarin) was released completely within a period of less than 8 h. The nonoptimized nanoparticles exhibited fast (burst) release >40% within the first 10 h, whereas the optimized nanoparticles showed good biphasic release profiles having small burst release values (10%–20%) followed by a controlled release phase compared to that of the nonoptimized nanoparticles.
Figure 14 Open in figure viewer PowerPoint Drug release profiles of the free linamarin and optimized and nonoptimized linamarin-loaded PLGA (50:50) nanoparticles.
Drug release profiles of the free linamarin and optimized and nonoptimized linamarin-loaded PLGA (50:50) nanoparticles.
The burst release values observed in the current research using optimized PLGA nanoparticles are considered good compared to values previously reported by Jiang and his coworkers 82 and Teixeira and coworkers, 83 which are 20%–45% and 30%–70%, respectively. However, nonoptimized nanoparticles showed a total of about 60%–80% drug release after 120 h, the optimized nanoparticles released only about 25%–35% of their encapsulated drug. These results are good compared to the results found by Cohen-Sela and coworkers, who reported 89.2% drug release from the PLGA nanoparticles in only 6 h. 27 The slow controlled release of PLGA nanoparticles observed in the present study correlates with those found by Mu and Feng, 84 who reported a release of only 50%–70% of the encapsulated anticancer drug (Paclitaxel) from the PLGA nanoparticles in 30 days. It could be concluded that the optimized nanoparticles exhibited better release (slow and controlled) properties compared to both nonoptimized nanoparticles and the free drug (linamarin).
Conclusions
Successful linamarin-loaded PLGA nanoparticles were fabricated by using the double emulsion solvent evaporation method. The effect of the most effective process parameters, namely polymer (PLGA) concentration, surfactant (PVA) concentration, and homogenization speed on the NP yield, particle size, and DEE was evaluated. The optimal values of the studied process parameters were determined with the aid of both a two-factor full factorial design and Box–Behnken design with surface response methodology (RSM). The final optimum values for the PLGA concentration, PVA concentration, and homogenization speed were determined at 9.8 mg/mL, 50 mg/mL, and 23,000 rpm, respectively. The experimental results were found to be in good agreement with those predicted by the empirical model. Thus it could be concluded that RSM represents an effective and potential technique for optimization of linamarin-loaded PLGA nanoparticles. Interestingly, the optimized nanoparticles showed a sustained and controlled drug release (lasting for days) compared to both the free drug as well as the nonoptimized drug.
