## Materials Methods
PLGA (molecular weight: 15-25 kDa, LA:GA = 50:50) and PLGA-NH2 (molecular weight: 30-40 kDa, LA:GA = 50:50) were bought from PolySciTech (Akina, Inc., West Lafayette, IN, USA). 1,1'-Dioctadecyl- 3,3,3',3'-tetramethylindocarbocyanine perchlorate (DiI), human serum albumin (HSA), and poly(vinyl alcohol) (PVA) were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich Co. (St. Louis, MO, USA). Phloretin and sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) were obtained from Tokyo Chemical Industry Co. Ltd. (Tokyo, Japan). Dulbecco's modified Eagle's medium (DMEM), fetal bovine serum (FBS), penicillin, streptomycin, and phosphate buffered saline (PBS) were acquired from Gibco Life Technologies, Inc. (Carlsbad, CA, USA). All other reagents were of analytical grade and obtained from commercial sources.
2.2. Preparation and characterization of phloretin-loaded NPs
PLGA and PLGA-NH2 were dissolved in chloroform-d (CDCl3) for 1H NMR analysis (Varian FT-500 MHz; Varian Inc., Palo Alto, CA, USA). The chemical composition of PLGA-NH2 was measured by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS; K-Alpha+, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA). XPS was used for measuring the atomic concentration of C (1s), N (1s), and O (1s) in PLGA-NH2. Spot size was 30-400 mm (5 mm step).
Phloretin-loaded PLGA NPs and PLGA-NH2 NPs were prepared using a modified emulsification-solvent evaporation method , . Briefly, phloretin (6 mg) was dissolved in dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO; 0.2 mL) and it was mixed with dichloromethane (DCM; 1.3 mL). PLGA or PLGA-NH2 (60 mg) was solubilized in that solution and mixed with DCM (1.5 mL). That organic phase was added to 15 mL PVA solution (0.5%, w/v) at a rate of 1 mL/min. That emulsion was sonicated for 10 min in the ice using a probe sonicator (VC-750; Sonics & Materials, Inc., Newtown, CT, USA) and the organic solvent was eliminated by stirring for 1 h at room temperature. The aqueous dispersion of drug-loaded NPs was then centrifuged at 13,200 rpm for 30 min and the pellet of NPs was collected by discarding the supernatant. It was resuspended in distilled deionized water (DDW; 3 mL). The centrifugation and resuspension step was repeated three times to remove unencapsulated drug and residual PVA completely. Drug-loaded NPs were lyophilized and stored at -20 degC for further uses.
The mean diameter, polydispersity, and zeta potential of developed NPs were measured using dynamic light scattering (DLS) and laser Doppler methods (ELS-Z1000; Otsuka Electronics, Tokyo, Japan). The encapsulation efficiency (EE) of phloretin in NPs was quantitatively measured using a high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) system equipped with a pump (PU-2089 Plus; Jasco, Tokyo, Japan), an automatic injector (AS-2050 Plus), and an UV/Vis detector (UV-1575). NPs were dissolved in DMSO and diluted with mobile phase of HPLC assay. The mobile phase was composed of acetonitrile, DDW, and phosphoric acid (50:50:0.08, volume ratio) and the flow rate was maintained at 1 mL/min. A reverse phase C18 column (Gemini, 250 mm x 4.6 mm, 5 mm; Phenomenex, Torrance, CA, USA) was used and the injection volume was 20 mL. The absorbance of eluent was measured at 288 nm wavelength. The inter- and intra-day variances were within the acceptable range. The morphological shape of phloretin-loaded NPs was observed using a variable pressure-field emission-scanning electron microscope (VP-FE-SEM; SUPRA 55VP, Carl Zeiss, Oberkochen, Germany). NPs were mounted on stubs and coated with Pt under vacuum.
2.3. Solid state studies
Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) thermograms of phloretin, PLGA/phloretin NPs, and PLGA-NH2/phloretin NPs were acquired using DSC-Q100 model (TA instruments, New Castle, DE, USA). Aliquot of each sample was scanned in the range of 30-275 degC at 10 degC/min rate under a nitrogen atmosphere.
X-ray diffractometry (XRD) analysis of phloretin, PLGA/phloretin NPs, and PLGA-NH2/phloretin NPs was conducted with D8 ADVANCE with DAVINCI model (Bruker, Germany). CuKa-radiation (1.5418 A) was used in 8-45deg 2th range with 0.02 step and 0.5 s/step scan speed at 40 mA and 40 kV of generator conditions.
2.4. In vitro stability of NPs
The stability of PLGA/phloretin NPs and PLGA-NH2/phloretin NPs was evaluated in HSA solution (5%, w/v), FBS (50%, v/v) solution, and PBS (pH 7.4). Freeze-dried NPs were dispersed in each medium at 5 mg/mL and the mean diameter of NPs was monitored by DLS method as described previously. The mean diameters of PLGA/phloretin NPs and PLGA-NH2/phloretin NPs were measured at 0 h (pre) and 24 h.
2.5. In vitro drug release
The release of phloretin from prepared NPs was assessed in PBS media (pH 5.5, 6.8, and 7.4) containing 0.3% (w/v) SDS. Aliquot of NPs dispersion (150 mL), including 200 mg phloretin, was loaded into a mini GeBA-flex dialysis tube (14 kDa molecular weight cut-off; Gene Bio-Application Ltd., Kfar Hanagide, Israel). That dialysis tube was then put into 10 mL PBS (pH 5.5, 6.8, and 7.4 adjusted with phosphoric acid) including 0.3% (w/v) SDS. Those samples were incubated in a shaking bath at 37 degC with 100 rpm agitation speed. Aliquots (200 mL) of release media were collected at determined times (2, 4, 6, 24, 48, 72, 96, and 168 h) and an equivalent volume of fresh media was supplemented. The released amounts of phloretin in the release media were quantitatively determined by described HPLC method. The stability of phloretin in each release medium (pH 5.5, 6.8, and 7.4 buffers including 0.3% SDS) was also measured by described HPLC method. Excess amount of phloretin was added to each medium and vortex-mixed. That sample was centrifuged at 13,200 rpm for 5 min and the supernatant was filtered through syringe membrane filter with 0.2 mm pore size. After incubating at 37 degC for 168 h, the drug concentration was determined and the degradation degree was calculated.
2.6. In vitro cytotoxicity
Hep-2 cells were acquired from the Korean Cell Line Bank (Seoul, Korea). Hep-2 cells were cultured in DMEM including 10% (v/v) heat-inactivated FBS, 1% (v/v) penicillin (100 U/mL), and streptomycin (0.1 mg/mL) in 95% relative humidity and 5% CO2 atmosphere at 37 degC. The cytotoxicity of blank (drug-unloaded) PLGA NPs and PLGA-NH2 NPs was evaluated by colorimetric method using a tetrazolium compound [3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-5-(3-carboxymethoxyphenyl)-2-(4-sulfophenyl)-2H-tetrazolium, inner salt; MTS] and an electron coupling reagent. Hep-2 cells were seeded onto 96-well plates at a density of 1.0 x 104 cells per well and incubated for 24 h. Blank PLGA NPs and PLGA-NH2 NPs with various concentrations (25, 50, 100, 250, 500, and 1000 mg/mL) were added to cells and incubated for 48 and 72 h at 37 degC. CellTiter 96 Aqueous One Solution Cell Proliferation Assay Reagent (Promega Corp., Madison, WI, USA), containing a tetrazolium compound (MTS) and an electron coupling reagent, was added to cells and incubated for 4 h according to the manufacturer's protocol. The absorbance was measured at 490 nm using an EMax Precision Microplate Reader (Molecular Devices, Sunnyvale, CA, USA) and the cell viability (%, compared with control group) was calculated.
Cell viability after incubation of phloretin solution and phloretin-loaded NPs was also evaluated in Hep-2 cells. Solution and NPs containing 1, 2.5, 5, and 10 mg/mL phloretin was added to Hep-2 cells and incubated for 72 h. Other procedures were done as described above.
2.7. Cellular uptake
The cellular uptake efficiency and cellular distribution of PLGA NPs and PLGA-NH2 NPs in Hep-2 cells were evaluated by flow cytometry and confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM). As a fluorescent dye, DiI was incorporated into the NPs instead of phloretin at 1:40 weight ratio (dye to polymer) according to the described method in Section 2.2. Hep-2 cells were seeded onto 6-well plates at a density of 6.0 x 105 cells per well and incubated for 24 h at 37 degC. Aliquots of NPs dispersion (containing 1 mg/mL DiI) were incubated for 4 and 24 h and cells were washed with PBS (pH 7.4) at least three times. Cell pellets were collected by centrifugation and they were resuspended with PBS containing FBS (2%, v/v). The fluorescence intensity of each group was measured using a FACSCalibur fluorescence-activated cell sorter (FACS(tm)) equipped with CellQuest software (Becton Dickinson Biosciences, San Jose, CA, USA).
Intracellular distribution of DiI solution and DiI-loaded NPs in Hep-2 cells was observed by CLSM. Cells were seeded onto culture slides (BD Falcon, Bedford, MA, USA) at a density of 1.0 x 105 cells per well (1.7 cm2 surface area per well) and incubated for 24 h at 37 degC. DiI solution, DiI-loaded PLGA NPs, and DiI-loaded PLGA-NH2 NPs (at 1 mg/mL concentration of DiI) were incubated for 4 and 24 h at 37 degC. Those cells were washed with PBS (pH 7.4) at least three times and fixed with 4% (v/v) formaldehyde solution for 10 min. Liquid contents were eliminated by drying and VECTASHIELD mounting medium including 4',6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI) (H-1200; Vector Laboratories, Inc., Burlingame, CA, USA) was added to stain the nuclei of cells and prevent fluorescence quenching. The fluorescence signals of DiI, indicating the intracellular distribution of NPs, were observed by CLSM (LSM 780; Carl-Zeiss, Thornwood, NY, USA).
2.8. Tumor penetration
Hep-2 cells were seeded onto the round-bottomed 96-well plates coated with agarose layer (2% [w/v] in Hank's balanced salt solution) at 500 cells per well density and incubated in 5% CO2 atmosphere and 95% relative humidity at 37 degC with gentle agitation. Cell culture medium used in two-dimensional cell culture studies was replaced every other day.
Tumor penetration of developed NPs in Hep-2 spheroid model was assessed by CLSM. DiI-loaded PLGA NPs and PLGA-NH2 NPs (at 1 mg/mL concentration of DiI) were incubated for 24 h at 37 degC. Hep-2 spheroids were washed with PBS (pH 7.4) and the distribution of NPs was observed by CLSM (LSM 880; Carl-Zeiss, Thornwood, NY, USA).
Spheroid growth inhibition study was started after reaching around 300 mm mean diameter of spheroid. Drug solution (5 mg/mL), drug-loaded NPs (5 mg/mL), and blank NPs were added and incubated for 24 h. After removing drug solution or NPs, fresh cell culture media were added and replaced every other day. Morphological shape of Hep-2 spheroids was monitored by inverted microscopy (Eclipse TS100-F, Nikon, Tokyo, Japan) for 4 days. The volume (V, mm3) of spheroid was calculated by following formula; V = 0.5 x longest diameter x (shortest diameter)2.
2.9. Statistical analysis
All experiments were repeated at least three times and the data are shown as the mean +- standard deviation (SD). Statistical analysis was performed using two-tailed Student's t-test and analysis of variance (ANOVA).

## Results
3.1. Preparation and characterization of NPs
Amine-functionalized polymeric NPs (PLGA-NH2 NPs) were developed for the delivery of phloretin in this investigation. The solubility of phloretin in water was 15.3 mg/mL in this study. Polymeric NPs were developed for the solubilization and delivery of phloretin. In particular, the influences of amine groups located on the outer surface of NPs on cellular accumulation and distribution and tumor penetration were investigated with PLGA-NH2 NPs. In this study, although polymers with different molecular weights have been used, their influences on the fabrication of NPs and drug encapsulation may be insignificant. However, biodegradation of those two NPs in the blood stream may be different, thus it may be further investigated.
PLGA-NH2 was synthesized by amide bond formation between COOH group of PLGA and NH2 group of ethylenediamine (Fig. S1). Shift "a" in the 1H NMR spectrum of PLGA-NH2 indicates the successful introduction of ethylenediamine into the PLGA (Fig. S1B). The content of N atom in PLGA-NH2 was further measured by XPS (Fig. S2). The results of XPS provide chemical composition of atomic layers on the surface of PLGA-NH2. The atomic contents of C 1s, O 1s, and N 1s were 60.77%, 37.34%, and 1.89%. It indicates the successful introduction of amine group in PLGA.
As shown in Fig. 1 and Table 1, PLGA/phloretin NPs and PLGA-NH2/phloretin NPs exhibited 222 and 237 nm hydrodynamic mean diameter, respectively, and narrow size distribution (<0.1 polydispersity index). Although PLGA-NH2 NPs had a less negative (close to zero) zeta potential value than PLGA NPs, they still exhibited a negative zeta potential value due to the ionized carboxyl group of PLGA. The EE values of drug in developed NPs were 44-50% in this study. Due to insolubility of phloretin in DCM at designed concentration, it was dissolved in DMSO first and mixed with DCM. Unlike DCM, DMSO can be miscible with the aqueous phase (PVA solution in this study) and some portion of phloretin in DMSO may leak out from the oil phase during the hardening process of NPs by stirring. It may influence on the observed EE of drug. Observed mean diameters of fabricated NPs seem to be appropriate for passive tumor targeting via an EPR effect . Spherical shape and narrow size distribution of developed PLGA/phloretin NPs and PLGA-NH2/phloretin NPs were observed by SEM (Fig. 1). Similar particle size, as measured by DLS method, was also shown in SEM images.
Data are presented as mean +- standard deviation (SD) (n = 3).

3.2. Solid state studies
Thermodynamic properties of drug encapsulated in NPs and its interaction with polymer matrix were investigated by DSC and XRD analyses (Fig. S3). In DSC thermogram of phloretin (Fig. S3A), sharp endothermic peak was observed at 270.5 degC, indicating the melting point of phloretin. On the contrary, in DSC thermograms of PLGA/phloretin NPs and PLGA-NH2/phloretin NPs, that endothermic peak of phloretin was absent and a broad peak was shown at 50-51 degC which is assumed to be a glass transition temperature of PLGA . The disappearance of crystallinity of phloretin in PLGA NPs and PLGA-NH2 NPs implies the drug amorphization during the preparation process of NPs. In XRD pattern of phloretin (Fig. S3B), sharp diffraction peaks are shown in 2th value of 9.5deg, 13.9deg, 17.7deg, 19.0deg, 20.9deg, 27.0deg, and 27.9deg. Those sharp diffraction peaks indicate the crystalline characteristic of phloretin. However, those sharp peaks were absent in the XRD patterns of PLGA/phloretin NPs and PLGA-NH2/phloretin NPs. Like the results of DSC analysis (Fig. S3A), it can provide the evidences for drug amorphization and the molecular dispersion of drug in the polymer matrix.
Transformation of drug from crystalline to amorphous state for the development of nanocarriers aimed at intravenous administration has been reported , . Drug amorphization during the preparation step of NPs and its molecular dispersion into the polymer matrix may contribute to efficient drug encapsulation into NPs and controlled drug release from NPs. Observed thermodynamic properties of drug and drug-loaded NPs suggest drug amorphization and its dispersion in the NPs.
3.3. In vitro stability of NPs
In vitro stability of phloretin-loaded PLGA NPs and PLGA-NH2 NPs was evaluated in biological fluid-simulated media by measuring particle size of NPs (Fig. 2). Hydrodynamic size of NPs in water cannot predict the actual size in the blood stream after intravenous administration as reported , . Opsonin proteins in the blood serum can bind to non-stealth NPs and they can be easily recognized by the macrophages of the mononuclear phagocytic system (MPS), which may lead to the elimination of NPs prior to its sufficient delivery to the target site . Formation of abnormal aggregates of NPs can also block normal blood circulation and provoke related unwanted effects.
The alteration of hydrodynamic size of drug-loaded NPs according to the incubation time (up to 24 h) in HSA (5%, w/v) solution, FBS (50%, v/v) solution, and PBS (pH 7.4) was observed (Fig. 2). Although the mean diameters of PLGA/phloretin NPs and PLGA-NH2/phloretin NPs after 24 h incubation in HSA solution were slightly higher than those in PBS group, they were still in the range of 200-300 nm (216 nm and 261 nm, respectively). Considering the initial particle sizes of both NPs, there was no significant increment in the mean diameter after 24 h incubation in HSA, FBS, and PBS media. NPs based on polyester derivatives did not show any obvious aggregation based on their interaction with serum components as reported previously , . In vitro stability of NPs can attribute to the prolonged circulation of NPs in the blood stream after intravenous administration.
3.4. In vitro drug release
In this investigation, phloretin release from PLGA NPs and PLGA-NH2 NPs was measured at pH 5.5, 6.8, and 7.4. Determined pH values (5.5, 6.8, and 7.4) represent the pH value of endocytic compartments (i.e., endosomes and lysosomes), tumor microenvironment, and normal physiological conditions, respectively. Regardless of the type of NPs (PLGA NPs versus PLGA-NH2 NPs), the released amounts of phloretin until 24 h were similar at all pH values (Fig. 3). However, at longer incubation time (~ 168 h), the released amounts of drug were higher at pH 5.5 rather than pH 7.4 in both kinds of NPs groups. The released amount of drug from PLGA NPs, after 168 h incubation, at pH 5.5 was 1.78-fold higher than that at pH 7.4. In case of PLGA-NH2 NPs group, the ratio of released amounts of drug at pH 5.5-7.4 was 1.63. Notably, around 100% drug was released from PLGA-NH2 NPs at pH 5.5 media. Slight declining pattern was also observed in the drug release profiles at pH 7.4, rather than pH 5.5. It may be due to the faster degradation of drug at pH 7.4, compared with pH 5.5. The degradation degree of phloretin in each release medium was ordered as follows: pH 5.5 < pH 6.8 < pH 7.4.
Taken together, higher drug release at pH 5.5, compared with pH 7.4, might be explained by the faster degradation of PLGA and higher stability of drug at acidic pH , . Higher released amount of drug at acidic pH implies the higher exposure of drug and improved antitumor efficacies in cellular organelles and tumor microenvironments rather than normal tissues and organs. Observed pH-dependent release pattern may be regarded as one of ideal properties of injection formulations for cancer therapy as well as a sustained release profile.
3.5. In vitro cytotoxicity
The cytotoxicity of blank (drug-unloaded) PLGA NPs and PLGA-NH2 NPs was assessed in Hep-2 cells (Fig. 4). Hep-2 cells were originated from human laryngeal carcinoma and they have been used for evaluating antitumor efficacies of many chemotherapeutic agents , . Cell viability (%), after 48 and 72 h incubation of various concentrations (~1 mg/mL) of blank PLGA NPs and PLGA-NH2 NPs, was measured by colorimetric assay. Even at the highest concentrations of polymers (1 mg/mL), the cell viability was around 100% and it indicates the negligible toxicity to Hep-2 cells. The biocompatibility and biodegradability of PLGA or PLGA derivatives, based on the hydrolysis of PLGA into lactic acid and glycolic acid, have been widely studied . Cytotoxicity data of blank NPs suggest the safe application of developed NPs for cancer therapy.
In vitro anticancer activity of phloretin-loaded NPs was also evaluated in Hep-2 cells (Fig. S4). Cell viability after incubating phloretin solution, PLGA/phloretin NPs, and PLGA-NH2/phloretin NPs for 72 h was measured. Due to the different cellular uptake mechanism (passive diffusion of drug solution versus endocytosis of drug-loaded NPs), cytotoxicity of phloretin-loaded NPs was not higher than that of phloretin solution significantly. However, PLGA-NH2/phloretin NPs exhibited a higher cytotoxicity rather than that of PLGA/phloretin NPs at 5 and 10 mg/mL drug concentrations (p < 0.05). This result was further confirmed by following cellular uptake studies in Hep-2 cells (Fig. 5, Fig. 6).
3.6. In vitro cellular uptake
Cellular uptake efficiency and intracellular distribution of PLGA-NH2 NPs, compared with PLGA NPs, were evaluated in Hep-2 cells (Fig. 5, Fig. 6). Instead of phloretin, DiI (a hydrophobic fluorescent dye) was incorporated into the NPs for detecting its fluorescent signals by flow cytometry and CLSM. Released amounts of DiI from PLGA NPs and PLGA-NH2 NPs in PBS (pH 7.4) after 24 h incubation were 15.6 +- 1.5% and 5.7 +- 0.8%, respectively. Considering those values, the detection of fluorescent dye can indicate the intracellular distribution of NPs as reported previously , . Cellular accumulation of PLGA NPs and PLGA-NH2 NPs was compared after 4 and 24 h incubation in Hep-2 cells (Fig. 5). The mean fluorescence intensity values, indicating the cellular accumulation efficiency, of PLGA-NH2 NPs group after 4 and 24 h incubation were 4.7 and 3.6-fold higher than those of PLGA NPs, respectively (p < 0.05). Also, the cellular accumulation of NPs increased after 24 h incubation compared with 4 h incubation. The fluorescence intensity values of PLGA NPs and PLGA-NH2 NPs after 24 h incubation were 97% and 52% higher than those of 4 h incubation, respectively. Intracellular distribution of DiI-loaded NPs was also assessed by CLSM imaging (Fig. 6). As shown in Fig. 6, the fluorescence signal of PLGA-NH2 NPs group was stronger than that of PLGA NPs group regardless of the incubation time. The fluorescence intensity of 24 h incubation group was higher than that of 4 h incubation group irrespective of formulation types. Although the fluorescent signal of PLGA NPs group was not higher than that of DiI solution group due to different uptake mechanism (endocytosis versus passive diffusion), PLGA-NH2 NPs group exhibited higher fluorescence intensity rather than the other groups. They also indicated improved cellular uptake of PLGA-NH2 NPs compared with PLGA NPs. Both 2D and 2.5D images implied the location of NPs in the cytosol and intracellular compartments rather than the surface of cells.
It was reported that size, shape, material, surface charge, and hydrophobicity of surface of NPs may influence on their cellular uptake . In particular, charge density, charge type, and the type of cells (phagocyte or nonphagocyte) can affect the cellular entry and intracellular localization of NPs . In nonphagocytic cells, NPs with cationic surface charge may have higher cellular uptake and greater cytotoxicity rather than anionic charged NPs . In this investigation, PLGA-NH2 NPs exhibited an actual negative zeta potential, but they can be also classified as a neutral charge, in spite of the modification of PLGA with amine group . It can prevent severe cytotoxicity (Fig. 4) and significantly enhance the cellular accumulation efficiency (Fig. 5, Fig. 6), as shown in the case of chitosan-modified PLGA nanospheres in previous report .
3.7. Tumor penetration
Tumor penetration and spheroid growth inhibition of developed NPs were assessed in Hep-2 spheroid model (Fig. 7). DiI-loaded PLGA NPs and PLGA-NH2 NPs were incubated in Hep-2 spheroid model and their distribution was monitored by CLSM. As shown in Fig. 7A, red fluorescence signal of PLGA-NH2 NPs group was stronger than that of PLGA NPs group. In accordance with the results of cellular uptake study (Fig. 5, Fig. 6), tumor penetration efficiency of PLGA-NH2 NPs was higher than that of PLGA NPs. The more positive zeta potential value (regarded as a neutral charge) of PLGA-NH2 NPs, rather than PLGA NPs, may contribute to the improved tumor penetration efficiency. Spheroid growth inhibitory effects of phloretin-loaded NPs were also evaluated in Hep-2 spheroid model (Fig. 7B). After 1 day incubation with drug solution, drug-loaded NPs, or blank NPs, the volume of spheroid was monitored for 4 days. As shown in Fig. 7B, the spheroid volume of PLGA-NH2/phloretin NPs group was lower than those of control, phloretin solution, PLGA/phloretin NPs, and blank PLGA-NH2 NPs (p < 0.05). PLGA-NH2/phloretin NPs group exhibited better growth inhibitory effect rather than PLGA/phloretin NPs group, mainly due to the interaction of amine group located in the surface of NPs with tumor cell based on the electrostatic interaction. Interestingly, considering the difference in spheroid volume between PLGA-NH2/phloretin NPs and blank PLGA-NH2 NPs, the improved growth inhibitory effect of PLGA-NH2/phloretin NPs seemed to be induced by the pharmacological efficacy of drug and NPs' functions, not by the toxicity of blank polymeric NPs.
After reaching the tumor site, NPs may encounter new barriers, such as abnormal and irregular vasculature, high interstitial fluid pressure, dense stroma (i.e., basement membrane, fibroblasts, immune cells, and extracellular matrix), and interaction with tumor associated macrophage, for deep penetration into the tumor core . Several properties of NPs, such as size, surface charge, shape, targeting ligand, and drug release pattern, are related to the tumor penetration efficiency . It is interestingly noted that NPs with neutral (from -10 mV to 10 mV) zeta potential value may move longer distance and distribute homogeneously in the tumor tissue. Highly anionic or cationic NPs can interact with the opposite charged cellular components, thus it can impede the efficient penetration of NPs into the tumor core region. Their interaction with serum proteins and subsequent accelerated blood clearance may also be drawbacks after intravenous administration. It is reported that neutral NPs may show the least interaction with reticuloendothelial system and prolonged circulation . As shown in Table 1, PLGA-NH2/phloretin NPs with around -6 mV zeta potential are expected to have improved cellular uptake efficiency and anticancer activity in spheroid model compared with unmodified PLGA NPs, without the disadvantages of positively charged NPs.

## Conclusion
PLGA-NH2 NPs, aiming at improved cellular uptake in cancer cells and penetration into the solid tumor, were developed for the delivery of phloretin. Nano-sized particles were prepared for the delivery of phloretin (as an anticancer drug) for cancer therapy in this study for the first time, to the best of our knowledge. It is hypothesized that amine group located in the outer surface of NPs may interact with the cellular membrane and their cellular uptake and tumor penetration can be augmented. PLGA-NH2 NPs with 237 nm mean diameter, narrow size distribution, and around -6 mV zeta potential were fabricated for the delivery of phloretin. Developed NPs maintained their initial particle size after incubation in biological fluid-simulated media (HSA solution, FBS solution, and PBS) and sustained drug release profiles were observed. Marked enhancement of cellular accumulation of PLGA-NH2 NPs, compared with PLGA NPs, was shown in Hep-2 cells. In Hep-2 spheroid model, stronger inhibitory effect for spheroid growth was detected in PLGA-NH2/phloretin NPs group. Without the introduction of intricate biofunctional moieties, simple strategy based on amine-functionalization of PLGA NPs achieved the enhancement of cellular uptake and antitumor efficacies compared with unmodified PLGA NPs. Considering all experimental findings, PLGA-NH2 NP can be a promising candidate of tumor targetable and penetrable nanocarriers for cancer therapy.
