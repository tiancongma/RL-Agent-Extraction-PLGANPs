2.5. Shape and morphology

The imaging of the shape and morphology of the obtained nanocarriers were studied by atomic force microscopy (AFM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM). The AFM imaging was provided by
VeecoNanoScope Dimension V instrument with an RT ESP Veeco tube
scanner. The scanning speed was 0.5 Hz and a low-resonance-frequency
pyramidal silicon cantilever resonating at 250-331 kHz was employed
(at a constant force of 20-80 N/m). The amplitude of the resonance was
set manually to the lowest possible amplitude for stable imaging within
the contamination layer present on the surface. Samples were prepared
by adsorbing a NCs droplet on a freshly cleaved mica surface for 18 h.
The excess of substrate was then removed by rinsing the mica plates in
double distilled water for 1 min and drying in 2 h at room temperature.
In the case of TEM, the imaging of the dry NCs was performed with an
FEI Tecnai G2 20 X-TWIN electron microscope. Few drops of the diluted
NCs were placed on Cu-Ni grid and left to dry in 20 h at room temperature. The NCs were stained with 2% uranyl acetate, and then the
image was captured.

## Results
3.1. Characteristics of PEG-ylated nanocarriers loaded by hybrid cargo

The challenge of co-encapsulation of both hydrophilic and hydrophobic compounds into any nanocarriers lies in the technique of providing
chemical and physical compatibility between the active molecules and
other nanocontainer components, i.e., polymers and surfactants while

protection and biocompatibility, three types of polyester-origin PEG-ylated
polymers were applied in this study (PEG-PLGA, PEG-PCL, PEG-PDLLA),
as bio-safe, biodegradable and pharmaceutical-oriented stealth compounds of the loaded nanocontainer shell . The appropriate concentrations of surfactants and polymers and the ratio of organic to aqueous
phase were selected according to previous studies  or were determined experimentally. Nine types of polymeric nanocarriers (systems
1-9 from Table 1) were thus successfully synthesized. Their mean diameter (DH), polydispersity index (PdI), zeta potential (z), encapsulation
efficiency of the model therapeutic compound (EEDNA) and diagnostic
agent (EETO), were assessed on the day of the theranostic NCs production
as well as 30 days after their storage in the room temperature. The mean
particle sizes, polydispersity and z-potential measurements of the obtained
nanocontainers stabilized by various surfactant and polymer types displayed an average size between 143 and 189, PdI of ca. 0.2-0.3 and nearly
neutral surface from -4 mV to -1 mV, corresponding well with data
reported for other polymeric carriers designed mainly for anticancer
therapies . Concerning the influence of polymer and surfactant
type on the nanocarrier size, we observe that the smallest nanocarriers
with narrow size distribution (PdI ~0.22) were obtained for those stabilized by PEG-PLGA and CrA25, while those stabilized by PEG-PCL
showed the broadest range of particle sizes and polydispersity (up to 189
and 0.32 respectively for system 6) regardless of the surfactant type, which
can be caused by longer solubility of that polymer in organic phase during
the emulsification process. However, regarding the surfactant influence on
the NCs DH and PdI, it should be notice that the all nanosystems stabilized
by P407 revealed lowest quality for possible biological application, i.e., the
less unimodal size distribution resulting in PdI from 0.28 (for system 3) to
0.32 (for system 6). Furthermore, those NCs showed also low encapsulation efficiency of the active cargo between 72-80%, which is quite low
comparing with the EEs of the nanosystems stabilized by CrA25 (80-95%).

in the presented studies, water-in-oil-in-water (w/o/w) double (multiple)
emulsion evaporation procedure was successfully designed to obtain
polyester-origin and PEG-ylated (stealth) nanocarriers to co-encapsulate a
model theranostic cargo, i.e., hydrophilic deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA)
and hydrophobic Thiazole Orange (TO) dye in the nanocontainer double

According to the general strategy presented in Scheme 1, the w/o/w
double emulsion evaporation approach involves a three-step process. In
the first step, aqueous internal phase containing DNA is emulsified into the
continuous organic phase being a combination of hydrophobic surfactant
(S1), polymer and dye solution forming water-in-oil (w/o) nanoemulsion.
Next, the primary submicron emulsion is poured into hydrophilic surfactant (S2) aqueous solution and immersed in an ice water bath to create the
water-in-oil-in-water (w/o/w) emulsion. Then, the organic solvent evaporates through the emulsion/air interface to make the loaded NCs solidify
and hardening. Consequently, for the first phase of w/o/w process we

increases the adsorption density of hydrophobic alkyl chains on the particle surface providing its enhanced hydrophobicity. Thus, their adsorption
ultimately induced the emulsion phase inversion from O/W emulsion to
W/O emulsion . Furthermore, di-C12DMAB may interact with deoxyribonucleic acid and form stable DNA-surfactant complexes leading to

in mm) corresponding to different levels of the colloidal sample in the
measurement vial is marked on the abscissa axis. By analysing the distances
between the curves in BS profiles of the all nanosystems from Table 1 at 0
day (freshly prepared) and after 30 days of their storage in room temperature (Fig. 3), it is possible to determine the dynamics of the processes
