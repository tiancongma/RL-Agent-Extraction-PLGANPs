## Abstract
Alzheimer's disease remains an unsolved neurological puzzle with no cure. Current therapies offer only symptomatic relief,
hindered by limited uptake through the blood-brain barrier. Auranofin, an FDA-approved compound, exhibits potent antioxidative and anti-inflammatory properties targeting brain disorders. Yet, its oral bioavailability challenge prompts the
exploration of nanoformulation-based solutions enhancing blood-brain barrier penetrability. The study aimed to investigate
the neuroprotective potential of auranofin nanoparticles in streptozotocin-induced AD rats. Auranofin-containing polylacticco-glycolic acid nanoparticles were formulated by the multiple emulsion solvent evaporation method. Characterization was
done by determining entrapment efficiency, particle size distribution, surface charge, and morphology. An in vivo study was
performed by administering streptozotocin (3 mg/kg/i.c.v., days 1 and 3), auranofin (5 and 10 mg/kg), auranofin nanoparticles
(2.5 and 5 mg/kg), and donepezil (2 mg/kg) for 14 days orally. Behavioral deficits were evaluated using the open field test,
Morris water maze, objective recognition test, change in oxidative stress levels, and AD markers in the brain. Following the
decapitation of the rats, the brains were excised to isolate the hippocampus. Subsequent analyses included the quantification
of biochemical and neuroinflammatory markers, as well as the assessment of neurotransmitter levels. The characterization of
auranofin nanoparticles showed an entrapment efficiency of 98%, an average particle size of 101.5 +- 10.3 nm, a surface charge
of 27.5 +- 5.10 mV, and a polydispersity index of 0.438 +- 0.12. In vivo, administration of auranofin and auranofin nanoparticles significantly reversed streptozotocin-induced cognitive deficits, biochemical alteration, neuroinflammatory markers,
and neurotransmitter levels. The present finding suggests that auranofin nanoparticles have more significant neuroprotective
potential than auranofin alone. The therapeutic efficacy may be attributed to its antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties, as well as its positive neuromodulatory effects. Therefore, our findings suggest that it could be a promising candidate
for Alzheimer's disease therapy.

Unfortunately, FDA-approved therapies such as cholinesterase inhibitors, and N-methyl-D-aspartate receptor antagonists have proved their efficacy in reducing the symptoms of
AD but none of them can able to halt the disease progression
and stop the brain cell death associated with AD (Cummings

Auranofin (AF) is a sulfur-containing compound
chemically named as 2,3,4,6-tetra-O-acetyl-l-thio-b-Dglucopyrano-sato-S-(triethyl-phosphine). Corroborating
this, a study has explained that gold compounds, specifically auranofin, previously approved for the treatment of
rheumatoid arthritis, demonstrate significant potential as
agents with prospective anticancer, antiprotozoal, antiviral,

antifungal, and antibacterial properties (Bolan et al. 2012;
Elkashif and Seleem 2020; Fifer et al. 2016; Schaberle and
Hack 2014; Thangamani et al. 2017).
Furthermore, a study has reported that AF elicits positive
effects by activating antioxidant enzymes and anti-inflammatory mechanisms. These actions contribute to preventing
changes in neurotransmitter levels, enhancing mitochondrial
activity, and reducing protein accumulation in neurons (Wall
et al. 2021). Notably, in previous studies, AF has undergone
in vitro and in vivo investigations to elucidate its neuroprotective properties. These investigations specifically targeted the attenuation of glia-mediated neuroinflammation
, the inhibition of cytotoxic secretion
by human astrocytes, along with the consequent neuroprotective effects , and the mitigation
of amyloid-b plaque pathology in a transgenic AD mouse
model at a younger age .
But, a limitation associated with AF is that only a small
amount reaches the brain due to its poor absorption or limited passage across the blood-brain barrier (BBB) (Glennas et al. 1997; Kim et al. 2010; Madeira et al. 2013; Walz
et al. 1983). AF accumulates progressively in various tissues
throughout the body, with the highest gold (Au) concentrations detected in the kidneys, spleen, and lungs. Previous
research conducted by Walz et al.  demonstrated that
AF exhibits limited permeability across the blood-brain barrier (BBB). Moreover, accumulating evidence indicates that
auranofin penetrates the central nervous system (CNS) at
low micromolar concentrations (0.2-5 mmol/L). This suggests that sufficient quantities of auranofin can cross the
blood-brain barrier, thereby exerting in vivo protective
effects analogous to those observed in vitro .
Thus, nanoparticles (NPs) present a promising strategy
due to their facile formulation, predictable bio-behavior,
and brain-targeting potential at smaller sizes. They necessitate a reduced payload for achieving therapeutic drug levels
(Dadwal et al. 2018; Sharma, and Baldi 2018; Kaur et al.
2018), exhibit sustained/controlled release profiles (Bajwa
et al. 2021a, b), lower dose-dependent toxicity, and enhance
drug absorption (Kushawaha et al. 2024a), and bioavailability (Dewangan et al. 2023; Bajwa et al. 2022) owing to their
diminished particle size . In the preceding decade, there has been a focused exploration into nanoparticles, characterized by dimensions of about 100 nm and
possessing distinctive physio-chemical attributes (Dewangan
et al. 2023). This investigation has primarily centered on
their emergence as novel therapeutic modalities aimed at
addressing AD .

Preparation of nanoparticles

The water-in-oil-in-water (W/O/W) double emulsion solvent
evaporation technique was used to develop AF-loaded PLGA
NPs (Kushawaha et al. 2024a). AF (40 mg) was diluted with
2 mL of PBS, whereas PLGA (200 mg) was solubilized in
5 mL of dichloromethane. The organic polymer phase was
combined with an aqueous suspension of AF using ultrasonication at 20 amplitudes for 20 s at cold temperature to produce the primary emulsion (W/O). To 30 mL of polyvinyl
alcohol (0.5% w/v), the primary emulsion was added dropby-drop. The mixture was then agitated at 300 rpm for a further 6 h at a given temp. The NPs produced were separately
obtained by ultracentrifugation at 20,000 rpm for 20 min at
4 degC. The obtained NPs were rinsed using double-distilled
water twice and passed from a filter (0.22 um) to remove
extra PVA. Then, the particles were freeze-dried and stored
at -20 degC till further use. The formulation was optimized

expert software (Design Expert, USA), and tested further for

Morris Water Maze Spatial memory and learning capabilities
were assessed using the MWM test, as per the previously

Any Maze software. The acquisition trials spanned four consecutive days (25th to 28th), with a probe test conducted on
the 29th day. The MWM apparatus utilized was a circular
tank measuring (150 cm in diameter and 60 cm in height)
filled with water (25 +- 1 degC) to a depth of 30 cm. Rats underwent a 5-day training period, each session comprising five
trials with a maximum length of 120 s and an approximate
trial interval of 30 s. The primary measure assessed was
escape latency (in seconds), indicating the time taken by
rats to locate the concealed platform. In instances where
an animal failed to find the hidden platform within 120 s,
manual placement on the platform for 30 s ensued. This
standardized 1-day training pattern was consistent for all
rats, ensuring uniform evaluation of their learning abilities.
On the 29th day, corresponding to 24 h post the acquisition
phase, a probe test was executed by removing the platform
from the pool. Rats were permitted to navigate freely in the
water for a duration of 60 s, and the duration spent in the
target quadrant, where the concealed platform was formerly
located, was documented. The time allocated to the target
quadrant served as an indicator of the extent of memory
consolidation subsequent to the acquisition trial (Kushawaha
et al. 2024a).

A total of seven groups were created from all the animals
(n = 6). The whole protocol consists of 30 days. The first
group was treated with 5% DMSO solution which serves as
a sham and the remaining six groups were given ICV-STZ
(3 mg/kg, on days 1 and 3) followed by a different dose
of AF, AF NPs, and donepezil from the 15th day of STZ
administration to 29th days (Fig. 1, Table 1). Then, behavioral parameters were performed at the end of the protocol
by using a photoactometer, Morris Water Maze (MWM),
and Object Recognition Test (ORT). On the 30th day, rats
were sacrificed with an overdose of ketamine hydrochloride and xylazine respectively. Brains were isolated, then
the hippocampus was collected and homogenized for biochemical, inflammatory, neurochemical, and histopathological analysis.

Object Recognition Test The novel object recognition test
was implemented following the previously outlined methodology (Jaiswal and Kumar 2022) with specific adaptations.
To conduct the ORT, a wooden open box apparatus measuring 60 x 40 x 60 cm was utilized. The objects employed
in this study were made up of painted wood blocks with
two distinct shapes (triangle and square) and measured
10 cm x 5 cm x 10 cm. The day before testing (day 22),
rats were accustomed to the test box for 15 min per day for
two consecutive days in the absence of any objects. Following this, the rats were returned to their home cage. On
day 23, 24 h subsequent, the initial 3-min sample trial (T1)
was initiated. Two objects, denoted as sample objects FO1
and FO2, characterized by akin color and shape, were positioned at divergent corners within the enclosure. Following
the culmination of T1, the rats were reintroduced to their
respective home cages, and a time gap of 60 min was introduced as an intertrial interval prior to the commencement
of T2. In the course of the subsequent 3-min choice trial
(T2), one of the objects that had been introduced during
the initial T1 trial was replaced with a novel object (NO).
In order to mitigate potential biases arising from location
or object preferences, a systematic approach was employed
where all feasible permutations and spatial arrangements
of the objects were equitably utilized. Rigorous cleaning
procedures were implemented for both the apparatus and
objects after every trial to preclude the influence of olfactory cues. Exploratory behavior was characterized by the
act of orienting the nose toward the objects at a proximity
of 2 cm or less, and/or making physical contact between the
nose and the objects. During both T1 and T2, the durations
that the rats spent exploring the two objects were measured
and recorded individually. A series of variables were subsequently computed, encompassing the cumulative duration
of exploration exhibited toward the two indistinguishable
objects during the initial trial (T1). The rats' capacity to
differentiate between the familiar and novel object during
the subsequent trial (T2) was ascertained by contrasting the
duration of exploration directed at the familiar object with
that directed at the novel object.

Characterization of optimized AF NPs

The particle size, zeta potential, morphology, SEM, and
AFM of the AF-loaded PLGA NPs were evaluated in the
optimized formulation. The percentage yield of the AF
NPs was found to be 59.85 +- 11.62% and the entrapment
efficiency was found to be 98 +- 1.2%. The hydrodynamic
diameter of AF NPs was observed to be 101.5 +- 10.3 nm
and the low polydispersity index (PDI) indicated a monomodal size distribution. Additionally, the zeta potential of
the NPs was observed to be 27.5 +- 5.10 mV with a low PDI
of 0.438 +- 0.12, indicating uniform particle size distribution
of optimized NP (Fig. 3a, b). The scanning electron micrograph of AF PLGA NPs confirmed the particle size range
and non-fused spherical shape with its smooth surface. The
nanoparticles displayed a spherical shape, sub-micrometric
size, and no signs of aggregation. An approximately identical size range of NPs was also observed by analysis using
AFM (Fig. 4a, b), even though the addition of gold compound did not affect the shape of the NPs.

Histopathological analysis by hematoxylin and eosin
staining

Immediately following the last behavioral test, rats were
decapitated and slaughtered for histopathological analysis. The extracted brains were placed in a (10% v/v) formalin solution. Tissues were fixed in paraffin blocks and
using a microtome, divided into 3 mm slices. By the use of
hematoxylin and eosin, (5-10 um) slices of the brain were
stained and photographed using a binocular microscope. To
ensure meaningful comparisons of cognition and memory
using histology images of the hippocampus, it is crucial that
the images are taken from a similar region of interest. This
consistency allows for accurate assessment and comparison
of morphological or pathological changes. By maintaining a standardized approach in selecting and imaging these
regions, researchers can reliably compare the histological
features that correlate with cognitive and memory functions.

In the current study, the formulation of AF NPs was prepared and various characterization parameters like entrapment efficiency, polydispersity index, particle size, and zeta
potential were found to be appropriate for accessing the
pharmacological evaluation. Notably, the favorable small
particle size (101.5 nm) facilitated the effective traversal of
the BBB, as supported by previous studies (Chenthamara
et al. 2019; Garg et al. 2023). Additionally, the suitable zeta
potential indicated sustained stability of the nanoformulation, and the circular morphology of AF NPs was observed
to enhance a surface area, potentially optimizing drug
transport across the blood-brain barrier (Kushawaha et al.
2024a). This innovative approach shows promise in improving therapeutic efficacy and demonstrating neuroprotective
properties of AF in AD.
Drug-loaded NPs, when administered orally, undergo
complex biodistribution, forming a drug depot that sustains
slow release, as evidenced by preclinical and clinical studies. This mechanism aims for targeted delivery to the brain,
preventing burst release and mitigating dose-dependent toxicity in target organs. The positive charge on NPs is expected
to interact with caveolae-mediated processes in endothelial
cells at BBB, influencing targeted drug entry and eliciting the desired pharmacological action (Kushawaha et al.
2024a).
In line with the preliminary study, the STZ-induced rats
model exhibited a significant decrease in beam cross time,
an increase in escape latency, a decreased time spent in the
target quadrant, and changes in the ORT (Gong et al. 2020;
Nakaya et al. 2011). Similarly, administration of AF and AF
NPs significantly reversed the locomotor activity, memory,
and learning test, and change in object location task against
STZ-induced AD models. The impact of AF NPs surpassed
that of AF alone was also found significant.

## Discussion
The study presents a novel therapeutic strategy for Alzheimer's disease involving AF NPs tailored for targeted
brain delivery. This approach aims to overcome the challenge of efficiently transporting therapeutic drugs across the
blood-brain barrier, which is crucial for effective Alzheimer's therapy. The study's findings suggest that these AFloaded NPs demonstrated neuroprotective properties against
AD induced by ICV-STZ infusion.
ICV injection of STZ recapitulates similar pathological
changes as AD; it gradually decreases cognitive abilities,
increased neuroinflammatory mediators, elevated oxidative stress, and causes neurochemical alterations (Ahn et al.
2020; Ravelli et al. 2017). STZ-induced Alzheimer's in rats
was confirmed by using behavioral instruments like an actophotometer, MWM, and ORT, along with these biochemical, neurochemical, and inflammatory markers were also
assessed.
The growing research suggests that AF has neuroprotective and anti-neuroinflammatory properties at low micromolar doses AF prevented neurotoxicity caused by human
microglia and astrocytes (Madeira et al. 2013; Madeira et al.
2014). In contrast, AF has been demonstrated to inhibit the
microglial secretion of nitric oxide and reduce the expression of the Cox-2 inflammatory enzyme and the proinflammatory cytokines TNF-a and IL-6 .
Similarly, the literature shows that the density of Ab in the
CA1 hippocampal area in 14-month-old male APPNL-G-F/
NL-G-F AD mice was considerably reduced by AF (Upite
et al. 2020). These properties of AF indicated its ability to
modulate the cognitive deficit effect of STZ-induced AD.
Notably, PLGA NPs have been reported to improve drug
penetration across the BBB both in vitro and in vivo for
donepezil, galantamine, and memantine .
Moreover, the administration of PLGA has demonstrated
potential in attenuating Alzheimer's disease pathology in
animal models and safeguarding cultured mouse cortical
neurons against Ab induced lethality . Indeed, AF NPs could show a better effect against
STZ-induced AD due to its change in the physicochemical
properties of a drug such as lipophilicity and bioavailability,
and showed no signs of toxicity.
Recent reports by authors demonstrated that aurothioglucose nanoparticles have neuroprotective properties against
AD induced by ICV-STZ due to their appropriate targeting
and efficient transport of therapeutic drugs across the BBB
(Kushawaha et al. 2024a).

Previous studies demonstrated that ICV injection STZ
causes neuronal injury by increasing proinflammatory
cytokines including IL-6,and TNF-a in rat brains (Abutaleb
and Seleem 2020; Ganz et al. 2018; Kinney et al. 2018). In the
current investigation, the ICV-STZ-administered rats had considerably higher TNF-a and IL-6 levels. However, treatment
with AF and AF NPs showed a significant decrease in the level
of TNF-a and IL-6 in the rat's hippocampus. Significantly, the
impact of AF NPs surpassed that of AF alone. Consequently,
it suggests a possible anti-inflammatory property, which may
act by decreasing cytokine levels in the rat brain.
The modulation of hippocampal activity is acknowledged
to be influenced by various neuromodulators and neurotransmitters. Previous studies have illustrated the high enrichment
of cholinergic and monoaminergic axon terminals in the hippocampus (Rimpi & Rahul 2020; Wang et al. 2019). These
neurotransmitters are recognized for their pivotal roles in the
encoding, storage, and expression of memory (Cui et al. 2018;
Wang et al. 2018). The current study demonstrated that ICVSTZ-infused rats had significant elevation in AChE activity
and significantly reduced monoamine levels including NE,
DA, and 5-HT, while treatment with AF and AF NPs demonstrated a considerable recovery of AChE and NE, DA, and
5-HT3 levels in hippocampus rats, indicating a positive neuromodulator effect. Significantly, the impact of AF-loaded
nanoparticles surpassed that of AF alone.
