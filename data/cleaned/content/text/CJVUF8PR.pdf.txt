A B S T R A C T
Ropivacaine loaded microspheres offer an attractive alternative for prolonging the duration of local anesthetics for
the pain management. However, traditional microcapsulation methods could not provide microspheres with desired
drug loading efficiency and a narrow size distribution, leading to poor patient compliance as well as preparation
repeatability. In this study, we proposed to combine O/W emulsion method with novel premix membrane emulsi-
fication technique. After the optimization of fabrication procedure, ropivacaine loaded microspheres with narrow
size distribution (span 0.469), high drug loading efficiency (49%) and an ideal constant release behavior have been
obtained. Especially, it was found that solidification process affected the internal structure of microspheres and the
physical state of encapsulated ropivacaine. Furthermore, it was interested that lower polymer molecular weight
resulted in higher encapsulation efficiency, which was not consistent with reported results. It was found that this was
ascribed to the interaction of PLGA-ropivacaine. All these aspects had considerable influences on the characteristics
of microspheres. In addition, the cytotoxicity on different cell lines (SHSY5Y and HaCaT cells) was detected quali-
tatively and quantitatively. It showed that ropivacaine loaded microspheres did reduce the Reactive oxygen species
(ROS) and cytotoxicity prominently compared to free ropivacaine, which could be attributed to the sustained and
steady drug release behavior. This study provided some pioneering ideas for encapsulating small molecule drug with
poor solubility. The optimized microspheres could be chosen as an ideal candidate for the ropivacaine sustained
release with its better drug adherence, lower toxicity of drug burst release and best therapeutic effect.

1. Introduction
Despite numerous advances in basic and clinical investigation of
new therapeutic agents, the management of post-surgical pain in the
healing period remains to be a major issue [1]. In recent years, Local
Anaesthetics (LAs), administered via peripheral or central routes, are
widely acceptable to postoperative analgesia, obstetrics and the therapy
of chronic pain [2]. Among these drugs, ropivacaine (RVC), a novel
enantiomeric isomers amide local anesthetic, plays a significant role in
the regional control of major pain and has been gradually promoted as a
priority option in clinic on account of its rapid onset and relative higher
length of action effect [3]. Nevertheless, postoperative pain generally
causes persistent pain for several days, a single injection of ropivacaine
just provides a nerve block lasting only 3–4 h, making it difficult to
achieve desirable analgesia effect [4].
Nowadays, to meet the long-time analgesia requirement, multiple
methods such as catheter implantation in vivo or patient controlled
analgesia (PCA) device have been applied [5,6]. Although these ap-
proaches have made progress in clinical, their wide-spread use has been
hampered by the limited efficacy, potential adverse clinical complica-
tions, poor patient compliance and the lack of portability. In particular,
catheter infusion may cause infection and migration, requiring super-
numerary constant monitoring which absolutely increases the patient
cost. In addition, the frequent administration of PCA carried risk of the
excessive absorption of RVC, leading to cardiovascular and central
nervous system (CNS) toxicity [7].
For purpose of increasing the bioavailability of RVC, with respect to
its effectiveness and safety, alternative controlled release systems have
been paid considerable attention [8–10]. Liposomes, micro-nano par-
ticles, hydrogel and other biodegradable polymer which can release
drugs in a sustained manner and reduce adverse effects with low sys-
temic uptake have been used in drug delivery system. Among them,
poly (lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) microsphere has attracted much
interest due to its stable physicochemical property [11]. Current reports
have indicated that RVC loaded microspheres were able to prolong
pharmacokinetic parameters and pharmacodynamic activity [12–15].
Nevertheless, there are still several issues restricting the application of
ropivacaine microspheres in clinical. First, ropivacaine loaded micro-
spheres prepared by conventional methods have broad size distribution,
which might lead to poor repeatability of preparation, release behavior
and drug loading efficiency [15,16]. Furthermore, RVC easily escaped
to the water phase in the microsphere preparation process on account of
its small molecule weight character, which led to quite a low drug
loading efficiency [13,17,18]. Therefore, it is required to increase the
injection dose of microspheres, which will make patient discomfort.
Thirdly, it is well known that exposure to excessive concentration of
ropivacaine will cause neurotoxicity [19,20]. Hence, high initial burst
effect or inappropriate release profile of ropivacaine loaded micro-
spheres may lead to transient high ropivacaine concentrations, which is
suspected of causing neurotoxicity [21,22].
In this study, to eliminate the drawback of the board particle size on
the repeatability of preparation, premix membrane emulsification
technique was developed for the formulation of ropivacaine loaded
microspheres with narrow size distribution (Fig. S1). Furthermore, in
order to obtain high drug loading efficiency and ideal release profile,
we successfully designed a novel microsphere in which ropivacaine and
PLGA form the microsphere skeleton together. Meanwhile, formulation
process parameters which are crucial to encapsulate small molecules
were investigated in detail. In addition, the effects of ropivacaine
loaded microspheres on SHSY5Y and HaCaT cell vitality and apoptosis
at different concentrations and times were examined.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Materials
PLGA with number-average molecular weights (Mn) of 12000,
25000, 45,000 with corresponding Mw/Mn value of 1.70, 1.56 and 1.58
were purchased from Dai Gang Company (Shandong, China). The Mn
and Mw/Mn value were obtained with gel permeation chromatography
(GPC, Waters, USA) using polystyrene as a standard. Ropivacaine base
was provided by Humanwell Pharmaceutical Co., Ltd. (Hubei, China).
Poly (vinyl alcohol) (PVA-217, degree of polymerization 1700, degree
of hydrolysis 88.5%) was provide by Kuraray (Japan). Fast Membrane
Emulsifier (FM0210/500 M) and micro-porous membrane were pro-
vided by Senhui Microsphere Tech (Suzhou) Co., Ltd. Acetonitrile
(HPLC grade) was purchased from Dikma Co, Ltd. (USA). Sodium di-
hydrogen phosphate and sodium hydrogen phosphate were purchased
from Xi Long Company (Guangdong, China). All other reagents were
analytical grade. Human neuroblastoma cells (SHSY5Y) and Human
immortalized keratinocytes (HaCaT) cells were cultured in DMEM
medium (Gibco, USA) containing 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and
100 U/ml penicillin. All the cells were maintained in a humidified 5%
CO2 incubator at 37 °C.
2.2. Preparation of microspheres
Uniform-sized microspheres loaded with ropivacaine were prepared
by premix membrane emulsification technique combined with oil-in-
water (O/W) emulsion method. First, PLGA (500 mg) and ropivacaine
(500 mg) was dissolved in 10 mL dichloromethane (DCM) as oil phase
(O). The oil phase (O) was subsequently emulsified into 100 mL 1%
PVA solution (W) by homogenizer at 6000 rpm to form coarse emul-
sions of O/W. Then, the coarse emulsions were further refined by ex-
truding the emulsion through the membrane (pore size of the mem-
brane was 35 μm) under proper pressure to obtain emulsion droplets.
The obtained uniform emulsions were solidified at room temperature at
300 rpm for 5 h (magnetic stirring) or under vacuum pressure by rotary
evaporator for 6 min. Finally, the obtained microspheres were collected
by centrifugation at 6000 rpm for 6 min, washed with distilled water for
5 times and then freeze dried.

The microspheres were sputter-coated with gold in 30 mA for 120 s
(JFC-1600, JEOL, Japan), then observed by SEM. The volume mean
diameter of microspheres dispersed in distilled water was measured by

2.5. In vitro release study release study
The 10 mg ropivacaine loaded microspheres were incubated in 1 mL
of phosphate buffer saline (PBS) containing 0.1% Tween 80 and 0.1%
Tween 20 medium (pH 7.4) under agitation at 37 ℃. Supernatants were
collected at predetermined intervals by centrifugation and replaced
with fresh buffer of equal volume. The concentration of ropivacaine in
the supernatant was determined by HPLC. All the release experiments
were executed in triplicate.
2.6. X-ray powder diffraction (XRD)
In order to identify the quality of crystals existed in the micro-
spheres as well as to detect the physical state of ropivacaine and PLGA,
the powder XRD patterns of the residues were collected using a Rigaku
Smartlab diffractometer fitted with a Cu tube and operated at 45Kv and
200 mA. XRD data were collected from 5° to 90° (2θ) in 0.02° steps at
9°/min speed.
2.7. Measurement of precipitation rate of ropivacaine and PLGA
In order to analyze the influence of solidification method on the
ropivacaine distribution and state in microspheres, the precipitation
rate of PLGA and ropivacaine were investigated during evaporation of
DCM by transmittance measurement. Certain amounts of ropivacaine
and PLGA according to the theoretical drug loading efficiency were
separately dissolved into DCM, then the solutions were transferred into
cuvettes. The transmittance measurement was carried out at room
temperature using a UV/visible spectrophotometer (Ultrospec 2100
pro, GE Healthcare Life Sciences, America) at 550 nm. After every
measurement, each cuvette was put inside the laboratory hood for the
evaporation of solvent. The real-time date of the transmittance was
recorded per 6 min until the reading date did not change.

3. Results and discussion
3.1. Preparation of ropivacaine loaded microspheres with a narrow size
distribution
Premix membrane emulsification technique combined with double
emulsion (W1/O/W2) proved to be an excellent method for preparing
uniform microsphere, especially for the microencapsulation of bioma-
cromolecule such as Exenatide and Human growth hormone [23,24].
However, in this study, ropivacaine and PLGA, as microsphere matrix,
were co-dissolved into oil phase. It would affect the character of dis-
persion phase which may probably further influence the premix mem-
brane emulsification process. Therefore, the process parameters for the
membrane emulsification were investigated to obtain ropivacaine
loaded microspheres with narrow size distribution.
3.1.1. Effect of the tran-membrane pressure (TMP)
TMP plays a significant role in the premix membrane emulsification
technique [25]. The O/W emulsion was pressed through the micro-
porous membrane under different TMP, and the relationship between
the TMP and the size distribution of the microspheres for the membrane
with pore size 35 μm was investigated. As shown in Fig. S2-A, the
average particle size of microspheres got smaller with increasing the
pressure from 10 to 50 kPa. High TMP led to a high shear stress, which
broke primary emulsions into smaller size droplets (Fig. 1-C). On the
contrary, when TMP decreased, large size microspheres appeared since
droplets could pass through the membrane pores slowly. (Fig. 1-A).
When the membrane pressure was adjusted to 30 kPa, the most uniform
microspheres were obtained with a span value of 0.572 (Fig. 1-B and
Fig S2-A).
3.1.2. Effect of the tran-membrane cycle
It has been shown that there is a positive correlation between tran-
membrane cycle time and uniformity of microspheres [26]. The larger
droplets would break into smaller ones by every passing cycle until the
size of droplet small enough to pass though membrane freely. As shown
in Fig. S2-B, 2 or 3 times was supposed to be the proper tran-membrane
cycle times to obtain ideal microspheres. Finally, uniform-size PLGA
microspheres with a span value of 0.491 were prepared through 2 times
of tran-membrane cycle (Fig. 1-E).
3.2. The effect of process parameters on drug loading efficiency and in vitro
release
For the encapsulation of small molecule drug into microspheres,
achieving high drug loading efficiency remains to be a major challenge
due to the escape of drug from the dispersion phase into the continuous
phase during the whole preparation process [27–29]. Therefore, in this
study, the effects of solidification method, PLGA molecular weight, and
pH of external water phase on drug loading efficiency and in vitro re-
lease profile have been investigated.
3.2.1. Effect of solidification method
To investigate the effect of different solidification methods, solvent
under the ambient pressure evaporation (ASE) and the solvent under
vacuum negative pressure evaporation (NSE) were compared. Firstly,
ropivacaine loaded PLGA droplets with uniform size were successfully
prepared by premix membrane emulsification technique. Subsequently,
the emulsion was solidified to form microspheres under ASE and under
NSE respectively. As shown in Table 1 (Formulations 1 and 2), solidi-
fication method has a significant impact on drug loading efficiency.
Higher drug loading efficiency (37.28%) was achieved by the NSE
method while relative lower loading efficiency (28.19%) was obtained
by the ASE method. The cumulative in vitro release profiles of ASE
microspheres and NSE microspheres were completely different as
shown in Fig. 2A. The ASE microspheres exhibited a relative high initial
burst release (28.3% within 2 h), then release rate gradually slowed
down and cumulative release reached 81.8% at the 7 days. As for NSE
microspheres, constant release profiles with low initial burst release
(5.6% within 2 h) was observed, and 96.1% was completely released
within 7 days.
According to the SEM images of microspheres, smooth external
surfaces with few pores were observed at both ASE and NSE micro-
spheres (Fig. 3A1 and A3). Nevertheless, the cross-sectional view of the
SEM images showed that microspheres prepared by two kinds of soli-
dification method had different internal structures as shown in Fig. 3A2
and A4. It is extremely different that the ASE microspheres (Fig. 3A2)
exhibited honeycomb structure with macroscopic crystals embedded in
the internal channels, whereas NSE microspheres (Fig. 3A4) showed
compact inner structure without pore and crystal. Besides, the X-ray
powder diffraction scans of ASE and NSE microspheres further de-
monstrated the phenomenon as shown in Fig. 3B. The ASE micro-
spheres exhibited diffraction peak at 2θ = 10° (Fig. 3B- (c)) which were
consistent with those observed for RVC powder (Fig. 3B-(a)). The re-
sults indicated that the RVC in the ASE microspheres was the crystal
state. The NSE microspheres did not exhibit peak correlated with
crystalloid RVC (Fig. 3B- (d)), suggesting that RVC was present in
amorphous state in the microspheres. Additional, the crystal state of
ropivacaine in ASE microsphere has also been testified by Differential
Scanning Calorimeter (DSC) test (Fig.S3). In consequence, different

3.2.2. Effect of polymer molecular weight
As shown in Table 1, formulation 2, 3 and 4 presented that the
higher LE% was obtained by using PLGA with lower molecular weight.
In addition, the LE% decreased along with the increase of molecular
weight. This phenomenon was not consistent with previous study, in
which polymer molecular weight exhibited positive correlation with the
organic phase’s viscosity that subsequently influenced the drug escape
rate in the preparation process [27,30].
In this study, we proposed that the presence of carboxylic acid
groups (COOH) of PLGA terminal groups and the amine groups of ro-
pivacaine molecule strengthened the electrostatic interaction [31].
(Fig. 4). Additional, ropivacaine and PLGA were both hydrophobic. The
methyl of PLGA and the methyl of ropivacaine were easy to attract
mutually due to the hydrophobic interaction [32,33]. The interaction of
ropivacaine-PLGA comprising of electrostatic interaction and hydro-
phobic interaction hindered the small molecule drug to escape of the
droplets during the preparation process. For low molecular weight of
PLGA, the amount of carboxylic acid terminal group of PLGA increased.
The electrostatic interaction became stronger because that more amine
groups of ropivacaine had the ability to interact with more carboxylic
acid terminal group of PLGA. Hence, the drug loading efficiency in-
creased. The in vitro release profiles of the three formations are shown
in Fig. 2B. Microspheres prepared by the PLGA of low molecular weight
had the fastest release rate. The results suggested that there were two
opposite factors contributed to the ropivacaine release from micro-
spheres. The degradation rate was faster for the lower molecular PLGA,
undoubtedly corresponding to a fast release. However, the strength of
ropivacaine-PLGA interactions was greater for low molecular PLGA,
yielding slow release. The overall release profile could be reconciled by
considering the balance between the rate of polymer degradation and
the strength of interaction which is specific to the number and type of
drug-PLGA combinations. In this study, the release profile indicated
that the faster degradation of low molecular PLGA clearly overwhelmed
the interactions of ropivacaine-PLGA, causing an overall relative fast
release for low molecular PLGA.
3.2.3. Effect of pH of external water phase

small molecule drug to the external water phase [34]. In this study, for
ropivacaine, an ionizable drug with a pH-dependent water solubility, it
was reasonable to adjust the pH of external water phase to a value of
low drug solubility. As shown in Table 1 (Formulations 3,5 and 6), once
the pH of external water phase was raised from 5.2 to 9.0, the LE%
increased from 42.13% to 49.06%. It was because that alkaline en-
vironment reduced the solubility of ropivacaine base in the external
water phase, further reducing the escape of ropivacaine. Nevertheless,
alkaline pH of the external aqueous phase would cause hydrolysis of

3.3. Cytotoxicity of ropivacaine loaded microspheres
Several studies have reported the toxicity of ropivacaine to neuro-
cyte and epidermal cells in recent years [7,35–37]. Therefore, another
purpose of developing ropivacaine loaded microspheres is to avoid
toxicity and side effects of excessive doses. In this study, SHSY5Y and
HaCaT cells were exposed to blank microspheres group (Blank Micro-
spheres), free ropivacaine group (Free Ropivacaine) and ropivacaine
loaded microspheres group (Ropivacaine-Microspheres), respectively.
As showed in Fig. 5, no obvious toxicity was observed for Blank Mi-
crospheres group, which proved that PLGA possess excellent safety as
biodegradable copolymer [38]. In addition, the results in Fig. 5 shows
that Free Ropivacaine remarkably inhibited cell proliferation on
SHSY5Y and HaCaT in time-dependent and dose-dependent manner.
Fortunately, as shown in Fig. 5A–C, Ropivacaine-Microspheres group
had higher percentage of cell viability rates with 71.32% and 56.59%
(1 mM) after 3 days and 5 days incubation, which were approximately
2- and 11-fold compared to Free Ropivacaine group (32.45% and
4.89%), respectively. The similar trend was also observed on HaCaT
cells (Fig. 5D–F), which illustrated that microsphere as a sustain-release
drug carrier possess an excellent performance on reducing cytotoxicity.
3.4. Cell apoptosis
Many studies have shown that intracellular ROS burst is one of the
main factors leading to acute damage in nerve cells [39], because the
