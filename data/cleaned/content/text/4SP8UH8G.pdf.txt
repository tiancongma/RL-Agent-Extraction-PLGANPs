Abstract
In this study, we designed nanoparticles (NPs) based on polylactic acid
glycolic acid modified with chitosan and folic acid to optimize the anti-
cancer, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant effects of arctiin (ARC), and we
measured its effects on cancer cells, including colon cancer. NPs were syn-
thesized using the W1/O/W2 double-emulsion solvent evaporation method.
Physicochemical characteristics of synthesized NPs (ARC-PCF-NPs), including
average particle size, dispersity index (PDI), zeta potential (ZP), field emis-
sion scanning electron microscope figures, and encapsulation efficiency (EE),
were evaluated. 2,2′-Azino-bis(3-ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulfonic acid) (ABTS),
2,2-diphenyl-1-picrylhydrazyl (DPPH), and ferric reducing antioxidant power
(FRAP) methods were carried out to determine the antioxidant properties of NPs.
3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-diphenyl-2H-tetrazolium bromide assay was per-
formed to investigate cytotoxicity effects on cancer cells and normal fibroblasts.
Quantitative polymerase chain reaction was also performed on inflammatory
and antioxidant genes. The obtained results indicated that the synthesized
NPs have a size of 100 nm, a DPI of 0.36, a ZP of 26.30 mV, and EE was
calculated at about 87.5%. The antioxidant influence of ARC-PCF-NPs was
confirmed by inhibiting ABTS and DPPH free radicals and ferrous reduction
in the FRAP method. Moreover, the reduction of inflammatory and antiox-
idant genes confirmed the anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties of
NPs. These results indicate the modification of the surface of NPs in order to
increase the bioavailability, stability, and effectiveness of medicinal compounds
in therapeutic applications.

INTRODUCTION
Colon cancer is recognized as one of the most common
cancers in the world, affecting many people every year.
Despite the therapeutic advances in the field of cancer ther-
apy, colon cancer has caused many deaths due to drug
resistance and metastasis.1 Toward this end, the attention
of researchers has been drawn to discovering new and
effective medicinal compounds for the treatment and pre-
vention of colon cancer. In line with this, the identification
of effective compounds from biological and natural sources
in the treatment of various cancers with low side effects
and drug resistance has been prioritized.2
Arctigenin (AR) is a natural compound derived from
Arctium lappa, which is used as a medicinal supple-
ment against many infectious diseases such as influenza.3
The glycoside compound arctiin (ARC) is known as the
main and active compound of the dried ripe fruit of A.
lappa. It has been found that these secondary metabolites
are effective in treating many diseases, including cancer,
with anti-cancer, antioxidant, and antimicrobial effects.4–6
However, the lack of solubility of AR in water is one
of the limitations of using this compound as an antitu-
mor drug in new therapeutic approaches.3 Therefore, the
design of nanoparticles (NPs) containing ARC can improve
its pharmacogenetic properties.
In line with this, polylactic acid glycolic acid (PLGA)
copolymer is one of the most common compounds used
in the design of NPs to improve the drug delivery sys-
tem by increasing the stability of various drugs, reducing
side effects, and optimizing the release of drugs, which
has also been approved by the FDA.7,8 However, thanks to
modifying PLGA-based NPs with other compounds such
as chitosan (CS), researchers have overcome the limita-
tions of PLGA, such as rapid processing and lack of direct
interaction with cells or proteins.2,9 Furthermore, target
therapy is one of the effective methods for optimizing the
drug delivery system, especially in the treatment of solid
tumors. In this regard, folate receptors with a high expres-
sion on the surface of tumor cells are a suitable option for
targeted therapy.2,10 According to the points, we designed
PLGA-based NPs modified with CS–folic acid (FA) con-
taining ARC compound and investigated its mechanism of
action against tumor cells, especially colon cancer cells.

METHODS AND MATERIALS
2.1
Synthesis of ARC nanoparticles
based on PLGA modified with
chitosan–folate
To load ARC (Golexir) on PLGA NPs, the W1/O/W2
double-emulsion solvent evaporation method was used.11
Briefly, first, 50 mg of polylactic co-glycolic acid (PLGA,
Merck) was dissolved in 12 mL of dichloromethane
(Merck), and then, ARC was added to the organic phase
at a concentration of 10 mg/mL. Thereafter, polyvinyl alco-
hol (PVA) 2% (Merck) was added to the mentioned solution
and sonicated for 3 min. Next, 10 mL of PVA (0.1%) was
also added to obtain the W1/O/W2 double-emulsion sol-
vent. After the evaporation of the solvent, the obtained
mixture was centrifuged, and the supernatant was sepa-
rated to evaluate the entrapment rate of ARC . In the next
step, 6 mg of FA (Merck) was dissolved in DMSO, and 1-
ethyl-3-[3-dimethylaminopropyl]carbodiimide hydrochlo-
ride (Merck) and N-hydroxysuccinimide (Merck) were
added to the solution. Thereafter, FA was added dropwise
to dissolved CS in 0.1% acetic acid and stirred for 24 h in
order to bind FA to CS (Sigma-Aldrich). After the centrifu-
gation of the resulting solution, the FA–CS precipitate was
prepared to determine the amount of FA binding. In order
to modify PLGA NPs with FA–CS, PLGA NPs and FA–CS
sediment were dissolved separately in deionized water, and
then, FA–CS solution was added dropwise to PLGA NPs.
After 2 h of incubation, the final mixture was centrifuged
and then lyophilized.
2.2
Characterization of ARC-PCF-NPs
To determine the physicochemical characteristics of NPs,
dynamic light scattering (DLS) and Zetasizer methods
were used to investigate the dispersity index (PDI) and
surface potential of NPs, respectively. Besides, field emis-
sion scanning electron microscope (FESEM) and Fourier
transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) were also used to
examine the morphology and evaluate the interaction of
the functional groups of NPs.12

2.3
Evaluation of drug encapsulation in
ARC-PCF-NPs
The percentage of drug encapsulation in NPs was eval-
uated using the spectrophotometer absorption method
and indirect method.13 Different concentrations of ARC
were prepared, and their absorbance was measured at a
wavelength of 226 nm, and a standard graph was drawn.
Finally, the encapsulation efficiency (%EE) was calculated
as described previously.12

DPPH assay

solution with ethanol. After 30 min of incubation, the
absorbance of the sample was evaluated with a plate reader
at a wavelength of 517 nm, and the percentage of free rad-
ical inhibition of ARC-PCF-NPs was measured with the
following formula:

2.4.3
FRAP assay
Additionally, the antioxidant capacity of ARC-PCF-NPs
was measured by evaluating the iron reduction power
using the ferric reducing antioxidant power (FRAP)
reagent. The buffer reagent was prepared according to
the established protocol described previously. After com-
bining different concentrations of ARC-PCF-NPs with the
prepared buffer, it was incubated for 5 min in a dark
atmosphere.12 Finally, the absorbance of the samples was
read at a wavelength of 539 nm. It is worth noting that
FeSo4 was used as the standard and drawing the standard
curve.15
2.5
Cell culture
In this study, the required cells (MCF-7, HT-29, fibrob-
last [HFF], and PANC) were prepared from the Pasteur
Institute (Tehran, Iran) and kept in appropriate conditions
(Incubation at 37◦C and containing 5% CO2) and a related
culture medium (Gibco-BRL, Life Technology) containing
heat-inactivated fetal bovine serum (Gibco-BRL, Life Tech-
nology) and 1% penicillin-streptomycin (Gibco-BRL, Life
Technology).
2.5.1
Examining the cytotoxic effect of
ARC-PCF-NPs by MTT assay
The
3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-diphenyl-2H-
tetrazolium bromide (MTT) method was conducted
to assess the toxicity of NPs on cells based on the reduction
rate of tetrazolium salt to purple formazan crystals. After
24 h of exposing the cells to various concentrations of NPs
(7.80, 15.60, 32.25, 62.5, 125, 250, and 500 μg/mL), we added
100 μL of MTT solution to each well and incubated the
plate for 4 h at 37◦C. After incubation, we removed the
MTT solution and added 100 μL of DMSO. The formation
of purple color is an indicator of living cells, and the
intensity of the produced color was measured at the
wavelength of 570 nm.
2.5.2
Quantitative real‑time PCR (qRT‑RTnt
Quantitative real‑time polymerase chain reaction method
was conducted to investigate the expression changes of
studied genes (catalase, superoxide dismutase, IL-1, IL-6)
due to ARC-PCF-NPs treatment. After 48 h of cell treat-
ment with ARC-PCF-NPs, the total mRNA of the cells was
obtained using the RNA extraction kit (Biofact) accord-
ing to the kit manuscript. Next, the RNA concentration
was calculated using the nanodrop method. RNA extracted
by a cDNA synthesis kit (Pars Tous biotechnology) was
reverse transcribed based on the manufacturer’s protocol.
Finally, the cycle threshold of the samples was evaluated
by a real-time thermal cycler (Biorad) and analyzed by the
2ΔΔCt method. Glyceraldehyde 3 phosphatedehydrogenase
(GAPDH) was used as a control gene in this method.2

Experimental data analysis
Obtained data were analyzed by SPSS statistical software.

3
RESULTS
3.1
ARC-PCF-NP characterization
The results of the physicochemical characteristics of ARC-
PCF-NPs implied that the particle size was 100 nm, the
average hydrodynamic diameter was 291.45, the PDI index
was 0.36 nm, and the zeta potential (ZP) was 26.30 mV.
By examining the morphology of NPs by FESEM, we
found that the ARC-PCF-NPs are dispersed with spheri-
cal shapes, and the size of the particles is in accordance
with the data of DLS. The FTIR spectrum indicated a peak
at 2930 cm−1 representing N–H stretching vibration and
a peak at 1730 cm−1 for the C=O stretching vibration of
carbonyl group. In addition, the peaks in the range of 1040–
1046 cm−1 indicated the stretching vibrations caused by
N–O and C–O bonds, respectively (Figure 1).16,17
3.2
The amount of drug encapsulation
By comparing the amount of absorption of the supernatant
from the synthesis of ARC-PCF-NPs with the obtained
standard graph from different concentrations of ARC, we
found that the %EE of ARC in NPs is about 87.5% (Figure 2).
3.3
Antioxidant capacity of
ARC-PDF-NPs
3.3.1
Inhibiting of free radicals
Investigating the inhibitory influence of RC-PCF-NPs on
free radicals in DPPH and ABTS tests showed that the
inhibitory effects of RC-PCF-NPs are dependent on the
concentration of NPs. In this regard, the rate of inhibi-
tion of free radicals has a significantly positive relation-
ship with an increase in concentrations of RC-PCF-NPs.
According to the figures, the IC50 for inhibiting ABTS and
DPPH free radicals was calculated to be about 125 and
150 μg/mL, respectively (Figure 3a,b). Moreover, the results
of examining the power of RC-PCF-NPs in ferrous reduc-
tion with the FRAP method also confirmed the antioxidant
effect of RC-PCF-NPs (Figure 1c).
3.4
Cytotoxic effect of ARC-PCF-NPs
Related data indicated that the toxicity effect of ARC-PCF-
NPs against breast cancer cells (MCF-7), colon cancer cells
(HT-29), and pancreatic cancer cells (PANC-1) was inves-
tigated in different concentrations. According to Figure 4,
the viability of MCF-7 and HT-29 cell lines decreased in
a concentration-dependent manner. The IC50 values of
ARC-PCF-NPs for MCF-7 and HT-29 cells are about 163
and 14.47 μg/mL, respectively (Figure 4a,b). However, the
results of investigating the cytotoxic effect of ARC-PCF-
NPs on PANC-1 do not show serious dependence on the
concentration, and the IC50 was calculated to be about
462 μg/mL (Figure 4c). In addition, the toxicity effect of
ARC-PCF-NPs against normal HFF cells was investigated.
The results implied the negligible toxicity of ARC-PCF-
NPs against normal cells. Therefore, we observed that
500 μg/mL of ARC-PCF-NPs reduced about 30% viability of
HFF cells (Figure 4d). Due to the high sensitivity of colon
cancer cells to ARC-PCF-NPs, further tests were performed
on colon cancer cells (HT-29).
3.5
Catalase and superoxide dismutase
gene expression
To investigate the antioxidant influence of ARC-PCF-
NPs on HT-29 cell lines, we investigated the expression
of superoxide dismutase (SOD) and catalase (CAT) after
48 h of treating the cells with ARC-PCF-NPs. Antioxidant
enzymes SOD and CAT are able to neutralize superox-
ide and convert H2O and O2. Changes in the antioxidant
capacity of cancer cells, including changes in SOD and
CAT gene levels, have been reported in the progression of
different cancer cells.18,19 The analyzed data implied that
increasing the concentration of ARC-PCF-NPs leads to a
decrease in the gene expression of mentioned enzymes in
colon cancer cells (Figure 5).
3.6
Pro-inflammatory gene expression
We evaluated the expression of two pro-inflammatory
genes (IL-6 and IL-1) to clarify the anti-inflammatory
properties of ARC-PCF-NPs on HT-29 cells. As illustrated
in Figure 6, IL-6 gene expression was dose-dependently
decreased due to treatment with ARC-PCF-NPs. In addi-
tion, increasing the concentration of ARC-PCF-NPs has led
to a decrease in IL-1 gene expression. However, doses of 15
and 30 had a significant reduction in IL-1 gene expression
compared to the untreated group (Figure 6).

DISCUSSION
In the current study, NPs based on PLGA modified
with CS–folate containing ARC were synthesized by the
W1/O/W2 method. Analysis of the physicochemical char-
acteristics of ARC-PCF-NPs implied that the ARC-PCF-
NPs have an average size of 100 nm, PDI of 0.36, a ZP of
26.30 mV, and EE of 87.5%. Recently, polymer NPs, includ-
ing NPs synthesized based on PLGA, have attracted the
attention of many researchers in the field of pharmaceu-
ticals due to their high biocompatibility, high solubility,
and low toxicity.20,21 The W1/O/W2 method was conducted
for the synthesis of PLGA-based actin-containing NPs. In
line with this, evidence has been reported that the syn-
thesis of NPs by the W1/O/W2 double-emulsion solvent
evaporation method leads to an increase in high drug
loading.12 However, it has been reported that PLGA-based
NPs have limitations, such as a lack of proper drug release,
lack of interaction with body cells and proteins, and rapid
decomposition.22 Given these points, researchers are try-
ing to adjust the limitations of PLGA-based NPs. Research
to date suggested CS to improve the efficiency of PLGA-
based NPs.9,23 Studies have known that the use of CS to
coat the PLGA NPs surfaces is effective in improving drug
release and increasing uptake by cells.9,24,25 Moreover,

CONCLUSION
In the current study, NPs based on PLGA modified with
CS–folate containing ARC were synthesized with the
aim of optimizing drug delivery against tumor cells. Our
results indicated the significant cytotoxic effects of NPs on
colon cancer cell lines. In addition, the anti-inflammatory,
antioxidant, and antimicrobial properties of NPs were
shown by different methods. However, the conducted stud-
ies in the field of drug delivery of ARC have been very
limited. Toward this end, it is suggested to design different
NPs containing ARC and investigate the mechanism of its
effect on different tumor cells, as well as in vivo studies.
