A B S T R A C T
The present work aimed to load propranolol hydrochloride (PN), a beta-blocking agent with low oral
bioavailability, into PLGA-lipid hybrid nanoparticles (PLHNPs) for augmenting its efficacy. PLHNPs contain
phospholipid (PC) in addition to PLGA to augment the potential of PLGA nanoparticles in the intranasal delivery
and PN avoidance of the blood–brain barrier for the management of migraine. PLHNPs were prepared by single
emulsion/ solvent evaporation method and then optimized by applying 23 full factorial design using PC amount
(mg) (X1), PLGA amount (mg) (X2), and surface active agent type (X3) as independent variables, whilst their
effect was inspected for entrapment efficiency percent (EE%) (Y1) and particle size (PS) (Y2). Design-Expert® was
utilized to choose the optimum PLHNPs for more explorations. The optimum PLHNPs formulation (F2) had EE%
of 78.00 ± 0.71 %, PS of 104.50 ± 2.04 nm, polydispersity index of 0.429 ± 0.033, and zeta potential of 23.70 ±
0.10 mV. The optimum PLHNPs formulation was stable for up to 90 days. Moreover, it showed a sustained
release profile compared to PN solution. It also showed a spherical shape under a transmission electron mi­
croscope. The optimized PN-loaded PLHNPs formulation was radio formulated with radiolabeled isotope
([99mTc]Tc) in maximum radiolabeling yield (91.40 ± 1.85 %) of [99mTc]Tc-PLHNPs to be used in radiological
evaluation for in-vivo biodistribution and brain targeting after oral and intranasal administration. [99mTc]Tc-
PLHNPs showed higher brain targeting (5.80 ± 0.12 % ID/g) with a high brain-to-blood ratio of (2.42 ± 0.14) at
0.5 h after intranasal administration in addition to controlled blood levels and sustained release up to 8 h that
confirm the efficacy of PLHNPs for brain targeting.
1. Introduction
Migraine is a neurovascular illness characterized by a severe head­
ache and activation of the trigeminovascular system including the pro­
duction of calcitonin gene-related peptides (Saleh et al., 2021). The
migraine’s intensity correlates with the quantity of dilatation of extra­
cranial arteries and the vessels of the bulbar conjunctiva. Moreover, it is
relieved by agents that cause cerebral vasoconstriction (Widerøe and
Vigander, 1974). Falck et al. revealed that the diameter vessels include
both alpha- and beta-adrenergic receptors and that beta-blocking drugs
may have an impact on cerebral blood flow (Falck et al., 1968). The
effectiveness of beta-blocking drugs in the management of migraine
headache has been demonstrated clinically (Rabkin et al., 1966; Elta­
beeb et al. 2024c) and via controlled studies (Malvea et al., 1973; Weber
and Reinmuth, 1971). Widerøe and Vigander proved clinically the po­
tency of propranolol hydrochloride (PN) in the prophylactic manage­
ment of both classic and common migraine (Widerøe and Vigander,
1974).
PN is classified as a beta-adrenergic blocker that is utilized mostly for
arrhythmias, angina, myocardial infarction, sinus tachycardia, and

hydrophobic chain which may provide amphiphilic properties (Teaima
et al., 2022; Abdellatif et al., 2022). It is usually administrated orally,
however, it has a short plasma half-life due to its extensive first-pass
hepatic metabolism (Mansur et al., 1998). Hence, the intranasal route
is suggested as an alternative route to deliver the appropriate dose of PN
to the brain. Intranasal drug delivery is practical, non-invasive, and
prevents dose waste that may result from emesis during a migraine
attack. It also helps to avoid the blood brain barrier, allowing for direct
targeting of the brain via absorption through the olfactory mucosa
(Dechant and Clissold, 1992; Gulati et al., 2013).
Since 1995, there has been a rise in the utilization of nanoparticles
for the systemic delivery of the drugs. Shape, flexibility, and size are
critical physical factors impacting nanoparticles penetration and trans­
port through mucosal membranes (Nowak et al., 2020; Saleh et al.,
2021). Nanoparticles are submicron colloidal drug carriers consisting of
natural or synthetic polymers (Gulati et al., 2013). The polymer based
nanoparticles have various advantages including stability, controlled
drug release, simplicity of manufacture, and low cost (Mohanty et al.,
2020; Elmahboub et al., 2024).
Poly (lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) is possibly the most widely
utilized polymer since it is FDA-approved, has adjustable physico­
chemical characteristics, and is biodegradable and biocompatible
(Ramalho et al., 2019; Ramalho and Pereira, 2016). However, the
drawbacks of polymer based nanoparticles such as their small payload,
the requirement for organic solvent during manufacturing, and short
circulation half-life, increase the need for integrating polymer based
nanoparticles with liposome structural components to create hybrid
carriers (Mohanty et al., 2020; Zhang and Zhang, 2010). The benefits of
both polymer-based nanoparticles and liposomes are combined in
polymer-lipid hybrid nanoparticles. This hybrid technology offers the
advantages of liposomes such as the high efficacy, high targeting ability,
as well as decreasing drug toxicity (Abdelbari et al., 2023a), overcomes
the constraints of polymers and lipids, and has a tremendous possibility
for use in nanomedicine (Zhang et al., 2008). The hybrid system can be a
powerful drug delivery platform with high encapsulation efficiency,
sustained release kinetics, high stability, well triggered tissue, cellular,
and molecular weight characteristics, and long circulation half-life
which helps maintain therapeutic drug levels over a longer period,
enhance the targeting of nanoparticles to the desired site, hence
improving their efficacy. In the present manuscript, to augment the
potential of PLGA nanoparticles in the nasal delivery of PN for man­
agement of migraine, phosphatidylcholine (PC) was incorporated to
prepare PLGA-lipid hybrid nanoparticles (PLHNPs).
Radiobiological techniques are used to evaluate the in-vivo bio­
distribution of new formulations or different routes of administration to
estimate the potency and efficacy of the new formulation via a specific
route of administration and to determine the pharmacokinetic param­
eters. The radiological evaluation technique proceeds by one of three
methods; direct radiolabeling of new optimum formulation with radio­
isotopes such as technetium-99m (99mTc) (Sayed et al., 2021), radio­
labeling the drug or newly synthesized drug then radio formulation with
the new optimized formulation (El-Safoury et al., 2021b) or using
radiopharmaceutical indicator (El-Halim et al., 2020). Radio formula­

Passive intercellular absorption, intracellular and transcytosis path­
ways are the predominant pathways by which a drug can be absorbed
into the blood across nasal mucosa (Azarmi, M. et al., 2020; Homayun,
B.et al., 2019). On the other hand, low molecular weight, lipid soluble
drugs can reach the brain by crossing the blood brain barrier (BBB) after
intranasal administration. Also, most drugs can maneuver the BBB and
get directly absorbed into the brain via the olfactory region (the olfac­
tory and trigeminal neural pathways) (Shadab et al., 2018). Drug de­
livery through the olfactory pathway enables direct transport to the
brain via olfactory neurons, whereas the trigeminal nerve pathway may
enhance drug absorption through the nasal mucosa and subsequent
dissemination to the central nervous system (Brand, 2006).
As far as we are aware, this work is the first to encapsulate PN into
PLHNPs to augment its efficacy in the treatment of migraine. Thereby,
our research aimed to determine the potential of PLHNPs for optimizing
PN intranasal administration. To achieve the aforementioned goal,
different parameters impacting nanoparticle characteristics were
investigated, the optimum formulation containing PN was radiolabeled
by [99mTc]Tc to evaluate its in-vivo biodistribution, efficacy and phar­
macokinetic parameters after intranasal administration compared to
oral route.

3. Methods
3.1. Formation of PN loaded PLHNPs
PLHNPs were fabricated utilizing a single emulsion/solvent evapo­
ration as previously reported by Chung et al. (2020). In brief, PLGA,
surface active agent (SAA) (50 mg), and PC were dissolved in 2 mL of
acetone: chloroform mixture (1:1 %) (v/v). On the other hand, PN (40
mg) was dissolved in 10 ml distilled water and dropped into the organic
phase and allowed to be stirred overnight at 37 ◦C to eliminate any
residue from the organic solvent. Finally, probe sonication (Vibra
Cell-Sonics Material, 130 W, 20 kHz, USA) was used to sonicate the
fabricated dispersion for 5 min (3 s on and 3 s off) at 40 % amplitude to
formulate PLHNPs. After that, PLHNPs were stored at 4 ◦C for
maturation.
3.2. Characterization of PN loaded PLHNPs
3.2.1. Assessment of EE%
1 mL of each formulation was centrifuged for 1 h at 4 ◦C and 20,000
rpm in a cooling centrifuge (Sigma 3-30 KS, Germany) (Albash et al.,
2022). The sediment was destroyed with methanol and PN content was
evaluated at λmax 289 nm (Teaima et al., 2022; Eltabeeb et al., 2024a)
via UV/VIS spectrophotometer (Shimadzu UV1650, Spectrophotometer,
Kyoto, Japan). PN EE% was calculated by applying the equation below
(Albash et al., 2020):

3.2.2. Assessment of PS, PDI and ZP
PS, PDI and ZP were assessed for the fabricated PN loaded PLHNPs
applying Zetasizer 2000 (Malvern Instrument Ltd., UK). Suitable dilu­
tion of formulations was done to the first faint haziness to ensure the

Three times the optimum formulation was evaluated, and the average
result was reported (Abdelbari et al., 2023b; Albash et al., 2023;
Mosallam et al., 2021b).

3.2.3. Experimental design and selection of the optimum PN loaded
PLHNPs
To determine the effect of several independent variables on the
creation of PLHNPs, 23 full factorial design was utilized. Three variables
were assessed: PC amount (mg) (X1), PLGA amount (mg) (X2), and SAA
type (X3). As dependent variables, EE% (Y1), and PS (Y2) were selected
(Table 1). Utilizing Design-Expert® software, an analysis of variance
(ANOVA) test was performed on each parameter (Version 7, Stat-Ease
Inc., USA). P-value < 0.05 was utilized to determine statistical signifi­
cance. The optimization aimed to identify the formulation with the
maximum EE% and the lowest PS. The desirability function, facilitating
the concurrent analysis of all constraints, constituted the basis of the
recommendation. The formulation with the greatest desirability result
was selected.
3.2.4. In-vitro release study
The in-vitro release pattern of PN from the optimum PLHNPs
formulation was compared to an aqueous solution of the same concen­
tration through a dialysis method. To summarize, 5 cm long dialysis
bags closed from one end enclosed the volume of each formulation
equivalent to 4 mg PN, then tightly closed from the other end. These
were immersed in dark-colored bottles containing 50 mL of phosphate
buffer. Then, the bottles were mechanically shaken at 100 strokes per
minute and 37 ◦C. For up to 6 h, one-hour-interval samples (2 mL) were
withdrawn and replaced with fresh buffer solution (Eltabeeb et al.,
2024b). The samples were spectrophotometrically analyzed at 289 nm
for their PN content. The release profiles were then obtained by plotting
% released vs time (h) (ElShagea et al., 2023).
3.2.5. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM)
The optimum PLHNPs formulation morphology was identified
applying TEM (Joel JEM 1230, Tokyo, Japan). A sample that had been
stained with an aqueous solution of phosphotungstic acid (2 %) was
placed on a copper-covered carbon grid and allowed to dry until TEM
tests were undertaken (Albash et al., 2024; Mosallam et al., 2021a).
3.2.6. The impact of storage on the optimum PLHNPs
To test the physical stability of the optimum PLHNPs formulation, it
was kept at 4–8 ◦C for 90 days (Abd-Elsalam et al., 2018). Appearance,
EE%, PS, PDI, ZP, and Q6 h of the optimum PLHNPs formulation were
tested before and after storage. The acquired data were statistically
examined using the student t-test ( was set at 0.05) in SPSS® version
22.0.
3.3. In vivo studies
3.3.1. Histopathological studies
The histopathological examination was adopted to test the biocom­
patibility and safety of the different components of PLHNPs. Eight
Wistar rats (150–200 g each) were divided randomly into two groups:
group I acted as control, while group II were treated with the optimum
PLHNPs formulation. Application of the optimum PLHNPs formulation
onto the nasal mucosa was done three times daily for one week, then
ketamine was utilized to sacrifice the rat and nasal mucosal samples
were excised for further examination (Tawfik et al., 2023).
3.3.2. In-vivo radio-formulation of the optimum PN loaded PLHNPs
The optimum PN loaded PLHNPs formulation was radiolabeled by
[99mTc]Tc via direct labeling technique utilizing a suitable reducing
agent such as sodium dithionite (Na2S2O4) that reduce the [99mTc]Tc to
make coordinate covalent bond with the denoting groups on the surface
of the formula as a result of using PLGA. The complexion between
nanoparticles and radioisotope in high radiolabeling yield facilitates the
radiotracking of the formula post mice administration to ensure that the
[99mTc]Tc-PLHNPs reach to target site (brain) and delivery the PN. The
factors affecting the radiolabeling process such as formulation concen­
tration, sodium dithionite concentration, pH of the reaction medium,
and reaction duration time were investigated to maximize the radio­
labeling yield of the Radio-formulation (99mTc-PLHNPs) (Ibrahim et al.,
2020).

4. Results and discussion
4.1. Analysis of factorial design
Screening experiments were carried out to evaluate the independent
parameters that could influence the fabrication of PN enclosed PLHNPs.
Design Expert® program was used to fabricate PLHNPs in accordance
with full factorial design. The created design proposed eight experi­
mental runs to represent all conceivable combinations of the various
levels of the investigated factors. 2 FI was the model selected for EE%
and PS analysis. The model’s capacity to navigate the design space was
verified by the use of adequate precision (Mouhram et al., 2025). As
evidenced by all of the responses examined, a ratio greater than four is
preferable (Table 2) (Albash et al., 2019; Mosallam et al., 2021a). In all
of the responses studied, the predicted (R2) values matched adjusted
(R2) values, which reflects the model’s quality (Abdelbari et al., 2024).
Fig. 1 depicts the impact of formulae factors on the explored responses.
4.2. The impact of formulation variables on the EE%
The EE% was determined by a spectrophotometric method that was
specific, linear (R2=1), and valid (the Coefficient of variation percentage
(CV%) ranged from 3.30 % to 3.44 % and the relative error ranged from
0.15 % to 3.91 %). High EE% is desirable to increase PLHNPs efficiency
(Ramalho et al., 2019). The percentage of PN included inside PLHNPs
fluctuated between 52.00 ± 1.41 % and 78.00 ± 0.71 % (Table 3). It was
found that PC amount (mg) (X1) significantly (p < 0.0001) impacted EE
% of PN. According to the outcomes, the EE% value was augmented as
PC amount increased. This might be attributed to an increase in the
space available for PN loading into PLHNPs (Lu et al., 2011), this
outcome was consistent with Jahanfar et al., who reported that when the
amount of PC increased, the EE% of green tea extract was augmented
during their preparation of liposomes to augment its antioxidant effect
(Jahanfar et al., 2021).
Considering the PLGA amount (mg) (X2), it was noticed that
increasing the PLGA amount resulted in a significant (p < 0.0001)
decrease in the EE%. The certain hydrophilic property (amphiphilic
drug) of PN might be the reason behind the reduction of the EE%.
Because the polymer is water insoluble and PN possesses a certain hy­
drophilic character, the interaction between the two is too low (Fazil
et al., 2016). Abdelghany et al. reported that the hydrophobic nature of
the polymer, as opposed to the drug’s hydrophilic properties, might be a
limiting issue in the fabrication of gentamicin (hydrophilic drug)
loading PLGA nanoparticles (Abdelghany et al., 2012).
According to statistical analysis, SAA type (X3) had a significant (p <
0.0001) impact on the EE%. Pluronic 188-fabricated PLHNPs had a
higher EE% than PVA-fabricated PLHNPs. This could be explained by
the higher hydrophilicity of Pluronic 188 (HLB= 29) compared to PVA
(HLB= 18). The higher the HLB value (greater hydrophilicity), the more
successful the PN entrapment inside the bilayer PLHNPs, resulting in a
greater EE% (Cort´es et al., 2021).The impact of formulation variables on
the PS.
PS of PLHNPs is a crucial character, impacting their bio-distribution,
half-life, and cellular internalization (Ramalho et al., 2019). PS of the
fabricated PLHNPs fluctuated between 104.50 ± 2.04 and 203.60 ±
25.41 nm (Table 3). Fig. 1 displays that PC amount (mg) (X1) impacted
the PS of the fabricated PLHNPs significantly (p < 0.0001). According to
the results, the PS of the fabricated PLHNPs was reduced upon
increasing PC amount. As the quantity of PC increased, a greater number
of PC molecules became available to act as a surfactant, thereby stabi­
lizing and promoting the formation of smaller PLHNPs (Zhao et al.,
2015).
Considering the PLGA amount (mg) (X2), PS decreased significantly
(p < 0.0001) with increasing PLGA amount. This correlated to the EE%
outcomes as an increase in PLGA amount resulted in low EE% values
(Hathout et al., 2007). Statistical analysis displayed that SAA type (X3)
had also a significant (p < 0.0001) influence on the PS. PLHNPs fabri­
cated using PVA were larger than those fabricated using Pluronic 188.
This might be justified by the variance in the molecular weight between
PVA (30,000–70,000 Dalton) and Pluronic 188 (7680–9510 Dalton)
(Srihaphon et al., 2021). As a result, the bulkier PVA resulted in a larger
PS of PVA-PLHNPs than Pluronic 188-PLHNPs. Furthermore, a rise in
molecular weight correlates to an increase in nano-dispersion viscosity
which might cause aggregation and PS amplification (Yang et al., 2013).
Srihaphon et al. reported that the PS of Morsa Alba stem extract loaded
PLGA nanoparticles fabricated utilizing PVA were larger than those
fabricated utilizing Pluronic 188 (Srihaphon et al., 2021).
It is worth to mention that nano-ranged size facilitates BBB pene­
tration, as it enhances permeability, therefore deliver the drugs more
effectively to the brain (Lima and Mortari, 2022).

4.4. Determination of the optimum PLHNPs formulation
The optimum PLHNPs formulation was found utilizing the Design-
Expert® tool and had the following properties: the greatest EE% and
smallest PS. F2 composed of 100 mg PC, 50 mg PLGA, and Pluronic 188
as SAA was chosen by the program as the optimum PLHNPs with the
greatest desirability value (0.99), and it was subjected for more
explorations.
4.5. Assessment of ZP of the optimum PN loaded PLHNPs
The optimum charged surface can provide electrostatic repulsion
between particles, preventing them from accumulation or fusion and so
promoting physical stability (Ruckmani and Sankar, 2010). The opti­
mum PLHNPs (F2) revealed a positive ZP value of 23.70 ± 0.10 mV as it
was reported previously that PN is a positively charged bioactive
(Abruzzo et al., 2012). The charge influences the ability of the NPs to
permeate BBB. As the positively charged NPs are internalized more
easily into the cell, which influences the permeability through cellular
membranes and impacts the passage of NPs across the BBB (Lima and
Mortari, 2022).
4.6. In-vitro release study
Fig. 2a demonstrates the sustained release profile of PN from the
optimum PLHNPs formulation, compared to PN solution. It could be
seen that the aqueous solution nearly released all its PN content after the
first hour. The difference in release pattern could be explained in the
light of the fact that PN is a hydrophilic drug, and in aqueous solution,
there are no factors that can hinder its diffusion across the dialysis
membrane. On the other hand, PN in PLHNPs is enclosed by both PC and

F2 was submitted to a short-term stability assessment for 90 days.

4.9.1. Histopathological studies
Fig. 3 revealed the histopathological photomicrographs of hema­

4.9.3. In-vivo biodistribution of [99mTc]Tc-PLHNPs
The groups of mice A and B were orally and intranasally given
[99mTc]Tc-optimum PLHNPs, respectively. The [99mTc]Tc-optimum
PLHNPs concentration (%ID/g) in the brain, blood, and liver were
calculated at predetermined time points up to 8 h. The intranasal
administration of [99mTc]Tc-optimum PLHNPs showed significantly
higher brain concentration (brain Cmax) 5.80 ± 0.12 %ID/g at 0.5 h
(brain Tmax) than brain concentration at all-time points after oral
administration of [99mTc]Tc-optimum PLHNPs. These results confirmed
the improved brain targeting efficacy by the intranasal administration of
the optimum PN loaded PLHNPs as presented in Fig. 5a. Additionally,
results showed a continued high brain concentration of [99mTc]Tc-op­
timum PLHNPs to 1.30 ± 0.30 %ID/g at 8 h after intranasal adminis­
tration, so the optimum PN loaded PLHNPs formulation can be
considered a sustained release formulation.

cient targeting of the brain which was confirmed by our results.
5. Conclusion
As introduced in this research, we fabricated PLHNPs as an innova­
tive system for the intranasal delivery of PN. The encapsulation of PN
was done utilizing a single emulsion/ solvent evaporation method with
the application of 23 full factorial design using the Design Expert®
program. The program identified F2 as the optimum PLHNPs formula­
tion with the greatest desirability value. F2 morphology appeared as
spherical with a narrow size. It had a high EE%, good stability, and
sustained PN profile compared to PN solution. The radiobiological
evaluation for in vivo biodistribution study of intranasal administration
of [99mTc]Tc-PLHNPs relative to oral administration confirmed the ef­
ficiency of PLHNPs in brain targeting of PN with high brain Cmax of
[99mTc]Tc-PLHNPs and high brain to blood ratio.
