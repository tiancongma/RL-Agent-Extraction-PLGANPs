Nanoparticle Characterization

TrH loaded nanoparticles were checked for size and distribution, zeta

potential, morphology and encapsulation efficiency. Mean particle size,

size distribution, and zeta potential of TrH loaded nanoparticles were

determined by dynamic light scattering (Z3000, Nicomp, Port Richey, FL,

USA). Samples were diluted with ultrapure water (1:200) and measured

in triplicate.

The encapsulation efficiency was found by calculating the ratio of the

active substance trapped in the nanoparticles over the total amount of

active substance. For this purpose, the amount of unloaded active

substance was measured from the supernatants obtained in the

centrifugation process of TrH nanoparticles by HPLC (Agilent 1100,

California, USA). These amounts were calculated by subtracting the

initial amount of active ingredient added to the formulation and the

encapsulation efficiency (%) values were obtained [17, 18]. If the HPLC

In Vitro Drug Release

Drug release study of TrH loaded nanoparticles versus free active

nanoparticles (1 mL) were poured inside a dialysis bag (cut-off 12-14

kDa) and both ends were closed to prevent any leakage. The same

amount of TrH solution was poured into a dialysis bag and dialysis bags

were prepared using the same procedure. Each bag was located inside a

beaker that contain 30 mL of PBS (pH 7.4) as release medium. Then, the

medium was stirred at room temperature at 200 rpm. At specific time

intervals, 1 mL of aliquot was taken from the incubation medium to

evaluate the release rate of the TrH. The removed solution with the same

content of fresh buffer was replaced and then for each sample, amount

of TrH was determined using HPLC analysis.

Infrared Spectroscopy (FT-IR) Analysis

For the infrared spectrum analysis of the drug, polymer and prepared

nanoparticles, FT-IR analysis was performed with the device (Spectrum

400, Perkin Elmer, USA) in the 450-4000 cm-1 range.

Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC) Analysis

Thermal analysis of drug, polymer and the prepared nanoparticles were

carried out with a Differential Scanning Calorimeter (DSC) (Jade, Perkin

Elmer, USA). Samples were evaluated between 25-300@C on an

aluminum pan. The aluminum blank pan is used as a reference. Analysis

was performed at 20@C / min temperature increase and 20 mL / min

nitrogen flow rate.

SEM Analysis

The morphology of optimal formulation was investigated using a

scanning electron microscope (SEM). Surface images of lyophilized

nanoparticles were observed SEM (Zeiss Evo 40, Jena, Germany) after

covering with gold. A drop of the formulation were placed on 200 mesh

copper grids and excess of formulation was taken with filter paper. The

sample was stained with a 2% uranyl acetate solution for 2 min, they

were dried at under vacuum and visualized at 120 kV.

Stability Studies

For determination of the storage stability, nanoparticles were stored in

various temperatures and particle size, polydispersity index and zeta

potential of nanoparticles were evaluated . For this purpose, the

lyophilized nanoparticle formulation was stored at room temperature, 25

degC, + 4@C at refrigerator and -20@C in deep freezer for 3 months, and

then particle size, polydispersity index and zeta potential values were

checked.

Moreover, in order to examine the stability of the nanoparticles in the

infusion solutions, formulations were placed in 0.9% sodium chloride, 5%

dextrose, 20% mannitol, 10% dextran, 6% hydroxyethyl starch in 0.9%

sodium chloride and 4% succinyl gelatin solutions for 24 hours. Particle

size, polydispersity index and zeta potential of formulations were

evaluated after incubation time.

Determination of Drug Release

TrH release profiles from free TrH solution and formulated drug loaded

polymer nanoparticles were shown in Figure 2. The drug release

analysis of free TrT showed a burst release around 90% within the first

two hours. All nanoparticles showed slower drug release rate compared

to drug solution. The release rate was influenced by increasing the

partial amount of PLGA in the nanoparticles. (Figure 2). The burst

release at the first stage is a moderately normal fact in numerous drug

release systems . However, the initial burst release was observed in

all formulations between only 0-0.5 h time zone and burst release was

decreased to 15-35 % and a sustained release was gotten for at least 30

h owing to the entrapment of TrH into PLGA matrix in the nanoparticles.

The unloaded water soluble drug may not be moved away from

nanoparticles surface during washing step. It is assumed that

solvent will present an initial burst release due to the drug adsorbed on

the surface . Burst release can be useful to enhance the penetration

of drug while sustained release delivers the drug over extended period of

time. It should also be kept in mind that all nanoparticles show a slow

release, but increase the partial amount of PLGA and decrease the

homogenization rate, increase the drug release rate . Formulations

F4, F9 and F14 showed similar release patterns. When the first 24 hours

were compared, less first-pass effect was observed in the F14

formulation compared to free tramadol and the F4 formulation that

releases the same amount of active ingredient. F14 formulation was

considered suitable in terms of release.

Tramadol hydrochloride (TrH) is a synthetic opioid agonist analgesic

drug [5, 6] which has an analgesic potency less than morphine and

fentanyl , equal to meperidine and higher than codeine . Its

analgesic action is driven by opioid and non-opioid mechanisms. It was

shown that 30% of its action can be antagonized by naloxone and major

part of its effect is mediated through non-opioid mechanisms . TrH

shows similar effects with morphine in severe pain, but it has lower side

effects such as respiratory depression when compared to opioid drugs

which has similar effects. It has a very low abuse potential. Moreover

TrH does not have prostaglandin inhibition-mediated adverse effects

which is commonly seen in nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug use.

However the use of TrH is still limited due to its historical concerns and

lack of knowledge .

In spite of the fact that intravenous (i.v.) and oral TrH drugs are the first

choice in treatment, subcutaneous (s.c.) infusions can be used in the

treatment of patients whose pain and symptoms cannot be managed

otherwise pharmacologically due to the difficulty of accessing i.v.

administration and inadequacy to tolerate oral drugs. Although the

needle application site and the thickness of the patient's adipose tissue

affect the absorption of the active substance, s.c. infusion is not

contraindicated in cachectic patients . However studies showed that

the tolerability and stability of s.c. infusion of TrH is better in isotonic

solutions .

In the clinical setting, the use of infusion bag for systemic administration

of opioids are very simple and convenient practice. Among the other

drugs, TrH has been using for the treatment of pain due to its proven

clinical efficacy, safety and drug abuse and dependence are of minor

clinical relevance, contrary to other opioids .

Stability Studies

Stability of lyophilized nanoparticles were evaluated after incubation of

nanoparticles for 3 months at given temperatures (Table 2) . It was

determined that there was no significant difference between the particle

size of the formulation kept at -20 degC and +4 degC (p>0.05), while the

particle size and the PDI of the formulation increased after incubation at

25@C for 3 months (p <0.05). It was observed that aggregation occurs

and widespread polydisperse distribution is formed. Lalani et al. found

high stability of nanoparticles at -20 degC and +4 degC may be the storage

studies. The stability of PLGA at different temperatures was examined

and it was determined that polymer degradation was minimum at -16@C

after one year [38, 39]. It has been determined that at 25degC, particles

tend to aggregate under the effect of temperature, and therefore particle

size and distribution increase . When stability studies were

examined, it can be seen that zeta potentials were very close to each

other at -20degC and 25degC in 3 months. Due to the low zeta potential,

particles may tend to aggregate. When the particle size in the 3-month

stability study was examined, it was determined that the particles

aggregated after 3 months at room temperature (25degC). In this case, it

was thought that keeping the particles at -20degC would be more

appropriate for their stability.
