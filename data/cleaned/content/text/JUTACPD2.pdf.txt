METHODS ARTICLE
Towards scale-up and regulatory shelf-stability testing
of curcumin encapsulated polyester nanoparticles
Charitra N. Grama & Vinod P. Venkatpurwar &
Dimitrios A. Lamprou & M. N. V. Ravi Kumar
Published online: 19 April 2013
# Controlled Release Society 2013
Abstract This study reports scale-up and shelf-stability of
curcumin encapsulated poly(lactic acid-co-glycolic acid)
(PLGA) nanoparticles. The curcumin encapsulated PLGA
nanoparticles were prepared by emulsification solvent evap-
oration/diffusion, and large quantities were made by varying
the homogenisation time (5, 15 and 30 min). The particle
size decreased as the homogenisation duration increased
from 5 to 30 min, and the particles were spherical as con-
firmed by atomic force microscopy. For the large-scale
preparations, the mean particles size was found to be
288.7±3.4 (polydispersity index 0.15±0.01) with curcumin
entrapment 52.5±4.3 %, which were comparable to the lab-
scale preparations. The curcumin encapsulated nanopar-
ticles were freeze-dried using sucrose (5 %, w/v) as a cryo-
protectant. The freeze-dried nanoparticles were subjected to
6-month stability study as per the International Conference
on Harmonisation guideline at room temperature and refrig-
erated storage conditions. Intermediate sampling was done
(monthly), and the nanoparticles were thoroughly character-
ised for particle size, entrapment efficiency, surface mor-
phology and crystallinity, which were compared to fresh
preparations. The curcumin encapsulated PLGA nanopar-
ticles were found to be stable at refrigerated as well as room
temperature storage test conditions indicated by their parti-
cle characteristics. X-ray diffraction results confirm amor-
phous nature of curcumin on nano-encapsulation that stays
intact after freeze drying and 6-month stability testing. To-
gether these data offer possibility of producing large quan-
tities of polymer nanoparticles that are suitable for room as
well as refrigerated storage conditions opening up possibil-
ities to conduct repeated dosings in a chronic setting or
regulatory toxicology studies of such nanomedicines.

Introduction
Nanoparticles have traditionally been used to address
solubility- and permeability-related problems of active phar-
maceutical ingredients [1]; more recently, these particles are
believed to possess potential of targeting specific tissue/cell
of interest [2, 3]. However, translation in this area of re-
search is very poor owing to ambitious research goals,
difficulties in scale-up and regulatory concerns [4]. The
versatility of the polymer nanoparticles, particularly poly(-
lactide-co-glycolide) (PLGA), is demonstrated by their abil-
ity to deliver a range of bioactives both by improving the
efficacy as well as minimising the toxicity [5]. A lot of this
work is done at academic level and the focus being proof of
concept at in vitro and in vivo with very little or no attention
paid on scale-up, shelf-stability issues that hampers the
progress in this area of research [4]. While the surface
area-to-volume ratio makes nano the most preferred ap-
proach to address solubility-limited bioavailability issues,
this favourable quality plays a detrimental role in shelf-
stability [6]. Freeze-drying has been considered as a good
technique to improve the long-term stability of colloidal
nanoparticles [6]. Ideally the efforts should be in place to
develop a product suitable for shelf-storage, i.e. which does
not require ‘cold-chain’ transport/storage and which would
favourably impact on product cost. A variety of lyoprotec-
tants/cryoprotectants (e.g. sucrose, mannitol, trehalose) have

Recently, we have demonstrated that PLGA nanopar-
ticles improve peroral bioavailability of curcumin at least
9-fold compared to plain curcumin by the same route [7].
Bioactives such as curcumin and other antioxidants have
been used at very high doses [8, 9] for a variety of reasons
that includes but not limited to (a) poor peroral bioavail-
ability, (b) supplements/antioxidants label, (c) dose not
established, (d) lack of systematic pre-formulation studies,
and (v) focus on free radical pathways to tackle pathology,
where the pathway itself is not established to confidence etc.
Unfortunately, doses (therapeutic) for molecules like curcu-
min were not established as they are used as supplements
and numerous clinical trials are underway claiming the
safety of natural products such as curcumin which may not
be true always [10]. To establish dose of any compound, it is
very important to establish product profile so that a well-
defined dosage form can be administered. In general, mol-
ecules like curcumin are started at high doses based on the
literature reports and scale-up, and stability becomes all the
more important in chronic settings [11, 12]. The present
study reports scale-up and regulatory stability testing of
curcumin encapsulated PLGA nanoparticles.
Experimental details
Materials
PLGA (Resomer RG 50:50 H; intrinsic viscosity 0.32–
0.44 dl/g) was purchased from Boehringer Ingelheim (now
with Evonik Industries), Germany. Curcumin was purchased
from Indsaff (Punjab, India). Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) (mo-
lecular weight 30,000–70,000) was procured from Sigma-
Aldrich (USA). Organic solvents and mobile phases for
HPLC such as ethyl acetate (AR grade), acetone (AR grade),
glacial acetic acid, methanol (MeOH; HPLC grade) and
acetonitrile (ACN; HPLC grade) were purchased from Fish-
er Scientific UK Limited. Millipore water (in house supply)
was used for all experiments.
Lab-scale preparation of curcumin encapsulated PLGA
nanoparticles
Lab-scale preparation of curcumin encapsulated PLGA nano-
particles was carried out by following previously reported
method in our lab [7, 11, 12]. In brief, PLGA (50 mg) and
curcumin (7.5 mg) were dissolved in ethyl acetate (2.5 ml)
under stirring over a period of 1 h. PVA (50 mg) was dissolved
in water (5 ml) under stirring for 1 h. The PLGA+curcumin
solution (organic phase) was emulsified into PVA solution
(aqueous phase) over 30 min under stirring at 1,000 rpm.
The emulsion was homogenised (Polytron PT4000; Polytron
Kinematica, Switzerland; the diameter of the rotor was 9 mm
and the external diameter of static shaft was 11.8 mm that is
suitable for volumes up to 250 ml) at 15,000 rpm for 5 min.
The nano-emulsion was then transferred to 25 ml of water
under constant overnight stirring (1,000 rpm) to ensure com-
plete evaporation of ethyl acetate. The curcumin encapsulated
nanoparticles suspension was centrifuged at 14,000×g for
30 min, and the pellet was collected separating from free
curcumin and PVA. All the preparations have been performed
in triplicates.
Scale-up of curcumin encapsulated PLGA nanoparticles
(5-fold and 10-fold)
Scale-up production of curcumin encapsulated nanoparticles
was similar to that of the standard lab-scale preparation [7, 11,
12] with exceptions in homogenisation duration and the ma-
terial quantities used. In brief, the procedure is as follows:
PLGA and curcumin were dissolved in ethyl acetate under
stirring over a period of 1 h. PVAwas dissolved in water under
stirring for 1 h (recipe details in Table 1). The PLGA+curcu-
min solution (organic phase) was emulsified into PVA solu-
tion (aqueous phase) over 30 min under stirring at 1,000 rpm.
The emulsion was homogenised (Polytron PT4000; Polytron
Kinematica, Switzerland) at 15,000 rpm using for 5 or 15 or
30 min. The emulsion was then transferred to water (250 ml)
and stirred (1,000 rpm) overnight to ensure complete evapo-
ration of ethyl acetate. The curcumin encapsulated nanopar-
ticles suspension was centrifuged at 14,000×g for 30 min, and
the pellet was collected as above. All the preparations have
been performed in triplicates.

ume of water (30 ml) to which 5 % (w/v) sucrose was added
and vortexed until sucrose was dissolved completely. The
2 ml of nanoparticles suspension was placed in each glass
vial (5 ml) and frozen at −80 °C overnight. Freeze drying
process was carried out in a bench top freeze drier system

Characterisation of curcumin encapsulated nanoparticles
Particle size by DLS
The size of nanoparticles was determined by means of
dynamic light scattering (DLS) technique (Nano ZS,
Malvern Instruments, Malvern, UK), taking the average of
five measurements. The polydispersity index (PDI) which is
a dimensionless number indicating the width of the size
distribution, having a value between 0 and 1 (0 being for
mono-dispersed particles), was also obtained.
Particle size and topography by atomic force microscope
The topography of the nanoparticles was analysed using
atomic force microscope (AFM). Five microlitres of the
particle suspension (50 μl of the pellet further diluted) was
deposited onto a freshly cleaved mica surface (G250-2 Mica
sheets 1×1×0.006″; Agar Scientific Ltd, Essex, UK) and air
dried for 30 min before AFM imaging. The images were
obtained by scanning the mica surface in air under ambient
conditions using a Bruker MultiMode with NanoScope IIID
Controller Scanning Probe Microscope (Digital Instruments,
Santa Barbara, CA, USA; Bruker NanoScope software Ver-
sion 6.14r1) operated in tapping mode. The AFM measure-
ments were obtained using sharp soft silicon probes (FESP;
nominal length, lnom=225 μm; width, wnom=28 μm; tip
radius, Rnom=8 nm; resonant frequency, fnom=75 kHz;
spring constant, knom=3 N m−1; Bruker Instruments SAS,
Dourdan, France). AFM scans were taken at 512×512 pixels
resolution and produced topographic images of the samples
in which the brightness of features increases as a function of
height. Typical scanning parameters were as follows: tap-
ping frequency 73 kHz, integral and proportional gains 0.3
and 0.5, respectively, set point 0.5–0.8 Vand scanning speed
1.0–1.5 Hz. AFM images were collected from two different
samples and at random spot surface sampling (at least five
areas per sample).
X-ray diffraction
The X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of native curcumin,
sucrose, curcumin encapsulated nanoparticles and 6 month
stability samples were obtained using the X-ray diffractom-
eter (Bruker axs, D8 advance) with Cu source of radiation.
Measurements were performed at a voltage of 40 kV and
25 mA. The scanned angle was set from 2°≤3≥50° and the
scan rate was 3° min−1.
Percent entrapment efficiency
To the freeze-dried sample in the vial, 1 ml water was added
to re-suspend and then centrifuged at 14,000×g for 30 min.
The supernatant was discarded and to the pellet, 50 μl of
ethyl acetate and 950 μl of acetonitrile were added and
vortexed and centrifuged using a table top centrifuge at
13,000 rpm for further 10 min and supernatant was analysed
for drug content by using a validated high-performance
liquid chromatography (HPLC) method. Thermo-HPLC
system (Thermo, USA) consisting of photodiode array de-
tector and Thermo ODS 100 RP-18 (150×4.6 mm) end-
capped 5 μm column (Thermo, USA) was used. Acetoni-
trile, methanol and 2 % acetic acid were used as the mobile
phase with a flow rate of 1 ml/min with gradient flow
(Table 2). The injection volume was 50 μl and retention
time of curcumin was at 6 min. The detection wave-
length (λmax) for curcumin was 425 nm. The percent
entrapment efficiency was calculated using the

Results and discussion
Scale-up and characterisation of curcumin encapsulated
PLGA nanoparticles
It is widely accepted fact that the formulations prepared
in a laboratory environment often face significant chal-
lenges in production to large quantities; therefore, it is
important to move in an incremental fashion from lab to
pilot before taking it to commercial scale. This is due to
the fact that the instruments intended for the laboratory
setting differ from the ones required for large-scale
production. In case of nanoparticles, preparation homog-
enisation of the emulsion seems to play a major role in
controlling the particle size as well as poly-dispersity.
The conventional homogeniser in this case seems to be
good for moving from lab to pilot scale; however, this
may not work beyond 250 ml volumes; however, other
shafts that can work up to volumes of 2,500 ml are
available.
The feasibility of scale-up of curcumin encapsulated
PLGA nanoparticles has been successfully demonstrated.
These studies demonstrate that homogenisation time plays
a pivotal role in the scale-up (Table 3), while the rest of the
parameters stay similar to that of lab-scale preparations [7,
11, 12]. As the homogenisation (15,000 rpm) duration in-
creased from 5 to 30 min, the particle size decreased from
431 to 288 nm and the particles tend to be more uniform
0.25 to 0.15 (PDI) (Table 3; Fig. 1a). The curcumin encap-
sulated nanoparticles are spherical in shape with smooth
surfaces as depicted in the AFM images (Fig. 1b). The
particle size determined by AFM correlates to that of DLS
results; however, the sample sizes used to make the meas-
urements need to be kept in mind.
The lab-scale batches were compared for particle size
and entrapment efficiency against 5- and 10-fold
increases in batch sizes. The data presented in Table 4
indicate that the homogenisation duration of 30 min at
15,000 rpm works well for both 5- and 10-fold increase
in batch sizes, while 5 min is good enough for a lab-

Freeze drying
It is known that the freeze-drying process can result in
aggregation and affect the surface morphology and the
internal structure of the nanoparticles; to some extent, lyo-
protectants/cryoprotectants can avoid the damage caused
during this process. The use of sucrose as a cryoprotectant
for curcumin encapsulated nanoparticles was previously
reported by our group [7]. Though the previous process
led to easy to re-suspend intact fluffy cake with aesthetic
appearance, it resulted in increase in the particle size; there-
fore, we modified the process, where in the current process
we have frozen the samples at −80 °C and freeze-dried at
−50 °C and under vacuum (0.003 mBar) (also see the
supplement data). Figure 2 represents the particle size dis-
tribution obtained from the zeta sizer, and insert is an AFM
image of re-suspended freeze-dried curcumin encapsulated
nanoparticles. The freeze-drying process has not introduced
any significant changes (p>0.05) in the particle size or
entrapment efficiency when compared to those of fresh
samples (size 285±12 nm and entrapment efficiency
56.8±5.6 %). The AFM image further confirms that the
particles were spherical in shape with smooth surfaces after
freeze-drying process. These results once again suggest that
freeze drying can be effectively used to preserve the nano-
particles for longer duration without having their properties
changed from those of freshly prepared particles.

pharmacological interventions with curcumin need re-
peated dosing, rather in very high doses [13–15],
though nanoparticles is not a preferred approach for
high-dose compounds (certain degree of risk associated
with this statement, but true to large extent) by the rule
of thumb [4]; however, academic research is still on
with a hope that nanoparticles will lead to dose reduc-
tion [7, 11, 12, 16, 17]. Under these circumstances, it is
very important that the shelf-life is assessed so that
formulation is made and stocked for chronic preclinical
studies. Occasionally there are studies documented in
the literature claiming stability of the curcumin formu-
lations proven; however, the studies are not conducted
according to regulatory requirements [18]. In the current
study, curcumin encapsulated PLGA nanoparticles were
stable at room temperature as well as refrigerated
storage conditions without shrinkage and collapse at
the end of 6 months. No significant changes were
observed in the particle characteristics such as size
(Fig. 3a, c and insert images of Figs. 4 and 5) and
PDI (p>0.05), while the physical appearance remains
unchanged (Fig. 3b, d) during the studies period at both
the conditions. This indicates that the freeze drying was
successful and preparation was free of moisture render-
ing good stability during the test conditions. From the
AFM analysis, the size of nanoparticles, shape and
surface properties are unaltered when compared to those
of freshly prepared samples (Figs. 4 and 5). Figure 6 a,
b shows crystalline nature of native sucrose and curcu-
min. However, the freeze-dried curcumin encapsulated
nanoparticles did not show any characteristic peaks of
curcumin and sucrose suggesting that these formulations
are in amorphous state (Fig. 6 c). The curcumin encap-
sulated in the nanoparticles stayed amorphous after the
6-month stability testing period as indicated by the
XRD spectra (Fig. 6 d, e). The stability test conditions
did not affect the encapsulated curcumin, and the en-
trapment efficiency remained almost comparable to fresh
samples (p>0.05) (Fig. 7).
Conclusion
Curcumin encapsulated PLGA nanoparticles can be made in
large quantities in the laboratory that are stable on shelf-
storage. The homogenisation duration plays a critical role in
the scale-up process where a 50-mg batch size requires
5 min as opposed to 30 min for a 0.5-g batch size without
needing to increase the homogenisation speed (15,000 rpm).
The freeze-drying process adapted in the current study does
not affect the nanoparticle properties and is comparable to
fresh preparations. The nanoparticles further retained the
characteristics such as size, PDI, surface morphology, re-
suspendability, amorphous state and entrapment efficiency
during and after accelerated stability testing both at room
temperature as well as refrigerated conditions.
