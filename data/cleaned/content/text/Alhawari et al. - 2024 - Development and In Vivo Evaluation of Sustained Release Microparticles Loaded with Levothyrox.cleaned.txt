Microparticle Synthesis and Purification

The microparticles were produced using the emulsification-solvent evaporation method. To produce the internal phase of the emulsion, 50 mg of PLGA or PLA were dissolved in 3 mL of acetone. A one
mL solution of methanol containing 10 mg of sodium levothyroxine
was added dropwise to the acetonic polymer solution as it was stirring on a magnetic stirrer. The mixture was formed in a vial that was
closed tightly before and after the addition of the drug solution to
prevent solvent(s) evaporation. The external phase was produced by
dissolving an appropriate amount of lecithin in mineral oil to form a
0.5% w/v solution. Emulsification took place when the drug loaded
polymeric solution was added to 10 mL of the external phase in a
centrifuge tube and then emulsified by simple shaking for 1 min.
Once emulsification had taken place, the emulsion was left stirring
on a magnetic stirrer operating at 400 rpm overnight to allow for
complete solvent(s) evaporation. Sodium levothyroxine is light sensitive; therefore, all solutions were produced/ kept in amber glass vials
or beakers covered with aluminum foil.

The next day, 10 mL of n-hexane were added to each formula in

another 10 mL of fresh n-hexane. The mixture was centrifuged again

More recently, the work of several groups has been aimed at sustaining levothyroxine release through its loading into 3-(aminopropyl) triethoxysilane modified porous silicon nanoparticles,41 or
chitosan nanoparticles.42 However, the use of biodegradable, biocompatible, and FDA approved polymers such as PLGA and PLA is more
realistic and closer to clinical translation.

For these reasons, formulating levothyroxine within a polyesterbased delivery system capable of circumventing the absorptive disadvantages of the oral route and capable of extending release, thereby
diminishing the need for patient adherence to daily dosing, would be
greatly advantageous. In this work, the ability of polyester polymers
to encapsulate levothyroxine within microspheres and then control
its release after parenteral administration in animal models will be
investigated.

The encapsulation efficiency and drug loading were determined
directly by dissolving the produced microparticle formulations and
analyzing the amount of drug released. To do so, each produced formulation was initially dissolved in 1 mL of acetone in a tightly closed

place. The next day, all mixtures were centrifuged at 4000 rpm for
10 min (Centurion Scientific Ltd.,UK), and the supernatant was collected for analysis using high performance liquid chromatography

Determination of Microparticle in Vivo Release

The in vivo drug release from PLGA microparticles was determined by measuring serum total thyroxine (T4) levels prior to and
after the injection of loaded microparticles into rats. PLGA microparticles were chosen as a representative formulation. Adult female Rattus albus were obtained from the animal house unit at the University
of Jordan. All animals were kept in a well-ventilated room at
22 SS 2 degC with a 12 h light-dark cycle. They were also provided with
a standard rate diet and ad libitum water. The rats, weighing 130
-140 g, were randomly divided into 2 groups (n = 4), and treated
with either (i) drug-free PLGA microparticles (negative control), or
(ii) drug-loaded PLGA microparticles. The drug-loaded microparticles
had a drug dose of around 0.4 mg levothyroxine. A specific amount of

introduced in the retro-orbital venous sinus of the rat's eye directed
towards the opposite ear.45 The collected blood samples were then
placed in Eppendorf tubes and kept refrigerated for serum collection.

Microparticle Drug Loading and Encapsulation Efficiency

Microparticle in Vitro Release

The ability of PLGA and PLA of relatively similar molecular weights
to encapsulate levothyroxine sodium within microspheres was investigated. Drug loading and encapsulation efficiency results can be seen
in Fig. 2.

The release of levothyroxine sodium from loaded PLGA and PLA

polymers exhibited an initial phase of rapid drug release. Four hours
into the experiment, PLGA and PLA microspheres had released almost
identical amounts of the loaded drug (i.e., 27.6 % and 27.7 %, respectively). Thereafter, the release patterns diverge, and a deceleration of
the release rate is seen with PLA microspheres. On day two, 87.7% of
the drug had been released from PLGA microspheres as compared to
66.0% from PLA microspheres. Release was terminated on day four
when the cumulative drug released from PLGA microspheres was
96.2 %, while that from PLA microspheres was 82.0 %. This demonstrates the ability of PLA to control the rate of drug release over a longer period of time. Additionally, such a pattern of biphasic release is
commonly seen with polyester-based microparticles.52

Both PLGA and PLA microspheres were prepared using the same
method and produced microparticles of a very similar size and polydispersity. Their drug loading and encapsulation efficiencies were,
however, significantly different. Microspheres prepared using PLGA
had a drug loading of 4.4 SS 0.2 % and an encapsulation efficiency of
26.1 SS 1.5 %. Meanwhile, those prepared using PLA had a drug loading and encapsulation efficiency of 6.1 SS 0.2 % and 36.8 SS 1.2 %,
respectively.

Accordingly, results demonstrate that with an increase in polymeric hydrophobicity, a significant (p value = 0.0023) increase in
drug loading and encapsulation can be obtained. The more hydrophobic nature of the PLA is due to the presence of additional methyl
groups.12,51 Such an increase in hydrophobicity would provide a barrier against drug outflux upon implant formation. This barrier
reduces drug loss and thereby increases drug loading and encapsulation efficiency.

Two main mechanisms govern drug release from polyester-based
drug delivery systems: diffusion and degradation/erosion.53 While
initially the rate of drug release is said to be mainly controlled by diffusion of the drug through water filled pores; during the later stages,
the rate is determined by polymeric degradation/erosion.53

The overlapping initial phase of rapid release exhibited by microspheres of both polymers suggests that a similar large proportion of
the drug was present at or near the surface of the microspheres. The
dissolution of the drug at the surface, and the diffusion of drug molecules near the surface of the microspheres would take place promptly
upon microparticle hydration or in vivo administration.18

Microparticle in Vivo Release

The in vivo release behavior of PLGA microparticles was investigated using a rat model by measuring serum total T4 levels at different time points. Total T4 represents both the free and bound forms of
T4. The latter form, which is bound to thyroid binding globulin, is not
readily available for uptake by cells like the free form and thus represents a storage pool with a longer half-life.54

## Conclusion
Both PLGA and PLA microspheres were prepared using the same
emulsification-solvent evaporation method and produced microparticles of a very similar size, polydispersity, and zeta potentials. However, the more hydrophobic PLA microparticles had a significantly
higher drug loading and encapsulation efficiency and had the ability
to control drug release for a longer period of time. Both in vitro and in
vivo release behaviors displayed a biphasic pattern with an initial
phase of rapid release followed by a later phase of slower release.
