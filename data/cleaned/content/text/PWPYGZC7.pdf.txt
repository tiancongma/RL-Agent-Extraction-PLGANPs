Abstract
Nanocarrier systems are currently being developed for peptide, protein and gene delivery to
protect them in the blood circulation and in the gastrointestinal tract. Polylactic acid (PLA) and
poly(lactic-co-glycolic) acid (PLGA) nanoparticles loaded with a new antimicrobial GIBIM-
P5S9K peptide were obtained by the double emulsion solvent extraction/evaporation method.
PLA- and PLGA-NPs were spherical with sizes between 300 and 400 nm for PLA and 200 and
300 nm for PLGA and <0.3 polydispersity index as determined by dynamic light scattering and
scanning electron microscopy), having the zeta potential of >20 mV. The peptide-loading
efﬁciency of PLA-NP and PLGA-NPs was 75% and 55%, respectively. PLA- and PLGA-NPs
released around 50% of this peptide over 8 h. In 10% human sera the size of peptide loaded
PLA- and PLGA-NPs increased between 25.2% and 39.3%, the PDI changed from 3.2 to 5.1 and
the surface charge from −7.15 to 14.6 mV. Both peptide loaded PLA- and PLGA-NPs at 0.5 μM
peptide concentration inhibited the growth of Escherichia coli O157:H7 (E. coli O157:H7),
methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) and Pseudomonas. aeruginosa (P.
aeruginosa). In contrast, free peptide inhibited at 10 μM but did not inhibit at 0.5 and 1 μM.
These PLA- and PLGA-NPs presented <10% hemolysis indicating that they are
hemocompatible and promising for delivery and protection system of GIBIM-P5S9K peptide.

1. Introduction
The worldwide infectious diseases caused by resistant
microorganisms are turning in pathologies of difﬁcult ther-
apeutic treatment. A particularly serious public health pro-
blem is the increasing prevalence of antibiotic resistant
bacterial infections [1, 2]. The emergence of antibiotic resis-
tant bacteria is associated with inappropriate antibiotic use,
generating a selective pressure leading to the appearance of
antibiotic resistance genes[3, 4]. Consequently, the devel-
opment of novel antimicrobial compounds, acting on a broad
variety of targets, is required [5]. Antimicrobial peptides
(AMPs) have recently emerged as one of these compounds
due to their multiple mechanisms of action, broad activity
spectrum,
relatively
low
bacterial
resistance
and
great
potential for clinical use [6, 7].
The AMPs are effector molecules of the innate immune
system often isolated from plants, animals and bacteria [8, 9].
AMPs are usually amphipathic cationic peptides, around 30
amino acids in length (sizes lower than 10 kDa), net charge
between +2 and +9 at physiological pH because the high
lysine and arginine content and characteristical secondary
structures [10–12]. These peptide properties are mainly
responsible of the interaction with the cell membrane of
pathogenic bacteria [13–15]. However, the AMP therapeutic
use is restricted because their susceptibility to inactivation and
degradation in human ﬂuids due to the acidic stomach pH, the
enzyme proteolytic action or the high ionic strength [16].
Fortunately, the AMP stability against extreme conditions
faced in the human body can be improved by using different
techniques, for example AMP loading into nanoparticles
(NPs). In fact, peptides and proteins loaded in NPs display
low degradation and toxicity, versatility for oral administra-
tion, site-speciﬁc release, biocompatibility with tissues and
cells and improved bioavailability [17–21].
Polymeric NPs are solid colloids of polymeric assemblies
prepared from synthetic and natural polymers. They are often
used as drug carriers [22], that can be classiﬁed as: (1)
nanospheres when the drug is evenly dispersed throughout the
polymer matrix (typically as a solid solution), or (2) nano-
capsules when the drug is in the core, either in aqueous or oily
solution [23]. Some of the techniques developed to prepare
NPs loaded with peptides and proteins are emulsion/solvent
evaporation, double emulsion, solvent spread, coacervation,
nanoprecipitation, ionotropic gelation and salt precipitation
[24–28].
NPs from natural polymers have been used for biome-
dical applications despite their limitations such as purity
variations and the frequent required crosslinking to increase
their stability [22]. In particular, NPs derived from both
polylactic acid (PLA) or the copolymer of lactic and glycolic
acid (PLGA) are broadly used in drug controlled release
because they are biodegradable, biocompatible and have been
Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved for parenteral
route drug delivering [23, 29, 30]. Moreover, the PLGA-NPs
can be modiﬁed to control the structure, encapsulation degree,
possible applications, the administration route, the drug
release and degradation rate [25, 31].
In this work, PLA- and PLGA-NPs loaded with the
GIBIM-P5S9K peptide were prepared by the double emul-
sion-solvent evaporation technique; characterized by dynamic
light scattering (DLS), zeta potential and SEM. The in vitro
antimicrobial activity of free peptide and peptide-loaded NPs
against MRSA, E. coli O157: H7 and P. aeruginosa was
evaluated and compared. These NPs were non-toxic and
stable (size and surface charge) in human sera.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Materials
L-amino acids protected at N-terminal with Fmoc group were
purchased from IRIS Biotech; GmbH, TBTU, HBTU, HoAT,
DCC and DIPEA was obtained from Alfa Aesar, and Rink
amide 4MBHA resin from Merck Millipore.
PLA (MW 85–160 KDa) and PLGA (50:50, MW
38–54 KDa), Poloxamer 407 (MW 12.5 KDa), ethyl acetate
and polyethylenimine (PEI, 2 KDa) were obtained from
Sigma-Aldrich. Acetonitrile (ACN) and methanol (MetOH)
HPLC grade (J.T BAKER), dichloromethane (DCM) and
growth media Mueller Hinton (MH) from Merck, the brain
heart infusion (BHI) from OXOID. E. coli O157:H7, MRSA
and P. aeruginosa strains were donated by the School of
Microbiology at Pontiﬁcia Universidad Javeriana, which is a
certiﬁed institution belonging to the World Federation of
Culture Collection. These three strains were kept at −70 °C in
10% glycerol. E. coli O157:H7 and P. aeruginosa were
grown in Luria-Bertani (LB) and MRSA in MH at 37 °C.
2.2. Peptide synthesis and characterization
Peptide GIBIM-P5S9K was synthesized via multiple solid
phase peptide synthesis [32], using the tea-bag procedure
reported by Houghten [33], using 0.63 substituted rink amide
4MBHA resin (100–200 mesh; loading: 0.63 mmol g−1) and
Fmoc amino acids [34]. These peptides were cleaved by
treatment with triﬂuoroacetic acid (TFA)/triisopropylsilan
(TIS)/ethanedithiol/H2O (92.5/2.5/2.5:2.5) for 2 h and then
precipitated with cold diethyl ether [35]. The peptides were
desalted by gel exclusion chromatography using G-10 col-
umns (Amersham, USA).
Peptides were puriﬁed by reverse phase-high perfor-
mance liquid chromatography (RP-HPLC) (Jasco Corpora-
tion, Tokyo, Japan) with the Vydac C-18 preparative column,
mobile phase of buffer A: H2O with TFA 0.1% (v/v) and

using the gradient 5%–70% of B during 30 min at 1 ml min−1

2.3. Circular dichroism
CD spectra between 190 and 260 nm of designed and analog
peptides were carried out at 25 °C in a 1 mm path length
cuvette in a CD Spectrometer (J-815 Jasco Corporation,
Japan) using a 0.2 mM peptide solution dissolved in 50 mM
sodium phosphate buffer, (pH 7.4)/30% (v/v) 2,2,2-tri-
ﬂuoroetanol [33]. Each spectrum was obtained as an average
of three scans taken at 20 nm min−1 with a spectral band of
1 nm [38].
2.4. Preparation of polymeric NPs
NPs were prepared using double emulsion solvent evapora-
tion method as reported by Cohen-Sela et al with some
modiﬁcations [39]; 1 ml of (1 mg ml−1) GIBIM-P5S9K pep-
tide solution (W1 phase) was dispersed in 4 ml of DCM
containing 10 mg PLA or 4 ml of ethyl acetate containing
10 mg PLGA (O phase). The W1 and O phases were vigor-
ously mixed for 30 s to form a simple W1/O emulsion using a
homogenizer-disperser (IKA Ultra-turrax T-18) at maximum
speed (15 500 rpm). This ﬁrst emulsion was then dispersed in
10 ml 1% Poloxamer 407 surfactant solution (W2 phase). The
suspension was then homogenized at 15 500 rpm during 30 s
to obtain W1/O/W2 double emulsion. Finally, DCM and
ethyl acetate was removed in a conventional rotary eva-
porator. Thus, to guarantee NPs with positive surface charges
that facilitate the electrostatic interaction with the bacterial
membrane we have added polyethylenimine. This suspension
was spinned at 22 000 g to obtain the pellet that was washed
and lyophilized. The dried product was resuspended in aqu-
eous medium.
2.5. Physicochemical properties of NPs
The size and charge of NPs was measured by DLS and zeta
(Z) potential in Zetasizer Nano Series Nano-zs90 equipped
with 633 nm laser at 25 °C; all measurements were performed
by triplicate and the polydispersity index was lower than 0.3.
SEM microscopy in STEM and conventional mode was used
to determine morphology and conﬁrm sizes of gold coated
NPs in FEI Quanta 650 microscope at Microscopy Labora-
tory, Guatiguará Technology Park, Universidad Industrial de
Santander, Colombia [40].

indirect quantiﬁcation method, measuring the peptide con-
centration that remained in solution after separation from NPs

2.7. In vitro release of peptide from NPs
GIBIM-P5S9K-NPs (2 ml suspension) were diluted in PBS
(20 ml) 50 mM, pH=7.4 and incubated at 37 °C under
magnetic stirring (180 rpm). This buffer was used because is
one of the buffers that resembles physiological conditions in
which the in vitro and in vivo bacteria grow; particularly its
pH and its ionic strength. At predetermined time points, a
sample of the NP suspension (0.2 ml) was withdrawn, cen-
trifuged at 22 000 g and the amount of GIBIM-P5S9K in the
supernatant was quantiﬁed at 220 nm in a Nanodrop Thermo
Scientiﬁc [39]. The volume of the assay was kept constant by
adding 0.2 ml of PBS pH 7.4, after every sample removing.
The release assays were carried out in triplicate.
2.8. Determination of minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC)
and minimum bactericidal concentration (MBC)
Bactericidal activity was evaluated by broth microdilution
method [42, 43]. Initially, the preinoculum of E. coli O157:
H7, P. aeruginosa in LB and MRSA in MH were grown at
37 °C, shaking at 200 rpm during 12–14 h; then the culture of
each strain was set at 0.5 in McFarland scale (108 CFU ml−1);
100 μl aliquots of these cell suspensions were mixed with
100 μl NPs solution at different peptide concentrations in a 96
well microplate and incubated at 37 °C in an orbital shaker
(200 rpm for 8 h). The growth kinetics of each microorganism
was determined by triplicate measuring absorbance at 595 nm
in Elisa lector (Bio-Rad, iMark). The controls were (1) culture
media, (2) culture media plus bacteria, (3) culture media,
bacteria and Oﬂoxacina and (4) culture media, bacteria and
non-loaded NPs. MIC at 50% (MIC50) was the lowest con-
centration of nanocomposite inhibiting 50% of the bacterial
growth of these bacterial strains. After incubation for 8 h,
100 μl of these bacterial cultures were poured in 900 μl of
BHI, incubated at 37 °C for 24 h; then 10 μl of these cultures
were seeded over BHI-agar petri dishes, incubated for 1 day at
37 °C and the appearance of colonies were determined. The
MBC was the lowest nanoparticle concentration producing
>99.9% reduction of colony-forming units (CFU) [44].
2.9. Serum stability assay
To evaluate the NP stability near to physiological conditions,
3.0 mg freeze-dried NPs were resuspended in 1.5 ml of 10%
v/v human serum (HS) in PBS. The suspensions were incu-
bated at 37 °C, samples of this suspension were analyzed by
NP size and zeta potential at ﬁxed time intervals (0, 60, 120,
and 240 min) to determine aggregations, growth and changes
in the zeta potential of these NPs produced by the serum [45].

2.10. Peptide loaded NP hemolytic activity on human
erythrocytes
Peptide hemolytic activity was evaluated on human ery-
throcytes; 1 ml of human blood obtained by venipuncture on
citrate tubes was spun at 1000×g per 10 min at 4 °C, the
plasma and the white blood cells were discarded and the
erythrocytes were washed four times with isotonic PBS.
Aliquots of 100 μl of 2×107 erythrocytes ml−1 suspension
in PBS were transferred to 1.5 ml tubes and mix with different
free peptide or peptide loaded NP, incubated at 37 °C for 4 h.
Then the erythrocyte suspension was spun at 13 000 ×g for
5 min and 80 μl supernatant was transferred to a 96 well plate
to measure hemoglobin released at 550 nm in a plate reader.
The percentage of hemolysis was calculated using the fol-
lowing equation:
%hemolysis=(As−A0)/(A100−A0)×
100, where As is the absorbance of the sample, A100 is the
absorbance of the completely lysed erythrocytes in 0.1%
Triton X-100 and A0 is the absorbance in the complete
absence of haemolysi [46].
2.11. Statistical analysis
Normalized data were expressed as the mean ±SEM and
analyzed using the Statistica software (StatSoft Inc., Tulsa,
OK, USA). The analysis of variance (one-way ANOVA) and
Student’s t-test comparisons were used to assess statistical
signiﬁcance at p<0.05.
3. Results and discussion
3.1. Synthesis and characterization of GIBIM-P5S9K loaded
polymeric NPs
Amino acid sequences of candidate antibacterial peptides
were generated by genetic algorithm optimization strategy.
The algorithm generated new peptide sequences with high
probability to be antibacterial using the charge, isoelectric
point, hydrophobicity and instability index as physicochem-
ical descriptors and the CAMP database of antimicrobial
peptide sequences as the parents to generate the new peptide
sequences. GIBIM-P5 was one of the sequences produced by
this algorithm; GIBIM-P5S9K was generated as analog of this
peptide replacing Ser in the position 9 by Lys peptide
(table 1). In theory, this peptide displayed >95% probability
to be antimicrobial, a low instability index and positive
charge according to the CAMP database (http://www.camp.
bicnirrh.res.in/) and Protparam (http://web.expasy.org/cgi-
bin/compute_pi/pi_tool) (table 1). These peptides were solid
phase synthesized and puriﬁed by RP-HPLC; both presented
antimicrobial activity but GIBIM-P5S9K peptide, which
presented a molecular mass of 1804 KDa by MALDI-TOF
and an alpha helix in its secondary structure as determined by
circular dichroism, presented higher antibacterial activity than
GIBIM-P5 (manuscript sent to publication). For this reason
and taking into account that drug formulations at nanoscale
allow a more effective drug interaction with bacterial mem-
branes and enhance the drug bioavailability at infection sites
[47, 48] GIBIM-P5S9K was chosen to be loaded in NPs. The
NP-systems chosen as carriers of GIBIM-P5S9K were PLA-
and PLGA-NPs; considering the properties of GIBIM-P5S9K,
PLA and PLGA the NP preparation was performed with the
double emulsion-solvent evaporation method. Indeed, this
technique has been considered one of the most suitable for the
encapsulation of water-soluble molecules such as peptides
and proteins [49]. The PLA- and PLGA-NPs obtained in this
work were between 350 and 250 nm in diameter with poly-
dispersity index <0.3 as determined by DLS and SEM
(ﬁgure 1). The NPs covered with a thin gold layer were
spherical with diameters between 300–400 nm for PLA and
200–300 nm for PLGA, displaying homogeneous dispersion
as determined by SEM. The particle size is one of the most
important characteristics because is related to stability, cel-
lular uptake, release proﬁle, degradation rate and biodis-
tribution [54, 55]. These results indicated that the nanoparticle
population was homogeneous with a monomodal size dis-
tribution (ﬁgures 1(a) and 2(a)); and were similar to peptide
loaded PLA- and PLGA-NPs obtained in previous reports
using similar methodologies but different peptides. This
indicates that this methodology can be used with peptides
with very different physicochemical characteristics. In fact
peptide loaded PLGA- and PLA-NPs have been synthesized
having sizes between 100 and 419 nm, polydispersity index
between 0.135 and 0.180, zeta potential of −21.0±2.5 mV
and encapsulation efﬁciency of 70.2%±3.3% [50–53].
NPs were stabilized using the non-ionic surfactant
Poloxamer 407 (a mix of ethylene oxide and propylene oxide
blocks) and polyethylenimine (PEI). Poloxamer 407 interacts
with both the NP surface and the aqueous media through the
hydrophobic PP and hydrophilic ‘heads’, respectively but
decreases the NP zeta potential [56]. On the other hand, PEI
increases the positive surface charge of PLA- and PLGA-NPs
through its high density of primary amino groups, enhancing
the probability of interaction with negatively charged bac-
terial membrane [57]. In fact the zeta potential was +22 and

Peptide loaded PLA- or PLGA-NPs presented signiﬁcant
growth inhibition of E. coli O157:H7, MRSA and P. aeru-
ginosa, even at 0.5 μM peptide concentration in a dose
dependent manner (table 3). There was no signiﬁcant differ-
ences in bacterial growth inhibition between peptide loaded
PLA- and PLGA-NPs, except that against E. coli O157:H7
the activity of PLA-NPs (MIC50<0.5 μM) was higher than
PLGA-NPs (MIC50 between 1 and 10 μM). In contrast, free
peptides did not inhibit at 0.5 and 1 μM, but at 10 μM showed
almost complete bacterial growth inhibition (ﬁgures 2–4).
This higher antibacterial activity of peptide loaded PLA- or
PLGA-NPs than free peptide could involve an electrostatic
interaction between the NP-positive surface charges (+22 and
+30 mV) and the negative surface charges of both gram-
negative and gram-positive membrane [59, 60]. This inter-
action increases the probability of having a high local peptide
concentration on bacterial surface, resulting in higher anti-
bacterial activity [61–64]. This interaction could be mediated
by teichoic acid, lipoteichoic or lipopolysaccharides and
phospholipids on the bacterial surface. Non-loaded PLA- and
PLGA-NPs showed no signiﬁcant growth inhibition of the
three bacterial strains used in the study.
P. aeruginosa was more susceptible to peptide loaded
PLA-NPs and PLGA-NPs than the other two strains. We
suggest that P. aeruginosa repels the negative charge of PLA
and PGLA that could be exposed during the contact with the
NPs in lesser intensity than E. coli and MRSA taking into
account that P. aeruginosa (−1.12(μm s−1)/(V cm−1)), is
less electronegative than MRSA (−2(μm s−1)/(V cm−1)),
and E. coli (above −3(μm s−1)/(V cm−1)); moreover, E. coli
and P. aeruginosa cells are moderately hydrophilic (θ=34
and θ=43, respectively) and MRSA cells exhibited more
hydrophobic characteristics (θ=72) [65].

3.4. PLA- or PLGA-NP stability in human sera
The NP stability in serum was evaluated because is critical for
the intravenously delivered of the nanocompound. Initially
the sizes of peptide loaded PLA- or PLGA-NPs in 10% HS
were 65% of the size in aqueous solution and started to lin-
early increase along incubation time (r square >0.96); at
240 min reach 82% for PLGA-NPs and 92% for PLA-NPs of
the size in aqueous solution (table 4). The PDI was between
3.2 and 5.1, indicating a homogenous NP population during
the incubation time in serum. The surface charge of the
PLGA- and PLA-NPs in 10% HSwas negative, increasing
along the incubation time, especially for PLA-NPs increasing
50% during 240 min of incubation in serum. These results
suggest interactions between NPs and serum molecules that
could be electrostatic interactions between negatively charged
protein domains and positive NP-charges. One of these serum
molecules could be human serum albumin, which is the most
abundant serum protein and has positive and negative charged
domains. Other possibilities are an osmotic effect exerted by
the salts present in the buffer and HS and peptide dissolution
on
the
surface
and
NP-shrinking
due
to
peptide
release [45, 67].
3.5. Hemolytic activity of peptide loaded PLA- or PLGA-NPs
Human erythrocytes were incubated with peptide loaded or
non-loaded PLA- and PLGA-NPs at two concentrations
(0.5–1% w/v); the hemolysis percentages caused by these
NPs were below 10% (ﬁgure 6). Additionally, the peptide
loaded NP hemolytic activity was lower than the free peptide
(25% hemolysis). Considering that the peptide target is the
membrane structure, this result suggests a speciﬁc peptide
effect on bacterial membrane. These peptide loaded NPs
could be considered hemocompatible substances which could
potentially be administered parenterally because a compound
causing <10% hemolysis is considered non-hemolytic or
hemocompatible and causing >25% hemolysis is classiﬁed as
hemolytic and [53, 68].
4. Conclusion
GIBIM-P5S9K peptide has been successfully loaded in PLA
and PLGA NPs via double-emulsion solvent evaporation. In
vitro, around 50% of this peptide was released from these NPs
during the ﬁrst 8 h. Moreover, these NPs were stable in
human sera. These peptide loaded NPs presented higher
antibacterial activity than the free peptide against E. coli,
MRSA and P. aeruginosa. These synthesized NPs are a
promising alternative method for GIBIM-P5S9K peptide
delivery and even more important as a protection system of
enzymatic peptide degradation.
