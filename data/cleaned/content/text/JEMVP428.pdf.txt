Abstract
Multidrug resistance (MDR) is a key determinant for hepatocellular
carcinoma chemotherapy failure. P-glycoprotein is one of the main
causes of MDR by causing drug efflux in tumor cells. In order to solve
this thorny problem, we prepared a sorafenib-loaded polylactic acid-
glycolic acid (PLGA) - d-α-tocopheryl polyethylene glycol 1000 succinate
(TPGS) nanoparticles (SPTNs). SPTNs were successfully synthesized
through an ultrasonic emulsion solvent evaporation method with a
favourable encapsulation efficiency of 90.35%. SPTNs were almost
spherical in shape with uniform particle size (215.70 ± 0.36 nm), narrow
polydispersity index (0.27 ± 0.02) and negative surface charge (−26.01 ±
0.65 mV). In the cellular uptake assay, the intracellular coumarin-6 (C6)
fluorescence of TPGS component-based PLGA nanoparticles (C6-PTNs)
was 1.63-fold higher relative to that of PVA component-based PLGA
nanoparticles (C6-PVNs). The half-maximal inhibitory concentration and
apoptosis ratio of SPTNs against HepG2/MDR cells were 3.90 μM and
75.62%, respectively, which were notably higher than free SF and
sorafenib-PLGA-PVA nanoparticles (SPVNs). The anti-drug efflux
activities of SPTNs were assessed by the intracellular trafficking assay
using verapamil as a P-gp inhibitor. SPTNs could effectively inhibit the

drug efflux in tumor cells detected by flow cytometry, and suppressed
relative MDR1 gene as well as P-glycoprotein expression in tumor cells.
Attributed to the MDR reversion effect of SPTNs, the in vivo antitumor
efficacy experiment showed that SPTNs significantly inhibited the tumor
growth of HepG2/MDR xenograft-bearing nude mice, and obviously
reduced the toxicity against liver and kidney compared with SF
treatment. In summary, SPTNs, as highly efficient and safe antitumor
nano delivery systems, showed promising potential for hepatocellular
carcinoma therapy through reversing P-glycoprotein-mediated MDR.

INTRODUCTION
Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) is a malignant tumor that severely
affects human health. The morbidity and mortality associated with HCC
are increasing rapidly. Globally, approximately 500,000 new patients are
diagnosed with HCC every year (1, 2). Sorafenib (SF) is multi-target and
multi-kinase inhibitor of first-line therapy drug for advanced HCC (3, 4).
However, SF still faces some problems in clinical application of HCC.
Among them, multidrug resistance (MDR) represents an important
reason for invalid of many chemotherapeutic agents in HCC therapy
(5,6,7). MDR forces patients with SF treatment to discontinue
chemotherapy early due to the desensitization effect of anticancer drugs
to cancer cells (8,9,10).
Current researches have confirmed that the mechanisms of MDR mainly
included enhancing efflux transporters and reducing drug influx,
lysosome entrapment and anti-apoptotic pathways (11). P-Glycoprotein
(P-gp), as an overexpressed transporter on the tumor cell membrane,
such as in cervical and liver cancer, is closely related to the MDR effect
(12,13,14). P-gp can expel its bound substances or drugs from the cell at
a reverse concentration utilizing the energy produced by ATP hydrolysis
resulting in MDR. Recently, studies demonstrated that the combination of
chemotherapy drugs and P-gp inhibitors (verapamil, cyclosporine A, et
al.) can significantly enhance the antitumor efficacy (15, 16).
d-α-Tocopheryl polyethylene glycol 1000 succinate (TPGS) is an
amphiphilic compound of the P-gp inhibitor with excellent biocompatibility

(17, 18). TPGS affects the fluidity of the cell membrane by specific
binding to the P-gp, which disorders the conformation of P-gp and
decreases drug outflow, thereby reversing MDR and increasing the
accumulation of drugs in the tumor site (19, 20). Recent researches have
confirmed that TPGS can significantly enhance the tumor cellular uptake
of docetaxel-loaded nanoparticles in breast cancer treatment (21,22,23).
Therefore, the amphiphilic chemical structure of TPGS can be used as a
“P-gp inhibitor” active material to prepare nano-carriers.
In order to overcome the MDR of SF and improve the antitumor efficacy
of HCC, we developed a nano delivery system based on poly (lactide-
glycolic acid copolymer) (PLGA)-TPGS matrix for SF delivery (SPTNs).
On the one hand, TPGS as a surfactant was used in the preparation of
PLGA nanoparticles to improve the encapsulation efficiency of SF. On
the other hand, TPGS can improve the tumor cellular uptake and
aggregation of antitumor drugs as P-gp inhibitor by reversing MDR.
Intravenously administrated SPTNs can spontaneously accumulate at
the tumor site by EPR effect. Then, the released TPGS suppressed the
expression of the MDR1 gene and P-gp, which facilitated the diffusion of
SF into the tumor cells. This research focused on whether the
incorporation of TPGS into PLGA nanoparticles could improve the
antitumor efficacy against MDR in vitro.
MATERIALS AND METHODS
Materials

SF (> 99.9% purity) and TPGS were obtained from Shanghai Yuanye
Biomaterial Co., Ltd (Shanghai, China). PLGA (8.8–11.7 kDa) was
purchased from Daigang Biomaterial Co., Ltd (Jinan, China). 3-(4,5-
Dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5 diphenyltetrazolium bromide (MTT) was
provided by Solarbio Life Sciences Co., Ltd (Beijing, China). TRIzol
reagent (Roswell Park Memorial Institute 1640) was obtained from
Thermo Fisher Scientific (Waltham, MA, USA). Dimethyl sulfoxide, DAPI,
sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS), antifade reagents, penicillin, streptomycin,
Annexin V-FITC apoptosis detection kit and BCA protein assay kit were
supplied by Beyotime Biotechnology Co., Ltd (Shanghai, China). Fetal
bovine serum (FBS) was obtained from Sigma (St. Louis, MO, USA).
Coumarin-6 (C6) was obtained from Baoman Biotechnology Co., Ltd
(Shanghai, China). The Quanti Nova SYBR green RT-PCR kit was
purchased from Qiagen (Dusseldorf, Germany).
Cell Lines and Animals
HepG2/MDR cells (a hepatoblastoma cell line) were provided by the
Institute of Pharmacology, Anhui Medical University. Cells were cultured
in RPMI 1640 medium containing 10% (v/v) FBS and 50 μg/mL of
penicillin/streptomycin at 37°C in a humidified incubator under 5% CO2
(Thermo 3111, Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA).
Babl/c mice (each weighing 20 ± 2 g) were purchased from the
Experimental Animal Centre of Anhui Medical University (Hefei, China).
These mice were adaptively fed for at least 5 days and maintained under
a 12-h light/dark cycle prior to the experiment. All animal protocols were

approved by the Animal Ethics Committee of Anhui University of Chinese
Medicine (Approval No. KC: 027-15 and KC: 027-16) and conducted in
accordance with the NIH principles of laboratory animal care guidelines.
Preparation of SPTNs
SPTNs were prepared through an ultrasonic emulsion solvent
evaporation method (24). The encapsulation rate as index, single-factor
experiment and Box-Behnken experimental design (BBD) were designed
to screen out the optimal prescription. For preparation of SPTNs, SF (3.2
mg) was dissolved in 8 mL of dimethyl sulfoxide and mixed with 2 mL
acetone containing PLGA (37.0 mg). Then, probe sonication (90 W, 5
min) was performed to obtain the primary emulsion of the compound.
Subsequently, the primary emulsion was slowly poured into TPGS (1.24
mg) mixed with deionized water (55.6 mL) at a flow rate of 0.5 mL/min.
The resultant dispersion was simultaneously sonicated at 200 W for 5
min in a pulse mode to obtain O/W emulsion. At room temperature, the
obtain O/W emulsion was used a rotary evaporator to evaporate organic
solvent for 4 h to obtain SPTNs. The excess TPGS was removed
through ultracentrifugation at 10,000 rpm for 10 min at 4°C. PVA was
used to replace TPGS to obtain SPVNs. Blank nanoparticles were
prepared with the similar method without SF (PTNs). Coumarin-6 (C6)
was used to replace SF to prepare C6-PTNs and C6-PVNs.
Characterization of SPTNs
The morphology of SPTNs was observed by transmission electron
microscopy (TEM, JEOL-2010, Japan Electron Optics Laboratory Co.,

Ltd., Tokyo, Japan). The particle size, polydispersity index (PDI) and
zeta potential of the SPTNs were measured via a Malvern Zetasizer
Nano 90 system (Malvern Instruments Ltd., UK). The phase transition
temperature of the lipid complex membranes was measured by
differential scanning calorimetry (DSC, Q200, TA Instruments, USA). The
HPLC was used to detect the drug encapsulation efficiency (EE). Briefly,
SPTN suspension was prepared through high-speed centrifugation at
12,000 rpm for 20 min. Free drug was then measured through high-
performance liquid chromatography (HPLC; Agilent Technologies 1200
Series, Santa Clara, CA) coupled with a Kromasil C18 column (250mm ×
4.6 mm, 5 μm) at a flow rate of 1.0 mL/min and the absorption
wavelength of 266 nm. In addition, the total drug content was determined
using SPTNs dissolved in acetonitrile. The mobile phase was a mixture
of acetonitrile:water (0.03% triethylamine, 63:37, v/v) and the injection
volume of clear solution samples was 20 μL. The specificity of this
method was studied in support materials Figure S1. The EE of SF was
calculated according to the following formula:
$$ EE\%=\left({W}_T-{W}_S\right)/{W}_T\times 100\% $$
where WT represents the total amount of SF used to prepare SPTNs and
WS represents the amount of free SF present in the supernatant.
In Vitro Drug Release
The in vitro release of SF from SPTNs was studied by the dialysis
method. Briefly, SPTN solutions (5 mL) were placed in a dialysis bag
(MWCO: 10 kDa) and incubated in 100 mL of PBS with Tween 80 (1%)

on a rotary shaker at pH 5.0 or 7.4, respectively. The samples were kept
at 37°C and shaken at 120 r/min. At predetermined time intervals, 2 mL
of release medium was collected and equal volume of corresponding
fresh medium was replenished. The quantities of SF in the supernatant
were determined by HPLC. The methods of HPLC were the same as
those in the “Characterization of SPTNs” section.
Stability Assay
The change of particle size was measured to evaluate storage stability of
freeze-dried powder at 4°C and 37°C within 30 days. At prearranged
time (10, 20, 30 days), samples were withdrawn and determined by a
dynamic light scattering laser particle analyzer (ZetasizerNano 90,
Malvern, Worcestershire, UK).
Cellular Uptake
Briefly, HepG2/MDR cells were seeded at an initial density of 1 × 105
cells/well in 6-well plates and incubated overnight. Free C6, C6-PTNs
and C6-PVNs were added in each well at a C6 concentration of 20 μM,
and the mixture was incubated about 4 h for the qualitative analysis. The
cells were washed thrice with PBS and then fixed in 75% ethanol for 10
min. The cellular uptake of different groups was further investigated by a
fluorescence inverted microscope (Olympus IX81; Olympus Corporation,
Tokyo, Japan).
In Vitro Cytotoxicity

The MTT assay was performed to measure the cytotoxicity of SPTNs,
SPVNs and SF solution. HepG2/MDR cells were seeded in 96-well
plates at an initial density of 1 × 104 cells/well and cultured in 100 μL of
RPMI 1640 overnight. After removal of the culture medium, the cells
were treated with SPTNs, SPVNs and SF solution at predetermined
concentrations from 0.5 to 80 μM for 24 h and 48 h. At the same time,
PTNs were used to explore the toxicity of the preparation materials. At
the end of the treatment, 20 μL of MTT solution was added into each well
for further 4 h to stain the cells. After removing the MTT solution, the
incubation medium was replaced with 150 μL of dimethyl sulfoxide. The
absorbance of samples in various groups was measured by a microplate
reader (Molecular Devices, Sunnyvale, CA, USA) at 490 nm. The cell
variability and half-maximal inhibitory concentration (IC50) were
calculated as previously reported (25,26,27).
Cell Apoptosis Assay
To study the changes about the nucleus, we seeded cells (1 × 105) into
wells of 6-well plates and incubated for 24 h. Cells were treated with SF
solution, SPVNs and SPTNs at a concentration of 20 μM for 24 h,
whereas the culture medium was added in the control group. The cells
were washed thrice with PBS and fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde for 20
min. Next, 0.2 μg/mL of DAPI solution was added to each well to stain
the cell nucleus for 10 min, and the cells were subsequently washed with
PBS. Finally, scanned images were obtained using a fluorescence
inverted microscope (Olympus IX81; Olympus Corporation, Tokyo,
Japan).

For qualitative analysis of cell apoptosis, cells were seeded in 6-well
plates at the logarithmic phase and incubated overnight. After removing
the medium, we added different preparations into the cells. The cells
were washed thrice with PBS. Then, 10 μL of 2.5% Annexin V-FITC
binding buffer and 5% propidium iodide were added at room temperature
in the dark. Intracellular apoptosis was measured through flow cytometry.
SPTNs Overcome P-gp-Mediated Drug Efflux In Vitro
HepG2/MDR cells in the logarithmic growth phase were seeded in 6-well
plates and treated with the following drugs for 24 h: SPVNs, SPTNs,
RPMI 1640 medium (without FBS) and verapamil (P-gp inhibitor). Next,
cells were washed thrice with PBS. 1 mL of Rho123 (5 μM) was then
added into cells and incubated for 2 h in the dark. Finally, the cells were
collected in a flow tube and examined through flow cytometry.
SPTNs Inhibit MDR1 Gene and P-gp Expression
To explore whether the treatment with SPTNs has effect on reversing the
MDR of cancer cells, quantitative real-time polymerase chain reaction
(qRT-PCR) was performed to examine the expression levels of MDR1
mRNA. TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA) was used to isolate
total RNA from cells, and cDNA was synthesized from total RNA by
using the Revert Aid First Strand. qRT-PCR was performed using the
SYBR Green PCR master mix in the ABI Step One Real-Time PCR
system. GAPDH was used as an internal control. The sequences of
primers used in PCR are listed in Table S1. Furthermore, the protein
level of P-gp was also detected by western blotting.

Total protein was extracted by washing with PBS and subsequent lysis
with RIPA buffer (Beyotime, China). The supernatant was collected after
centrifugation at 12,000 rpm for 20 min at 4°C. The BCA protein
quantitative kit (Beyotime, China) was used to analyse the protein
concentration. Equivalent amounts of the protein (20 μg/lane) were
separated through 15% SDS-PAGE and transferred to PVDF
membranes. The membrane was incubated in 5% skimmed milk and
subsequently cultured with the primary monoclonal antimouse P-gp
overnight at 4°C. The next day, the membrane was treated with
horseradish peroxidase–labelled secondary antibodies for 1 h at room
temperature. Finally, image master software was used to analyse the
density of specific protein bands.
In Vivo Antitumor and Safety Evaluation
HepG2/MDR tumor-bearing Babl/c mice (male, 16–18 g) were used to
investigate the antitumor efficacy of SPTNs in inhibiting tumor growth.
We injected 0.1 mL of cell suspension (1 × 107 cells/mL) in the right
axillary vein to establish a tumor model. After inoculation for 7 days, mice
were randomly divided into six groups with eight mice in each group
intravenously injected with saline, free SF, SPVNs (6 mg/kg) and SPTNs
(3 mg/kg, 6 mg/kg and 9 mg/kg). All groups were administered injections
every 3 day for 21 days. The body weight and tumor volume was
recorded every other day. We measured the length-diameter (a) and
short-diameter (b) of tumors by using a vernier caliper every other day
and then calculated them using the equation: (a × b2)/2. At day 21,
subcutaneous tumors were surgically excised and isolated. The mouse

tissue was embedded in paraffin, sectioned and then stained with
haematoxylin and eosin (H&E). Finally, we observed and photographed
the sections under a microscope. The tumor inhibitory rate (%) was
calculated as follows:
$$ \mathrm{Tumor}\ \mathrm{inhibitory}\ \mathrm{rate}\left(\%
\right)=\left(1-\frac{W_{\mathrm{drug}}}{W_{\mathrm{control}}}\right)\ast
100 $$
where Wdrug is the tumor average weight of the drug group and Wcontrol is
the tumor average weight of the control group.
Statistical Analysis
SPSS (version 17.0) was used to perform the data analysis. All data
were expressed as mean ± standard deviation (SD). One-way ANOVA
was used to determine statistical significance. The difference was
considered statistically significant at *p < 0.05, **p < 0.01 and ***p <
0.001.
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
Preparation and Characterization of SPTNs
The SPTNs were prepared by the modified emulsion solvent evaporation
method. Firstly, the single-factor experiment and BBD experimental
design were performed for formulation optimization of SPTNs. As shown
in Table S2 and Figure S2, the results suggested that the optimal mass

ratio of SF to PLGA was 1:11.56. The optimal volume ratio of the O
phase to W phase was 1:5.56, and the optimal TPGS concentration was
0.03% in SPTN preparation. Next, we systematically characterized the
physicochemical properties of SPTNs. The size and zeta of SPTNs were
215.70 ± 0.36 nm and −26.01 ± 0.65 mV, respectively (Figure 1A and B).
According to TEM characterizations, SPTNs showed a spherical shape
and the size was consistent with the results obtained by the dynamic
light scattering method. The EE of SPTNs was significantly higher than
that of SPVNs (90.35 ± 1.54% vs. 85.11 ± 0.86%, p < 0.05), suggesting
that TPGS was more suitable for drug encapsulation compared with
PVA. Multiple researches showed that TPGS had a promising potential
in enhancing emulsifying properties due to hydrogen bonding (28, 29).

DSC and Stability Studies of SPTNs
DSC was performed to examine the physicochemical status of drugs in
nanoparticles. As shown in Figure 1C, characteristic absorption peak of

SF was detected at 203°C, which indicated the crystal structure was
stable. Besides, the endothermic peaks of PLGA and TPGS were
49.83°C and 34.93°C, respectively. However, the crystal melting
endothermic peaks of TPGS and SF were obviously weakened or even
disappeared in DSC curve of SPTNs. This indicated that TPGS and SF
exist in an amorphous form in the obtained nanoparticles (30). These
results proved that we have successfully prepared SPTNs. Furthermore,
we investigated the stability of SPTNs in storage at 4°C and 37°C. The
average size of SPTNs could be stabilized at around 210 nm within 30
days (Figure S3). This ensured its stability and reliability in the later
application.
In Vitro Drug Release
As depicted in Figure 1D, SPTNs exhibited a typical two-phase drug
release profile, with rapid drug release in the first 24 h, followed by
subsequent slow drug release up to 120 h. In addition, we found that
SPTNs were released faster in a weak acid environment at 24 h. The
cumulative release of SPTNs in pH 5.0 PBS and 7.4 PBS was 73.81 ±
4.53% and 34.89 ± 4.52%, respectively, in 24 h. The results were
consistent with literature reports that the acidic environment might be
beneficial to drug release (5).
Cellular Uptake Study
We used the C6 to replace SF in PLGA-based nanoparticles to
investigate cellular uptake. As shown in Figure 2A and B, the intracellular
C6 fluorescence of C6-PVNs and C6-PTNs was significantly higher than

that of free C6 group in 4-h incubation, suggesting the PLGA
nanoparticles were favourable to cellular uptake. Notably, the
fluorescence intensity of C6-PTNs was 1.63-fold stronger than that of
C6-PVNs, indicating that TPGS was more conducive to cellular
internalization compared with PVA in nanoparticles. The increased cell
uptake induced by SPTNs is conducive to further improvement of
antitumor efficacy (21, 31,32,33).

Cytotoxicity Study of SPTNs
The cytotoxicity of SPTNs to HepG2/MDR cells was evaluated by MTT
assay, and the free SF served as the control (Figure 2C). Firstly, the
PTNs did not show inhibitory effect against cell proliferation which means
that the preparation materials were safe and reliable. In comparison,
SPTNs exhibited a stronger cytotoxicity than SPVNs and SF groups. The

IC50 of SPTNs was decreased by 4.27-fold compared with SPVNs after
48-h treatment (3.90 μM vs. 16.67 μM), which was associated with the
higher cellular uptake and probably beneficial to drug delivery inside the
tumor tissues. In summary, the above results indicated that SPTNs
effectively inhibited the cell proliferation of cells in vitro which is due to
better capacity of cell uptake.
Cell Apoptosis Induction
We investigated whether the incorporation of TPGS could further
enhance the pro-apoptosis efficiency of PLGA nanoparticles on HepG2/
MDR cells. As shown in Figure 3A, a few apoptotic bodies were
observed in SF solution, while there were bluer hyperchromatic
phenomena, accompanied by nuclear aggregation or deformation in the
SPVNs and SPTNs groups. Results suggested that SPVNs and SPTNs
showed an apoptosis-inducing effect on cells. Total apoptosis ratio of
cells was 17.99% and 75.62% after treatment with SPVNs and SPTNs,
respectively (Figure 3B). SPTNs displayed 5.41- and 4.20-fold increase
in promoting cell apoptosis compared with SF and SPVNs, respectively,
indicating that TPGS possesses a significant contribution on enhancing
apoptosis by promoting drug uptake.

SPTNs Overcome P-gp-Mediated Drug Efflux In Vitro
In order to determine the anti-drug efflux effect caused by the TPGS
component in SPTNs, the intracellular trafficking assay was performed,
and the fluorescence of Rho123-label tumor cells was detected by flow
cytometry. As shown in Figure 4, the Rho123 accumulation in the SPTNs
group was higher than that in the SPVNs group, which indicated that
SPTN effectively inhibited the Rho123 efflux in tumor cells. This result
was consistent with the group of the P-gp protein inhibitor of verapamil
(23, 34, 35). Therefore, we believed that TPGS has the same inhibitory
function as verapamil.

SPTNs Inhibit MDR1 Gene and P-gp Expression
To further investigate the anti-MDR mechanisms of SPTNs, the MDR-
related P-gp and gene of MDR1 were determined by western blot and
real-time qPCR experiment, respectively. P-gp is a key member in the

MDR protein family and is encoded by the MDR1 gene. As shown in
Figure 5A, there was no significant change in MDR1 mRNA between the
SPVNs and control group. However, the verapamil and SPTNs groups
exerted lower MDR1 mRNA levels compared with the control group.
Furthermore, the expression of MDR1 in the gradient-dose SPTNs group
was concentration-dependent, which was similar to that in the verapamil
group (Figure S4). The above results verified that SPTNs could
effectively reduce the MDR1 gene expression in tumor cells.

Western blot was then performed to determine the P-gp protein
expression in tumor cells treated with SPTNs. As presented in Figure 5B,
the levels of P-gp protein in SPTNs and verapamil groups were both
lower than those in the control and SPVNs group. Significantly, P-gp
protein in the SPTNs group was decreased by 2.05-fold to the SPVNs
group during the treatments. In summary, the PLGA nanoparticles
entrapped within TPGS can effectively overcome the MDR in tumor cells

through inhibiting MDR1 gene and P-gp expression (18, 36, 37).
In Vivo Antitumor and Safety Evaluation
To validate the advantages of combining TPGS into PLGA-based
nanoparticles, the antitumor efficacy was performed through intravenous
injection of SPTNs on the HepG2/MDR cell-bearing mice, with saline, SF
and SPVNs as the controls. As shown in Figure 6A, the saline group
showed the fastest tumor growth within 21 days. The treatment of
SPVNs and SPTNs exhibited an obvious inhibition of tumor growth
compared with SF, which could be attributed to an improved intratumoral
accumulation of nanoparticles based on EPR effect. With the
incorporation of TPGS, the medium dose of SPTNs (6 mg/kg) further
inhibited the tumor growth compared with SPVNs (6 mg/kg) highlighting
that TPGS facilitated a prolonged retention in the tumor cells and a more
efficiently antitumor effect of SPTNs. As shown in Figure 6A, the tumor
size of saline, SF (6 mg/kg) and SPVNs (6 mg/kg) groups enhanced by
4.76-, 4.21- and 2.97-fold during the treatments, respectively. By
comparison (Figure 6B), the tumor size only grew up 1.85-fold after
treatment with SPTNs (6 mg/kg). At the end of the treatment, the tumors
of mice were isolated (Figure 6B), and the tumor weight and mice weight
were respectively measured to evaluate the tumor inhibition rate (Figure
6C and D). The tumor weight values of mice treated with SPTNs (6 mg/
kg, 9 mg/kg) were 0.69 ± 0.04 g and 0.39 ± 0.02 g, respectively, which
were obviously lower than those in other treatments. Notably, the tumor
inhibition rate of high dose of SPVN (9 mg/kg) treatment was 65.42 ±
2.24%, which was the highest among all the groups (Figure 6D),

verifying the high-efficiency antitumor effect of SPTNs against
hepatocellular carcinoma. Next, we evaluated the potential toxicity of the
various groups against normal organs. The saline group was made into
H&E-stained sections (Figure 7), and we found low levels of
inflammatory infiltration in the liver and kidney sections of SF-treated
mice (as shown in black arrows). However, there were no obvious
pathological changes of the heart, liver, spleen, lung and kidney in the
SPTNs group.

Conclusion
In this study, we successfully synthesized and applied PLGA-TPGS
nanoparticles as SF carriers (SPTNs) for overcoming MDR. SPTNs
rapidly released SF when exposed to the acidic environment. As a P-gp
inhibitor, TPGS increased the EE of PLGA nanoparticles and facilitated
the cellular uptake of drugs. SPTNs could effectively inhibit tumor cell
proliferation and induce cell apoptosis in a low drug concentration.
Furthermore, SPTNs exhibited an enhanced antitumor activity compared
with SF and SPVNS in vivo. This study provides a PLGA-TPGS nano
delivery strategy for overcoming MDR in chemotherapy of hepatocellular
carcinoma.
