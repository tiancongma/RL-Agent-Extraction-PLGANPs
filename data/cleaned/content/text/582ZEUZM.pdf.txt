A B S T R A C T
Dry eye syndrome, a commonly prevalent disease is characterized by irritation, redness and tear film instability.
The main objective of the current study included the formulation and evaluation of Eudragit-PLGA nanoparticles
for lifitegrast delivery via gellan gum in-situ drops. Nanoparticles were prepared using a quasi-emulsion method
followed by characterization for various parameters such as size analysis, zeta potential, entrapment efficiency,
FTIR and DSC studies. These nanoparticles were loaded into gel and then subjected to histopathological studies of
murine model. The evaluated formulation demonstrated particle size of 393 ± 1.42 nm, zeta potential of 2.44 ±
0.02 mV, PdI of 2.44 ± 0.02 and entrapment efficiency of 90.45 ± 0.05 %. The nanoparticles displayed an initial
rapid release of 55.95 ± 0.03 % followed by release sustained for 48 h ranging from initial 27.84 ± 0.01 % to
88.22 ± 0.01 % and 24.81 ± 0.01 % to 77.71 ± 0.01 % with and without gelling system. The gelling system was
characterized by a pH of 4.56 ± 0.04, clarity index of 99.27 ± 0.05 %, and viscosity changing from 5.283 ±
0.012 to 15.511 ± 0.160 in STF. The histological finding displayed significant healing in damaged areas of dry
eyes. Furthermore, when compared to marketed formulation, the formulation demonstrated an enhanced corneal
thickness and reduced inflammation.
1. Introduction
Dry eye disorder (keratoconjunctivitis sicca) is the most under­
diagnosed condition with a prevalence rate of 11.59 ± 0.04 % [1]. The
current lifestyle and demographic changes predict that the global dry
eye patient population will increase substantially from 17.2 % to 19 % in
2023 [2]. The disorder is mainly caused due to imbalance in the ocular
fluid homeostasis with instability of tear film and reduced the produc­
tion of tear fluid. In addition to lifestyle factors like extensive use of
contact lenses, extreme use of digital screens, and relatively less fre­
quency of blinking, a careful history should be obtained for diseases like
systemic lupus erythematosus, diabetes, connective tissue disorder,
rheumatoid arthritis and thyroid abnormalities. Dry eye can be further
associated with secondary causes like vascular allergy, cigarette smok­
ing, hormone replacement therapy, dry climate and history of corneal
transplantation, refractive surgery, and extracapsular cataract proced­
ures. Furthermore, long-term medications like antidepressants, antihis­
tamines, diuretics, cholesterol-lowering agents, psychotropics, oral
contraceptives and beta-blockers lead to dry eyes.
Dry-eye disorder (DED) can be treated in number of ways, the latest
trends include use of intranasal and external tear neuro-stimulators [3],
osmoprotectants (trehalose, levocarnitine and erythritol) that help in
prevention of damage caused by hyperosmolarity and humectants like
sodium hyaluronate for the promotion and retention of water on surface.
Moreover, hydroxypropyl guar gum utilized to thicken tears and in­
crease the retention in aqueous layer whereas oils like flaxseed oil,
mineral and castor oil for simulation of the lipid layer in the tear film
[4]. Further studies for DED include development of tacrolimus gellan
gum nanoparticles [5], anti-oxidative and mucin compensating dual
action nano-eye drops [6] and rebamipide liposomal formulations for
the management of dry eye syndrome[7].
Studies performed on the protein profiles obtained from ocular sur­
face after comparison between normal and dry eyes via ELISA revealed
reduction in levels of epidermal growth factor and lactoferrin. Further, a
study demonstrated increased secretion of inflammatory cytokines
interleukin-1 alpha and IL-1 beta [8]. Moreover, according to studies,
DED caused lymphocytic infiltration in the ocular tissue resulting in
amplified levels of pro-inflammatory chemokines and cytokines which
caused further impairment to the ocular tissues. DED patients

Lifitegrast is a tetrahydroisoquinoline derivative, was permitted by
USFDA in 2016 for treatment of DED [13]. This molecule not only tar­
gets cytokines and chemokines secreted during immune response but
also reduces migration, activation, adhesion and propagation of lym­

self-amplification and cell destruction of immune response generated by
the body in response to DED. Lifitegrast inhibits LFA-1 and hinders the
activity of ICAM-1, an immunoglobin superfamily responsible for
cellular bonds in endothelial cells during inflammation and overex­
pressed in conjunctival and corneal tissues in DED patients [14].
To improve the retention time of formulation on eye surface, gellan
gum, a natural physical crosslinking and gelling agent exhibiting sol-to-
gel transition at lowered temperature was used by Ref. [15]. Further­
more, when compared to another ion-sensitive gelling agent on basis of
pH, clarity and viscosity to determine of optimum gel for ocular prep­
aration sodium alginate, gellan gum provides optimum viscosity for
liquid formulation and transparency. Furthermore, hyaluronic acid, an
anionic linear polysaccharide, is generally found in vitreous cavity of
eye in aqueous humor and incorporated into ophthalmic formulations
for its corneal protection, wound healing mucoadhesive and
water-binding properties [16]. As aforementioned, it is suggested as an
effective treatment and excipient for dry eye syndrome by lubricating,
space-filling and cushioning the vitreous humor. Hyaluronic acid (HA)
chains maintain a hydrated vitreous network by enhancing the chemical
stability of collagen fibrils and preventing aggregation of collagen
[17–20].
Furthermore, to increase corneal uptake, accelerate the drug pene­
tration as well as avoid systemic absorption, nano-particulate system
was considered as a potent to transport the drug to the targeted site
without losing its activity and effectiveness [21]. PLGA is considered
one of the best classes of decomposable polymers for continuous drug
delivery displaying hydrolytic degradation into non-toxic monomer and
oligomer, glycolic acid and lactic acid [22,23]. Lifitegrast-loaded
nanoparticles formulated from PLGA were also considered expansively
in numerous characteristics for their application in ophthalmic irritation
and evidenced to have decent stability and ophthalmic acceptance [24].
The combination displayed sustained and targeted delivery of drug at
pH 7.4, concerning Eudragit RL100 imparting positive stimulating
quaternary ammonium groups to bind with anionic-charged mucin and
PLGA for enhancing mucoadhesion to the corneal membrane. The pre­
sent effort was focused on designing lifitegrast-loaded PLGA-Eudragit
nanoparticles and incorporating them in hyaluronic acid-based gel
where two gelling systems were compared including gellan gum in-situ
drops and sodium alginate gel.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Materials
The poly (lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) polymer (50:50) was pro­
cured from Nomisma Healthcare, India. Eudragit RL100 was purchased
from Evonic Deggusa, Mumbai, India. Lifitegrast was obtained as a gift
sample from Microlabs Ltd. India. Poly(vinyl alcohol) (cold pressed) was
provided by Fischer Scientific, India. Gellan gum and hyaluronic acid
were acquired from Research lab, India. Sodium alginate was obtained
from SRL labs, India. Potassium sorbate was obtained from Purenso Pvt.
Ltd, India. All other solvents and ingredients utilized were analytical
grade.
2.2. Methods
2.2.1. Preparation of lifitegrast-loaded nanoparticles (LEP)
LEP were formulated by means of varying weight proportions of
concentrations of Eudragit RS100 and PLGA (1:1, 1:2, and 1:3 w/w)
using the quasi-emulsion solvent diffusion technique. To identify
appropriate concentration for the preparation of lifitegrast-loaded
Eudragit RS100 nanoparticles (EUD), 100 ppm drug and varying con­
centrations (25, 50 and 75 mg) of Eudragit RS100 (EUD) were dissolved
in 6 mL DMSO and then injected into 1 % PVA solution under constant
stirring speed of 500 rpm and subjected to high-pressure homogeniza­
tion at 30,000 psi for 6 cycles (LM20 Microfluidizer, USA). The EUD
nanoparticles were then subjected to 15,000 rpm centrifugation at 4 ◦C
for 30 min (Beckman Coulter, USA) and collected after washing them
twice with double distilled water (ddw).
Further, for the preparation of nanoparticles with combination of
polymers, lifitegrast (100 ppm) was incorporated in a rounded flask and
liquefied in 6 mL DMSO under continuous stirring till indistinct solution
was obtained. Additionally, different proportions of PLGA with constant
concentration of Eudragit RS100 were liquefied in the above solution
under continuous overnight stirring. The resulting formulation was
injected in 40 mL solution (1%w/v) consisting of PVA as a stabilizing
agent under continuous stirring. This sample was then subjected to high-
pressure homogenization and the final nanosuspension was centrifuged
at 15,000 rpm at 4 ◦C for 30 min (Beckman Coulter, USA). The nano­
particles were washed with ddw for removal of surface PVA and excess
DMSO.
2.2.2. Preparation of hyaluronic-based in-situ drops
Gellan gum drops were prepared by varying concentrations of
deacetylated gellan gum where, the formulation was dissolved with HA
(0.1 %), mannitol (4.75% w/v), Polysorbate 80 (4 % w/v), EDTA (0.03
% w/v) and potassium sorbate (0.01 %). Briefly, mannitol, Polysorbate
80 and EDTA were added in 20 mL of ddw under stirring at 500 rpm.
Gellan gum was solubilized in 10 mL ddw and kept undisturbed to hy­
drate for 10 min, followed by heating the solution at 80 ◦C under con­
stant stirring until a transparent homogenous solution was obtained.
Both the solutions were mixed to 80 ◦C and gellan gum solution was
added into mannitol solution at constant stirring (500 rpm). After
obtaining the homogenized solution, the formulation was filtered using
Whatman® filter paper. Subsequently, the water loss caused because of
evaporation was adjusted by adding ddw and the formulation was

3. Characterization of LEP2 in GA2 in-situ drops
3. 1. Characterisation of LEP
3.1.1. Particle size (PS), surface charge (ZP) and polydispersity index
(PdI)
PS, ZP and PdI of formulations BEP1, BEP2, BEP3, LEP1, LEP2 and
LEP3 were studied by employing the dynamic light scattering method
(Nano Zeta Sizer, Malvern ZS 90, UK). The prepared formulations were
mixed with 15 mL ddw for dilution before analysis at 25 ◦C and were
subjected to Nanozeta sizer (NanoZS90, Malvern, UK). The analysis was
accomplished in triplicate at 25 ◦C and the mean value was calculated.

The formulations LEP1, LEP2 and LEP3 were dispersed in ddw and

the supernatant was withdrawn from the LEP1, LEP2 and LEP3 and
analysis was done using UV–Visible spectrophotometer at 260 nm and
the % EE was measured using the below formula:

3.1.3. In-vitro drug release studies
The drug release of formulations LEP1, LEP2 and LEP3 were calcu­
lated utilizing dialysis tube (M.W. Cut-off: 10,000) technique under
simulated tear fluid pH 7.2. Formulations LEP1, LEP2 and LEP3 were
disseminated in dialysis membrane and the ends were clamped. The
aliquots were reserved at different time intervals like 15 min, 30 min, 1
h, 2 h, 4 h, 8 h, 24 h and 36 h. The samples were subjected to UV–visible
spectrophotometer at a wavelength of 260 nm to measure the % drug
release.
From the above formulation, LEP2 demonstrated the minimum
particle dimension (393.17 ± 1.42 nm) along with zeta potential of 2.44
± 0.016 mV (P < 0.0001). Furthermore, the PdI was detected to be 0.43
± 0.03 indicating undeviating colloidal scattering (P < 0.0001).
Formulation LEP2 displayed a determined %EE of 90.45 ± 0.05 with
sustained release outline up to 48 h (P < 0.0001). Based on the PS, %EE,
ZP and in-vitro release profile, LEP2 was chosen as a supplementary for
superficial morphology analysis, FTIR, and DSC and further evaluated
after incorporation in in-situ gel systems for in-vitro and ex-vivo release
studies.
3.1.4. Thermal analysis
The thermal analyses of lifitegrast PLGA, EUD3, Eudragit RS100,
BEP2 and LEP2 were analyzed by Differential Scanning Calorimeter
(DSC Mettler Toledo DSC 1 Star ͤ, Switzerland). Sample between 2 and 4
mg was subjected in an aluminium pan and the thermal profiles were
further attained between 30◦and 300 ◦C.
3.1.5. Scanning Electron Microscope
The surface morphologic features of the BEP2 and LEP2 were
examined under Scanning Electron Microscope (Quanta200 ESEM sys­
tem). Prior to studies, LEP2 was positioned on aluminium stubs and
covered with gold to form a conductive surface and scanned within the
range of 30–300 ◦C.
3.1.6. Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR)
Lifitegrast, PLGA, Eudragit RS100, BEP2 and LEP2 were scanned
using light absorbance standards in the infrared range (PerkinElmer,
USA) after scanning the spectra 32 times on a resolution of 4 cm−
1.
3.2. Characterization of in-situ drops
Two in-situ systems including GA and SA were examined for pH,
gelation time, viscosity and clarity for further selection of the delivery
system to deliver LEP. GA was selected for the preparation of in-situ
drops and a comparative evaluation for justification was performed
based on pH, clarity and viscosity change when the formulations were
subjected to simulated tear fluid.
3.2.1. pH
The formulations SA and GA were tested using PICO + pH meter (Lab
India, India). Before analysis, the pH meter was standardized using
standard solutions of pH values 4, 7 and 10 pH standards at 25 ◦C. After
testing the formulations in triplicate, the average value was considered.
3.2.2. Clarity
A quantitative estimation was predicted by subjecting the formula­
tions GA1, GA2, GA3, GA4, GA5, SA1, SA2, SA3, SA4 and SA5 to per­
centage light transmittance in the perceptible region at 490 nm using an
UV–Visible spectrometer(UV-1800), Shimadzu, Japan with reference to
ddw as a standard (100 % mean transmittance).
3.2.3. Rheological characterization and viscosity testing
GA and SA formulations were tested for optimum viscosity using
spindle 61, DV-III ultra-programmable rheometer at 25 ◦C for obtaining
viscosities. Shear stress and shear rate were kept constant, and the
formulation viscosity was measured after 100 s. Readings were taken
into triplicates and the average value was considered.
Apart from the above test, the preparations GA2 were subjected to
viscosity profiling under simulated tear fluid conditions to understand
their behavior in the ocular environment. The formulation was tested
using Geometry: DIN rotor – Concentric Cylinder assembly at tempera­
ture: 32 ◦C and subjected to shear rates: 0.1 to 3000 1/s to understand
the effect of blinking on the behavior of gel.
3.2.4. Gelation time
A system was prepared in a beaker containing 2 mL of gel and STF
prepared with sodium salt (0.67 g), NaHCO3(0.20 g), and CaCl3 de­
hydrates (0.008 g) in ddw to 100 mL and pH value was adjusted to 7.2.
The time taken by the liquid formulation to form gel was observed
visually concerning change in viscosity and was reported. Based on the
above criteria, GA2 was selected for further evaluation like osmolality,
in-vitro, in-vivo and e.v. studies.

3.2.5. Osmolality
The osmolalities of ophthalmic formulations GA2, GA3 and GA4
were tested (Osmometer Fischer scientific, USA) to avoid hyper­
osmolality leading to irritation of the eye, discomfort and ophthalmic
surface damage. The acceptable range of ophthalmic formulation is

3.3. Characterization of lifitegrast-loaded LEP2 in GA2 in-situ drops
(LLN)
3.3.1. In-vitro drug release studies
The formulation LEP2 was incorporated into GA2 and release was
measured using Franz diffusion method. A nylon membrane (M.W. cut-
off = 10,000) was used with STF pH 7.4 and the formulation was sub­
jected to the membrane under constant stirring. The aliquots were
collected at several time intervals like 15 min, 30 min, 1 h, 2 h, 4 h, 8 h,
24 h, 36 h and 48 h. The experiment was performed in triplicates.
3.3.2. Ex-vivo studies
The e.v. release studies were carried out using goat cornea where the
corneas were prepared and positioned on Franz diffusion cell. LNN was
subjected to the Franz diffusion system with STF as the release medium
and the permeation release profile was compared with the release of
LEP2 for the period of 48h.
3.3.3. In-vivo studies
The animal studies were accomplished on C57BL/6 mice of 20–25 g
and were subjected to a supervised environment with a temperature of
22 ◦C and light-dark cycle of 12:12-h throughout the study and given
unrestricted admission to water and food. All experimentations were
approved under IAEC guidelines protocol number CPCSEA/IAEC/P54/
2022. The animals were separated into 4 groups each containing six
mice including positive and negative control groups, mice tested with
marketed formulation and LLN. Except for group I, all mice were treated
with 5 μL PBS containing 0.2 % benzalkonium chloride twice a day for
inducing dry eye. After inducing dry eyes, the animals were tested using
phenol-dipped cotton thread to evaluate the induction of DED. The
marketed formulation was introduced BID ~12h apart and LLN formu­
lation was administered once daily with an interval of 24h using a
positive displacement micropipette for 15 days. On the 16th day of drug
administration, eyes were withdrawn from each group and subjected to
histopathological studies using Haematoxylin-Eosin stain(H&E) to un­
derstand the healing process of dry eye.
4. Results and discussion
4.1. Preparation and optimization of lifitegrast-loaded Eudragit-PLGA
nanoparticles (LLN)
The PSs of BEP and LEP nanoparticles were reported in the range of
270.93 ± 2.55 to 512.52 ± 2.65 nm. For identifying the appropriate
concentration of Eudragit RS100, nanoparticles were prepared with
varying concentrations of Eudragit (EUD1, EUD2 and EUD3) as
mentioned in Table 1. Further, based on the PS, ZP and % EE, EUD2
batch was selected with PS of 219.2 ± 0.29 nm, ZP of 16.97 ± 0.21 mV,
PdI of 0.56 ± 0.15 and EE of 84.66 ± 0.012 %. There was a decline in PS
with increasing concentration of Eudragit RS100 due to decrease in in­
ternal phase with cumulative polymer concentration creating intact
structure. The integral structure resulted into improved %EE of the
nanoparticles due to upsurge in organic phase and intensification in the
organic phase viscosity resulting into better drug loading capacity [25].
After the selection of EUD2, 50 mg of Eudragit RS100 was combined
with varying concentrations of PLGA for the preparation of nano­
particles. Table 1 displays PS, ZP, PDI and % EE of nanoparticles with
increasing concentrations of PLGA.
Different Eudragit RS100 and PLGA concentrations displayed vary­
ing predominant effects on the particle size of nanoparticles. Table 1
signifies that the PS was declining or expanding with PLGA concentra­
tions, indicating the upsurge in polymer concentration in the surface
matrix of nanoparticles. The increasing PS was further justified by the
fact that the interior phase viscosity was altered with the changing
concentration of polymers and the upsurge in concentration led to rise in
the interior phase viscosity. Furthermore, during the formation process
of nanoparticles, the viscosity of the emulsion interior phase was more
than supplementary polymer compositions. The outcomes attained
interrelated well with the aforementioned experimentations and
considered the impact of Eudragit RL100 and PLGA polymers on the
dimensions of nanoparticles [26].
The % EE of LEP preparations was established to be in the range of
83.06 ± 0.04 to 92.13 ± 0.01 %. As shown in Tables 1 and it was
detected that with an increasing amount of PLGA, the %EE increased
since lifitegrast is a water-insoluble drug but is very well soluble in
organic solvents like DMF, ethanol and DMSO. Moreover, by increasing
PLGA polymer concentration, there was a higher availability of poly­
meric phase resulting into elevated dissolution area for lifitegrast.
Furthermore, the ZP of LEP2 was observed to possess a cationic charge
because of the presence of Eudragit RL100 and decreased to 1.66 ± 0.01
mV from 7.66 ± 0.01 mV with an increase in PLGA concentration from
25 to 75 mg. The surface charge remained positive by varying concen­
trations of Eudragit RL100 since only a minimal amount of Eudragit
RS100 was required to maintain a positive charge. Analogous results
were obtained in an analysis [27]. The anionic surface charge can be
considered important since it is responsible for increasing precorneal
holding time by interacting with the negative charge of mucin in corneal
tissue. As shown in Fig. 1, the % release amplified with the concentra­
tion of PLGA due to increasing PS of nanoparticles and enhanced
interface of the polymers with the drug and elevated dissolution area for
lifitegrast. Based on PS (393.17 ± 1.42 nm), ZP (2.44 ± 0.02 mV), PdI
(0.43 ± 0.03) and EE (90.45 ± 0.05 %), the formulation LEP2 was
further selected for incorporation in the gelling system and subjected to
FTIR, DSC and SEM analyses. LEP2 also displayed in-vitro release of

4.1.1. DSC
The thermal analysis of lifitegrast displayed a peak at 48.32 ◦C and
119.29 ◦C confirming the amorphous structure of the drug. PLGA
exhibited a melting peak at 260 ◦C [28] whereas Eudragit RS100
demonstrated a glass transition temperature of 190 ◦C with peak onset of
396.97 ◦C [29]. BEP2 depicted a peak of PLGA and Eudragit RS100 with
a melting behaviour at 260 ◦C for PLGA whereas the structure of BEP2
thermogram displays a similar pattern as that of EUD. The characteristic
peak of lifitegrast diminished in the LEP2, represents the loosely
entrapped lifitegrast on the surface of nanoparticles [23].
4.1.2. SEM
As reported in Fig. 2 nanoparticles were found to be smooth and
spherical demonstrating the greater probability of rapid clearance and
uniform distribution of particles in formulation in ocular cavity under
314.10 nm–345.23 nm range. BEP2 formulation demonstrated a particle
size distribution of 183.3 nm–283.30 nm. Moreover, these particles were
distributed uniformly in the formulation and displayed absence of
ruptured exterior, such surface would consequence in measured clear­
ance from the cavity. Moreover, non-spherical particles lead to damage
on the corneal surface and scratching effect on the cornea and hence the
resulting morphology displays the feasibility for delivering ocular for­
mulations as reported earlier by Ref. [30,31].
4.1.3. FTIR
Peaks obtained at 1732.60 cm−1 signified the formation of C––O on
terminal aryl group in the range of 1740-1720 cm−1. Peaks at 532.01
cm−1 and 1027.42 cm−1 were obtained between 600 and 800 cm−1 and
1000-1200 cm−1 demonstrated the occurrence of C–Cl bond and C–N
bond, respectively. Two peaks were obtained between 3252.23 cm−1
and 1617.21 cm−1 representing N–H group present in lifitegrast struc­
ture. The presence of Eudragit RS100 was confirmed by sharp charac­

4.2. Characterisation of in-situ drops
4.2.1. pH, clarity and gelation time
Since tear fluid shows a pH value of 7.4 in its physiological state and
hence the acceptable ocular range is 4.5–8.5. As reported in Table 2, the
pH values displayed a range of 4.46 ± 0.04 to 4.63 ± 0.04 which is in
the tolerable range of ocular pH. For clarity testing, the formulations
were subjected to UV–Visible spectroscopy and measured against ddw at
visible wavelength of 490 nm (100 % mean transmittance). The gelation
time was calculated to understand the time required for sol-to-gel tran­
sition time and retention in the cul-de-sac region. GA formulations
demonstrated a gelation time of 5–10 s whereas, SA formulations dis­
played a range of 15–25 s after the in-situ systems were incorporated in
STF. The quick transition time of GA formulations might be due to the
interaction of anions in STF with anionically charged helices of poly­
saccharide with mono- and di-valent atoms of gellan gum. This result
depicts the formulations as a better alternative for the quick action and
signifies reduced loss of formulation from eye after administration. The
study concluded that the in-situ formulation of GA was relatively clear
than SA due to its structured matrix formation and characteristic white
color resulting into transparent gel formation and qualifying the use of
GA formulations for transparent in-situ drops.

4.2.3. Osmolality
The osmolality of GA2 was measured to be 167.33 ± 1.25 mOsm/Kg
which falls in the tolerable range of 200–500 mOsm/Kg. Formulations
GA3 and GA4 demonstrated osmolalities of 186.67 ± 4.99 and 194.33
± 4.78 mOsm/Kg, respectively. The acceptable range of osmolality is
160–354 mOsm/kg. It has been suggested that hypo-osmolar lubricants
may be more effective at lowering tear film osmolarity than iso-osmolar
drops. Hypo-osmolar lubricants is reported to be more effective at
reducing tear film hyperosmolarity over time, as well as symptoms.

4.2.5. In-vitro –ex-vivo correlation
As reported in above results, LEP2 displayed a cumulative in-vitro
release of 0.7167 × 10−4 mol/cm2 for LEP2 in comparison to 0.6313 ×
10−4 mol/cm2 for LLN whereas the e.v. release was found to be 0.6042
× 10−4 mol/cm2. The release of the drug from LLN was rapid in first
hour due to presence of surface drug and elevated concentration
gradient between the contributor and the receiving compartment [37].
Further, when LEP2 release was compared with LLN, it was observed
that the release significantly decreased by 10 % owing to the presence of
helical matrix structure formed due to the interaction of gellan gum with
tear fluid cations. Moreover, tear film consists of 3 layers including
corneal fat layer, liquid components and mucin layers. Mucin is
considered as the main interaction protein of ocular surface and results
in improved drug retention and mucoadhesion [38]. When HA is com­
bined with Eudragit-PLGA polymers, there is a significant increase in
mucin binding due to positive zeta potential of Eudragit and hence the e.
v. release is hindered due to enhanced retention of nanoparticles to the
surface as well as gellan gum binding with tear fluid.
4.2.6. Histopathological studies
Except for group I, all mice were treated with 5 μL PBS containing
0.2 % benzalkonium chloride twice a day for inducing dry eye. After
inducing dry eyes, the animals were tested using phenol thread test the
induction of dry eyes where the length of damp thread was compared
before and after induction of dry eyes. The marketed formulation was
introduced b.i.d. ~12h apart and LLN was administered once daily with
an interval of 24h using a positive displacement micropipette for 15
days. On 16th day of drug administration, corneas were withdrawn from
all mice from each group and subjected to histopathological study to
understand and observe the healing effect of the formulation on dry eye
syndrome.
Later, for histopathological studies the samples were subjected to
H&E stain for recognition of corneal inflammation markers as well
changes in lens and it was detected that BAC-induced DED occasioned in
amplified corneal epithelial width and inflammatory cell infiltration as
well as edema beneath the epithelium. The group treated with LLN
demonstrated an enhancement in the histopathologic conclusions like
increase in corneal epithelial width and corneal thickness outline as well
as reduced inflammatory infiltration. The histopathological findings
displayed in Fig. 5(A) and (B), the cornea and lens displayed improve­
ment in the corneal infiltration and inflammation when compared to the
negative control group indicating the reduction in inflammation
markers causing damage to the corneal surface.
5. Conclusions
The study reports the formulation and characterization of LEP2
nanoparticles for treatment of dry eye syndrome. The results indicate
that the varying concentration of PLGA affected the PS and ZP of the
nanoparticles as well as displayed increased %EE. The nanoparticles
displayed an EE, PS and ZP of 90.45 ± 0.05 %, 393 ± 1.42 nm and 2.44
± 0.02 mV, respectively which improves the corneal binding of nano­
particles to mucin present on ocular surface. The in-vitro profiles of
nanoparticles and final formulations were 88.22 ± 0.01 % and 77.72 ±
0.01 %, respectively signifying the sustained release of lifitegrast and
reduced frequency of dosing. The formulation significantly healed the
corneal infiltration caused due to induction of dry eye and demonstrated
increase in corneal thickness, signifying the improvement in dry eye
condition and healing in the murine model. These outcomes specify that
LEP when compared to the available marketed formulation, show the
better prospects as drug delivery systems in dry eye syndrome.
