A B S T R A C T
Present investigation examines the simultaneous encapsulation and delivery of DOX and NAR through PLGA-NPs
and CS-coated PLGA-NPs to improve the efficacy and synergistic anticancer activity of DOX, and reduce its
toxicity. Drug-loaded F3 and F4 were prepared by double-emulsion solvent-evaporation technique. PS, PDI, ZP
and morphology of the NPs were determined using Zetasizer and SEM, respectively. %EE and %DL of both drugs
were determined by indirect method. Dialysis tubing was used for in vitro release studies in phosphate buffer
saline (pH 6.8) with SLS (0.1% w/v). Storage stability of the NPs was performed at 30 ◦C for 6-months. In vitro
cytotoxicity was determined by MTT-assay, using MCF-7 cells. Cytotoxic effects of the NPs were also observed by
visualizing the morphological changes in MCF-7 cells. PS, PDI and ZP of F4 were 366.8 ± 36.7 nm, 0.352 ± 0.033
and +35.57 ± 4.25 mV, respectively and those of F3 were 302.2 ± 45.4 nm, 0.283 ± 0.016 and −4.69 ± 2.66
mV. Increased PS and high positive ZP were found after CS-coating. Smooth-surfaced PLGA-NPs were observed
by SEM, and slightly rough-surfaced NPs were found after CS-coating. %EE and %DL for DOX were 66.01%,
4.26% and 76.26%, 3.28% in F3 and F4, respectively. For NAR, these values were 77.82%, 3.22% and 71.85%,
4.64% in F3 and F4. The in vitro release profile revealed a sustained delivery of the drugs with 51% and 63% of
DOX, as well as 80% and 78% of NAR at 48 h, from F3 and F4, respectively. Relatively high amounts of DOX and
NAR were rapidly released from DOX-NAR-AqS at 2 h only. Release kinetic investigations justify the sustained
release property of F3 and F4, and they followed the “Korsmeyer-Peppas model”. Release-exponent values
suggest that the mechanism of the drug(s) release was Fickian-diffusion. Stability study revealed acceptable
physicochemical changes for 6-months F4 showed the highest cytotoxicity (IC50, 1.52 μg/mL), when compared to
F3, free DOX-NAR combination and DOX alone. Cytotoxicity and cell morphology represented the highest
cellular uptake of F4. Conclusively, F4 could be an effective nano-carrier for DOX and NAR to improve their
encapsulation, sustain their release, stability and simultaneous delivery against MCF-cells.
1. Introduction
As an anthracycline antibiotic, doxorubicin is one of the most
commonly used chemotherapeutic agents in various types of cancer
therapies [1]. The hydrochloride salt is the crystalline form of doxoru­
bicin (DOX), which is hydrophilic as well as hygroscopic, and favors the
good bioavailability of the drug. DOX inhibits DNA and RNA synthesis
by blocking replication and transcription in the dividing cells and
generating free radicals (iron-facilitated), which causes destruction of
DNA, proteins and cell membranes [2]. Apart from the potent thera­
peutic effects, DOX has a poor safety profile, in which cardiotoxity and
myelosuppression are its most harmful side effects. The drawbacks of
DOX
can
be
reduced
using
the
nano-carriers
(e.g.
polymeric-nanoparticles), which not only sustain and control the de­
livery of entrapped drugs but also provide a targeted delivery.

Nanotechnology and nanomedicine have shown a marked role for
targeted delivery approaches in cancer therapy [3]. Tumor targeting is
usually achieved through what is known as enhanced permeability and
retention (EPR) effect [4]. Compared to the rapid elimination of drugs
from conventional dosage forms, nano-carriers provide a sustained,
controlled and targeted delivery from a few days to months [5]. By
applying nanomedicine approaches, the simultaneous delivery of DOX
and other anticancer agents/bioactive directly to the tumor cells can
lead to synergistic effects. The DOX-loaded PEG-PLGA-gold NPs was
developed to facilitate the combined therapies (i.e. chemo and heat) by
near-IR radiation [6]. In another study, DOX was conjugated to
hydrazone-bond to entrapped Imiquimod (immunotherapeutic agent)
[7]. Also, DOX and sorafenib was co-delivered by developing a nano­
composite (DOX-containing PVA nano-core and human serum
albumin-sorafenib nano-shell), which has been reported against hepa­
tocellular carcinoma [8]. Similarly, naringenin-loaded PLGA-DOX-NPs
was developed for simultaneous delivery of DOX and naringenin using a
single nano-carrier for improved efficacy, as well as better synergistic
and reduced toxic effects of DOX [9].
Naringin (NAR) is a flavanone-glycoside with two rhamnose sugars
in its structure, connected to naringenin [10]. The flavonoids are a
family of polyphenolic phytochemicals, which have numerous phar­
macological actions such as antioxidant, anti-inflammatory and anti­
cancer
activities
[11].
NAR
(4’,
5,
7-
trihydroxyflavanone-7-rhamnoglucoside) is abundant in citrus fruits.
Citrus fruits (e.g., oranges and grapefruits), other plant species (e.g.,
flowers and black elderberry) [12], and fruits of Grewia asiatica [13] are
among the important sources of aglycone (naringenin) and glycoside
(NAR) flavanones [14].
Biodegradable polymeric nano-carriers loaded with antitumor drugs
are among the most successful approaches in minimizing their toxic
effects [15]. Being a biocompatible, biodegradable, and low toxic
material, PLGA is one of the most frequently used polymers to prepare
nanoparticulate carriers for therapeutic cancer treatments and other
drug deliveries [16]. PLGA has been approved and regarded as safe by
European Medicines Agency and the US Food and Drug Administration
for drug delivery systems [17,18]. The developed NPs were further
coated with low molecular weight chitosan (CS). CS is one of the most
frequently used non-toxic, naturally-occurring polysaccharide polymers.
CS is used as a nano-carrier for the targeted delivery of chemothera­
peutic agents [19]. CS-coating by electrostatic deposition on PLGA-NPs
was found to be an efficient technique for the sustained release of
encapsulated drugs, and for enhancing the cellular internalization of
PLGA-NPs with their improved therapeutic index [20,21]. A blend of CS
(cationic) and alginate (anionic)-coated PLGA-NPs was developed for
the controlled release and photostability of resveratrol [22]. The
CS-coated PLGA-nanoparticles were prepared for improved apoptotic
activity of resveratrol in the treatment of lung cancer [18]. The
CS-coated PLGA-NPs encapsulated with paclitaxel [23,24] and epi­
rubicin
[25,26]
were
developed
for
cancer
therapy.
Dexamethasone-loaded CS-coated PLGA-NPs was evaluated for its
internalization by mouse fibroblast and C3A cells [27], exendin [28] for
intestinal delivery and diosmin [29] to prolong the gastric retention of
NPs and potentiate the anti-ulcer activity of the drug.
However, there are no available reports regarding CS-coated PLGA-
NPs and co-loading of DOX and NAR for improved anticancer activity of
DOX in breast cancer cell lines (MCF-7). Therefore, in the present
investigation, DOX and NAR-loaded PLGA-NPs were prepared to
improve the efficacy and synergetic effect of DOX, and to reduce its
toxicity equally or superiorly to their monotherapy. PLGA-NPs were
prepared using a double-emulsion solvent-evaporation method. To
achieve the set objectives, the developed NPs were characterized by
determining the particle size, PDI, zeta-potential, and surface
morphology. From this process, in vitro release profiles and release ki­
netics of the encapsulated drugs (DOX and NAR) were determined. The
effect of CS-coating and use of mannitol as a cryoprotectant were
assessed on the physical characteristics of the NPs, including the
encapsulation efficiencies and aggregation behavior of the NPs. The
time-dependent evaluation (stability) of the physical characteristics, and
the cumulative drug(s) release from the uncoated and CS-coated PLGA-
NPs, were also performed on the stored samples for up to 6 months (kept
at 30 ◦C). In vitro cell viability and morphological changes in the incu­
bated cells, with the drug containing formulations (DOX-aqueous solu­
tion, DOX-NAR-aqueous suspension and the developed PLGA-NPs), were
analyzed using MCF-7 (breast cancer cell lines). MCF-7 was chosen as
the model cell line because breast cancer is one of the leading causes of
cancer-related deaths, and MCF-7 is one of the most common types of
cancer cells.

2.2. Methods
2.2.1. Formulation development
PLGA-NPs were prepared through double-emulsion solvent-evapo­
ration (w/o/w) [30–32]. The ingredients with their quantities are
mentioned in Table 1. In brief, PLGA (100 mg) and NAR (5 mg) were
dissolved in 0.5 mL of dichloromethane (DCM) and added drop-by-drop
with continuous magnetic stirring to 5 mL of 0.25%–0.5% aqueous so­
lution of polyvinyl alcohol (PVA), where DOX (5 mg) was previously
dissolved with magnetic stirring (at the speed of 800 rpm). The mixture
was then homogenized at 21,000 rpm for 5 min (IKA®-WERKE, GMBH
& Co., Germany). Approximately 15 mL of PVA solution was slowly
added to the obtained primary emulsion with continued magnetic stir­
ring, followed by homogenization and pulsative sonication (Sonics &
Materials, Inc. Newtown, USA) at 40% power for 60 s (10 s each cycle) in
an ice bath. The chitosan (CS) coated PLGA-NPs was prepared as re­
ported [33–35]. Here, 30 mg of CS was previously added to 5 mL of the
aqueous phase (0.25–0.5%, PVA) and 1%, v/v glacial acetic acid was
used in the aqueous phase for proper solubilization of the CS. The
remaining process was the same as for the PLGA-NPs, mentioned above.
The organic solvent (DCM) was evaporated by overnight magnetic
stirring. The resultant drug(s) loaded PLGA-NPs were purified and
collected by washing (three times) with Milli-Q water through ultra­
centrifugation (30,000 rpm, 10 ◦C for 30 min). The collected pellets
were redispersed in 5 mL of Milli-Q water [35]. To evaluate the
comparative release profiles, the suspension of the DOX and NAR was
prepared in Milli-Q water containing 0.5%, w/v, PVA as a suspending
agent.
2.2.2. Freeze-drying of drug-loaded uncoated- and CS-coated NPs
The 5 mL dispersion of drug(s) loaded PLGA-NPs (uncoated and
coated) were filtered through a 450 μ Millipore filter unit and freeze-
dried using a FreeZone-4.5 Freeze Dry System (Labconco Corporation,
Kansas City, MO, USA). For the CS-coated NPs, 2.5%, w/v of mannitol
was added as cryoprotectant before freeze-drying [32]. Freeze-drying
was completed after 24 h at −50 ◦C, and at 0.01 mm Hg of pressure.
The freeze-dried products were stored at −80 ◦C for further studies.
2.3. Chromatographic analysis of DOX and NAR
A reverse phase (RP) high-performance liquid chromatography
(HPLC) with UV-detection (dual wavelength) was used for the quanti­
fication of DOX (at 254 nm) [36] and NAR (at 282 nm) [37]. The Wa­
ters® HPLC system (1500-series controller, USA) was used, which was
equipped with a Waters® UV-detector (2489, dual absorbance detector,
USA), Waters® binary pump (1525, USA), Waters® automated sampling
system (2707 Autosampler, USA). The HPLC system was monitored by
“Breeze software”. Both drugs (DOX and NAR) were analyzed by
injecting 30 μL of the supernatant into the analytical column. A C18
column (Macherey-Nagel 150 × 4.6 mm, 5 μm) at room temperature
was used. The mobile phase consisted of 60:40 v/v of acetonitrile and
Milli-Q water (the pH of water was adjusted to 3.2 by Orthophosphoric
acid), and was pumped isocratically at 1 mL/min of flow rate. The total
run time was 8 min.
2.4. Characterization
2.4.1. Particle size, PDI, zeta-potential determination
Particle size, size-distribution and the PDI of the purified PLGA-NPs
(uncoated and CS coated) were measured by dynamic light scattering
(DLS), using Zetasizer Nano-Series (Nano-ZS90, Malvern Instruments,
England, UK). The zeta-potentials of the NPs were also checked by the
same instrument, which measures the electrophoretic mobility of the
particles by DTS-Version 4.1 software (Malvern, England), following the
reported method [38].
2.4.2. Scanning electron microscopy
The surface morphology of the NPs was evaluated with scanning
electron microscopy (SEM) [31,38]. The samples were coated with gold
using an Ion Sputter, at 20 mA for 6 min. The observation was performed
at 5 kV accelerating voltage. The working distance was 6.5 mm and the
magnification power was set at 54–58 KX.
2.4.3. Attenuated total reflectance (ATR)-FTIR analysis
The attenuated total reflectance (ATR) FTIR spectra of solid and
dried samples were recorded using Bruker ALPHA spectrometer (Bruker
Optics, Germany). The spectrometer was equipped with a diamond
prism with single bounce reflection ATR accessory. A thin layer of solid
sample was kept over the ATR accessory and the spectra was recorded
using the OPUS software (version 7.8) in the wavenumber range of
400–4000 cm−1.
2.4.4. Encapsulation efficiency and drug loading capacity
For drug encapsulation and loading capacity, 10 mg of drug-loaded
NPs was dissolved in the mixture of 1:1 v/v mixture of acetone and
DCM by magnetic stirring. The solvents were then evaporated [31]. The
residue obtained was dissolved in 1:1 v/v mixture of methanol and
ethanol by stirring and sonication. The obtained suspension was
centrifuged at 13,500 rpm, for 10 min at 4 ◦C. The supernatant was
collected, and the encapsulated DOX and NAR were quantified with the
HPLC-UV method (as mentioned above). The same process was applied
to determine the loading capacity in the CS uncoated and coated
PLGA-NPs. The encapsulation efficiency (EE %) and drug loading ca­
pacity (DL %) in percentage, were calculated with the following equa­
tions (Eqs. 1 and 2).

2.4.5. In vitro drug release and data analysis
On the basis of physicochemical characteristics, two formulations
(F3 and F4) were chosen for the in vitro release experiment. The release
profile of the two drugs from these formulations was then compared
with the release profile of the drugs from the aqueous suspension of DOX
and NAR (DOX-NAR-AqS). The dialysis bag method was used to conduct
the in vitro release studies [32,36,39]. Briefly, 2 mL of each
NPs-suspension (freeze-dried NPs were suspended in Milli-Q water) as
well as DOX-NAR-AqS were transferred into the dialysis bags (MWCO
12 KDa). Both ends of the dialysis bag were closed tightly and placed in
the beakers containing phosphate buffer saline with 0.1%, w/v, Sodium
Lauryl Sulfate (SLS). The overall pH was kept at 6.8, as release medium
for both drugs. Three beakers containing 50 mL of release medium for
each formulation were used for this experiment. All beakers were placed
in water (allowed to be shaken at 100 rpm and maintained at 37 ◦C). The
infusion of drugs from the bags into the release medium was assayed by
taking the samples at different time points. The obtained samples were
centrifuged (13,500 rpm for 10 min at 4 ◦C) to collect the supernatants,
and were analyzed with the HPLC-UV method (as mentioned above).

The data obtained from the in vitro release experiments were fitted
into different release kinetic model equations: zero-order, first-order,
Higuchi-matrix, Hixson-Crowell and Korsmeyer-Peppas. The best-fitted
model for the release of DOX and NAR from the uncoated and CS-

also known as the release/diffusion-exponent, was calculated to deter­

2.4.6. Stability of drug-loaded NPs
The stability study of drug-loaded PLGA-NPs (uncoated and CS
coated) was conducted as per the reported methods [38,42]. Approxi­
mately 10 mg of freeze-dried NPS was packed into tightly-capped glass
containers, and stored for 6 months at 30 ± 1 ◦C [as per the climatic zone
of Saudi Arabia (IVa), which is the recommended temperature for
long-term stability]. The time-dependent changes in the mean
particle-size, PDI, zeta-potential, encapsulation efficiency and in vitro
drug release were monitored periodically (at 7th day, 1, 3 and 6 months)
to check the stability of the developed formulations.
2.5. In vitro cytotoxicity studies
2.5.1. Maintenance and growth of cell line
The cell line (MCF-7) was cultured at 37 ◦C under 5% CO2, using the
Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM) (UFC Biotech, Riyadh
KSA). The MCF-7 media was supplemented with 1% mixture of
Penicillin-Streptomycin (ThermoFischer Scientific, USA), 10% fetal
bovine serum (FBS, Alpha Chemika, India) and 1% of L-glutamine
(BioWest, USA). The MTT was purchased from Sigma Aldrich, MO, USA.
2.5.2. MTT assay
The activities of DOX-Aq-Sol, DOX-NAR-Aq-suspension, DOX-NAR-
loaded PLGA-NPs (F3) and DOX-NAR-loaded CS-coated PLGA-NPs
(F4) against MCF-7 were investigated by measuring the cells’ viability.
The MTT assay was performed with enzymatic reduction of [3-(4, 5-
dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2, 5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide], a water-
soluble dye into an insoluble formazan. The quantitative measurement
of formazan indicates the relative viable cells [43,44]. The cells were
seeded in a flat-bottomed 96 well microplate in 100 μL medium, and
were left overnight to grow. Subsequently, four types (DOX-Aq-Sol,
DOX-NAR-Aq-suspension, F3 and F4) of solutions/suspensions were
prepared. The concentration of DOX ranged from 0.25 μg/mL to 8
μg/mL using the same media as solvent. Afterward, the medium was
removed and the cells were exposed to the prepared drug-loaded for­
mulations for 48 h. The control sample consisted of the appropriate cells
immersed in the medium only. After 48 h of exposition, the cells were
treated with 100 μL of the MTT solution (5 mg/mL in PBS). The medium
from the overnight grown cells were replaced by medium containing no
drugs, which were served as a negative control for 72 h. Later, cells were
treated with 20 μL of MTT (5 mg/mL in PBS) and incubated for 4 h at
37 ◦C. Subsequently, the culture fluid was discarded and MTT crystals
were dissolved in a mixture of DMSO, acetic acid and sodium dodecyl
sulfate (99.4 mL: 0.6 mL: 10 g) at an ambient temperature for 15 min.
The absorbance was measured at 570 nm, using a spectrophotometric
microplate reader (Synergy HT, BioTek Instruments, VT, USA). The
percentage cell viability was calculated using equation (Eq. (4)). The
results were normalized compared to those obtained for viable control
cells (taken as 100%) and presented as plots of viability versus log
concentration of drug(s). The IC50 value was determined for the used cell
line against each formulation in GraphPad Prism 5, using nonlinear

2.5.3. Morphological changes in MCF-7 cells
The MCF-7 cells (1 X 107) per well were seeded in 6-well plates
containing DMEM, and were incubated at 37 ◦C in 5% CO2 for 24 h in
order to study the phase contrast microscopy. After adherence, cells in
each well were treated with different formulations containing 2.5 μg/mL
of DOX and were incubated for 48 h. The cells were observed on phase
contrast microscopes (Olympus CLX 41) for the morphological changes,
to record apoptosis and photographs.
2.5.4. Statistical analysis
The experiments were performed in triplicate (n = 3) and the data
were expressed as mean with (±) standard deviation (SD). The data were
analyzed and compared by Paired t-test using GraphPad Prism 5, USA,
where p < 0.05 was considered as statistically significant.
3. Results and discussion

developed two formulations of PLGA-NPs (F1 and F3), and two
CS-coated PLGA-NPs (F2 and F4) as represented in Table 2. The signif­

tion of PVA increases, particle size was increased with the constant
amount of PLGA. Particle size was significantly increased (p < 0.05)
after CS-coating at 0.25% PVA, while no significant increase (p < 0.05)
in size was observed even after CS-coating at 0.5% PVA. The concen­
tration of PVA increased, which resulted in an increase in %DL at the
same amount of PLGA. These increases are evident in F3 (0.25%PVA)
compared to F1 (0.25% PVA), and F4 (0.5%PVA) compared to F2
(0.25% PVA). After coating with CS (30 mg), a slight increase in %DL
was observed in F4 compared to F2, which agrees with the previous
report [34].
In general, CS coating on NPs are achieved by incubation of NPs into
the CS aqueous solution. In the present investigation, as the PLGA-NPs
were developed by emulsification-solvent evaporation technique, the
CS coating was done by surface adsorption (steric/electrical interaction)
[45]. The CS was used at the time of PLGA-NPs preparation. Here, 30 mg
of CS was added in the aqueous phase (containing DOX) to interact with
the PLGA present in the organic phase containing the NAR, which was
also followed for the surface modification of PLGA-NPs by CS, loaded
with a lipophilic drug Carmustine [46]. The CS coating of PLGA-NPs
caused the surface modification (from slightly negative to high posi­
tive) of the developed NPs, and improved their physicochemical prop­
erties with a slight increase in mean particle size of the PLGA-NPs. The
high positive zeta-potential was found with the CS-coated PLGA-NPs,
which was indicative of the proper coating to get the cationic-shell NPs.
Moreover, low negative zeta potential of F1 (−1.25 mV) was converted
to a high psitive zeta potential in F2 (+38.83 mV), while a slightly more
negative zeta potential of F3 (−4.69 mV) was converted to a slightly less
positive zeta potential (+35.57 mV) for F4, indicating the efficient
CS-coating on F2 and F4.

3.2. Freeze-drying and cryoprotection
For stable and long-term storage shelf-life, the NPs were freeze-dried.
The PLGA-NPs were free flowing after freeze-drying, but the CS-coated
NPs were aggregated and sticky due to the bio-adhesive property of
the naturally occurring polysaccharide, CS. Therefore, mannitol as
cryoprotectant/bio-protectants was used to prevent the aggregation of
the CS-PLGA-NPs and to reduce mechanical stress during freeze-drying
at low temperatures and pressures. Thus, to provide good flow prop­
erty, complete preservation and proper re-dispersion of CS-coated NPs,
2.5% (w/v) of mannitol as cryoprotectant was used. The amorphous
state of mannitol after solubilization and freeze-drying provided
“pseudo-hydration” to the CS-coated PLGA-NPs, which might be
attributed to the hydrogen-bonding interaction between mannitol and
CS adsorbed on the surfaces of the NPs [41,47]. The “pseudo-hydration”
provided a shielding-effect from the ice crystals which may damage the
CS-coated PLGA-NPs during freeze-drying and when the re-dispersion of
the NPs will be needed.
In the present investigation, an optimum concentration of mannitol
(2.5%, w/v) was chosen as a cryoprotectant, based on the previous
report [34,41]. The freeze-dried CS-coated PLGA-NPs with 2.5% (w/v)
mannitol were compared for its size, PDI, zeta potential, %EE of DOX
and NAR as well as aggregations of the NPs (observed under light mi­
croscope). The observation suggested a slight increase in particle size.
The PDI was low compared to the NPs without cryoprotection. Also, the
surface charges were not altered due to mannitol (used as cryoprotec­
tant). The freeze-dried F2 and F4 have shown almost equal encapsula­
tion of both drugs. More importantly, the lowest magnitude of
aggregation of the CS-coated PLGA-NPS was found after the addition of
cryoprotectant, which is evidenced by the lower PDI values (Table 3).
3.3. Characterization of the nanoparticles
3.3.1. Particle size, PDI and zeta potential
The average particle size of the optimized CS-coated PLGA-NPs i.e.,
F4 (366.8 ± 36.7 nm) with an average PDI value of (0.352 ± 0.033) and
its average zeta potential distribution (+35.57 ± 4.25 mV) are repre­
sented in Fig. 1a and b, respectively. Similarly, the mean particle size of
the optimized PLGA-NPs (F3) without CS-coating was (302.2 ± 45.4
nm), with the mean PDI value of (0.283 ± 0.016) and its average zeta
potential distribution was (−4.69 ± 2.66 mV) are represented in Fig. 1c
and d, respectively. Although the PDI values for all developed NPs are
not too low, the visual appearance of the colloidal system and its size
distribution curve indicate homogenous dispersion of the formed NPs
(uncoated or CS-coated).
A low negative surface charge of the drug(s) loaded PLGA-NPs was
converted to a high positive upon CS-coating, due to the electrostatic
deposition of positive amino groups of CS on to the negatively charged
surface of the PLGA-NPs. The high positive zeta potentials of F2 and F4
indicate the film coating by the cationic layer (amino groups) of CS over
the surfaces of the PLGA-NPs, which agree with previous reports [34,48,
49]. The high magnitude of zeta potential (either positive or negative)
signifies good stability of the colloidal system containing the suspended
CS-coated PLGA-NPs [50,51]. The narrow range variations in zeta po­
tentials of F1 and F3 (−1.25 mV to −4.69 mV) and F2 and F4 (+38.83
mV to +35.57 mV) might be due to remaining traces of PVA, which
could not be washed out completely during the ultracentrifugation from
the surface of NPs due to its strong surface-adsorption and their steri­
cally stabilization [2].
The induction of high positive zeta potential of the PLGA-NPs due to
CS-coating may be the best approach to preparing the sustained release
of DOX and NAR together. This can enhance the cellular uptake of the
NPs carrier, which in turn would improve the therapeutic potential of
the drugs [52,53] as well as the therapeutic index of the carrier [21].
Particle size analysis indicates that the average size of the PLGA-NPs
would have an obvious effect due to CS coating with a statistically sig­
nificant difference (p < 0.05) between the uncoated and CS-coated
PLGA-NPs. For the CS-coated NPs, the increased size might be exclu­
sively attributed to the presence of CS coating around the NPs. Also, the
enlarged size of CS-coated NPs might be due to the increased viscosity of
the CS-containing aqueous phase that interferes with the interfacial
hydrodynamic phenomena (responsible for an impulsive emulsification)
among the NPs. This may increase the hydrodynamic diameter of the CS-
coated PLGA-NPs [54].
A relatively smaller size of the PLGA-NPs might be due to the use to
PVA as a stabilizer, which helped generate smaller-sized particles, as
PVA formed a boundary around the droplets during emulsification that
restricted the aggregation of the dispersed particles. Secondly, the
organic solvent (DCM) reduced the average particle size because of
reduced viscosity of the organic phase and increased dilution of PLGA.
Moreover, magnetic stirring, followed by homogenization and probe
sonication favor the shear force to generate the smaller uncoated PLGA-
NPs [51]. The size of the NPs is an important property. The nanoscale
size range of the anticancer-containing carriers (PLGA-NPs/CS-coated
PLGA-NPs) can provide an enhanced tumor cell accumulation by
exploiting the EPR effect [54].

3.3.4. Encapsulation and drug loading
The encapsulation of DOX was 60.64% and 66.01% in cases of F1 and
F3 (PLGA-NPs), respectively, while it was 63.73% and 76.26% in the
cases of F2 and F4 (CS-coated PLGA-NPs), respectively. The %DL for
DOX was 2.91% and 4.26% in F1 and F3, and 3.18% and 3.28% in F2
and F4, respectively. For NAR, the encapsulation were 60.51% and
77.82% in F1 and F3, while 65.45% and 71.85% was found with F2 and
F4, respectively. The %DL for NAR was 2.64% and 3.22% in F1 and F3,
while it was 2.24% and 4.64% in F2 and F4, respectively.
No significant difference in the encapsulation of DOX was found
between F1 and F3. A significant difference (p < 0.05) was noted be­
tween F2 and F4, however. A significant difference (p < 0.05) in the
encapsulation of NAR was noted between F1 and F3, but there was no
significant difference between F2 and F4. Observations regarding the
encapsulation of DOX might be due to its high aqueous solubility (a
hydrochloride salt was used) in the aqueous phase containing PVA. The
low aqueous solubility of NAR might influence its encapsulation. During
the CS-coating, the acidic aqueous phase (due to the presence of acetic
acid) might increase the solubility of both drugs, which in turn enhanced
their encapsulation [17]. Similar changes in the %DL were observed for
DOX and NAR in the four optimized formulation, which was also
attributed to their aqueous solubility in the presence (F2 and F4) and
absence (F1 and F3) of acidified aqueous phases [2].
The encapsulation efficiency and drug-loading capacity are among
the most controlling factors for deciding the efficiency of the PLGA-
based NPs. The highly viscous, organic phase containing PLGA pre­
vents drug(s) movement towards the aqueous phase, subsequently
entrapping more drug(s) and loading in the polymer matrix [65]. The
encapsulation of both drugs was comparatively higher in the CS-coated
PLGA-NPs, which harmonized the previous report [66]. The presence of
PVA (stabilizer) and CS together in the aqueous phase increased the
viscosity of the aqueous phase, which eliminated the loading of DOX and
NAR at the interface of the two phases. This resulted in efficient loading
of the drugs [51,67].

0 and 0.5 for the two formulations (Table 5), suggesting a Fickian-
diffusion type of release mechanism.

the diffusion of aqueous release media into the polymer matrix. This
simultaneously causes the swelling as well as dissolution/erosion of the
matrix. This swelling and erosion can be evidenced in the present
investigation, as both the nanocarriers (F3 and F4) exhibited sustained
and controlled release profiling of both encapsulated drugs, which can
be substantiated by a previous report [68].

3.3.6. Stability of the NPs
The results of the stability study indicate that the NPs demonstrated
acceptable physicochemical changes under the mentioned storage con­
dition for 6 months (Table 5). No obvious changes were noticed in the
mean particle size, PDI, zeta-potential, encapsulation efficiency and in
vitro drug(s) release up to 1 month. Some minor changes in the physi­
ochemical characteristics were found at 3 and 6 months in both NPs (F3
and F4). A gradual increase in particle size was found, especially in the
CS-coated PLGA-NPs, which was attributed to the moisture adsorption
and swelling property of CS. The largest particle size occurred at 6
months (378.5 ± 36.8 nm), which is still acceptable. A similar finding
was reported in the storage stability of CS-coated PLGA-NPs [29].
However, only a minor increase in size was noted for PLGA-NPs (F3).
Almost equal values of PDI were noted compared to the initial values,
which was a good indicator of the stable colloidal dispersion even after
long term storage. It also indicated the absence of any thoughtful ag­
gregation of the NPs. This was in agreement with previous findings of
low PDI of CS-coated PLGA-NPs even after 12 months of storage [71].
Slightly decreased values of zeta-potential were observed in the CS-
coated NPs, which can be assumed due to the trapping of the cryopro­
tectant (mannitol) on the surfaces of the NPs. Upon storage, however,
these NPs may come closer with each other, which increases their size.
In the present investigation, upon storage, a slight increase in size of CS-
PLGA-NPs was found. This caused a decrease in the overall surface area,
which might lead to a slight decrease in their zeta-potentials. A similar
remark was found in a reported study, where the zeta-potential of CS-
coated NPs decreased even at 1 month storage [18]. Although, no
obvious changes in zeta-potential were noted with the uncoated
PLGA-NPs. This finding was also reported in a previous experiment [72].
A slightly greater reduction was found in the encapsulation and the
cumulative release of DOX and NAR from F4, compared to F3 at 3 and 6
months. This might be either due to leakage of the encapsulated drugs
from the core of the NPs, or to the moisture adsorption characteristics of
CS. This reduction resulted in a surface fracture of the NPs and degra­
dation of the polymer-matrix that lead to the drug expulsion [73].

4. Conclusion and future prospects
The physicochemical, morphological and in vitro release character­
istics exhibited that simultaneous encapsulation of NAR and DOX into
PLGA-NPs and CS-PLGA-NPs were successfully developed by
emulsification-solvent-evaporation. The reported HPLC-UV method was
effective for analyzing DOX and NAR at dual wavelengths. The in vitro
release profiling illustrated the sustained release of DOX and NAR in
phosphate buffer, with 0.1% SLS as release medium (pH 6.8) for up to
48 h. Release kinetics investigation indicated that the sustained release
of DOX and NAR followed a “Korsmeyer-Peppas model”, primarily from
F3 and F4. The release-exponent value suggested the Fickian diffusion
mechanism of the DOX and NAR release from both the NPs. Shelf storage
at 30 ◦C for 6 months indicated good physical stability of F3 and F4. The
in vitro cytotoxicity study against MCF-7 cells resulted in the lowest
value of IC50, when the cells were incubated with F4. Therefore, ac­
cording to the observed IC50 values, the activity of developed formula­
tions against the employed cells can be arranged as F4 > F3 > DOX-
NAR-AqS > DOX-Aq-Sol. Moreover, the cytotoxicity seen by changes in
the morphology of treated cells signifies enhanced efficacy of F4 than
that of other formulations and free DOX. Thus, use of DOX and NAR
loaded CS-coated PLGA-NPs will reduce the amount of drug required to
treat cancer. This reduction will eventually reduce the associated side
effects of the drugs. Potential application of the developed PLGA-NPs
can be evaluated further by in vivo pharmacodynamic studies.
