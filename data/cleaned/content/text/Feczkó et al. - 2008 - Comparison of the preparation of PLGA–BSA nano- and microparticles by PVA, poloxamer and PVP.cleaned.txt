## Abstract
The objective of our study was to prepare nano- and microparticles economically considering some practical parameters such as size and
encapsulation efficiency as well as ability of particle recovery. Bovine serum albumin (BSA) model protein was encapsulated by poly(d,l-lactideco-glycolide) (PLGA) using a multiple water-in-oil-in-water emulsion-solvent evaporation technique. The effect of three surfactants: polyvinyl
alcohol, poloxamer, and polyvinyl pyrrolidone, used in the outer water phase, on the properties of particles was investigated. The emulsifier/PLGA
mass ratio played an important role in the preparation procedure of the particles. This ratio was found to be approximately 1 for polyvinyl alcohol
(PVA) if the aim was to formulate nanoparticles with narrow size distribution (<220 nm), high yield and good encapsulation efficiency (>90%).
Although, a ratio of 2:1 was sufficient to produce submicron particles by poloxamer with high yield, more than 70% and 90% encapsulation
efficiency required minimum 4 and 10 emulsifier/PLGA mass ratio, respectively. Five times more PVA and 10 times more poloxamer than the
PLGA mass were necessary to obtain nanoparticles which were easy to redisperse after centrifugation. Microparticles released more BSA than
nanoparticles prepared by PVA, however, the situation was reverse with poloxamer. Microparticles formulated by polyvinyl pyrrolidone (PVP)
showed the fastest in vitro release.

2.2. Preparation of nano- and microparticles

Nano- and microparticles containing BSA were formulated by a double emulsion-solvent evaporation technique .
Briefly, BSA solution (20-100 mg/ml) in 0.25-1 ml phosphatebuffered saline (PBS, pH 7.4) was added to 2.5-10.0 ml
dichloromethane containing 100-200 mg PLGA. This twophase system was emulsified for 15-60 s with a probe sonicator,
Model W-220 (Heat Systems-Ultrasonics, Inc.) at setting #6 in
an ice bath. The resulting water-in-oil emulsion was further
homogenized into 20 ml of an aqueous solution of emulsifier
(PVA, poloxamer or PVP) for 30-180 s in an ice bath. The emulsifier concentration was changed in a wide range: 0-5% (w/v).
This second homogenization was achieved by a sonicator at setting #8 or by a magnetic stirrer at 800 rpm. The dichloromethane
was evaporated under magnetic stirring at 800 rpm for 2 h. The
nano- and microparticles formed were isolated by centrifugation

3.2. Size of particles

The size of the particles influences substantially their ability to act as drug delivery vehicles. 0.1-m-sized nanoparticles
had about 10-fold higher intracellular uptake in an in vitro cell
culture model compared to 10-m-sized microparticles .
<0.1 m nanoparticles have about 27-fold higher transfection
compared to >0.1 m size nanoparticles . Nanoparticles
have a further advantage over microparticles, because they are
better suited for intravenous delivery. Since the smallest capillaries in the body are 5-6 m in diameter, particles distributed
into the bloodstream must be much smaller than 5 m and they
must not form aggregates . A not negligible benefit of particles, which are smaller than 220 nm, is that they could be easily
sterilized by filtration as the size of bacteria and viruses is bigger.

into the incubation medium was higher for 0.1 m particles
compared to the other sizes. The higher amount of PVA associated with 0.1 m particle could influence the degradation of
the polymer and also the release of the encapsulated protein.
