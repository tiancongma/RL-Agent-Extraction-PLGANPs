Cisplatin is widely used for the treatment of various cancers including cervical, ovarian, lung and head and neck,
however, its clinical success is limited owing to the dose-dependent adverse effects, mainly nephrotoxicity and
neurotoxicity. In order to address this limitation, the present study was undertaken to investigate growth inhib-
itory effect of cisplatin in combination with a triterpenediol (3a, 24-dihydroxyurs-12-ene and 3a, 24-
dihydroxyolean-12-ene, TPD) on human ovarian cancer cell line. Poly(dl-lactic-co-glycolic) acid nanoparticles
loaded with TPD (TPD-PLGA-NPs) were successfully developed by emulsion solvent evaporation method. The
TPD-PLGA-NPs were characterized for size distribution and zeta potential which was in order of 152.56 ±
3.01 nm and −17.36 ± 0.37 mV respectively. The morphological evaluation was carried out by transmission
electron microscopy and the formulation was also characterized using Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy
(FTIR) and differential scanning calorimetry (DSC). The drug loading of the optimized formulation was
51.03 ± 1.52 μg/mg and the formulation exhibited sustained drug release proﬁle. The in vitro cellular uptake
study of coumarin-6 loaded PLGA nanoparticles in OVCAR-5 cells demonstrated a time dependent increase in up-
take efﬁciency. Further, growth inhibitory effect of cisplatin was investigated in combination with TPD-PLGA-
NPs. The combination index (CI) was b1, indicating a synergistic interaction. Further, at 75% of cell growth inhi-
bition (ED75) the dose of cisplatin was reduced to 3.8 folds using this combination. The results indicated the po-
tential of cisplatin and TPD-PLGA-NPs combination in order to reduce to dose limiting toxicities of the former.

1. Introduction
Conventional single drug chemotherapy (mono-therapy) offers lim-
ited accessibility of drug to tumor tissues that requires a higher dose,
leading to adverse effect [1]. Also, repeated treatment with single drug
and administration of higher dose can result in resistance to the chemo-
therapeutics. For a decade, combination chemotherapy has been envis-
aged as a potential strategy to limit the draw-backs associated with
single chemotherapeutic agent. Unlike mono-therapy, combination
chemotherapy offers beneﬁts such as countering different biological tar-
gets in cancer cells, thereby maximizing therapeutic efﬁcacy and re-
duces the adverse effect [2].
Cisplatin is one of the potent chemotherapeutic agents for the treat-
ment of various solid tumors including cervical, ovarian, head and neck,
and lung cancer [3]. The biochemical mechanisms of cisplatin cytotoxic-
ity involve the binding of the drug to guanines base of DNA and forma-
tion of inter-strand and intra-strand DNA cross links. These complexes
may interfere with normal DNA replication and/or transcription mech-
anisms [4]. Besides the binding with DNA, cisplatin may covalently bind
to proteins and other cellular targets, and modulate the activity of en-
zymes, receptors, and other proteins that ends up in cytotoxic phenom-
ena [5]. The severe side-effects, including nephrotoxicity, neurotoxicity,
myelotoxicity, bone-marrow depression and acquired chemo-resis-
tance have limited the usage of cisplatin [6–8]. In order to overcome
the dose-limiting toxicities in clinical use of cisplatin, combined chemo-
therapy with other anticancer agents is a potential option [9]. A number
of drug combination with platinum compounds have been investigated
which improved clinical outcome and exhibited a positive impact on
survival of patient [10–12].
The combinations of cisplatin with the phytochemicals are also re-
ceiving recognition in order to limit cytotoxic manifestation of the

Combination chemotherapy with drugs having different mode of ac-
tion is advantageous compared to monotherapy [22]. As the biological
targets of cisplatin and TPD are different [4,20] and both the agents
are active on the same cancerous cell lines, hence, we investigated
TPD as a potential candidate for combination chemotherapy with cis-
platin. Further, the low aqueous solubility and high lipophilicity of
TPD [21], necessitating the development of appropriate formulation
for intravenous administration for its clinical application. Therefore
TPD loaded PLGA nanoparticles (TPD-PLGA-NPs) were developed and
characterized for various parameters including morphology, particle
size distribution, zeta potential, drug loading and drug release proﬁle.
The developed formulation was also characterized using Fourier trans-
form infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) and differential scanning calorimetry
(DSC). Additionally, the nano-formulation was evaluated for cellular
uptake behavior. Subsequently, in vitro growth inhibition of combina-
tion of cisplatin with TPD or TPD-PLGA-NPs was investigated for possi-
ble synergistic effect in order to address dose associated toxicities of the
cisplatin.
2. Material and methods
2.1. Materials
PLGA (MW 24–38 kDa) was purchased from Boehringer Ingelheim
(Germany). Cisplatin, polyvinyl alcohol (PVA, MW 31–50 kDa),
coumarin-6, dichloromethane (DCM), 4,6-Diamidino-2-phenylindole
dihydrochloride (DAPI), RPMI-1640 medium, sulforhodamine B (SRB)
dye, streptomycin and penicillin were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich
(India). Fetal bovine serum (FBS) was obtained from Gibco Life technol-
ogies, U.S.A. All other chemicals and excipients used were of analytical
grade. Ultrapure water (Millipore, India) was used throughout the
experiments.
2.2. Preparation of triterpenediol 3a, 24-dihydroxyurs-12-ene and 3a, 24-
dihydroxyolean-12-ene (TPD)
The isomeric mixture of TPD was obtained from boswellic acids by
the method as described previously by our group [20]. Brieﬂy, a
weighed amount of mixture of (α + β) boswellic acid was taken in
dry ether and diazomethane solubilized in diethyl ether was dropped
in the reaction mixture, until completion of the reaction. The solvent
of the reaction mixture and excess of diazomethane were removed to
obtain the mixture comprising the methyl esters. Subsequently, the
methyl ester derivative of boswellic acid was dissolved in dry ether
and lithium aluminum hydride (LiAlH4) was added to the reaction mix-
ture and stirred for 1 h under nitrogen conditions. Further, the ethyl ac-
etate was added to the solution to neutralize excess LiAlH4 and the
organic layer was washed with puriﬁed water. The desired product
(3α, 24-dihydroxyurs-12-ene and 3α, 24-dihydroxyolean-12-ene,
TPD) was obtained after concentrating the solvent and crystallized as
white crystals using hexane-ethylacetate solution.
2.3. Preparation and optimization of TPD loaded PLGA nanoparticles (TPD-
PLGA-NPs)
TPD loaded nanoparticles were prepared by emulsion solvent evap-
oration method [23], with minor modiﬁcation [Fig. 1]. The drug and
polymer ratio was optimized by taking different concentration of TPD
and a ﬁxed PLGA (35 mg) concentration, which were subsequently, dis-
solved in dichloromethane and emulsiﬁed (Labsonic M Ultrasonic Ho-
mogenizer, Sartorious, Germany) into aqueous phase containing PVA
(2%, w/v). The emulsion was agitated by magnetic stirrer until complete
evaporation of dichloromethane. The nanoparticles were collected by
centrifugation (19,000 rpm, 15 min; Sigma 3–30 K, Germany), and
reconstituted the pellet with water and centrifuged again. This step
was repeated thrice for washing of nanoparticles. Finally, the pellet
was dispersed in Milli-Q water and freeze dried (Advantage freeze
dryer, VirTis, Gardiner, NY, USA) with mannitol as cryoprotectant. The
freeze dried formulations were stored in desiccators till further use.
2.4. Characterization of nanoparticles
2.4.1. Particle size, polydispersity index and zeta potential measurement
Particle size, polydispersity index and zeta potential of the nanopar-
ticles were determined by dynamic light scattering (Zetasizer Nano
ZS90, Malvern Instruments, UK) at 25 °C after resuspension of formula-
tion in appropriate volume of phosphate buffer saline (PBS, pH 7.4).
Each measurement was performed in triplicate.
2.4.2. Morphological examination
The morphology of TPD loaded PLGA nanoparticles was examined
using transmission electron microscope (TEM, Philips Morgagni-268)
operating at 70 kV. The nanoparticles suspension was sonicated and a
drop of suspension placed on the copper grid. After drying, samples
were treated with phosphotungstic acid (2%) and subsequently, ob-
served under the TEM.
2.4.3. Drug loading
Weighed amount of TPD-PLGA nanoparticles was dissolved in aceto-
nitrile and vigorously vortexed and sonicated. Subsequently, this solu-
tion was centrifuged at 19,000 rpm for 15 min, supernatant was
collected and analyzed by high performance liquid chromatography
(HPLC) [20]. The analysis was performed at 30 °C by a RP-18 column
(E-Merck, 5 μm, 4.0 × 250 mm). The mobile phase was a mixture of
acetonitrile: water (0.5% acetic acid) in 95:05 ratios at a ﬂow rate of
1.5 mL/min and detection was performed at 210 nm.
2.4.4. In vitro release proﬁle
In order to determine the in vitro release pattern of TPD from PLGA
nanoparticles, weighted amount of nanoparticles were dispersed in
phosphate buffer saline (PBS, pH 7.4 and pH 5.5) containing tween-80
(0.5%, v/v) in a microcentrifuge tubes. The tubes were placed in a
water bath shaker incubator (New Brunswick Scientiﬁc, USA) at 37 °C
under gentle agitation. At a deﬁnite time intervals, the tubes were cen-
trifuged (19,000 rpm, 10 min) and the known volume of supernatants
were collected. After sampling, the equal volume of fresh PBS containing
tween-80 (0.5%, v/v) was added to the release media. The collected
samples were analyzed as described above using HPLC.

2.4.5. Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR)
FTIR spectra of TPD, PLGA, physical mixture of TPD and PLGA, and
TPD-PLGA nanoparticles were obtained using FTIR spectrophotometer
(Spectrum RXI, FTIR instrument, Perkin Elmer, USA). Each sample was
triturated separately with IR grade potassium bromide and compressed
into thin pellets. The obtained pellets were scanned over the range of
4000–500 cm−1 and the characteristic peaks of the samples were
recorded.
2.4.6. Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC)
Thermal analysis was performed for TPD, PLGA, physical mixture of
TPD and PLGA, and TPD-PLGA nanoparticles using DSC (Mettler-Toledo
International Inc., Switzerland). Two milligram of each sample was kept
into aluminum crucible and sealed with a standard aluminum lid. The
sample was ﬂushed with nitrogen at a rate of 30 mL/min under an
inert atmosphere and analyzed over a temperature range of 35–300 °C.
The temperature was increased at a rate of 10 °C/min.
2.5. Cell line experiments
2.5.1. Cell culture and growth conditions
Human ovarian cancer cell line (OVCAR-5) was acquired from Na-
tional Cancer Institute (NCI), USA and grown in complete growth medi-
um (RPMI-1640) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum, 100 μg/mL
streptomycin and 100 units/mL penicillin in carbon dioxide incubator
(New Brunswick Scientiﬁc, USA) at 37 °C, 5% CO2 and 98% RH.
2.5.2. In vitro cellular uptake study
2.5.2.1. Preparation of coumarin-6-loaded PLGA nanoparticles. To investi-
gate the in vitro cellular uptake of PLGA nanoparticles, coumarin-6
was used as a ﬂuorescence probe. Nanoparticles containing coumarin-
6 were prepared using the same method described for TPD nanoparti-
cles, except that 20 μL (1 mg/mL) dye was added instead of drug to
the PLGA solution before formation of nanoparticles. Unloaded couma-
rin-6 was removed from nanoparticles by centrifugation (19,000 rpm,
15 min). Finally, coumarin-6-loaded nanoparticles (C6-PLGA-NPs)
were washed, freeze dried and stored for further use.
2.5.2.2. Intracellular uptake of coumarin-6-loaded PLGA nanoparticles. Cel-
lular uptake of nanoparticles was evaluated by confocal laser scanning
microscopy (CLSM) in OVCAR-5 cell line. OVCAR-5 cells were seeded
in 6 well plates over glass cover slips at a density of 1 × 105 cells/well
and incubated overnight. Subsequently, the cells were treated with
ﬁxed concentration of C6-PLGA-NPs (0.125 mg/mL) and incubated for
1, 2 and 4 h. After incubation, the medium was removed and cells
were washed with ice-cold PBS and ﬁxed for 30 min with paraformalde-
hyde (4% in PBS) at room temperature. The formaldehyde solution was
removed and cells were then washed with ice-cold PBS and nuclei were
stained with 4′,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI, 1 μg/mL in PBS).
Finally, the cells were investigated under the confocal laser scanning
microscope (Olympus Fluoview FV1000).
2.5.3. In vitro growth inhibition assay
The sulforhodamine B (SRB) assay was performed to evaluate in
vitro growth inhibition of cisplatin, TPD and TPD-PLGA-NPs and their
combinations in the selected cell line [24]. Brieﬂy, cells suspension
(100 μL) were added in 96-well tissue culture plate at a density of
8000–12,000 cells per well 24 h prior to drug treatment. The cells
were exposed with different concentration of free drug, nano-formula-
tion and their combinations with ﬁxed molar ratio. Following 48 h treat-
ment cells were ﬁxed with ice-cold trichloro acetic acid (50%, 50 μL) in
all the wells for 1 h at 4 °C. The plates were washed with distilled
water and allowed to air-dry, followed by staining with SRB dye
(50 μL of 0.4% in 1% acetic acid) for 30 min at room temperature. Un-
bound dye was removed with acetic acid (1%, v/v). The plates were air
dried and solubilized the bound dye by adding 100 μL of 10 mM Tris
buffer (pH 10.4) to each well. The absorbance was recorded at 540 nm
on microplate reader (BioTek Synergy HT) and IC50 values were obtain-
ed by plotting optical density versus concentration.
2.5.4. Assessment of combination effects
The combined effect of two drugs was analyzed to know the na-
ture of the drug interaction from the median effect principle given
by Chou and Talalay [25], by using the CalcuSyn software (Version
2.11, Biosoft). The IC50 values obtained from single drug treatment
cell growth inhibition assays were used to design the combination
experiments. The cells were treated with cisplatin, TPD and TPD-
PLGA-NPs alone or in combination (cisplatin:TPD-PLGA-NPs or
cisplatin:TPD) at a ﬁxed molar ratio of 2:1 (cisplatin:TPD). The com-
bination index (CI) values for the selected combinations treatment
were determined using the equation: CI = (D)1 / (Dx)1 + (D)2 /
(Dx)2, where (D)1 and (D)2 are the concentrations of individual
drug in the combination resulting in x% growth inhibition, and
(Dx)1 and (Dx)2 are the concentrations of individual drugs alone to
elicit the same effect [26]. The CI values for drug interaction were
plotted as a function of fraction affected (Fa). CI values of b1, =1
and N1 demonstrate synergism, additive effect and antagonism re-
spectively. Further, we evaluated the dose reduction index (DRI) to
measure the dose reduction of individual drug in a synergistic com-
bination at a particular effect level compared with the doses of single
drug alone [22].
2.6. Statistical analysis
The results were presented as mean ± SD. The statistical analysis
was carried out using student's t-test (for two groups). Statistical signif-
icance was taken as p b 0.05.

3. Results
3.1. Optimization of TPD loaded PLGA nanoparticles
In order to optimize the amount of TPD incorporated into PLGA
nanoparticles, polymer to drug ratios e.g. 35:1.16 (E1), 35:1.4 (E2),
35:1.75 (E3), 35:2.33 (E4) were evaluated [Fig. 2]. The TPD loaded
PLGA nanoparticles were fabricated by emulsion solvent evaporation
method and drug loading was determined. It was observed that a max-
imum drug loading (≈51 μg/mg) was obtained for the E3 formulation
which was signiﬁcantly (p b 0.05) high as compared to E2 formulation.
Further increase in drug to polymer ratio does not increase the drug
loading signiﬁcantly. Therefore further experiments were performed
using E3 formulation.
3.2. Size, polydispersity index, zeta potential and morphology of optimized
formulation
The optimized TPD-PLGA nanoparticles were readily re-dispersible
in aqueous media and exhibited average size, polydispersity index and
zeta potential of 152.56 ± 3.01 nm, 0.168 ± 0.01 and −17.36 ±
0.37 mV respectively [Fig. 3A, B]. The TPD-PLGA nanoparticles showed
spherical shape under the TEM [Fig. 3C]. The particle size determination
by TEM was in accordance to the size as measured using photon corre-
lation spectroscopy.
3.3. Drug loading and release proﬁle
The optimized formulation showed drug loading of 51.03 ±
1.52 μg/mg of nanoparticles. The in vitro release of TPD from PLGA
nanoparticles was performed in phosphate buffered saline (PBS,
pH 7.4 and pH 5.5) at 37 °C [Fig. 3D]. The released TPD was measured
by HPLC technique as described previously. The typical biphasic release
pattern was observed at both pH values. A rapid release was observed in
the ﬁrst 12 h followed by a sustained release. The rapid release is con-
tributed by the presence of drug in outer layer of polymeric particles
while sustained release may be attributed to the drug in the inner
core matrix. Further, the drug release proﬁle of TPD nanoparticles was
slightly higher at pH 5.5 as compared to pH 7.4. In order to understand
the drug release mechanism from TPD loaded PLGA nanoparticles, data
were analyzed using kinetic models. A good correlation was observed
between experimental data and Higuchi equation for TPD-PLGA-NPs
at pH 7.4 and pH 5.5. Thus, the release of TPD from PLGA nanoparticles
assumed to be controlled by diffusion through the polymeric matrix and
subsequent release owing to the polymeric degradation. The release
data were also analyzed according to the Korsmeyer–Peppas model
and release exponent (n) was found to be 0.355 and 0.346 at pH 5.5
and 7.4 respectively, indicating that the release of TPD from PLGA nano-
particles follows Fickian diffusion kinetics.
3.4. Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR)
FTIR spectroscopy was performed to characterize the interaction be-
tween the drug and polymer [Fig. 4]. A characteristic band at 3390 cm−1
is attributed to\\OH groups stretching vibration in TPD. In the spectra
of pure PLGA, the peaks at 1760 cm−1 is attributed to the absorbance
of \\C_O in PLGA matrix. Further, the physical mixture and TPD-
PLGA nanoparticles showed mild peak at 3390 cm−1 due to \\OH
stretching of TPD and prominent peak at 1760 cm−1 owing to the absor-
bance of carbonyl group of PLGA. Additionally, the FTIR spectrum of for-
mulation showed mild peaks at 1569 cm−1 which may be attributed to
the stretching vibrations of\\C_O groups attached to hydroxyl groups
via hydrogen bonds of residual polyvinyl alcohol that was used as sur-
factant during nanoparticle fabrication process [27]. Overall, the results
indicated the absence of any drug polymer interactions.
3.5. Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC)
In order to investigate the thermal behavior of TPD-PLGA nanoparti-
cles, DSC was conducted for free TPD, PLGA, physical mixture of TPD and
PLGA, and TPD-PLGA nanoparticles. The existence of an endothermic
peak at 186 °C in the thermogram of pure TPD and physical mixture
characterizes the melting of drug [Fig. 5]. The disappearance of melting
endothermic peak of the drug in the nanoparticles formulation indicated
the presence of drug in molecular dispersion form within the polymeric
system. The pure PLGA and nanoparticles showed a peak at 50 °C,
indicating characteristic glass transition temperature of the polymer.

3.7. In vitro growth inhibition study
The growth inhibitory effect of cisplatin, TPD and TPD-PLGA-NPs
alone on ovarian cancer cell line was tested in order to evaluate the
cell line speciﬁc drug sensitivity. The IC50 values for cisplatin, TPD and
TPD-PLGA-NPs were 54.53 ± 3.2, 38.33 ± 2.08 and 32.8 ± 1.95 μM, re-
spectively, after 48 h.
3.8. Assessment of combination effects
In order to increase anti-neoplastic efﬁcacy of cisplatin by reducing
its concentration in presence of TPD, we have evaluated the growth in-
hibition potential of cisplatin and TPD-PLGA-NPs combination in the
ovarian cancer cell line. Based on the IC50 values of cisplatin and TPD-
PLGA-NPs, we selected 2:1 (cisplatin:TPD-PLGA-NPs; TPD amount in
nanoparticles calculated based on drug loading) ratio and performed
dose-range experiments. We observed that cisplatin (1–70 μM) alone
inhibited 6–63% proliferation after 48 h, whereas TPD-PLGA-NPs (0.5–
35 μM) alone exhibited 1–78% growth inhibition in OVCAR-5 cells. The
combination of cisplatin and TPD-PLGA-NPs, at this ﬁxed ratio, im-
proved the growth-inhibition (13–85%) in OVCAR-5 cells [Fig. 7A]. The
IC50 values of cisplatin and TPD-PLGA-NPs in this combination were
found to be 30.43 ± 3.78 μM and 15.23 ± 1.88 μM respectively. In

4. Discussion
Cisplatin is a widely used chemotherapeutic agent in the clinic, how-
ever, severe adverse effect associated with cisplatin, mainly nephrotox-
icity and neurotoxicity, hampers its clinical success. The management of
nephrotoxicity includes hydration in combination with the use of di-
uretics [29]. However, another dose limiting toxicity, neurotoxicity, is
still challenging and may lead to peripheral neuropathy and hearing
loss [30]. Previously, we investigated the potential of nano-formulations
to reduce the side effect of cisplatin [31,32]. In this study the combina-
tion of cisplatin with an anticancer pentacyclic triterpenoids obtained
from the natural source (gum resin of Boswellia serrata) is investigated
as a potential combination.
Taking into consideration the physiochemical characteristics of TPD
such as the high lipophilicity and low water solubility, it was encapsu-
lated into PLGA based nanoparticles for intravenous administration.
PLGA is FDA approved biodegradable and biocompatible polymer
which has been used since many decades for drug delivery application.
The biodegradation proﬁle of PLGA is well known as its biodegradation
leads to the formation of lactic and glycolic acid which are used in Krebs
cycle [33]. TPD-PLGA nanoparticles were fabricated by using emulsion
solvent evaporation method. Formulations of TPD loaded PLGA

The shape of TPD-PLGA-NPs was spherical as examined by TEM and
there was a good correlation of the size determined by dynamic light
scattering method and TEM. The in vitro release proﬁle of TPD from
PLGA nanoparticles at pH 7.4 and pH 5.5 showed a biphasic drug release
proﬁle characterized by an early rapid release and followed by sustained
release. At initial stage, loosely bound drug at the nanoparticle surfaces
was released in the media. Subsequently, partitioning of the drug local-
ized in the outer layer of the polymeric matrix occur into the release
media and at the later stages drug localized in the core of the nanopar-
ticles released through swollen and degraded polymer [37,38]. It was
also observed that the drug release was slightly higher at pH 5.5
(mimics the pH of tumor microenvironment) which could be attributed
to the relatively enhanced polymer degradation in acidic pH. This ﬁnd-
ing is in accordance to a previous investigation [39]. The observed pat-
tern of TPD release from nanoparticles could be beneﬁcial for avoiding
off-target effects in systemic circulation by diminished release at
pH 7.4 and favoring bulk of the release in tumors microenvironment.
The characterization of optimized formulation by FTIR spectroscopy
ruled out the possibility of any polymer drug interaction. The FTIR spec-
tral investigation revealed that the speciﬁc functional groups of poly-
meric carrier in the nanoparticles have almost the identical chemical
characteristics of the pure polymeric substance. Further, the absence
of endothermic peak of TPD in formulation indicated the amorphous na-
ture and suggesting encapsulation of the drug within the polymer
matrix.
The in vitro cellular uptake of PLGA nanoparticles performed in
OVCAR-5 cell, showed a time dependent increase in incorporation of
PLGA nanoparticles into the cytoplasm of the cells. As the incubation
time increases, the ﬂuorescent intensity increases, indicating an in-
crease in the nanoparticle uptake by the cells. The colloidal carrier in-
cluding nanoparticles are predominantly internalization into the cells
by the mechanism of endocytosis, however, there are other mechanism
as well for the entry into the cells [40,41]. The attribute of enhanced cel-
lular accumulation associated with the nano-formulations could trans-
late to better therapeutic efﬁcacy [42,43]. In our study, efﬁcient
cellular uptake of the nanoparticles in the OVCAR-5 cells infers efﬁcient
accumulation and subsequent release of the drug within the cells,
allowing greater intracellular drug concentration. This could result in
better anticancer activity of TPD and in turn, lead to lowering of cisplatin
concentration for the desired activity in combination.
The resurgence of chemotherapy using cisplatin is witnessed recent-
ly and new mechanism based drug combination is a potential area of re-
search [44]. The drug combination is intended to improve the
therapeutic efﬁcacy or reducing the dose with the same or improved ef-
ﬁcacy in order to avoid toxic manifestations. Mechanistically, cisplatin
mediates its cytotoxic effects by inhibiting DNA replication through its
binding with guanine residues. On the other hand, TPD has been
shown to induce apoptosis through extrinsic pathway by activating

through intrinsic pathway by activating caspase-9 and cleavage of

vestigated for possible synergistic effect. IC50 values are commonly
used to select appropriate ratio for drug combination as reported previ-

1.95 μM, respectively in OVCAR-5 cells. Therefore, combination effect
of cisplatin and TPD-PLGA-NPs was determined at a ﬁxed molar ratio
(2:1, Cisplatin: TPD). CI values were determined according to Chou

isobolograms was also generated and synergy evaluated on the basis
of ED50, ED75 and ED90 of drug combinations. The combination was
found to be synergistic at the selected drug ratio. In isobologram, the di-
agonal line connecting two 50% growth inhibition dose (ED50) points is
the theoretical line of additivity. The experimental ED50 point was
below the line of additivity, indicating synergistic effect. The similar re-
sults were obtained in the ED75 and ED90. At higher drug concentration,
the experimental effective dose points were far from the theoretical line
of additivity, which indicate that synergistic effect is more prominent in
the order of ED50 b ED75 b ED90. Further, the dose reduction index were
determined for the two drug combinations and it was found that at 75%
of cell growth inhibition in OVCAR-5 cells, TPD-PLGA-NPs reduced the
concentration of cisplatin by approximately four folds. On the other
hand, for the same degree of growth inhibition, the plain drug (TPD), re-
duced the concentration of cisplatin only by 1.17 folds. The reduced
dose of cisplatin coupled with the equal or improved therapeutic re-
sponse using ﬁxed drug combination could improve clinical success of
this widely used drug by reducing dose-associated toxic manifestations.
It has been reported that a combination of cisplatin with topoisomerase
inhibitors is a potential strategy to overcome acquired resistance of cis-
platin [29]. Further, it has been shown that a pentacyclic ring in the
boswellic acid is crucial for anti-topoisomerase property [17]. Therefore
this combination may also be beneﬁcial in context of acquired cisplatin
resistance.
In conclusion, keeping into consideration the clinical potential and
dose-limiting toxicities of cisplatin, a novel strategy was explored com-
prising a mechanism based drug combination with TPD, which was ob-
tained from Boswellia serrata having a history of traditional use.
Considering the physicochemical characteristics of TPD, a nano-formu-
lation was developed and characterized for various parameters. The
combination of cisplatin with TPD nano-formulation result in a syner-
gistic effect coupled with the reduction in the dose of cisplatin. Further,
the combination of cisplatin with TPD-PLGA-NPs was superior as com-
pared to the combination of cisplatin with TPD. The ﬁndings are of con-
siderable signiﬁcance keeping in view the dose-depending toxicities of
the cisplatin. However, further studies are required to evaluate potential
of this combination in suitable animal model.
