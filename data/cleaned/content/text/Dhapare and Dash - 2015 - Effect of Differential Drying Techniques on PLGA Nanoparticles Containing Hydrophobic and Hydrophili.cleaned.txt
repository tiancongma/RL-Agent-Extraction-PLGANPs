Chemotherapy with a combination of anticancer agents has been adopted to reduce
drug resistance and to improve therapeutic
effectiveness in cancer treatment . It is desirable for the drugs intended for use in combination to have non overlapping toxicities
and different molecular targets so as to avoid
cross-tolerance . Paclitaxel (PTX) and gemcitabine hydrochloride (GEM) is an extensively studied combination for use in variety
of cancers including advanced non small-cell
lung cancer (NSCLC) and breast cancer .
PTX is a widely used hydrophobic microtubule stabilizing agent which is US FDA
approved for palliative therapy of patients
with ovarian and breast cancer . Because
of its aqueous insolubility, PTX is administered intravenously using Cremophor EL and
ethanol (Taxol(r)). However, Cremophor EL is
known to exert a range of harmful biological
effects including severe anaphylactic reactions, hence much effort has been invested
in developing a better drug delivery system.

Nanoparticle formulations containing PTX
is one such approach . GEM, on the other
hand, is a hydrophilic antineoplastic antimetabolite used for a broad spectrum of cancers.
In a study by Kosmidis et al., it was predicted
that PTX possibly enhances the antitumor
activity of GEM and the combination was
shown to have an additive effect . Nanoparticle-assisted combination therapies achieve
the benefits of both of a combination therapy
as well as a nanodelivery system. The entrapment of multiple drugs in a single carrier
also offers the features of vehicle uniformity,
ratiometric drug loading and temporal drug
release in contrast to simultaneous delivery of
single drug-containing particles .
The entrapment of a hydrophobic and a
hydrophilic drug into a single matrix requires
a polymer with tunable physicochemical properties. Polymers allow the flexibility to be tailored for specific needs along with advantages
of biodegradability, higher stability, sustained
release and higher loading capacity for drugs

Formulation of PLGA nanoparticles
Poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) (300 mg)
and PTX (46.8 mg) were dissolved in an
acetone:dichloromethane
(1:2)
solution.
GEM
(19.9 mg) was dissolved separately in 1 ml propylene
glycol. The GEM solution was added drop wise to
the organic solution of PLGA and PTX. The organic
phase was added to 2% PVA solution in an aqueous
to organic phase ratio of 3:1 and sonicated at 30W
for 3 min (Sonicator 3000, Misonix sonicator, CT,
USA). This primary emulsion was then subjected to
three cycles of high-pressure homogenization (HPH)
(Microfluidics M110P, MA, USA) at 15,000 psi to yield
the nanoemulsion. The nanoemulsion was then either
spray dried (Buchi Mini Spray Dryer B-290, Switzerland) or lyophilized (Millrock Technology LD85, NY,
USA) following the evaporation of organic solvents in
an ice-bath under nitrogen flush to generate a colloidal
dispersion (n = 3).

. The dried powders can be used after reconstitution
or as powders for inhalation. Lyophilization and spray
drying are the two most commonly used drying techniques. Since freeze drying is more suitable for laboratory scale production there is a need to explore other
drying techniques like spray drying which is more
scalable at lower cost. The primary challenges of spray
drying are maintaining the particle size and increasing
the entrapment efficiency of the drug . The different
process conditions employed in lyophilization and spray
drying, however, may yield dried particles with different
physicochemical properties. Lyophilization and spray
drying are frequently employed drying techniques for
nanoparticles, yet very few studies describe its effect on
end product performance so as to obtain a product with
higher stability and better physicochemical properties.
The aim of this work was to investigate the feasibility of preparation of a PTX and GEM combination
loaded PLGA nanoparticle system with high entrapment and sustained release characteristics and to study
the effect of two different drying techniques on the
physicochemical characteristics of this nanodelivery
system.

Cellular uptake & subcellular localization
The cellular uptake of PTX and GEM from free drug
solution versus nanoparticles was investigated over the
course of 6 h in MDA-MB-231 (cancer) and MDCK
(normal) cells. The constant increase in the uptake
of PTX by MDA-MB-231 cells over a period of 6 h
(time dependent) was also seen by Trickler et al. .
This increase in PTX uptake over time was faster from
nanoparticles than PTX solution. The total amount of
PTX per mg of protein content taken up by both the cell
lines was higher in the case of lyophilized nanoparticles
than spray-dried nanoparticles. This may be attributed
to the smaller size of lyophilized nanoparticles. The
overall uptake of PTX and GEM from nanoparticles
was more in MDA MB 231 cells than MDCK cells.
This may be due to enhanced uptake of nanoparticles
by cancer cells than normal cells. Confocal microscopy
of MDA-MB-231 and MDCK cells further confirmed
the uptake of nanoparticles into the cells rather than the
adsorption of the particles to the cell surface. The present study demonstrated that Rhodamine 6G containing nanoparticles had accumulated in the cytoplasm in

Executive summary

Formulation
* Paclitaxel and gemcitabine were studied as a potential drug combination by simultaneous entrapment in
PLGA nanoparticles prepared by modified emulsification solvent diffusion technique with high-pressure
homogenization for size reduction and Lyophilization and spray drying as drying techniques.
Drug entrapment
* The entrapment efficiency of hydrophobic drug paclitaxel was higher than the hydrophilic drug gemcitabine.
Particle size and morphology
* The particle size was retained after lyophilization while it increased in case of spray dried by 1.7-times in case
of blank nanoparticles and 3.6-times in case of drug loaded spray-dried nanoparticles and particles were
found to be spherical in morphology.
Physical state of the drugs
* Both the drugs were found to be in a noncrystalline form in lyophilized and spray-dried formulations as
confirmed by DSC and x-ray diffraction analysis.
Cellular uptake
* The cellular uptake of paclitaxel in lyophilized NP was 1.6-fold higher while gemcitabine was 2.5-fold higher as
compared with spray-dried NP in the MDA-MB-231 cells.
Comparison of lyophilized and spray-dried formulation
* Particle size, morphology, moisture content, release characteristics and cellular uptake were found to change
with method of drying. However, zeta potential and physical state of the drug were found to be unaffected
by drying technique used.
