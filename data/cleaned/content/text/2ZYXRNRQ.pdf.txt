A B S T R A C T
Epirubicin (EPI) is a P-gp substrate antracycline analogue which elicits poor oral bioavailability. In the
present work, EPI loaded poly-lactide-co-glycolic acid nanoparticles (PLGA-NPs) were prepared by
double emulsion approach and superﬁcially decorated with polyethylene glycol (EPI-PNPs) and
mannosamine (EPI-MNPs). Average hydrodynamic particle size of EPI-PNPs and EPI-MNPs was found
248.63  12.36 and 254.23  15.16 nm, respectively. Cytotoxicity studies were performed against human
breast adenocarcinoma cell lines (MCF-7) conﬁrmed the superiority of EPI-PNPs and EPI-MNPs over free
epirubicin solution (EPI-S). Further, confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM) and ﬂow cytometric
analysis (FACS) demonstrated enhanced drug uptake through EPI-PNPs and EPI-MNPs and elucidated
dominance of caveolae mediated endocytosis for NPs uptake. Cellular transport conducted on human
colon adenocarcinoma cell line (Caco-2) showed 2.45 and 3.17 folds higher permeability of EPI through
EPI-PNPs and EPI-MNPs when compared with EPI-S (p < 0.001) while permeability of EPI was found
5.23 and 5.67 folds higher across rat ileum, respectively. Furthermore, pharmacokinetic studies
demonstrated 4.7 and 5.57 folds higher oral bioavailability through EPI-PNPs and EPI-MNPs when
compared with EPI-S. In addition, both, EPI-PNPs and EMNPs showed tumor suppression comparable to
indicated route (i.v. injection). EPI-MNPs showed 1.18 folds higher bioavailability and better tumor
suppression than EPI-PNPs.

hospitalization and nursing care thus offers better patient comfort
and conformity (Peltier et al., 2006). However, there are number of
issues of oral drug delivery like poor solubility, poor permeability,
ﬁrst pass metabolism and instability of drugs in GI milieu which
encircles the applicability of route. In last few decades, nano-
particulate systems have been extensively investigated as a
versatile tool for wide range of biomedical application as well as
for improved oral delivery of challenging molecules (Etheridge
et al., 2013). Increasing proclivity can be validated with the
following reports on the oral delivery of anticancer drugs; SEDDS
for etoposide (Negi et al., 2013) SLN for doxorubicin (Yang et al.,
1999; Yuan et al., 2013), polymeric micelles for paclitaxel (Yao
et al., 2011), Dendrimer for doxorubicin (Ke et al., 2008),
layersomes for doxorubicin (Jain et al., 2012) polymeric NPs for
etoposide and epirubicin respectively (Fatma et al., 2014; Tariq
et al., 2015). Moreover, a number of researchers demonstrated
superior pharmacokinetic, pharmacodynamic and safety proﬁle
for orally administered drugs by encapsulating them into nano-
particulate systems as NPs are able to evade P-gp mediated efﬂux
and CPY-450 mediated metabolism of drugs. In addition NPs are
taken up by enterocytes and Peyer’s patches which provide
additional drug transport pathway (Faderl et al., 1999; Florence,
1997; Gershanik and Benita, 2000; Hussain et al., 2001; Joshi et al.,
2014). Improved oral PK proﬁle of gemcitabine was demonstrated
by using PLGA NPs over plain solution (Joshi et al., 2014). Similarly,
better pharmacodynamic proﬁle was reported for doxorubicin and
paclitaxel by exploiting PLGA NPs (Bhardwaj et al., 2009; Jain et al.,
2011a). We have also demonstrated signiﬁcantly higher transport
of epirubicin across caco-2 cell line and rat ileum through PLGA
NPs which resulted into 4.0 fold enhanced oral bioavailability
(Tariq et al., 2015). In spite of being studied extensively,
nanoparticulate systems suffer from drawbacks; being trapped
within the thick and viscoelastic mucus gel layer and cleared out
rapidly by mucus clearance (Cone, 2009). In addition, non
speciﬁcity of nano-particulate systems limits their uptake via
enterocytes and M cells of payer’s patches (Florence, 1997).
Recently, manipulation of surface attributes of conventional NPs
are being investigated for further improving the delivery of drugs.
PEG decorated nanoparticles reported to have high diffusivity
and high penetration in thick mucosal layer which facilitate their
entry closer proximity to epithelial layer (Wang et al., 2008; Yuan
et al., 2013). In addition, a few researchers reported its bio-
adhesive property (Huang et al., 2000; Yoncheva et al., 2007), these
attributes were hold responsible for higher oral bioavailability
through Pegylated particles when compared with non-pegylated
one (Yuan et al., 2013; Zhang et al., 2014). Similarly, mannosylated
nanoparticulate systems demonstrated higher cellular accessibili-
ty and consequently higher uptake, higher permeation and higher
bioavailability in comparison to its conventional counterpart
(Ahire et al., 2013; Witoonsaridsilp et al., 2012). Mannosylated
nanoparticles have been proposed as a system with speciﬁc
distribution proﬁle in gut epithelium (Salman et al., 2006) as
mannose residue has high speciﬁc binding afﬁnity to lectin
presents on the cell surfaces (Jiang et al., 2010) which are widely
distributed in lymphoid and non-lymphoid organs i.e. enterocytes
as well as M cells of Peyer’s patches (Wagner et al., 2003).
In the present report, an attempt was made to study the effect of
surface decoration on pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic
proﬁle. Therefore, mannosylated and pegylated PLGA NPs were
developed and evaluated comparatively for the establishment of
their effectiveness in oral delivery of epirubicin. Epirubicin elicits
poor oral bioavailability due to which available commercially in
injectable form for clinical applications therefore chosen for
present investigations. Epirubicin loaded pegylated (EPI-PNPs) and
mannosylated PLGA NPs (EPI-MNPs) were subjected to various in
vitro cell line parameters, pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic
evaluation.

2.2. Preparation of epirubicin loaded mannosylated (EPI-MNPs) and
pegylated PLGA NPs (EPI-PNPs)
2.2.1. Preparation of epirubicin loaded conventional PLGA NPs (EPI-
NPs)
Conventional EPI-NPs were prepared by double emulsion
methods as reported previously (Tariq et al., 2015). In brief,
10 mg of EPI was dissolved in PVA solution (0.5% w/v, pH adjusted
to 3) and emulsiﬁed into dichloromethane containing PLGA
(100 mg/2 mL) over an ice bath under sonication for 60 s (25 W,
40% duty cycles, Sonopuls, Bandelin, Germany). Resulting 1
emulsion was further emulsiﬁed into PVA containing external
aqueous phase under sonication over an ice bath at 25 W and 40%
duty cycles for 2 min followed by solvent evaporation under
magnetic stirring (400 rpm) at room temperature. Obtained nano-
dispersion was centrifuged at 18,000 rpm for 20 min. Pellet was
washed and re-dispersed in water and lyophilized at 50 C and
0.015 millibar pressure for 24 h (Lab Conco., LPYH, Lock 6, USA
freeze dryer).
2.2.2. Mannosylation of EPI-NPs
For the surface decoration, 20 mg of EPI-NPs was dispersed into
0.1 M MES buffer followed by addition of 1 mL of mannosamine
solution (3 mg/mL in 0.1 M MES buffer) subsequently 0.5 mL EDC
solution (10 mg/mL in 0.1 M MES buffer). Resulting mixture was
allowed
to

approximately

CM6-MNPs and CM6-PNPs were prepared as described in

2.5. HPLC analysis
Quantitative estimation of epirubicin was done by an in house
developed and validated HPLC method. Separation module,
e2695HPLC system equipped with PDA detector (Waters, India
Pvt., Ltd.) was used for the analysis of samples. Separation was
achieved with mobile phase consisting of aqueous phase (0.16% o-
phosphoric acid solution, 60% v/v) and organic phase (80:20 v/v
mixture of acetonitrile and methanol, 40% v/v) using C18 analytical
column (4.6  250 mm, 5 mm, Purosphere, Merck) at the ﬂow rate
of 1 mL/min and column temperature 30 C. Analysis was carried
out at 233.5 nm.
2.6. Fluorescence analysis
Quantitative estimation of coumarin-6 (CM6) was conducted by
using spectroﬂuorometer at lex (440 nm) and lem (500 nm)
wavelength (Cary Eclipse ﬂuorescence spectrophotometer, Varian,
CA, USA).
2.7. Nanoparticles size, size distribution, zeta potential
The particle size, size distribution (polydispersity index, PDI)
and zeta potential analyses of EPI-PNPs and EPI-MNPs was done by
using dynamic light scattering technique (DLS) equipped with
computerized inspection system (Malvern Zetasizer, Nano-ZS,
Malvern, UK) and ‘DTS nano’ software.
2.8. Shape and surface morphological analysis
Shape
and

analyses of
developed

tance and scanning electron microscope. For TEM analysis,
300 meshes copper grid was soaked with a drop of nanoparticles
suspension followed by negative staining with a 1% aqueous
solution of phosphorous tungstic acid and subsequently air dried.
Sample was viewed under Transmittance electron microscope
(TOPCON 002B, Tokyo, Japan) operated at 200 kV. Images were

freeze dried samples evenly distributed on aluminium stubs using
both side adhesive carbon tape followed by covering with glass slip

out by using Smart SEM software program.
2.9. Drug loading
Freeze dried nanoparticles (2 mg) were dissolved in dimethyl
sulfoxide (HPLC, grade), ﬁltered through 0.2 mm syringe ﬁlter and
quantiﬁed by HPLC. Drug loading was expressed as drug amount
per mg of formulation.
For the quantiﬁcation of CM6 into respective NPs, samples were
processed as described above and analyzed by spectroﬂuorometer
at lex (440 nm) and lem (500 nm) wavelength (Cary Eclipse
ﬂuorescence spectrophotometer, Varian, CA, USA).
2.10. In vitro release study
Dialysis bag method was employed for the assessment of in
vitro release proﬁle of developed formulation. Brieﬂy, EPI-MNPs
and EPI-PNPs (equivalent to 2 mg of EPI) were added separately
into 5 mL of dissolution media and placed into dialysis bag (MW
cut off of 8–10 kDa, Spectra/Por1 Spectrum Laboratories, Inc.,
Rancho Dominguez, CA). The dialysis bag was then immersed in
100 ml glass beaker containing 50 mL of pre-warmed (37  0.5 C)
dissolution media. In vitro release study was carried out in
incubator shaker (SI6R, Shel Lab, Sheldon Mfg. Inc., Ave., Cornelius,
OR) at a speed of 100 rpm and at 37  0.5 C to maintain
temperature.

Samples were subjected to FACS analysis according tothe procedure
described in Section 2.11.4. Similar procedure was followed to
elucidate the uptake mechanism of EPI-PNPs.

2.13. Pharmacokinetic (PK) study
Rats were divided into three groups (n = 6) and each group was
treated as following; 1st group was administered with EPI-S
intravenously, 2nd and 3rd were subjected to EPI-MNPs and EPI-
PNPs orally. The dose of EPI in each case was taken as 10 mg/kg. At
regular time interval up-to 48 h, blood (200 mL) was withdrawn
from retro orbital choroid plexus under mild anesthesia and
collected into EDTA coated blood sampling tubes. Immediately,
blood samples were centrifuged at 5000 rpm for 10 min to separate
plasma, and the separated plasma was stored at 20 C till further
analysis. Quantitative estimation of samples was done by using
already validated HPLC method with slight modiﬁcation (Shin
et al., 2013).

2.14. In vivo biodistribution of CM6-MNPs and CM6-PNPs after oral
administration
Albino wistar Rats (weight 200–220 g) were randomly divided
into eleven groups (each containing 3 animals) starved for
overnight with free access to water before experimentation. One
group was administered with PBS (control), other 5 groups were
treated with CM6-MNPs and the remaining ﬁve groups were
subjected to CM6-PNPs, 4 mg/ml. At scheduled time (1, 2, 4, 8, 24 h
of post dosing), blood was collected from retro orbital choroid
plexus and animals were scariﬁed by cervical dislocation under
excessive anaesthesia. The body organs like stomach, jejunum,
ileum, colon, spleen, liver, kidney, heart and lung were excised out
immediately, gastrointestinal tissue were cut open and rinsed with
PBS to remove out any residual contents and nanoparticles at the
luminal surface. All the patted dried tissues were wrapped in
aluminium foil and stored in dark at 20 C till analysis. Blood was
allowed to clot and serum was stored at 20 C till analysis (Zhao
et al., 2007).
For the quantitative estimation of NPs, tissues were homoge-
nized in equivalent amount of distilled water then extracted into
acetonitrile under dark for 18 h at 25 C (Zhao et al., 2007).
Extracted homogenate was centrifuged at 10,000 rpm for 10 min
and supernatant was analyzed at lex 440 nm and lem 500 nm by
using ﬂuorescence spectrophotometer (Cary Eclipse ﬂuorescence
spectrophotometer, Varian, CA, USA). Similar treatment was given
to serum and analyzed in similar way as described above. Auto-
ﬂuorescence of tissues was estimated by giving similar treatment
to the tissues of control group animals which was deducted from
the ﬂuorescence of test samples to determine nanoparticles
generated ﬂuorescence.

3.3. In-vitro release study
Comparative in-vitro release proﬁle of EPI-MNPs and EPI-PNPs
were studied. EPI-MNPs and EPI-PNPs demonstrated 83.01 6.3%
and 84.07 4.37%, respectively in 48 h. Both the surface decorated
nanoparticles showed biphasic release pattern i.e. initial burst
release followed by sustained effect as observed for conventional
EPI-NPs in our previous work (Tariq et al., 2015). Initial burst effect
can be explained by rapid diffusion of EPI molecules present at the
surface while sustained release can be attribute to hydrophobic
polymer which probably limited penetrability of aqueous media
thus slow diffusion of drug molecules present within the core of
polymer matrix. In addition, longer diffusion path for those
molecules which were present at the core of NPs could be another
plausible reason. Initial burst effect would be advantageous to
achieve therapeutic concentration immediately while sustained
release proﬁle would favour therapeutic effect for an extended
period of time.
Release kinetic was further analyzed by ﬁtting release data into
different kinetic models; zero order, ﬁrst order, Higuchi model,
Korsmeyer–Peppas. Higuchi model was found to be best to explain
the mechanism of release proﬁle r2, 0.962 and 0.956 for EPI-MNPs
and EPI-PNPs, respectively. The release kinetic was further
conﬁrmed by semi empirical model “Korsmeyer–Peppas model”
which revealed the value of exponent ‘n’ at the linear portion of
curve which was found to be 0.45 suggesting Fickian diffusion
release proﬁle (Peppas, 1985) (Fig. 2).
3.4. Cytotoxicity study
In-vitro cytotoxicity of EPI through EPI-MNPs and EPI-PNPs
were assessed with respect to cell survival after 48 h cytotoxic
treatment. The IC50 values of EPI-MNPs and EPI-PNPs were found
to be 648  36.3 nM and 929  68.3 nM, respectively (Fig. 3). IC50
values for EPI-MNPs and EPI-PNPs were found to be signiﬁcantly
lower (p < 0.01) when compared with the IC50 of EPI-S as reported
in our previous work (Tariq et al., 2015). Signiﬁcant reduction in
IC50 value can be explained by intracellular trafﬁcking behaviour of
NPs via endocytosis which facilitated the retention of drug for
extended period of time in the cellular milieu, especially at the
nuclear region can be clearly seen by Fig. 5 (Gao et al., 2010; Jain
et al., 2011a; Zhang et al., 2009). Furthermore, on comparing EPI-
MNPs with EPI-PNPs, the former showed signiﬁcantly lower IC50
than later (p < 0.01). Lower IC50 value of EPI-MNPs can be
attributed to high binding afﬁnity of surfacial mannose molecules
to lectin presents on the cell surfaces resulting into relatively
higher uptake of EPI-MNPs (Jiang et al., 2010) while higher IC50 of
EPI-PNPs can be attributed to relatively lower uptake of particles
owing to its hydrophilic surface. Observations are in agreement
with previously reported results (Ahire et al., 2013; Behrens et al.,
2002; Yuan et al., 2013).

3.5. Cellular uptake by FACS
Intracellular uptake of nanoparticulate systems plays a pivotal
role in improved absorption of drug. In order to elucidate the
potential of the developed system, a comparative cellular uptake
study of EPI through EPI-MNPs and EPI-PNPs was carried out by
using FACS (Fig. 4). Since the work principally focuses on the oral
bioavailability enhancement, hence Caco-2 cell line was used as in
vitro intestinal model. Table 1 summarizes the intracellular uptake
of EPI through EPI-MNPs and EPI-PNPs. Both EPI-MNPs and EPI-
PNPs demonstrated higher ﬂuorescence within the cells at both the
selected concentrations when compared with EPI-S (p < 0.0001)
which can be attributed to endocytic uptake of nanoparticles
resulting into enhanced concentration of EPI within the cells (Gao
et al., 2010; Jain et al., 2011a). Further, on comparison between
nanoformulations, EPI-MNPs demonstrated signiﬁcantly high
cellular uptake at both the concentration (p < 0.001) when
compared with EPI-PNPs. High uptake of EPI through EPI-MNPs
can be attributed to high avidity of mannose residue to lectins
present on lymphoid and non-lymphoid cells resulting into
increased interaction with cell surface and consequently additional
endocytic uptake (Jiang et al., 2010; Wagner et al., 2003). Relatively
lower EPI-uptake through EPI-PNPs can be explained by pegylation
of particles leading to hydrophilic surface which results into
reduced interaction with biological membrane consequently lower
uptake (Behrens et al., 2002; Soenen et al., 2014; Yuan et al., 2013).
Cellular uptake of EPI through EPI-S, EPI-MNPs and EPI-PNPs
was further conﬁrmed by visual assessment through confocal
microscopy (Fig. 5). Cells treated with EPI-S showed a poor red
ﬂuorescence within the cells (Fig. 5a) which indicated the poor
uptake of EPI due to its hydrophilic nature as well as being a P-gp
substrate it would have been expelled out by P-gp efﬂux
transporter. EPI-PNPs and EPI-MNPs showed an intense red
ﬂuorescence spots at the peri-nuclear regions which can be
attributed to intracellular trafﬁcking of particles via endocytosis
(Fig. 5b and c). However, cells treated with EPI-MNPs demonstrat-
ed more dense and intense ﬂuorescence within the cell in
comparison to cells treated EPI-PNPs. Results are in corroboration
with observations of FACS studies and also in agreement with
previous reports (Ahire et al., 2013; Behrens et al., 2002; Soenen
et al., 2014; Yuan et al., 2013). Thus, it can be concluded that
absorption of a molecule could be improved by encapsulating it
into a nanoparticulate system as NPs have different characteristics
from free drug molecules and have a potential to be endocytosed
leading to improved absorption.

290.41  26.1 mm3 in animals treated with EPI-MNPs and EPI-
PNPs, respectively. Thus, tumor suppression through oral admin-
istration of EPI-MNPs and EPI-PNPs was lesser but comparable to
EPI-S intravenous treatment.
The poor oral absorption of EPI can be hold responsible for
continuous tumor growth in animal treated with EPI-S orally while
signiﬁcant reduction in tumor volume in animals treated with EPI-
PNPs and EPI-MNPs can be attributed to improved oral bioavail-
ability, 4.7 and 5.57 fold, respectively. In addition, high plasma drug
concentration for extended period of time and translocation of
intact particles across the intestine into blood and other body
organs probably in tumor also (via EPR effect) might have further
added the value (Bhardwaj et al., 2009; Jain et al., 2011a; Maeda,
1992). Animals treated with EPI-S intravenously demonstrated
maximum tumor reduction in the following way; initial rapid
suppression, followed by static subsequently mild increment
which can be explained by initial high EPI concentration at tumor
vicinity followed by a rapid clearance due to fast reduction in
plasma drug concentration as well as expulsion of EPI through P-gp
transporter (Jain et al., 2011a; Jain et al., 2012). Higher tumor
suppression by EPI-MNPs in comparison to EPI-PNPs can be
attributed to signiﬁcantly high oral bioavailability and probable
accumulation of translocated EPI-MNPs into tumor vicinity via
phagocytic uptake by tumor associated macrophages (Zhu et al.,
2013).
Weigh of each group was monitored intermittently. Animals
subjected to intravenous treatment (EPI-S) showed a signiﬁcant
weight reduction with respect to their own weight (p < 0.05) while
animals subjected to other treatment showed increment in weight
(p < 0.05). Weight reduction in animals treated with intravenous
EPI-S can be attributed to toxicity of EPI. Similar observations were
reported in previous study (Yao et al., 2011). The improved efﬁcacy
and safety proﬁle of EPI- through oral nanoformulations can be
attributed to low but effective plasma drug concentration for
extended period of time.
4. Conclusion
EPI-MNPs and EPI-PNPs were successfully developed and
evaluated for their potential in oral delivery. They demonstrated
superior in-vitro and in-vivo activities over EPI-S (oral). In addition,
they also showed high oral bioavailability and antitumor activity
when compared with their conventional counterpart. Both, EPI-
MNPs and EPI-PNPs exhibited antitumor activity comparable to
intravenous administration thus both are potential candidate for
improving oral delivery of challenging molecules. Interestingly,
EPI-MNPs illustrated better pharmacokinetic and pharmacody-
namic proﬁle when compared with EPI-PNPs which might be due
to speciﬁc binding of mannose residue with lectins present on
lymphoid and non-lymphoid organs. It can also be exploited for
targeting via tumor assisted macrophages. Conclusively, EPI-MNPs
showed best potential amongst EPI-S (oral) and EPI-PNPs (oral),
and can be investigated more extensively for future development.
