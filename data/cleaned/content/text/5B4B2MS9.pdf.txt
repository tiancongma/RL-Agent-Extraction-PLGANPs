A B S T R A C T
Linagliptin (Lina) is a DPP-4 inhibitor used to treat type II diabetes. However, it has a poor oral bioavailability of
29.5% due to both first-pass effect and P-glycoprotein efflux, offering a significant drawback to its general use.
Our research goals were to increase Lina bioavailability and develop an injectable sustained release nanoparticle
(NP) formulation with a lower administration frequency to improve patient compliance. All formulations were
prepared by single emulsion solvent evaporation technique using poly d,l-lactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA) as a
polymer according to a design of experiment (DoE). The dependent variables were lactide concentration,
polymer concentration, and organic phase ratio, while the independent variables were entrapment efficiency,
particle size, and in-vitro release after 7 days. In-vivo study on rats through evaluation of pharmacodynamics and
pharmacokinetics parameters. The selected formula consisted of a 65:35 lactide concentration, with 150 mg
PLGA, and 10 % organic solvent, while TEM of the formula showed a sphere-shaped structure with a smooth
surface. Particle size was 541.178 ± 10.4 nm, and entrapment efficiency was 67.134 %, with a long-term sus­
tained release reach of seven days. The pharmacokinetic results showed that AUC 0-∞ increased significantly
from 33.73 to 60.53 ng/ml.h, t1/2 was significantly prolonged (231.6 ± 43.9 vs. 11.8 ± 0.3 h), Mean residence
time (MRT) was 344.1 ± 61 vs. 3.9 ± 1.5 h, and Vd was 4731 ± 393.7 vs 161.4 ± 87.20 ng/ml, for Lina NPs and
oral Lina solutions, respectively (P > 0.05). Pharmacodynamics study of optimized formulation show obvious
decrease in blood glucose level in comparison to oral Lina. These findings suggest that the PLGA Lina NPs offer a
novel strategy for the development of a once-weekly injectable medicine to help manage blood glucose in in­
dividuals who have exhibited the capability to manage type 2 diabetes mellitus.
1. Introduction
High blood sugar levels are the consequence of diabetes mellitus
which is a metabolic and chronic disorder caused by insufficient secre­
tion or action of insulin [1]. Diabetes currently affects 425 million
people globally, and it is predicted that by 2040, 642 million more
people will be affected by this lifestyle disorder. Sulfonylureas, bigua­
nides, alpha glucosidase inhibitors, meglitinide analogues, and thiazo­
lidinediones are few families of oral antidiabetic drugs that have been
utilized as therapy substitutes [2].The potential for gastrointestinal side
effects, risk of hypoglycemia and mass increase, and contraindication in
renal problems and impairment are some of limits of current therapy.
Newer diabetes management strategies are now being developed by
pharmaceutical researchers [3]. Diabetic individuals are more likely to
suffer drug treatment difficulties since they take many drugs at different
interval time for disease management.
Linagliptin (Lina) is a considerably more potent and effective
dipeptidyl peptidase 4 (DPP-4) inhibitor due to its unusual pharmaco­
kinetic/pharmacodynamics profile and lack of any relevant medication
interactions. It was approved for diabetic patients by the U.S. Food and

2. Materials and methods
2.1. Materials
Lina was provided by (EIPICO), 10th of Ramadan City, Egypt. PLGA
of different viscosity grades and different ratios of lactide and glycolide
(65:35, 75:25, and 85:15) with molecular weights of 60,000–70,000 Da
and intrinsic viscosity (1.05 dl/gm) were purchased from LACTEL In­
ternational Absorbable Polymers, Birmingham, USA. Dimethyl sulfoxide
(DMSO) was provided by Merck, Germany. Methanol, methylene chlo­
ride, and acetonitrile (HPLC gradient grade) were acquired from Fisher
Scientific (New Jersey, USA). Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) (molecular
weight of 70000–100000 Dalton), dibasic potassium hydrogen phos­
phate, and monobasic sodium hydrogen phosphate were also provided
from Spectrum Chemical MFG. Corp, New Jersey, USA.
2.2. Experimental design
A pre-screening study was conducted prior to constructing the cur­
rent experimental design. Five variables were investigated such as the
concentration of PLGA, concentration of lactic acid in PLGA, stirring
rate, organic phase ratio. Based on the findings, three formulation pa­
rameters were chosen to further investigate their impact on the char­
acteristics of Lina nanoparticles. In this regard, a 33 Box-Behnken
Experimental Design was developed using Design-Expert® software
(version 11, Stat-Ease Inc., Minneapolis, MN, USA). The following were
the independent variables (formulation factors) chosen: Lactide con­
centration in PLGA (A), PLGA concentration (B), and Organic phase
ratio (C). Each independent variable in the design was investigated at
three different levels (−1, 0, +1). Entrapment efficiency (Y1), particle
size (Y2), and release percent after seven days (Y3) were the dependent
variables (responses) (Table 1).
Table 2 shows the design matrix constructed by the software which
comprised of 15 experiments and three of which were replicated runs.
To reduce the effect of inexplicable variability in the observed response,
all trials were performed in a random order. The design role was to es­
timate the primary impacts and the interaction effects on the researched
responses as well as to optimize the chosen formulation elements. The
computer program generates a single equation for each response that
illustrates how the independent variables affected that response.
In order to fit the experimental data to the desirable model and to
check the validity of the experimental design, analysis of variance
(ANOVA) was performed and R2 was calculated. The fitted model is
considered adequate if the model is significant (p < 0.05) and the lack of
fit is not significant (p > 0.05).
The current study’s goal was to develop injectable Lina nanoparticles
with desired high entrapment efficiency, small particle size, as well as
sustained release over seven days utilizing biodegradable PLGA polymer
at varied grades with different organic solvents ratios and polymer
concentration. The selected formulation, which demonstrated excellent
entrapment effectiveness, tiny particle size, and sustained in-vitro drug
release over seven days, was injected subcutaneously into rats to eval­
uate the drug’s pharmacokinetic profile in vivo. Reduced drug concen­
trations released from the NPs systems, as well as the drug’s extended
circulation effect in vivo, were sought to improve Lina’s effectiveness
and bioavailability. Because of its effectiveness in managing blood
glucose levels over time, such formulation has become an option for the
patient to lower drug dosing frequency, reduce side effects, and improve

2.4. preparation of lina nanoparticle formulations
Utilizing the single emulsion evaporation approach, Lina-loaded NPs
were created. PLGA and Lina powder were dissolved using a magnetic
stirrer through a solution of 2 ml of methylene chloride and 1:1 DMSO.
To create the emulsion, a tiny glass beaker containing 4 ml of 2.5 % PVA
was placed in a bath containing ice and the drug-PLGA solution was
added portion-wise. The mixture was then combined via a probe soni­
cator at 4 ◦C and 50 W. Using a rotating vacuum evaporator at reduced
pressure, 25 ◦C, and 100 rpm for 15 min, the organic solvents were
eliminated. To create formulations containing 2.5 mg of Lina per ml, the
produced polymeric NPs are diluted with 6 ml of lower concentration of
PVA (0.3%) and then kept in a refrigerator for further work [18].
2.5. characterization of nanoparticles formulations
2.5.1. Determination of particle size, polydispersity index (PDI), and zeta
potential of Lina loaded NPs formulations
The dynamic light scattering technique (DLS) was used to measure
the particle size, PDI, and zeta potential of the Lina polymeric NP for­
mulations using the Zeta sizer apparatus (version 6.32, Malvern in­
strument Ltd). To avoid the multi-scattering phenomenon, distilled
water were used to dilute samples for 2 min at 25 ◦C before testing [19].
2.5.2. Evaluation of entrapment efficiency & drug loading of NPs
formulations
To separate the free drug, the centrifugation technique was used to
measure the efficiency of entrapment of Lina NPs [20]. At 12000 rpm
and 4 ◦C, collected samples were centrifuged for 10 min. To make sure
that no free drug was present, the free Lina was measured by UV at a
wavelength at 294 nm. The supernatant was diluted using PVA solution
(0.3% w/v) and the same diluted medium was used as a blank. The
equation below is used to determine the entrapment efficiency:
Entrapment efficiency (%) = (Qt – Qs / Qt) 100
Where Qt: estimated quantity of Lina and Qs: actual quantity of Lina that
was found in the supernatant [21].
Drug loading (%) = Amount of drug found in the NPs/Amount of total
NPs × 100 [22].
2.5.3. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) study
The shape of the dispersed particles was examined using JEM-2100
transmission electron microscope (JEOL, Japan). Two drops of the
diluted preparation from the optimized Lina NPs mixture was exposed to
a carbon-coated grid after being diluted with distilled water. This was
allowed to sit for 2 min to enhance absorption on the carbon. The
samples were stained for 3 min via 1% uranyl acetate solution prior
examination by TEM.
2.5.4. Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC)
The calorimetric properties of Lina raw material, PLGA polymer,
polyvinyl alcohol, a binary mixture of PVA and PLGA Lina-free NPs
formulation (placebo), and optimized Lina loaded NPs formulation were
investigated. The samples were exposed to heating in closed aluminum
pans using a nitrogen environment at a constant heating rate of 10 ◦C/
min. Using a Model (DSC 4000, PerkinElmer, Waltham, MA, USA) with
equipment and a PC control unit at a heating rate of 10 ◦C/min and a
nitrogen atmosphere, DSC thermograms were produced at temperatures
ranging from 30 to 400 ◦C. Pyris™ Manager Software was used for data
analysis [23].
2.5.5. Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy
Using potassium bromide disc technique, the FTIR spectrum of pure
Lina, PLGA, polyvinyl alcohol, a binary mixture of PVA and PLGA Lina-
free NPs formulation (placebo), and optimized Lina loaded NPs formu­
lation were scanned. The resolution was 1 cm−1 and the scanning range
was 4000-500 cm−1. The FTIR spectra were achieved by FTIR spec­
trometer (Nicolet iS10, Thermo Scientific, USA) covered the range
400–4000 cm−1 [23].
2.5.6. In-vitro release study
An in-vitro release study of Lina from freshly made PLGA nano­
particle formulae were carried out via using dialysis bags of cellophanes
membrane (molecular weight cut-off 12–14 KDa). After centrifugation,
2 ml of the polymeric NPs were put into the bags which were then
suspended in closed containers having 100 ml of phosphate buffer (pH
7.4) and shaken at 60 rpm, 37 ◦C, in a shaking water bath. At 1, 2, 5, 8,
24, 48, 96, 124, and 168 h, 3 ml samples were taken out. As previously
mentioned, a spectrophotometric analysis of Lina concentration was
performed at 294 nm. The sink condition was attained via using 100 ml
volume of release. Substituting the withdrawn release medium with
fresh buffer to maintain volume is constant [24,25]..
2.6. In vivo evaluation of the optimized lina nanoparticles
2.6.1. Animals and ethical approval
We used mature male albino rats that weighed 200–250 gm. The
animals was bought from the institute for animal breeding at Zagazig
University in Egypt. Rats were housed for 7 day at room temperature in a
12 h light, 12 h dark cycle with free access to eat and drink before the
studies began. The Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee
(IACUC; number of approval: ZU-IACUC/3/F/157/2021) of Faculty of
Pharmacy provided recommendation. All procedures of experimental
design and animal handling were carried out according to the approved
guidelines of the Animal Ethics Committee of Zagazig University, Egypt
that comply with the ARRIVE guidelines and UK animals Act.

2.6.2. Diabetes induction in rats
The rats were given a high-fructose/high-fat/high-salt diet (10% w/v
fructose in drinking water, 25% vegetable fat, and 3% sodium chloride
in food) for four weeks to induce insulin resistance. After that, the rats
received a single intraperitoneal injection of streptozotocin (40 mg/kg)
to induce moderate hyperglycemia, which mimics the pathophysiology
of type 2 diabetes in humans. The rats exhibiting prolonged elevated
levels of fed glucose (ranging from 200 to 300 mg/dl) were classified as
diabetic for experimental reasons [26–28].
2.6.3. Animal groups
The rats were divided into four groups (n = 10) as follows.
Group 1: The control animals in this group were given regular doses
of purified water and standard meals for a period of six continuous
weeks.
Group 2: The diabetes-induced animals in this group were given a
placebo for a further two weeks.
Group 3: The diabetes-induced rats in this group were given daily
oral Lina solution (4 mg/kg) for 10 days.
Group 4: The diabetes-induced rats in this group were given one
injectable subcutaneous dose of Lina NPs (10 mg/kg) which con­
tained 150 mg PLGA 65:35 and a 10% solvent ratio/10 ml NPs
formulation.
The Lina NPs formulae were injected subcutaneously with 16 gauge
needles at doses of (10 mg/kg), and at 1, 2, 4, 24, 72, and 168 h (1
week), blood samples (0.5 ml) were taken from the orbital vein into
heparinized tubes. The drawn blood samples were then centrifuged for
10 min at 4000 rpm to get plasma [29].
2.6.4. Pharmacokinetic study
Eight diabetic rats were given to one of two experimental group: the
standard Lina group (Lina Oral), which administered one dose of 4 mg/
kg of lina as a suspension orally, and the nano formula group (Lina.
Nano), which received one dose of 10 mg/kg of lina as a pharmaceutical
formulation in the form of nanoparticles. Then, 1, 2, 4, 24, 72, and 168 h
(7 days) following treatment, 0.5 mL blood was drawn from the retro-
orbital plexus vein in heparinized tubes while being lightly sedated
with ether. This blood was used to determine the presence of Lina by
HPLC. To separate plasma, blood samples centrifugation for 10 min at
4 ◦C at 10,000 rpm. Until analysis, storage of samples at −80 ◦C.
Determination of pharmacokinetic parameters, plasma concentra­
tion against time data were used. Half-life (t1/2), Mean Residence Time
(How long drug molecules stay in the body, MRT), and apparent volume
of distribution at steady state (Vss) were the pharmacokinetic parame­
ters that were calculated. The results were shown as mean ± standard
error of the mean (SEM). Unpaired Student t-test was used to statistically
compare Lina oral with Lina Nano for t1/2, MRT and Vss,. Analysis done
by PK Solver ® software.
Plasma concentrations of Lina oral and Lina Nano was performed
using two-way ANOVA followed by a Tukey’s post hoc test.
2.6.5. Quantification of Linagliptin in plasma by HPLC analysis
A stock solution of Lina was dissolved in methanol (1000 μg/ml).
Drug solutions were produced in serial concentrations of 5, 10, 30, 50,
100, 200, 300, and 500 μg/ml. The calibration solutions were made by
combining 10 μL of working solutions with 90 μL of rat blank plasma,
with concentration ranges of 0.5–50 μg/ml 900 ml of methanol was then
added and vortexed for 5 min. Prior to being injected into the HPLC
system, total mixture was centrifuged at 12000 rpm for 15 min at 25 ◦C.
After that, it was filtered using a 0.45 μm syringe filter. At 294 nm, Lina
concentrations were observed. The drug analysis was conducted using
an HPLC device using a Venusil XBP, 5 m C18 column (25 cm × 4.6 mm)
from Thermo Electron Corporation in Bellefonte, Pennsylvania, USA.
Elution pumps operated an isocratic modified mobile phase containing
methanol (40:60% v/v) and potassium dihydrogen phosphate buffer
(pH 4.5). The auto sampler used methanol as a rinse fluid. The injection
volume was 20 μl, and the flow rate was 1 ml/min. The HPLC test was
run at 294 nm using a photodiode array detector. Sonication was used to
degas the mobile component [13].
2.6.6. Pharmacodynamics study
Assessment of the antidiabetic activity by rats’ post-meal blood sugar
was measured which were randomly divided into four groups (n = 10).
In all experimental groups, the blood glucose level was checked at 1, 3,
and 7 days after the oral glucose load. After an 8 h of fasting, the rats
received a continuous glucose load (0.5 gm). A glucose meter was used
to measure the blood glucose from the tail droplet after an hour. PK
Solver ® tool was used for analysis of the experiment parameters via a
two-way ANOVA followed by a Tukey’s post hoc test.
2.6.7. Statistical analysis
Through analysis of variance two way (ANOVA), the pharmacoki­
netic and pharmacodynamics parameters collected from the three
groups were analyzed using PKSolver ® software. An add-in program for
pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamics data analysis in Microsoft Excel.
3. Results
3.1. Data analysis
Results of pre-screening study showed that high conc. PLGA up to
300 mg of PLGA 65:35 showed aggregates during evaporation of organic
solvent so we shift to 150 mg as higher level.
Stirring effect have no significant effect on entrapment efficiency.
The lactide concentration (A), PLGA concentration (B), and solvent% (C)
were chosen as independent factors, with coding of −1, 0, and 1, while
entrapment efficiency (Y1), particle size (Y2), and release after seven
days (Y3) were selected as dependent variables (Table 1). The relation­
ship between the actual and predicted values of Y1, Y2, and Y3 is shown
in Table 3. Interaction plots graph were created in order to visualize the
impacts of the independent variables and interaction factors on the
obtained responses (Y1, Y2, and Y3).
3.1.1. Effect of independent factors on Y1 response
An acceptable F-value of 29.04 and a p-value of 0.0001 were applied
in the ANOVA analysis for the Y1 response (Table 4), thus indicating that

and adjusted R2 (0.8573) also demonstrated good correlation, with a
difference of less than 0.2, as required by the design programme. The
lack of fit was not significant, at p = 0.1822, so this was considered
acceptable.

3.3.2. Pharmacodynamics study
As shown in Fig. 9, the non-treated hyperglycemic rats (group 2)
displayed standard hyperglycemia profiles as evidenced by their
significantly higher postprandial blood glucose levels on days 1, 3, and 7
after diabetes was induced (p < 0.05). In the treated diabetic rats in
group 3, daily oral linagliptin (4 mg/kg) therapy offered excellent gly­
cemic control, as demonstrated by a significant decrease in blood
glucose levels at all selected time points, including days 1, 3, and 7
following the induction of diabetes (p < 0.05), as compared to the
diabetic rats in group 2 [36].
However, the single subcutaneous injection of the same dosage of the
lina. nano preparation (4 mg/kg) resulted in a sustained improvement in
glycemic control for the entire week for the diabetic animals in group 4
at days 1, 3, and 7 after induction of diabetes (p < 0.05), also in com­
parison with the untreated diabetic rats in group 2.

An increase in the organic solvent ratio was expected to potentially
minimize the Y1 response, consistent with the idea that the high lip­
ophilicity characteristics of lactide, which are greater than those of
glycolide, will lead to lower water uptake, accelerated NPs solidifica­
tion, and enhanced entrapment of linagliptin in NP systems with higher
concentrations of lactide in the PLGA polymer & higher PLGA amount
[40]. Furthermore, increasing the viscosity of the prepared mixture by
decreasing the solvent amount during the NP synthesis was also ex­
pected to improve the entrapment efficiency of the lina [41].
The influence of various independent factors on particle size distri­
bution was maximized by increasing the PLGA concentration and
minimized by increasing in the organic solvent ratio as required for
testing. In theory, high viscosity can be best achieved by reducing the
solvent quantity during the preparation of NP, and this could increase
the entrapment efficiency of linagliptin with respect to the outside
buffer. The resulting increase in particle size was indeed illustrated by
the fact that the thickness of the polymeric skin increased, as observed in
TEM imaging [42].
Increased PLGA concentrations thus increase the viscosity of the
polymeric solution, leading to the development of coarser emulsions and
an increased particle size during diffusion & EE% [15].
The zeta potential values of the nanoparticle formulae thus ranged
from −10.98 ± 0.82 to −16.64 ± 0.62 mV, highly charged. So demon­
strating regular dispersal across the preparations & stable preparation
[43].
The influence of independent factors on percentage release can be
summarized as follows: the increase of PLGA concentration highly
minimized the Y3 response (release %), while the increase in organic
solvent ratio also minimized the Y3 response up to the 20% level, after
which release increased. This was in agreement with the theory that an
increase in particle size causes an increase in the thickness of the poly­
meric nanoparticles, supporting the hypothesis that using lower quan­
tities of solvents during NP formulation increases the encapsulation
effect of high volumes of PLGA, resulting in increased entrapment effi­
ciency in the drug with respect to the external buffer, hence improving
the release% [44].
Fig. S5 demonstrates that the release of lina. NPs was reduced by
increased lactide concentrations of up to 85% at an organic solvent ratio
of 20% for 100 mg of polymer. As previously indicated, an increase in
lactide concentration could also potentially reduce the Y3 response,
consistent with the results of Park et al. [45].

By increasing the PLGA concentrations and lactide ratios, the zeta
potential values were also raised. The effect of the carboxylic moiety on
the polymer that forms NPs may be the main cause of the resulting
negative charge; regardless, these groups repel one another, resulting in
a stable suspension that is evenly dispersed and less likely to clump [46].
Uniformity of particle size and shape was also demonstrated by TEM as
well as being evidenced by the low PDI results [32].
The compatibility study utilized DSC and FTIR. In terms of the DSC
results, no change in the thermal behavior of the optimized NPs formula
was seen as compared with the placebo, and no lina peak was observed.
This suggests that complete incorporation and encapsulation of the lina.
into the NP system was achieved [13]. Optimized formula represent
change in spectrum of lina may due hydrogen bonding between PLGA &
DMSO solvent while no new peak appear that indicate no interaction
might occur. This work in good accordance with Ibrahim et al. [47].
With respect to the FTIR, there was a clear lack of linagliptin’s
distinctive peaks, and no new peaks appeared in the FTIR spectrum in
the optimized NP formulation. This indicated that the drug was found in
its amorphous form in the formulation of the generated NPs, with no
obvious chemical reactions between the drug and any other excipients.
These results are also in agreement with those provided by Hiremath
et al. [48].
Fig. 8 (a) displays the plasma linagliptin calibration curve, with an R2
of 0.9993 demonstrating excellent linearity over the measured concen­
tration range (0.5–50 g/ml).
Fig. 8 shows the kinetic results, demonstrating that prolongation in
half-life (t1/2) and AUC0-∞ was significant, thus comfortably sustaining
the effect over seven days, as required to encourage more patient
compliance based on lower frequency of administration. This offers a
clear advantage in the injectable lina nano formula as compared to the
standard oral lina oral formula [49].
As illustrated in Fig. 9, the pharmacodynamics results represent the
influence of the nano preparation of lina on the blood glucose levels on
diabetic rats, including a significant promising decrease. These results
support the hypothesis that the antidiabetic activity of the injectable lina
PLGA loaded nanoparticle formulation is enhanced as compared to that
provided by the conventional oral tablet, with the in-vivo study results
offering agreement with work done by Gomaa et al. using same biode­
gradable polymer PLGA [13]. [50].
Finally, Formulation of lina NPs injection with higher half life time,
better pharmacokinetic properties with prolonged release profile &
accepted patient compliance with lower side effects in comparison with
other combination.
5. Conclusion
To achieve our aim, a seven-day sustained release profile for lina NPs
were prepared by single emulsion evaporation, an optimized NPs for­
mula was selected (65:35, 150 mg of PLGA in 10% organic solvent). The
results indicates that, the half-life of the selected formulation was
significantly increased when tested in vivo (t1/2 = 231.6 ± 43.9 h vs.
11.8 ± 0.3 h for Lina NPs and lina Oral, respectively). The AUC0- was
also significantly higher for the NP formulation of lina, launching from
33.73 to 60.53 ng/ml.h. Subcutaneous administration of 4 mg per ki­
logram of lina. nano preparation improved glycemic control in diabetic
rats for 7 days. Therefore, it may be helpful in the management of dia­
betes mellitus to develop subcutaneous NPs formulations containing lina
that provide prolonged release profiles especially in the form of inject­
able pre-filled syringe. It is probable that this intervention will enhance
patient adherence and mitigate the adverse effects associated with re­
petitive administration of the same doses.
