ABSTRACT
Purpose Nelfinavir (NFV), a FDA approved antiretroviral
drug, has been reported to exhibit cancer cells growth inhibi-
tion and increased apoptosis. However, it requires a higher dose
leading to toxicity, thus limiting its potential clinical translation.
We aim to develop biodegradable (poly (lactic-co-glycolic acid))
PLGA nanoparticles of nelfinavir and determine their efficacy
to treat non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC).
Experimental Design HIV protease inhibitor, NFV, was
loaded into PLGA nanoparticles by double emulsion/solvent
evaporation method; and nanoparticles were characterized
for physicochemical characteristics including morphology
and intracellular uptake. Their anti-cancer efficacy in
NSCLC was assessed by in vitro assays including cytotoxicity,
cellular migration, colony formation; and 3D spheroid culture
mimicking in-vivo tumor microenvironment. Studies were al-
so conducted to elucidate effects on molecular pathways in-
cluding apoptosis, autophagy, and endoplasmic stress.
Results NFV loaded PLGA nanoparticles (NPs) were found to
have particle size: 191.1 ± 10.0 nm, zeta potential: −24.3 ±
0.9 mV, % drug loading: 2.5 ± 0.0%; and entrapment efficien-
cy (EE): 30.1 ± 0.5%. NFV NP inhibited proliferation of
NSCLC cells compared to NFV and exhibited significant
IC50 reduction. From the caspase-dependent apoptosis assays
and western blot studies (upregulation of ATF3), it was revealed
that NFV NP significantly induced ER stress marker ATF3,
cleaved PARP and further caused autophagy inhibition
(LC3BII upregulation) leading to increased cellular death. In
addition, NFV NP were found to be more efficacious in pene-
trating solid tumors in ex-vivo studies compared to plain NFV.
Conclusions Nelfinavir, a lead HIV protease inhibitor can be
repositioned as a NSCLC therapeutic through
nanoparticulate delivery. Given its ability to induce apoptosis
and efficient tumor penetration capability, NFV loaded
PLGA nanoparticulate systems provide a promising delivery
system in NSCLC treatment.

INTRODUCTION
Lung cancer is the leading cause of cancer deaths worldwide
in both men and women with Non-small cell lung cancer
(NSCLC) accounting for approximately 85% of lung cancer
cases (1). Even though there are major advancements in the
field of discovery and development of novel drugs and delivery
systems, it still remains a challenging task to treat NSCLC.
Moreover, there is growing evidence that cancer cells develop
resistance to chemotherapeutic agents upon chronic use,
which makes it imperative to identify newer drugs with excep-
tional anticancer activity. As it takes 12–15 years for develop-
ing a new drug in the traditional drug discovery process, drug
repositioning (repurposing) has been drawing significant at-
tention because of its ability to identify new use of already
FDA approved molecules and hence accelerate the develop-
ment process at lower costs (2).
A multitude of growth factors or signaling pathways are
involved in cancer pathogenesis, phenotypically represented
by uncontrolled cancer cell proliferation. NSCLC progression
is often associated with activation of several intracellular signal
pathways causing enhanced cellular proliferation and/or di-
minished apoptosis (3,4). Particularly, endoplasmic stress and
autophagy have been shown to play vital roles in regulation of
tumor microenvironment (5,6). NSCLC disease pathogenesis
is also characterized by dysregulation of cell death pathways
including autophagy apoptosis mechanisms for its pathogene-
sis (7), hence targeting these processes could be of considerable
interest. In addition, there are multiple studies reporting the
association between these two processes further leading to
tumor growth (8,9). Thus, finding a class of FDA approved
drugs that can show prominent effects on these processes
would be beneficial.
Protease inhibitors (PIs) are a novel class of antiviral
drugs which have been imperative tools in treatment of
Human immunodeficiency virus type 1 (HIV-1) infection.
First generation PIs (Sequinavir and ritonavir) were ap-
proved by the FDA in 1995–96; and since then several
newer generation PIs have been approved. PI therapy was
instrumental in approximately 3-fold reduction in HIV-
related deaths in the United States (10). Additionally, sev-
eral recent studies support that PIs also possess antitumor
activities including activities against lung cancer (4), breast
cancer (11), melanoma (12) and ovarian cancer (13), inde-
pendent of their HIV protease inhibitory activity. There
are other reports highlighting the possible use of PIs as
cancer therapeutics (14,15). Another study by Gills et al.
reported that anticancer activity of PIs is independent of
their HIV protease inhibition through assessing the poten-
tial of various PIs (16). Among the protease inhibitors
investigated for anticancer efficacy, nelfinavir (NFV), a
second generation antiviral PI, has been studied by vari-
ous researchers to explore its ability to inhibit the growth
of cancer cells and is reported to significantly exhibit can-
cer cells growth inhibition and increased apoptosis
(17,18). Nelfinavir was also studied for its efficacy to in-
duce growth arrest and apoptosis in NSCLC cell lines
such as NCI-H460, A549 etc. (4). Interestingly, NFV
was also reported to inhibit Akt signaling, thus sensitizing
NSCLC cells for chemotherapy (4). These studies raise
interesting points regarding the mechanisms exploited by
nelfinavir to inhibit autophagy and have implications for
clinical development of nelfinavir as an anti-cancer agent.
NFV makes an interesting choice because of its well docu-
mented in-vitro anti-tumoral effects and current clinical stud-
ies to confirm its efficacy (19). However, the dose required for
NFV’s anticancer effects is shown to be very high. In one of
the seminal studies, Gills et al. demonstrated NFV to be effec-
tive against NSCLC xenografts in mice with once-daily intra-
peritoneal injections of 100 mg/kg NFV (16), with a human
equivalent dose (HED) of 8.1 mg/kg (20). Given highly vari-
able oral bioavailability of NFV (20–80%), this HED of
8.1 mg/kg translates into 1–3 g/day oral dosing for an aver-
age 70 kg adult (21,22). In addition, the major route of elim-
ination for NFV is through hepatic and biliary clearance
which may result in significant liver accumulation on chronic
use (23). In one of the clinical trials, an oral dose of 1500 mg
(twice-a-day; 3 g/day) nelfinavir for 12 month period demon-
strated partial remission of liposarcoma along with adverse
effects including diarrhea and infections (24). Altogether, to
harness the potential clinical use of NFV as an anticancer
agent, a suitable drug delivery system facilitating enhanced
efficacy and dose reduction is required.
Nanoparticulate drug delivery systems have been re-
ported for their capability to encapsulate higher amounts
of drugs and to provide selective therapeutic activity with
less side-effects (25,26). In addition, several recent studies
have reported their capability in (i) accomplishing stable
formulations (27), (ii) enhancing drug efficiency (28) and
(iii) applicability in biotechnology (29). Incorporating
NFV into a nanoparticulate system will potentially reduce
the required dose to exhibit anti-cancer efficacy.
Nanoparticulate drug delivery systems such as polymeric
nanoparticle carrier systems can enhance specificity, tol-
erability and drug efficacy (30). Biodegradable polymeric
nanoparticles have been used regularly due to their ver-
satility and biocompatibility in encapsulation of both hy-
drophilic and hydrophobic drugs (31). Nanoparticles act
as efficient carriers for delivering drug to the tumor while
demonstrating higher cellular uptake and tissue penetra-
tion; thus exhibiting tumor targeting potential (32,33).
This study helps in understanding and exploring the
growth inhibiting effects of NFV-loaded nanoparticulate
systems while studying the mechanism of action to mod-
ulate cell death pathways; and provides a rationale to
further evaluate nelfinavir as an anticancer agent

MATERIALS
Poly (lactic-co-glycolic) (PLGA 50:50, 7-17 kDa, acid terminat-
ed), poly vinyl alcohol (PVA), and nelfinavir (NFV) were pur-
chased from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, MO, USA). A549,
H4006 and H460 NSCLC cell lines were obtained from
ATCC and maintained in RPMI-1640 medium (Corning
Inc., Corning, NY, USA) supplemented with 10% FBS
(Atlanta Biologicals, Minneapolis, MN, USA), sodium pyru-
vate, penicillin-streptomycin (Corning Inc., Corning, NY,
USA) at 5% CO2/37°C. Healthy non-cancerous porcine kid-
ney proximal tubule cell line LLC-PK1 was obtained from Dr.
Yong Yu (St. John’s university, NY, USA) and maintained in
BioWhittaker® Medium 199 (Lonza Walkersville, Inc., MD,
USA) with 5% FBS and 10% penicillin-streptomycin. 3-(4,5-
dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide
(MTT), dichloromethane (DCM), dimethyl sulfoxide, couma-
rin-6, crystal violet dye, 16% paraformaldehyde (PFA) solu-
tion, and HPLC grade solvents including methanol, acetoni-
trile (ACN) and water were purchased from Fisher Scientific
(Hampton, NH, USA). Molecular biology kits, supplies, and
antibodies were purchased from other commercial vendors
which are listed at appropriate places throughout the
manuscript.
METHODS
Development of UV Spectrophotometric Method
for Nelfinavir Determination
A stock solution of 1 mg/ml concentration was prepared by
dissolving nelfinavir in methanol. From the stock solution,
standard solutions of 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 60, 70, 80, 90 and
100 μg/ml were prepared by diluting with methanol. All the
standard solutions were scanned using VWR UV-1600 PC
Spectrophotometer (VWR, Radnor, PA, USA) in the UV
range 200–600 nm. In spectrum, NFV showed maximum
absorbance at 253 nm. The calibration curve was constructed
between concentration vs absorbance.
Formulation of Nelfinavir Nanoparticles (NFV NP)
NFV NPs were formulated through ultra-probe sonication by
modifying a method reported earlier with slight modifications
(34). Briefly, primary emulsion was prepared by ultra-probe
sonication of polymer PLGA 502 RH (60 mg), NFV solution
in DCM (5 mg/3 ml) with 0.5 ml of milliQ water for 2 min (10
Sec on-off cycle, 40% amplitude) (Qsonica-Q500, Qsonica,
Newtown, CT, USA). Secondary emulsion was prepared by
subjecting primary emulsion obtained in previous step with
aqueous 1% w/v PVA solution in water for 4 min (10 Sec
on-off cycle, 40% amplitude). The obtained emulsion was
stirred overnight at room temperature for removal of organic
solvents and were subsequently washed to remove excess PVA
and unprocessed drug/polymer. NFV-loaded NPs were sep-
arated from the aqueous suspension medium by centrifuga-
tion at 1000×g (4°C for 3 min); and then washed twice with
water through centrifugation 21,000×g for 15 min (4°C).
Collected nanoparticles were re-dispersed in 1 ml of milliQ
water and were further characterized.
CHARACTERIZATION OF NFV NPS
Particle Size, Poly Dispersity Index (PDI) and Zeta
Potential
Particle size, PDI and zeta potential were measured by dy-
namic light scattering (DLS) using Zetasizer Nano (Malvern
Instruments, Malvern, UK). Formulations were diluted 100-
fold with milli-Q water to obtain samples for measurement of
size and zeta potential. The respective samples were loaded
into the zeta cells (DTS 1070) and analyzed.

mulation, a direct particle lysis approach was carried out. To
40 μL of formulation, 960 μL of methanol: ACN (45:55) was

the analyte sample. Clear supernatant was collected, analyzed

In- vitro Drug Release Study
In-vitro drug release profile from NFV NPs was determined in
phosphate buffered saline (PBS pH 7.4) with 1% polysorbate
80 for a period of 72 h. Briefly, 50 μl of nanoparticle formu-
lation was dispersed in 950 μl of PBS with 1% polysorbate 80
(pH 7.4) in 2 mL microcentrifuge tubes. Tubes were placed in
a shaker incubator at 37°C (200 rpm). At each specific time
point (0.5, 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, 12, 48 and 72 h), the amount of drug
released was determined by analyzing the collected superna-
tants by UV spectrophotometer at 253 nm after centrifuging
the samples for 15 min at 17,500×g. Drug release testing was
performed in triplicate and data obtained were represented as
mean ± SD.
Morphological Analysis Using Transmission Electron
Microscopy (TEM)
Imaging of NFV nanoparticles was performed using transmis-
sion electron microscopy (TEM) utilizing our previously pub-
lished protocols (35). Briefly, Formvar® carbon-coated cop-
per grids 400-mesh (Electron Microscopy Sciences, Hatfield,
PA, USA) were made hydrophilic using glow discharge plas-
ma treatment for 60s. About 5 μL of diluted nanoparticles
sample was added to the grid and allowed to adhere for
1 min. The samples were negatively stained by adding 5 μL
of uranyl acetate (1%) and excess was removed by using
Whatman filter paper after 30s. This process was repeated
twice and the sample was finally allowed to air dry. The grids
were then imaged using FEI Tecnai Spirit TWIN TEM
(Hillsboro, OR, USA) operated at 120 kV voltage.
Atomic Force Microscopy Analysis (AFM)
The morphological characterization of AFA NP was per-
formed using AFM. Imaging was carried out using the canti-
lever in air using Bruker Multimode AFM 8 (Bruker Nano
Surfaces, Billerica, MA, USA). Briefly, nanoparticles were
suspended in water and were drop-casted on to freshly cleaved
mica surface and imaged with Scanasyst-Air probe in peak
force mode.
Stability Studies
Stability of NFV NPs was evaluated while storing the samples
at temperature of 4, 25 and 37°C for four weeks as reported
previously (36). Samples were withdrawn after week 1, 2, 3
and 4; diluted with water (100-fold) and analyzed for particle
size, PDI and zeta potential using Malvern Zeta Sizer.
Entrapment efficiency was determined by lysing the samples
as described above using UV spectrophotometer. All experi-
ments were performed in triplicate.
Accelerated Stability Studies
Nanoparticles were kept at −80°C for 6 h to freeze and then
placed in freeze dryer (FreeZone 6 Liter Benchtop Freeze Dry
system, Labconco, Kansas City, MO, USA) for at least 24 h.
For this study, 5 mg of lyophilized NFV NP powder was stored
in an isolated chamber at 40°C/75% relative humidity (RH).
Samples were withdrawn on day 0, 7, 14, 21 and 28; and were
analyzed for particle size, zeta potential and % entrapment
efficiency.
In-Vitro Cellular Uptake Studies
Cellular uptake studies were performed using a protocol
reported earlier (34). NPs for cellular uptake were formu-
lated by replacing NFV with coumarin-6, a fluorescent
dye, for easier tracking. In brief, A549 NSCLC cells were
plated in tissue culture treated 8-chambered cover glass
(Eppendorf, Hauppauge, NY, USA) at a seeding density
of 10,000 cells/chamber followed by overnight incuba-
tion. Next day, cells were incubated with coumarin-6, or
coumarin-6 loaded nanoparticles at 1 μg/mL concentra-
tion for 1 and 3 h. At each time interval (1 and 3 h), cells
were washed with ice cold PBS twice and fixed with 4%
PFA for 10 min. Fixed cells were washed again with ice
cold PBS twice. Then, the chamber was removed and
20 μL of vectashield hardset mount with DAPI nuclear
stain (H1500, Vector laboratories, Burlingame, CA, USA)
was placed on a glass slide dropwise followed by placing a
cover glass. After overnight hardening of mounting medi-
um through at 4°C, slide was imaged using EVOS-FL
microscope with 20X objective (Thermo Scientific,
Waltham, MA, USA).
Cytotoxicity Studies
NFV NPs along with plain NFV were evaluated for their
cytotoxicity in three different non-small cell lung cancer
(NSCLC) cell lines: A549, H460 and H4006 as reported
earlier with slight modifications (34,37). Briefly, cells were
grown in FBS supplemented RPMI-1640 media as de-
scribed in Materials section and were seeded in TC treated
96-well plates (Eppendorf, Hauppauge, NY, USA) at a
seeding density of 2500 cells/well (7500 cells/cm2), incu-
bated overnight for adherence at 37°C/5% CO2. Next
day, treatments were prepared in the culture media at
different NFV concentrations ranging from 0.39–50 μM
and corresponding volumes of NFV NPs were calculated
based on the drug entrapment efficiency. Blank culture
media was added as control. After 72 h of incubation,
% cell viability was determined by performing MTT assay
as described earlier (37), by reading the absorbance of
dissolved formazan crystals at 570 nm using a multi-

Scratch Assay
In-vitro scratch assay was used to study the cell migration.
Briefly, scratches were created on a confluent cell monolayer.
The cells on the edge of the scratch will migrate toward the
center to close the scratch, thus establishing new cell-cell con-
tacts. The assay was performed as previously reported (35,38).
Briefly, A549 cells were plated in 24 well cell culture plates at a
seeding density of 1 × 105 cells/well followed by overnight
incubation. Next morning, scratches were made along the
center of all wells with the help of a sterile p200 (200 μL)
pipette tip. All the wells were washed with PBS twice and
reference markings were drawn near the scratch area from
the bottom side of the plate with a fine tip marker. Then
scratch images were captured within the marked area using
inverted microscope (Laxco, Mill Creek, WA, USA) with 10X
magnification. Later treatments of control, NFV, NFV NP
(7.5 μM) were added to the respective wells and incubated
followed by imaging after 15, and 24 h. The images captured
were analyzed quantitatively to assess the inhibitory effect of
NFV and NFV-loaded nanoparticles on cell migration.
Scratch width was measured from all wells using ImageJ soft-
ware and % scratch closure was calculated further, consider-
ing reformation of cellular monolayer as 100% scratch
closure.
Clonogenic Assay
Clonogenic assay is an in-vitro cell survival assay which is based
on the single cell’s capability to grow into a colony (39). Using
this assay, the effectiveness of the NFV and NFV NPs toward
inhibition of colony formation was determined. The protocol
reported previously (34,39) was modified and followed in this
study. Briefly, A549 cells were seeded into 6-well cell culture
plates at seeding density of 500 cells/well, and plates were kept
for overnight incubation to allow time for cells to adhere. Next
day, media was replaced, and cells were treated with NFV or
NFV NPs (7.5 μM), or control for 48 h after which media was
replaced with fresh culture medium on alternative days for
7 days. On 7th day, the colonies were stained with crystal violet.
Briefly, the contents of the well were removed and washed with
ice-cold PBS buffer twice followed by fixation with 4% PFA
solution (in ice-cold PBS) for 10 min. Fixed cells were again
washed with ice-cold PBS twice followed by staining with
0.01% (w/v) crystal violet solution with 1 h incubation. After
staining, cells were washed with distilled water and images were
captured using digital camera. Cell colonies were counted by
colony counter software Open CFU (40).
3D Spheroid Cell Culture Study
An effective NSCLC therapy is not only determined by en-
hanced cellular uptake or cytotoxic potential, but also with the
improved penetrability of nanoparticles into solid tumors
(41,42). 3D spheroid cell culture studies are capable of mim-
icking the in-vivo features of tumors as reported in our previ-
ous studies (26). Briefly, 2000 cells per well were seeded in
Corning® spheroid microplate (Corning, ME, USA) 96-well
round bottom plates at a density of 2.0 × 103 cells/well and
were incubated overnight at 37°C/5% CO2. Then cells
allowed to grow further for 3 days and then all the wells were
observed for spheroid formation with a rigid margin. Next
day, media was replaced with either fresh media (control) or
respective treatment solutions of NFV or NFV NP to achieve
5 and 10 μM in the wells. Spheroids were subjected to two
kinds of dosing treatments i.e. a single dose and a multiple
dose treatment. Briefly, both single and multiple dosing spher-
oids were treated with 10 μM and 20 μM concentrations of
both NFV and NFV NP (to maintain original concentrations
of 5 and 10 μM employed in the beginning), and images were
captured. For single dosing, 100 μL of media was replaced
with fresh media on further days of imaging. Images were
captured using LAXCO LMI-6000 series inverted micro-
scope (LAXCO, Mill Creek, WA, USA) on day 3, 6, 9, 12
and 15 post treatment. In case of multiple dose study, spher-
oids were treated with corresponding concentrations of NFV
or NFV NPs on respective days by replacing half of the me-
dium (100 μL) from every well in a gentle manner to avoid
bubble formation and aspiration of spheroid itself. For multi-
ple dosing, wells were replenished with 5 and 10 μM concen-
trations of NFV, NFV NP or fresh media (control). Images
were captured on day 3, 6, 9, 12 and 15 days following treat-
ment. Spheroid diameters were calculated using ImageJ soft-
ware (Version 1.44) (National Institute of Health, USA) and
further spheroid volumes were calculated.

Mechanism of Action
Apoptosis: Caspase 3/7 Assay
Apoptosis, or programmed cell death, plays a critical role in
assessing the potency of drug delivery systems. Caspase-3 is
one of the major markers of apoptotic cell death, which can be
activated in cells by both extrinsic and intrinsic pathways (43).
Caspase-3 activity was measured using EnzChek™Caspase-3
Assay Kit (Molecular Probes, Eugene, OR, USA) and assay
was performed as per manufacturer’s specifications. Briefly,
A549 cells were seeded at a density of 1 × 106 cells per tissue
culture dish (100 mm diameter) (Thermo Scientific, Waltham,
NY, USA) and treated with NFV, NFV NP (7.5 μM) or con-
trol for 6 h followed by harvesting and washing of cell pellets.
Cell lysis was carried out using 1X cell lysis buffer while sub-
jecting to freeze-thaw cycle, followed by centrifugation at
5000 × rpm for 5 min. Supernatants obtained were then
transferred to 96 well plate to which 50 μL of 2X substrate
working solution (10 mM Z-DEVD-AMC substrate +2X re-
action buffer) was added and incubated for 20 min.
Fluorescence was measured at excitation/emission 360/
460 nm (44).
Cell Apoptotic Analysis by Fluorescence Microplate Measurement
Annexin V FITC assay was performed using Annexin V FITC
assay Kit (Cayman chemical, Ann Arbor, MI, USA). This
assay helps in assessing the ability of compounds or testing
treatments to induce apoptosis (45). In brief, A549 cells were
plated at density of 2.5 × 104 cells/well in Falcon, black walled
clear bottom 96 well plate (Fisher scientific, Hampton, NH,
USA) and were incubated overnight. Next day, media was
replaced with NFV 10 μM, NFV NP 10 μM, Blank NP
(equivalent volume for NFV NP) or drug-free control for
6 h. Then, plate was centrifuged for 5 min at 400×g at room
temperature followed by aspiration of the supernatants and
addition of 100 μl of 1X binding buffer to each well. Later,
plate was centrifuged for 5 min at 400×g at room temperature
followed by aspiration of the supernatants. To each well 50 μl
of annexin V FITC/Propidium Iodide staining solution was
added and incubated for 10 min at room temperature. Then,
plate was centrifuged for 5 min at 400×g at room temperature
followed by aspiration of the supernatants and addition of
100 μl of 1X binding buffer to each well. Dead cells were
stained by propidium iodide (Ex ~617 nm, Em ~535 nm;
propidium iodide filter) and early stage apoptotic cells were
stained by annexin V FITC. Fluorescence was measured by
using filter designed to detect fluorescein ((Ex ~485 nm, Em
~535; Annexin V FITC).
Western Blot Studies
A549 cells were grown in a petri dish (100 mm diameter) at a
density of 1.0 × 106 cells per TC dish (Thermo Scientific,
Rochester, NY, USA) followed by overnight incubation.
Next day, the cells were treated with either control or different
concentrations (7.5 and 25 μM) of NFV and NFV NP formu-
lation and incubated for 24 h. After 24 h of treatment, cells
were harvested from petri dishes and centrifuged to obtain cell
pellets. These were washed 3 times with PBS and lysed using
1% Triton® X-100 (BP151–500, Fisher Bio-Reagents,
Hampton, NH, USA), 1% Halt™Protease and Phosphatase
Inhibitor Cocktail (PI78441, Thermo-Fisher Scientific,
Waltham, MA, USA) in PBS followed by sonication for
60 min at 4°C. Lysed samples were centrifuged at 13,000×g
at 4°C for 15 min and supernatants (lysates) were collected.
Cell lysate protein was quantified by the DC™Protein Assay
Kit (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA). Following protein nor-
malization across all groups, samples were mixed with 2×
Laemmli buffer (#161–0737, Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA)
and 2-mercaptoethanol, and denatured at 100°C for 10 min.
For Western blot analysis, 25 μg of protein was loaded and
separated on 4–20% Mini-PROTEAN® TGX™Precast

Characterization of NFV NPs
Particle Size, Polydispersity Index (PDI), Zeta Potential
Figure 1A represents the size distribution of NFV NP. As can
be seen, most of the NPs in formulation fell in a very narrow
size range, presented by a single peak on the histogram, which
also represents a nearly monodispersed formulation (Fig. 1A).
Nanoparticle formulation (NFV NP) was found to have a par-
ticle size, PDI and zeta potential of 191.9 ± 10.0 nm, 0.2 ± 0.1
and −24.3 ± 0.9 mV respectively as represented in Fig. 1E.
As reported earlier, size of the nanoparticles affects their rate
of cellular internalization; lower the size, higher the cellular
uptake. Also, PDI and surface charge play vital role in regard
to product quality/stability (46,47). Hence, observed charac-
teristics of NFV NP were found to elucidate a desirable
formulation.
Drug Content
As shown in Table 1, NFV NP was found to have % EE and
% DL of 30.07 ± 0.5% and 2.5 ± 0.04%. Concentration of
entrapped NFV was found to be 1500.0 ± 10.5 μg/ml corre-
sponding to 2.6 ± 0.1 mM of NFV in nanoparticle formula-
tion as represented in Table 1. Hence, developed NFV NPs
were found to possess significantly high drug loading capacity.
In-Vitro Release Studies
In-vitro release studies were performed to determine the drug
release pattern from NFV NP in phosphate buffered saline
(PBS; pH 7.4, 37°C) mimicking physiological fluid environ-
ment. The NFV NP was found to exhibit a controlled release
pattern with about half the drug (54.9 ± 2.4%) being released
over a period of 72 h, with minimal burst release of 8.2 ±
0.5% (first 30 min) as shown in Fig. 1B. This reveals the ability
of nanoparticles to release drug in sustained pattern after
reaching physiological environment.
The release mechanism and kinetics were determined
through application of various mathematical models: zero-or-
der, first-order, Korsmeyer-Peppas model and Higuchi mod-
el. The representative release plots can be found in Fig. S2.
Release equations were obtained by fitting the data to each
model. The equations and correlation co-efficient values for
zero-order, first-order, Korsmeyer-Peppas model and
Higuchi model release kinetics were found to be y =
0.009x + 0.171, R2 = 0.6385; y = 0.021x + 1.59, R2 =
0.9042; y = 0.425x-0.911, R2 = 0.9582; and y = 0.076x +
0.213, R2 = 0.8956 respectively (Table 1). Hence,
Korsmeyer-Peppas model was observed as the best-fitting
model for drug release from NFV NPs based on the highest
R2 value. Diffusional constant (n) obtained from Korsmeyer-
Peppas equation allows a better understanding of the drug
transport mechanism. n value was found to be 0.3, suggesting
that the release of NFV from NPs is controlled by Fickian
diffusion (n = <0.45) (48).

Accelerated Stability Studies
Nanoparticles are required to be evaluated for their thermal
stability and sensitivity to moisture as these environmental
conditions may result in compromised formulation quality.
During accelerated stability testing, the rate of degradation
is accelerated under stressful conditions such as high temper-
ature and humidity where samples are evaluated for their
physicochemical characteristics (50,51). Recommended accel-
erated storage conditions as per International council for har-
monization of technical requirements for pharmaceuticals for
human use (ICH) guidelines include 40° ± 2°C/75 ± 5% RH
have been employed in the current study (50). As shown in
Fig. 2; physical characterization studies (particle size, zeta po-
tential: Figs. 2A&B) and percentage of drug remaining (Fig.
2C) in samples withdrawn every week for a 1-month study,
revealed high stability of NFV NP under accelerated stability
conditions. In brief, even after storing for four weeks at accel-
erated conditions of temperature and humidity, there were no
major changes observed in particle size, zeta potential or the
drug content (particle size: 277.3 ± 2.6 nm, −18.5 ± 3.6 mV,
and 31.4 ± 0.8%) compared to their respective values (particle
size: 270.5 ± 0.0 nm, −20.1 ± 0.0 mV, 30.1 ± 0.5%) at the
initial time point. From the observed results, it can be
interpreted that the negative surface charge (about -20 mV)
of NFV NP is hindering the aggregation/coalescence of par-
ticles, hence no detrimental effect on particle size or drug
entrapment was observed.
Stability Studies
Stability is a major concern while dealing with nano-sized
drug delivery systems due to emulsion instability and particle
aggregation (36). In addition to accelerated stability studies,
regular stability studies were also performed. As shown in Fig.
S3, stability analysis data revealed similar results that NFV NP
formulation was stable with no significant changes in particle
size (Fig. S3A), zeta potential (Fig. S3B) and entrapment effi-
ciency (Fig. S3C) for up to 4 weeks of storage.
In-Vitro Cellular Uptake Studies
Figure 3 represents intracellular uptake of nanoparticle for-
mulations using A549 NSCLC cells. Based on our recently
published studies (35), fluorescent dye (Coumarin-6) loaded
polymeric nanoparticles were formulated and utilized in the
current study thus making it possible to visualize the uptake
and accumulation, and to evaluate in-vitro cellular uptake.
Fluorescent images taken following 1- and 3-h incubation with
coumarin loaded nanoparticles clearly demonstrated
significant internalization compared to plain coumarin in
A549 cancer cells even after 1 h incubation period (Fig. 3).
Hence encapsulation of coumarin/drug into PLGA nanopar-
ticles enabled interaction of nanoparticles with cell surfaces,
further resulting in efficient cellular internalization. It can be
assumed that higher cellular uptake of drug loaded NPs will
ultimately lead to higher intracellular accumulation of
nelfinavir, and sustained release of drug inside the cells, thus
resulting in sustained and higher anticancer activity as com-
pared to plain nelfinavir. This observation comes in accor-
dance with previously published studies reporting higher cel-
lular uptake of drug loaded PLGA nanoparticles in A549 cells
(34).
Cytotoxicity Studies
Anti-cancer cell viability studies were performed using
MTT assay to evaluate the cytotoxic potential of NFV-
loaded NPs versus plain drug. From this study, it was
revealed that NFV cytotoxicity against NSCLC cell lines
was significantly enhanced by nanoparticle encapsulation.
Similar trend was found in all three NSCLC cell lines:
A549, H4006 and H460. Figure 4 illustrates the cytotoxic
effects of NFV and NFV NP in all three NSCLC cell
lines. The IC50 values for plain NFV and NFV NP were
found to be 18.1 ± 2.6 μM, and 8.3 ± 0.4 μM in A549 cell
line; 24.3 ± 7.5 μM, and 5.0 ± 2.6 μM in H4006 cell line;
20.3 ± 9.7 μM, and 4.0 ± 2.6 μM in H460 cell line re-
spectively (Fig. 4D). Smaller and consistent particle size
of NFV NP facilitates efficient internalization of nanopar-
ticles resulting in more accumulation of nanoparticles in-
side the cells, thus leading to higher cytotoxic potential at
the same dose. As can be seen from Figs. 4A-D, it was
observed that IC50 value of NFV against A549, H4006
and H460 cells was lower in case of NFV NPs compared
to plain NFV which reflects improved anti-proliferative
activity of NFV in three cell lines. This could be attribut-
ed to what was reported before in literature that; although
free drug enters the cells by passive diffusion, nanoparti-
cles could be internalized by endocytosis (52,53). This
mechanism of internalization could also occur in tumor
cells, resulting in higher intracellular NFV concentration
with nanoparticles as compared to plain drug (52). In-
vitro studies to establish safety of optimized nanoformula-
tion were performed on porcine kidney epithelial cell line
LLC-PK1. When LLC-PK1 cell line was treated with
blank drug-free NFV NP equivalent nanoformulation
equivalent to 5 and 10 μM NFV for 24 and 72 h, cell
viability of >90% following both 24- and 72-h treatment
at both concentrations suggested that optimized NFV NP
was not toxic to LLC-PK1 cells, a normal noncancerous
cell line. The safety profile of nanoparticles has been pre-
sented in Fig. 4E. These observations were expected since

Scratch Assay
Scratch assay is a well-established method to access cell-
cell interaction, and cellular migration (38). In this meth-
od, a scratch is drawn manually on a confluent layer of
cells, and the potency of drug/formulations to inhibit cel-
lular migration in the scratched area is analyzed as a
function of % scratch closure with respect to time. For
these experiments, a scratch was made as described earli-
er, cells were treated with NFV or NFV NP or fresh
media (control), and images of scratched area were taken
at respective time points. Representative images for the
experiment are shown in Fig. 5A at 0, 15 and 24 h fol-
lowing treatment in A549 cells. After 24 h, % scratch
closure was found to be 96.8 ± 1.3, 97.1% ± 0.7, and
53.5 ± 20.9% for control, NFV 7.5 μM and NFV NP
7.50 μM respectively in case of A549 (Fig. 5B). At an
earlier time-point of 15 h, NFV NPs showed significantly
better efficacy in inhibiting cellular migration with plain
NFV showing no efficacy (NFV 7.5 μM vs NFV NP
7.5 μM - p < 0.05; and 24 h: NFV 7.5 μM vs NFV NP
7.5 μM – p < 0.05). From Figs. 5A & 5B, it can be under-
stood that scratches treated with control and NFV show
migration of cells and those treated with NFV NP showed
significant inhibition of cellular migration or scratch clo-
sure. This indicates the efficacy of NFV NP with en-
hanced anti-migratory effects, thus reducing the tumor
metastasis probability.

Western Blot Studies
NFV, originally intended as an antiviral drug, has been reported
to induce ER stress markers like ATF3. ATF 3 has also been
reported to play a major role in maintaining normalcy in many
cancers (64,65). Another major mechanism for cancer survival is
autophagy, wherein cells recycle their components to generate
ATP and sustain stressful conditions encountered during cancer
proliferation. It has been reported that inhibition of autophagy is
followed by an induction of apoptosis which further leads to cell
death (7,16). Considering all these factors, in the current study,
expression levels of ATF 3, cleaved PARP-1 (apoptosis marker)
and LC3B (autophagy marker) have been assessed in A549 cells,
following NFV or NFV-NP treatment. Representative blots for
ATF3, cleaved PARP and LC3B proteins can be found in
Fig. 10C. As shown, NFV-NP upregulated the levels of ATF3,
cleaved PARP and LC3BII after 24 h’ treatments, indicating
induction of ER stress and apoptosis; and autophagy inhibition
(LC3BII). Thus, a potential mechanism of cancer growth inhibi-
tion and cellular necrosis for NFV may be autophagy inhibition
or apoptosis induction which can be supplemental to the induc-
tion of ER stress biomarker ATF3.

drug repositioning provides an expedited drug development
process and identifies potential new uses of old drugs, it has
been growing in the past years (2). Hypoglycemic agents like
metformin, rosiglitazone; and immunosuppressants like
rapamycin are under investigation as anti-cancer therapeutics.
Protease inhibitors, a class of antiviral drugs, have been under
investigation for repositioning them as anti-cancer therapeutics
(16). In 2019, Rengan et al reported that nelfinavir enhances
the efficacy of standard chemoradiotherapy in NSCLC (67). In
another study, Xiang et al reported repositioning ability of
NFV as a cervical cancer therapy (18). However, due to the
requirement of higher doses (about 1500 mg twice daily), it
lacks clinical relevance and interest. It is necessary to develop
an efficient delivery system to target the disease and conse-
quently lowering the dose (24). Moreover, NFV is a BCS class
IV drug (Biopharmaceutical Classification System) with low
aqueous solubility and low permeability, underlining the need
of an optimal delivery system. Polymeric nanocarriers, well-
known carrier systems capable of delivering both hydrophilic
and hydrophobic drugs, have been chosen in current study (68).
Several research studies have been reported highlighting the
significance of nanoparticles in targeting tumors through deliv-
ering therapeutics including several from our own research
group (26,33). Khan et al developed curcumin-loaded PLGA
NPs and reported improved anti-cancer efficacy of curcumin
(69). As of now, there are no studies reporting the efficacy of
NFV-loaded drug delivery systems in NSCLC. Hence, in the
present study, an attempt was made to develop
nanotechnology-based systems for repurposing NFV, by using
PLGA as polymer and to explore their anti-cancer efficacy.
NFV was included in the organic phase while formulating
NPs due to its hydrophobic nature (70). Developed NPs were
found to load higher amounts of NFV (about 1500 ±
0.0 μg/ml) indicating its successful encapsulation.
Physicochemical characteristics of NFV NPs such as particle
size revealed the NFV NPs presented a nano-sized carrier sys-
tem which attributed to their efficient cellular uptake, con-
firmed by intracellular uptake in A549 cells (Figs. 1 & 3).
Polydispersity index of NFV NP indicated uniform particle size
distribution which is in agreement with morphological analysis
performed through TEM and AFM analysis. Higher negative
zeta potential value of NFV NP assisted in preventing the like-
lihood of NP aggregation, often possible due to smaller size of
NPs (71). Stability studies performed at accelerated conditions
and at different storage temperatures revealed that there were
no major changes observed in the particle characteristics even
after storage at either ASA or different temperature conditions
for one-month period. When stability studies were performed
to determine stability of NFV nanoparticles at different temper-
atures, it was found that there was no significant difference in
particle size, zeta potential and drug entrapment over 4 weeks
indicating formulation integrity as seen in Figs. 2 and S3.
Stability of the formulated NFV NP can be referred to the
higher surface charge of particles as hypothesized to inhibit
aggregation (72,73).
Determination of drug release pattern from nanoparticles
is very important to understand their in-vivo behavior.
Korsmeyer-Peppas model put forth a simple relationship
which describes the drug release pattern from a polymeric
system (74). Developed NFV NPs were found to exhibit
sustained release of the drug, following Korsmeyer-Peppas
model and Fickian diffusion from polymeric nanoparticles
(Fig. S2). Fickian model indicates that drug release from the
polymeric matrices is governed by diffusion where the diffu-
sion or transport rate of solvent is greater than the polymeric
chain relaxation (75).
Due to efficient intracellular uptake, NFV NPs demonstrated
greater cytotoxicity in immortalized NSCLC cell lines, thus
underlining importance of encapsulating NFV into NPs (76).
Extensive in-vitro cell culture studies were carried out to investi-
gate the efficacy of NFV NP against NSCLC. A significant re-
duction in IC50 values was observed for NFV after encapsulating
into nanoparticles as seen in Fig. 4. Generally, cell migration is an
important characteristic of any cancer which involves movement
of cells from one location to another, associated with cancer
metastasis (77,78). Cell migration is also essential for many phys-
iological processes such as neoangiogenesis and tumor invasion
(79). Hence, it is essential to understand the behavior of migrating
cells through proper in-vitro assays as reported by Pijuan et al.
(80). Results from the scratch assay seen in Fig. 5 revealed that
NFV nanoparticles had greater ability to inhibit cell migration
compared to NFV hence revealing their ability to inhibit metas-
tasis. Results from clonogenic assay study as seen in Fig. 6 re-
vealed that NFV NPs can inhibit the ability of single cells to form
colonies. Hence, it ensures the effectiveness of NFV NP on long
term basis while preventing tumor recurrence.
Development of 3D in-vitro models is of utmost necessity to
mimic in-vivo solid tumor conditions which could be achieved by
using specific low attachment spheroid plates as reported by
Vaidya et al (26). These models facilitate self-assembly of cancer
cells into spheroid masses with different proliferation areas and
cellular behavior which cannot be replicated using 2D systems.
All these features of 3D in-vitro models present them as more
realistic models as reported by Amaral et al. and Bresciani et al.
(81,82). From results seen in Figs. 7-9, it can be understood that
nanoparticles were able to deliver the drug to spheroids’ core
efficiently in both single and multiple dose study ensuring their
clinical use. In addition, live dead cell assay results confirmed
ability of NFV NP (multiple dosing) to penetrate through solid
mass of spheroids leading to the higher RF/GF even at both 5
and 10 μM concentrations compared to NFV. % cell viability
results on day 15th of spheroid study revealed the same
supporting the observations of live-dead spheroid assay.
Apoptosis is often referred to as programmed cell death
mechanism, with many pathways playing key roles in the pro-
cess. One of the major contributors to apoptotic process are

caspases (cysteine-aspartic protease enzyme), responsible for
proteolysis. Caspase-3, also known as one of the executioner
caspases, gets activated in presence of death inducing stimuli
(7,83). From the results, it was observed that NFV NP treat-
ment induced caspase-3 levels, although not significantly as
compared to plain NFV, demonstrating their ability to induce
cell death through apoptosis as seen in Fig. 10. In addition,
annexin-V based apoptotic assays were performed which re-
vealed the ability of NFV NP to actuate apoptosis. Annexin V
acts a sensitive probe due to its high affinity towards
phosphatidylserine present on the apoptotic cell plasma mem-
brane (84,85). NFV NP treated cells have exhibited significantly
higher apoptosis at both 5 and 10 μM. These results correlate
with the potential of NFV NP in inducing apoptosis through
activation of caspase levels in the treated A549 cells. Overall,
these results are confirming the role of apoptosis in inhibitory
effect of NFV NPs’ on cellular growth and proliferation.
Extensive ER stress induction and autophagy inhibition can
cause non-apoptotic cell death (16). At increased intensity or
duration of cellular stress, these pathways has been reported to
activate cell death mechanisms (86). Activating transcription fac-
tor 3 (ATF3), activated in response to ER stress acts as biomarker
to assess the potentiality of treatments in cancers (16,87).
Ubiquitin-like conjugation system such as microtubule-
associated protein light chain 3B-II (LC3B-II) perform the key
processes in each phase of autophagy (7). LC3B-II is a quantita-
tive marker of autophagy since it is required for the formation of
autophagosomes and its level is proportional to the amount of
autophagosomes in the cells (88). From the western blots of A549
cells following NFV/NFV-NP treatment, an upregulation of
LC3B protein levels was observed. Apoptosis was also evaluated
by assaying for cleaved PARP, a general marker of apoptosis
(89). Immunoblotting analysis demonstrated the dramatic in-
crease in the levels of ATF3 and cleaved fragments of PARP
(Fig. 10), indicating induction of apoptosis which can be corre-
lated with the enhanced early apoptotic cells observed from
annexin V/FITC apoptosis assay.
Nanotechnology-based repositioning of antiviral drug,
nelfinavir could be a promising approach to target NSCLC,
beneficial with an efficient nanoparticulate system as a carrier.
Developed NFV-loaded PLGA nanoparticles were found to
be exerting superior anti-cancer efficacy compared to plain
drug against NSCLC. Taken together, results from the cur-
rent study including in-vitro cell culture studies as well as 3D
spheroid models illustrate their pronounced efficiency in
NSCLC treatment after encapsulating NFV into a
nanoparticulate system.
CONCLUSION
With the present investigations, it can be concluded that NFV-
loaded PLGA nanoparticles were successfully formulated
using double-emulsification technique and were evaluated as
a potential treatment for NCSLC. Compared to NFV alone,
NFV NPs exhibited higher cytotoxicity, enhanced inhibitory
effect on migration and colony growth. In addition, cellular
uptake studies and 3D spheroid studies; assays exhibiting ca-
pability of NFV nanoparticles in induction of ER stress, apo-
ptosis and inhibition of autophagy process are promising them
as a potential treatment strategy for NSCLC. While the pre-
liminary results are promising, its pre-clinical and clinical eval-
uation could be expedited to emphasize the drug reposi-
tioning of NFV to treat NSCLC.
