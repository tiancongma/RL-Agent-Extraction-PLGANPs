A B S T R A C T
Polymeric nanoparticles have been widely investigated as a controlled release drug delivery platform for the
treatment of tuberculosis (TB). These nanoparticles are readily internalised into macrophages and leads to high
intracellular drug concentration. In this study two anti-TB drugs, amikacin and moxiﬂoxacin were encapsulated
into PLGA nanoparticles. The novelty of this work includes: (1) the eﬃcient encapsulation of two hydrophilic
second-line anti-TB drugs, and (2) intra-macrophage delivery of this combination potentially for rapid treatment
of multi-drug resistant TB (MDR-TB). Two water-oil-water (w/o/w) emulsion strategies were employed in this
study to produce: (1) alginate coated PLGA nanoparticles, and (2) alginate entrapped PLGA nanoparticles. The
average particle size and polydispersity index (PDI) of the alginate coated PLGA nanoparticles were found to be
unfavourably high with values of 640 ± 32 nm and 0.63 ± 0.09, respectively. In contrast, the alginate en-
trapped PLGA nanoparticles were within the desired particle size range of 312–365 nm and the PDI was 0.14–
0.31, and therefore were chosen for subsequent studies. Alginate entrapped nanoparticles exhibited an en-
trapment of moxiﬂoxacin in the range of 10.1–18.7 μg/mg and an entrapment of amikacin in the range of
15–17.4 μg/mg. On the contrary, non-modiﬁed PLGA nanoparticles exhibited a signiﬁcantly less amikacin
loading of 6.2 μg/mg polymer and 4.2 μg/mg of moxiﬂoxacin. The release of alginate modiﬁed PLGA nano-
particles showed slower release in comparison with the non-modiﬁed PLGA nanoparticles. The alginate en-
trapped PLGA nanoparticles did not show signiﬁcant diﬀerence from the alginate coated PLGA nanoparticles in
the release pattern. To study the macrophage uptake eﬃciency, both formulations of the alginate entrapped
nanoparticle; non-calcium formulation and calcium, and alginate coated PLGA nanoparticles (that includes no
calcium) formulation was loaded with coumarin-6 as a marker, seeded to THP-1 derived macrophages infected
with Mycobacterium tuberculosis (H37Ra), and examined by confocal microscopy. Both formulations of alginate
entrapped particles (calcium and non-calcium) were readily internalised into the macrophages and highly
concentrated in the perinuclear region, and the non-calcium alginate entrapped formulation showed a maximum
internalisation. Furthermore, the anti-mycobacterial activity of the dually entrapped drug-loaded particles
(moxiﬂoxacin and amikacin) was evaluated using M. tuberculosis-infected macrophages, which revealed an en-
hanced inhibition of viable bacterial count compared to single drug loaded nanoparticle formulations, and
untreated cells. The dually entrapped nanoparticles showed bacterial viability of 0.6% relative to the untreated
group, compared to 6.49% for amikacin alone nanoparticles and 3.27% for moxiﬂoxacin alone nanoparticles. In
conclusion, the amikacin-moxiﬂoxacin alginate entrapped PLGA nanoparticles are promising for further in vivo
studies.

1. Introduction
Tuberculosis (TB) is a serious communicable infectious disease that
predominately manifests in the lungs and caused by various strains of
mycobacteria, primarily Mycobacterium tuberculosis [1]. The World
Health Organization (WHO) estimated that about 33% of the world
population is a carrier of the pathogen and more than 8 million of active
TB cases happen yearly [2]. TB pathogenesis is initiated by the contact
and engulfment of airborne bacilli by alveolar macrophages. This is
followed by the multiplication and invasion of neighboring alveolar
macrophages. Although epithelial type II pneumocytes and dendritic
cells are also involved in the prognosis of TB, alveolar macrophages are
the most elucidated target to treat TB [3–5]. For standard nonresistant
TB, WHO recommended a two-phase treatment, comprising an in-
tensive phase for two months and a continuation phase for another four
months. During the intensive treatment phase, patients are adminis-
tered four anti-TB drugs including isoniazid, rifampicin, pyrazinamide,
and ethambutol daily, while the continuation treatment phase involves
daily dosing of isoniazid and rifampicin [6]. This frequently results in
poor patient adherence that often led into multidrug-resistant TB
(MDR-TB) or extensive drug-resistant TB (XDR-TB) [7].
MDR-TB is an infection caused by M. tuberculosis strains resistance
to two of the ﬁrst-line anti-TB drugs, isoniazid and rifampin. Agents to
treat MDR-TB including aminoglycosides (i.e. amikacin, capreomycin,
and kanamycin) and ﬂuoroquinolones (i.e. moxiﬂoxacin and levo-
ﬂoxacin) [8]. Amikacin is one of the main injectable aminoglycosides
used in the management of MDR-TB. However, on prolonged use the
drug often leads to nephrotoxicity, neurotoxicity and irreversible oto-
toxicity [9]. Moxiﬂoxacin is another widely used drug to treat MDR-TB
[10]. The drug is a fourth-generation synthetic ﬂuoroquinolone which
has favorable pharmacokinetics, that include a large volume of dis-
tribution and the ability to permeate the epithelial-lining ﬂuid and
macrophages [11]. However, the extensive use of moxiﬂoxacin was
reported to lead to the emergence of extensively drug-resistant (XDR)
M. tuberculosis strains [12]. The combinative administration of these
two antibiotics have shown synergy against MDR-TB [13].
The utilization of polymeric drug nanoparticles has been widely
investigated for their potential to improve the eﬃcacy and targeting of
drugs for various diseases including TB [9,14–17]. Polymeric nano-
particles oﬀer the advantage of readily internalised into alveolar mac-
rophages by the same defence machinery employed in the engulfment
of M. tuberculosis [18–21]. Polymeric nanoparticles also provide a sus-
tained release system and improve the stability of labile drugs from in
vivo enzymatic degradation [22,23]. Anti-TB drug loaded polymeric
nanoparticles have been investigated for many routes of administra-
tions including oral, intravenous, and inhalation [24]. For instance, in a
preclinical evaluation of PLGA nanoparticles containing rifampicin,
isoniazid and pyrazinamide administered via either oral or aerosol
routes revealed the presence of the drugs above their minimum in-
hibitory concentration (MIC) in the lung, liver and spleen tissues up to
15 days [25,26]. In contrast, no drugs were detected following the
administration of free drugs after 12–24 h [26]. Therefore, nanoparticle
delivery system has the potential to reduce the dosing frequency,
minimize the incidence of resistance and better patient adherence to the
anti-TB regimen.
In the present study, moxiﬂoxacin and amikacin were encapsulated
into nanoparticles targeting for the treatment of MDR. The combinative
encapsulation of these two drugs into polymeric nanoparticles has the
potential to decrease the dosing of these drugs, thereby improves pa-
tient compliance to treatment, and potentially mitigates the undesirable
dose-associated side eﬀects. In this study, the drugs were incorporated
into PLGA nanoparticles using two water-oil-water (w/o/w) emulsion
strategies to produce: (1) alginate coated PLGA nanoparticles, and (2)
alginate entrapped PLGA nanoparticles (treated with calcium and not
treated with calcium). The particles were characterised in term size,
drug loading, morphology, in vitro release, Fourier transform infrared
(FTIR), and diﬀerential scanning calorimetry (DSC). These alginate
modiﬁed PLGA nanoparticles were then compared to nonmodiﬁed
PLGA nanoparticles. The macrophage phagocytosis eﬃciency of the
particles was also investigated using confocal microscopy and in vitro
intracellular killing using macrophages infected with M. tuberculosis
H37Ra model.

2.2. Formulation of drug loaded PLGA/alginate nanoparticles
Two strategies were employed for the formulation of the dual-drug
amikacin-moxiﬂoxacin alginate PLGA nanoparticles as illustrated in
Fig. 1. In the ﬁrst formulation, alginate was utilised as a coating
polymer of the PLGA nanoparticles. In the second formulation, alginate
was entrapped in the internal phase to increase the drug encapsulation.
In the ﬁrst formulation, alginate coated PLGA nanoparticles were
formulated by double emulsion evaporation. Brieﬂy, 5 mg of amikacin
and 5 mg of moxiﬂoxacin were dissolved in 0.3 ml of DI water (wa-
terinternal phase). The solution was subsequently emulsiﬁed with 1 ml of
DCM containing 50 mg of PLGA (oil phase) at a duty cycle of 40 and
intensity of 4 for 2 min using a probe sonicator. The resulted wa-
terinternal/oil (W/O) emulsion was further emulsiﬁed with 5 ml aqueous
solution of PVA 2.5% containing 50 mg alginate (waterexternal phase) to
produce a waterinternal/oil/waterexternal (W/O/W) system. The w/o/w
emulsion was stirred for 5 h to evaporate the organic solvent under
reduced pressure. Nanoparticles were subsequently centrifuged and
washed at 14,500 rpm for 20 min. Nanoparticles were then lyophilised
at −30 °C for 24 h followed by at 25 °C for 48 h in a freeze drier (Christ
Alpha 1–4 LOC-1 M, Martin Christ, Osterode am Harz, Germany).
In the second formulation, alginate entrapped PLGA nanoparticles
were fabricated using a modiﬁed emulsion evaporation technique. 5 mg
amikacin and 5 mg moxiﬂoxacin were dissolved in 2 ml of 10 mg/ml
alginate solution (waterinternal phase) and heated at 50 °C for 15 min.
The ﬁnal volume was then adjusted to be 1 ml. Subsequently, 0.3 ml of
the above solution was emulsiﬁed with 1 ml DCM containing 50 mg
PLGA (oil phase) using a probe sonicator at the same conditions de-
scribed above. The resulted w/o emulsion was further emulsiﬁed with
5 ml of PVA 2.5% with (calcium formulation), or without 10 mg CaCl2
(non-calcium formulation). The emulsion was then evaporated, wa-
shed, and lyophilised as mentioned earlier. Single drug-loaded nano-
particles (amikacin or moxiﬂoxacin) were also formulated in a similar
manner. The formulation of PLGA nanoparticles was prepared in a si-
milar manner except the alginate was omitted from the formulation.

2.8. Statistical analysis
Statistical analysis was performed using analysis of variance
(ANOVA) to compare the eﬀect of dose and formulation/drug used
(Graph-Pad Prism, San Diego, CA, USA). Particle size, Polydispersity,
and entrapment eﬃciency, were compared using student t-test to
compare the alginate coated PLGA nanoparticles or the alginate en-
trapped calcium formulation with the non-calcium formulation. Results
were presented as the mean ± SD unless stated otherwise. Rejection of
the null hypothesis was considered when p < 0.05.
3. Results and discussion

drug loading and zeta potential of the alginate coated and alginate

droplets, eventually leading to larger particles. However, this is not
surprising as previous studies on alginate based nanoparticles showed
the particles possessed wide particle size distribution with a relatively
high polydispersity [31,32]. Therefore, it was decided to change to new

formulation strategy that can produce smaller particle size.
For the above reason, alginate entrapped PLGA nanoparticles were
developed on the basis of entrapping the drugs with alginate in the
internal phase. Two formulation strategies were employed; calcium
containing formulation, and calcium free formulation. In the calcium
containing formulation, CaCl2 was added to the external phase to
complex with alginate/PLGA matrix. In the second formulation, no
calcium was added. Results showed comparable drug loading between
these formulations; 18.7 ± 7.1 moxiﬂoxacin μg/mg, and 15 ± 3
amikacin for the calcium containing formulation, and 10.3 ± 1.2 μg/
mg moxiﬂoxacin and 17.4 ± 1.6 amikacin for the formulation free
formulation. However; the particle size and the polydispersity were
signiﬁcantly larger for the calcium containing formulation. This can be
ascribed to the complexation of calcium ions with alginate tend to re-
sult in large particle size [33–36]. During the evaporation of the di-
chloromethane and the precipitation of PLGA, it is expected that cal-
cium chloride in the external phase can complex with alginate that is
entrapped in the nanoparticles. As a result, the matrix shell of the na-
noparticles is likely composed of PLGA and calcium alginate. Com-
paring the nonmodiﬁed PLGA nanoparticles with the alginate modiﬁed
PLGA nanoparticles showed that the latter have signiﬁcantly higher
drug loading. This is due to the presence of alginate in the formulation
that possibly bind to entrapped moxiﬂoxacin and amikacin, or provide
an additional barrier in case of alginate coated PLGA nanoparticles.
The calcium complexed alginate nanoparticles showed a particle
size of (∼400 nm) (Fig. 2B) and the size of the calcium-free particles
was visually concurred with the SEM observation (∼300 nm). The
nanoparticles were also spherical (Fig. 2C), which is appropriate for
internalisation into alveolar macrophages. Moreover, the drug loading
ﬁgures of moxiﬂoxacin and amikacin in the calcium free alginate
trapped PLGA particles were in agreement with other hydrophilic drugs
[37,38]. The zeta potential of the nanoparticles was found to be slightly
positive only with the formulation that contain calcium, this could be to
the adsorption of the calcium on the surface of the nanoparticles. This is
probably due to the presence of alginate-complexed calcium on the
surface of the nanoparticles.
The drug encapsulation eﬃciency of emulsiﬁed PLGA particles is
highly reliant on the shear forces and formulation conditions, such as
drug-polymer ratio, surfactant concentration, and the volume of the
external aqueous phase [39,40]. Using a similar double emulsion sol-
vent evaporation technique, Boysenx et al. encapsulated hydrophilic
isoniazid and hydrophobic rifampicin in PLGA nanoparticles with a
drug encapsulation eﬃciency of about 68% and 55%, respectively and
the size was between 229 and 382 nm [41]. In another study, Tripathi
et al. showed an encapsulation eﬃciency of 39–70% for rifampicin in
PLGA particles of 430–920 nm in size [42].
In vitro release of the antibiotics from the non-modiﬁed PLGA na-
noparticle formulation showed a typical biphasic pattern consisting of
an initial burst release followed by controlled release (Fig. 3). However,
alginate entrapped (non-calcium or calcium complexed) and alginate
coated nanoparticles showed a signiﬁcantly slower release than the
nonmodiﬁed PLGA nanoparticles. This initial burst release has been
seen previously with antibiotics loaded PLGA nanoparticles [9,43].
However; it has been shown previously that alginate entrapped PLGA
particles can release calcein at signiﬁcantly slower rate with a less burst
release compared to PLGA microparticles [44]. Moreover, alginate
chitosan polyelectrolyte complex coated PLGA nanoparticles have
shown signiﬁcantly slower release pattern of doxorubicin compared to
uncoated nanoparticles [45].
3.2. Fourier Transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR)
In contrast to PLGA nanoparticles or non-calcium alginate en-
trapped PLGA nanoparticles FTIR absorption spectrum, the complexa-
tion of calcium with alginate appeared as a distinct peak at band of
935 cm−1 in the calcium alginate entrapped drug loaded PLGA
nanoparticles (Fig. 4). This FTIR absorption band corresponds to the
C–O stretching vibration of pyranosyl ring and the C–O stretching and
contributions from C–C–H and C–O–H deformation [46]. Calcium al-
ginate entrapped PLGA nanoparticles show signiﬁcant diﬀerences in
band positions when compared to the FTIR spectrum of non-calcium
alginate entrapped PLGA nanoparticles or the alginate coated nano-
particle formulation. The PLGA band of 1762 cm−1 which corresponds
to C=O stretching is evident in the structure of all nanoparticle for-
mulations since all of them contain PLGA. When comparing the drug
loaded nanoparticles with the bank nanoparticles formulation, most of
the amikacin sulfate or moxiﬂoxacin HCl bands was masked by the
nanoparticles formulation constituents. For example, the peak at
1100 cm−1 which is mostly due to SO4−2 asymmetric stretching vi-
brations is hidden under the alginate and PLGA C–O stretching bands
[46,47]. The peak at 1250 cm−1, which was found in drug loaded na-
noparticles formulations, is likely due to SO4−2 asymmetric stretching
[48],. The moxiﬂoxacin absorption pattern at 3050 to 3156 cm−1 was
due to vibrational C–H aromatic, and the pattern at 1323-1256 cm−1
was due to -CH2 bending (Fig. 4) [49].

3.4. Nanoparticle internalisation by macrophages
To visualise the ability of diﬀerent formulations of nanoparticles
(the calcium-free alginate entrapped PLGA nanoparticles, calcium al-
ginate entrapped PLGA nanoparticles, and alginate coated PLGA na-
noparticles to be internalised by the macrophages, the nanoparticles
were loaded with coumarin-6, and nanoparticle suspensions (10 μg/ml)
were seeded to the THP-1 derived alveolar macrophages and viewed
under confocal microscopy.
In all formulations, nanoparticles were dispersed throughout the
cytosol of the macrophages, and more concentrated in the perinuclear
region. Furthermore, the ImageJ curves shows that the frequency of
pixels with high ﬂuorescence is the highest in the alginate entrapped
calcium free formulations (Fig. 6 I (A-C)). This suggested an extensive
distribution of the nanoparticles throughout the cytosol with more
particles concentrated in regions around nucleus. However, the cou-
marin-6, alginate entrapped non-calcium formulation showed the
maximum internalisation compared to calcium alginate PLGA nano-
particles showed signiﬁcantly less nanoparticles internalisation, and
alginate coated PLGA nanoparticles showed the minimum internalisa-
tion. This is because non-calcium formulation is negatively charged,
and has small particle size and polydispersity compared to the calcium
containing formulation. Also, the ability of calcium to complex into
strong matrix renders the nanoparticles less likely to internalize into
THP-1 cells. The cytosol of the macrophages was observed to be pre-
dominately-ﬂuorescing light corresponding to the coumarin-6 loaded
nanoparticles while the red color corresponds to the nucleus area (Fig. 6
II)
In general, the phagocytosis of particles is inﬂuenced by the size,
hydrophobicity, surface charge and morphology of the particle [50,51].
The calcium free particles were less than 1 μm, negatively charged, and
spherical therefore eﬃciently internalised into macrophages. It has
been shown that anionic nanoparticles have a higher cellular uptake
into phagocytic cells compared to cationic nanoparticles, presumably

due to the ingestion of negatively charged bacteria [50]. In one study,
anionic polystyrene nanoparticles demonstrated preferential accumu-
lation in the liver, where macrophages of the reticuloendothelial system
reside [52]. In another study, 75% of immortal primary human alveolar
type 1 (AT1) cells internalized negatively charged particles, while less
than 55% of the cells internalized positively charged particles [53]. The
positively charged nanoparticles have also demonstrated higher cyto-

the Tg of PLGA 50:50 is between 50 and 55 °C, suggesting the particles

human monocytes [55]. This property of the nanocarriers is promising
for targeting treatment of latent TB where the M. tuberculosis dormant
inside the macrophages [56].
3.5. In vitro viability of M. tuberculosis H37Ra growth within the host
The eﬃcacy of the drug-loaded nanoparticles was tested against M.
tuberculosis H37Ra infected macrophages. Fig. 7 shows the viable M.
tuberculosis (H37Ra) count after treatment with nanoparticles at various
concentrations. The dual entrapment of amikacin and moxiﬂoxacin into
alginate entrapped nanoparticles showed enhanced inhibition of bac-
terial growth compared to single drug loaded amikacin nanoparticles or
moxiﬂoxacin nanoparticles, but less inhibition compared to the free
drugs. These phenomena of the drug loaded nanoparticles being less
eﬃcacious than the free drug were also reported in a previous study
where gentamicin loaded PLGA nanoparticles showed less anti-
microbial activity compared to the free gentamicin against Pseudomonas
aeroginosa in vitro planktonic and bioﬁlm susceptibility assays. How-
ever, when the particles were administered intraperitoneally to the
mice that were infected with same P. aeruginosa strain, a signiﬁcant
reduction of bacterial count was observed in the blood and bronch-
oalveolar lavage ﬂuid after 96 h post-treatment compared to the free
drug treatment [16]. Similarly, the nalmefene PLGA microspheres de-
monstrated a controlled release proﬁle over 4 weeks in PBS (pH 7.4) at
37 °C, while the mean residence time was about 16 days when delivered
intravenously to mice [31]. Therefore, the alginate PLGA particles are
expected to exhibit a faster degradation and improved anti-TB activity
in vivo.
The enhanced activity of the (amikacin + moxiﬂoxacin) loaded
nanoparticles was demonstrated by comparing the colony forming units
(cfu) and hence the viability of the intracellular M. tuberculosis H37Ra
treated with (amikacin + moxiﬂoxacin) loaded nanoparticles with
single drug loaded nanoparticle formulations (amikacin or moxi-
ﬂoxacin). For example, amikacin (8.7 μg/mL) and moxiﬂoxacin
(5.2 μg/ml) alone loaded nanoparticles showed mycobacterial growth
of 34.9% and 3.27%, respectively. However, amikacin-moxiﬂoxacin-
loaded nanoparticles at similar concentrations showed a growth of
1.75%, which is signiﬁcantly lower than the single drug-loaded for-
mulations (Fig. 7A) (p < 0.0001). Therefore, the dual-drug nano-
particle formulation provides a useful platform for macrophage uptake
and controlled release of these 2 s line anti-TB drugs.
On the other hand, the free drug treated groups showed a sig-
niﬁcantly stronger bactericidal eﬀect than the nanoparticle formula-
tions. For example, free amikacin (6.25 μg/mL) and moxiﬂoxacin
(6.25 μg/mL) exhibited 13% and 0.5% bacterial viability, respectively.
While the free combination of amikacin and moxiﬂoxacin showed 0.3%
viability (Fig. 7B). The stronger activity of these free drugs compared to
their nanoparticle counterparts probably due to a higher eﬀective
concentration of the free drug formulations in the 96-well plate com-
pared with drugs in the nanoparticles which would take a longer time to
release. Nanoparticles have been widely investigated for their potential
to reduce the duration of anti-TB chemotherapy. Moxiﬂoxacin and
amikacin are second-line drugs that have shown synergistically inhibit
MDR-TB [13]. Moxiﬂoxacin acts on bacteria by inhibiting the bacterial
DNA through binding to DNA gyrase enzyme, while amikacin binds to
bacterial 30s ribosomal subunits resulting in misreading of t-RNA
[57,58]. As a result, co-administration of these two drugs with diﬀerent
mechanism of action coupled with a polymeric nanoparticulate matrix
could enhance clinical outcome. Furthermore, coupling polymeric na-
noparticles with inhalation delivery has gained signiﬁcant interest for
treatment of TB [41,59,60]. In our previous studies, we have success-
fully formulated anti-TB pure drugs including rifapentine, moxiﬂoxacin
and pyrazinamide into inhalable dry powders [61,62]. Further studies
are ongoing to formulate the free alginate entrapped PLGA nano-
particles into inhalable form and testing its eﬃcacy in TB in vivo
models.
4. Conclusion
Polymeric nanoparticles provide a useful platform for entrapping
and release controlling a wide variety of antibiotics. This is due to their
biodegradability and safety and satisfactory physical characteristics. In
this context, 2 s line anti-TB drugs were incorporated into alginate
modiﬁed PLGA nanoparticles. The improved entrapment and controlled
release of the drugs by the modiﬁcation of PLGA with alginate reﬂected
in the synergistic eﬀect of the dual entrapment of moxiﬂoxacin and
amikacin
when
compared
to
single
drug
loaded
nanoparticles.
Moreover; it is expected that these nanoparticles have superior anti-
mycobacterial activity compared to free drug administration in vivo.
