Abstract
Background
The aim of this study was to prepare
poly(D,L-lactide-co-glycolide)
(PLGA)
microspherical
implants containing teicoplanin (TCP) using a double
emulsion solvent evaporation method and to evaluate its
eYcacy for the local treatment of chronic osteomyelitis.
Methods
The particle size and distribution, morphological
characteristics, thermal behaviour, drug content, encapsula-
tion eYciency and in vitro release assessments of the for-
mulations were carried out. Sterile TCP–PLGA microspheres
were implanted in the proximal tibia of rats with methicillin
resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) osteomyelitis.
After 3 weeks of treatment, bone samples were analysed
with a microbiological assay and evaluated histopathologi-
cally.
Results
Microspheres between the size ranges of 2.01 and
3.91 m were obtained. Production yield of all formula-
tions was found to be higher than 82% and encapsulation
eYciencies of 33.6–69.8% were obtained. DSC thermo-
gram showed that the TCP was in an amorphous state in
microspheres. In vitro drug release studies had indicated
that the drug release rate of microspheres was decreased
upon increasing the polymer:drug ratio. Based on the in
vivo data, rats treated with implants and intramuscular
injection showed 1.7 £ 103 § 1.3 £ 103 and 5.8 £ 104 §
5.3 £ 104 colony forming unit of MRSA in 1 g bone sam-
ples (CFU/g), respectively (P < 0.01).
Conclusions
The in vitro and in vivo studies had shown
that the TCP–PLGA microspheres were eVective for the
treatment of chronic osteomyelitis in an animal experimen-
tal model. Hence, these microspheres may be potentially
useful in the clinical setting with the need for further inves-
tigation for optimal dosing of TCP–PLGA microspheres.

Introduction
Osteomyelitis is a diYcult infection to treat and eradicate
for orthopaedic surgeons and infectious disease specialists.
Osteomyelitis treatment has consisted of debridement of
infected tissues, irrigation with an antiseptic solution and
high serum concentrations of antibiotics for extended peri-
ods. These high serum levels may be associated with neph-
rotoxicity or ototoxicity, and gastrointestinal side eVects, an
increased cost and low patient compliance. Local treatment
with better compliance and fewer side eVects [1] has been
recognised, and several methods for locally controlled
release of drugs have been developed [2].
Local antibiotics may be given by various ways such as
irrigation systems [3], non-biodegradable systems such as
plaster of Paris [4], polymethyl methacrylate bone cement
and beads [5], ceramic blocks and composites [6] and bio-
degradable systems such as collagen sponges [7], and

use of a biodegradable drug delivery system does not pre-
vent the formation of new bone due to its degradation in
implantation area. Such biodegradable delivery systems
would also have the obvious advantage of eliminating the
need for an additional surgery to remove the drug carrier.
Teicoplanin (TCP), a glycopeptide antibiotic, is active
against most Gram-positive pathogens including methicillin-
resistant S. aureus [12], and a concentration of 0.2–3.1 mg/L
of TCP is suYcient to inhibit 90% of S. aureus strains [13].
High concentrations have been demonstrated in bone, with
2.6–14.4 mg/L in washings being achieved within 30 min of
a single dose of 400 mg [14]. TCP would, therefore, seem to
be particularly suited for the treatment of bone and joint
infections, as it has a long serum half-life that allows once-
daily bolus intravenous or intramuscular dosage, and it does
not require routine monitoring of serum concentrations
because of its wide margin of safety [12, 15].
In this study, the role of this new drug system (TCP–
PLGA microspheres) would be tested in an experimental
animal model of osteomyelitis by histological and microbi-
ological assessments and compared with the conventional
intramuscular route, anticipating this new local drug deliv-
ery system would be as eVective as the conventional routes
or even better in the treatment of experimental osteomyeli-
tis model.
Materials and methods
Materials
TCP was obtained from Aventis Pharma (Turkey).
Poly(D,L-lactide-co-glycolide) (PLGA, 50:50), Polyvinyl
alcohol (PVA, 30–70 kDa) and methyl cellulose (MC) from
Sigma, USA; Mueller-Hinton Agar from Oxoid, UK. All
other chemicals were of pharmaceutical grade.
Fabrication of microspheres
TCP–PLGA microspheres were prepared using double
emulsion solvent evaporation method [16]. 250 mg PLGA
was dissolved in 5 mL dichloromethane to form the oil
phase. DiVerent amounts of TCP (50, 100 and 250 mg)
were dissolved in 1 mL aqueous solution containing PVA
(12.5% m/v) and MC (1.25% m/v). Aqueous phase was
added dropwise into the oil phase and stirred using a high
speed homogenizer (CAT-X620, USA) at 13,500 rpm for
2 min. The resultant w/o emulsion was added dropwise into
75 mL of an aqueous PVA solution (1.5 and 2% m/v) at
13,500 rpm for 5 min using the same homogenizer. This
w/o/w emulsion was magnetically stirred overnight to
encourage solvent removal. The resultant microspheres
were separated by centrifugation at 5,000 rpm, washed
three times with distilled water and lyophilised (Lyovac GT
2E, Steris, Germany). Empty microspheres were prepared
as negative controls.
Characteristics of the microspheres
Thermal analysis using diVerential scanning calorimeter
(Model Q100, TA Instruments, USA) were carried out on
microspheres. Samples (»5 mg) were weighed into alumin-
ium pans and run at a heating rate of 10°C/min over a tem-
perature ranging from 25 to 400°C under an atmosphere of
nitrogen.
Particle size analyses were performed on aqueous sus-
pensions of microspheres using a Malvern Mastersizer
(Malvern Instruments, Mastersizer 2000, UK).
The morphology of microspheres was investigated using
scanning electron microscopy (JEOL JXA 840A, Japan) at
25 kV.
In order to determine actual drug content and encapsula-
tion eYciency of microspheres, 10 mg of microspheres
were accurately weighed and dissolved in 2 mL of dichlo-
romethane. Then, 4 mL of distilled water were added, and
the suspensions were shaken for 1 h to extract the TCP into
the aqueous phase. The supernatant was separated by cen-
trifugation at 1,000 rpm and analysed for TCP content at
240 nm by the HPLC method [17] (Shimadzu LC 10A
HPLC, Japan).
In vitro drug release
Five mg of TCP-loaded microspheres were placed in
Eppendorf tube and 1.5 mL of pH 7.4 phosphate buVer was
added. The tubes were shaken at 175 § 5 rpm in a Forma
orbital shaker (Thermo Electron Corporation, USA) at
37 § 1°C. At scheduled time intervals, the tubes were cen-
trifuged at 5,000 rpm for 5 min; 50 L samples were with-
drawn and replaced with fresh medium. QuantiWcation of
drug content was performed by the HPLC method. All
analyses were carried out in triplicate.
In vivo studies
Male Wistar albino rats were used for animal studies. All
investigations were performed according to the EU guide-
lines for animal experimentation [18]. The protocol was
approved by the Animal Care Committee of our institution.
The TCP loaded and empty microspheres were -irradi-
ated (25 kGy) using 60Co. The sterility of the microspheres
was checked according to USP30/NF25 sterility test proce-
dure [19]. The sterile microspheres were kept in sealed
vials until the experiment was performed.

Osteomyelitis was established using a modiWed Norden
foreign-body model of experimental osteomyelitis with a
Kirchner wire. Sodium morrhuate was not used as a sclero-
san agent [9, 20, 21]. Rats, 260–330 g, were fasted during
18 h and randomly divided into four groups of seven. The
proximal tibial metaphysis was exposed through an antero-
medial incision below the right knee of rats. A cavity was
made using a dental burr, and a Kirchner wire
(5.0 £ 1.0 mm) was inserted into the cavity. 200 L sus-
pension of a patient osteomyelitis strain of S. aureus resis-
tant to methicillin (MRSA) containing 1.0 £ 107 CFU/mL
was injected into the defect. Whole of the cavity was sealed
by applying a small amount of dental gypsum. The fascia
was repaired with a continuous chromic catgut suture, and
the subcutaneous tissue and skin were closed by interrupted
sutures. Lateral radiographs were taken before and at inter-
vals of 2 weeks after surgery till the end of the study
(Fig. 1). Sedimentation rate and C-reactive protein were
also evaluated and experimental osteomyelitis in rats was
conWrmed serologically.
After 3 months of the presumably suYcient duration for
a chronic osteomyelitis, the Kirschner wires were extracted
[22]. Most of the cases had encapsulated abscess formation
in the subcutaneous area which was excised. All infected
soft tissue and bone were removed and thereafter washed
with saline solution. In the Wrst group, sterile TCP–PLGA
microspheres, which are equivalent to intravenous dose of
10 days TCP treatment (140 mg/kg) were inserted to proxi-
mal tibia metaphysis cavity of rats. Another group was
treated with an intramuscular injection of TCP (14 mg/kg
per day) for 20 days as a comparison. Sterile empty micro-
spheres were also implanted as a control to the third group.
On the other hand, the fourth group consisted of rats with
experimental osteomyelitis that received no treatment. In
all groups, the subcutaneous tissue and skin were closed
with interrupted sutures.
After completion of the antimicrobial therapy (after
20 days treatment), the rats were killed by a lethal dose of
ether. The infected bones were aseptically removed,
weighed, and frozen. The frozen bone samples were grin-
ded, suspended in 1 mL of saline, serially diluted and ali-
quots (0.1 mL) were plated on Mueller Hinton agar plates
and incubated at 37 § 0.5ºC overnight. The plates were
examined for purity and colony morphology. The colonies
were counted and calculated as CFU/g of bone.
Histopathological analysis
After removal, bone samples were coded and Wxed in 10%
formalin solution. Following decalciWcation in hydrochlo-
ric acid containing EDTA, they were routinely processed
and embedded in paraYn. 5-m thick serial sections stained
with haematoxylin and eosin were examined by a patholo-
gist without any knowledge about treatment groups. Evalu-
ated variables were: polymorphonuclear leucocytes,
lymphocyte/plasma cells, foreign body type multinucleated
giant cells, newly formed capillaries, vasodilation, necrotic
bone, Wbrosis and new bone formation [9, 23]. The pres-
ence and extent of morphologic Wndings related to surgical
trauma were also noted. Each variable was scored semi-
quantitatively from absent (0) to extensive (3). The scores
were assigned by counting inXammatory cells and capillar-
ies per high power Weld (HPF) area, and by counting HPF
areas of necrotic bone, Wbrosis and new bone formation.
Vasodilation was evaluated as ratio of luminal diameter to
the thickness of the vascular wall. In each sample, intra-
medullary, periosteal and synovial areas were evaluated
separately, and the highest scores were recorded for each
area. For each case, Wbrosis and new bone formation scores
of these three areas were combined to obtain a Wnal “repair”
score. Scores of the remaining variables were combined to
obtain the “necro-inXammatory” score [23]. After the codes
were broken, these numerical scores were used to compare
groups by statistical analysis.

Antibiotic delivery system for the treatment of osteomy-
elitis should be non-reactive in the body and stable during
antimicrobial therapy [25]. Antibiotic loaded non-biode-
gradable polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA) carriers have
commonly been used as implant for local treatment of oste-
omyelitis [26]. Unsuitability of fabrication process of for-
mulations containing heat-labile antibiotics due to the heat
application [27], low antibiotic release from formulations,
developing antibiotic resistance due to bacterial growing on
the carrier surface [28], and the requirement of a second
operative surgery to remove the implant [29] are the major
disadvantages of non-biodegradable PMMA carriers. In
contrast, the biodegradable carrier systems release antibiot-
ics at concentrations exceeding those of the minimum
inhibitory concentrations (MICs) for the common patho-
gens responsible for chronic osteomyelitis, while maintain-
ing low systemic levels without adverse eVects avoiding a
second surgical intervention [9], and are gradually replaced
by ingrowing tissue [30]. Biodegradable polymers are also
most suitable ones for the preparation of implants contain-
ing thermo-labile antibiotics, because the most formula-
tions are fabricated in room temperature [27]. The
biomaterials were investigated as local antibiotic delivery
vehicles in many studies [10–14].
The eYcacy of TCP in the treatment of bone and soft tis-
sue infection due to MRSA has been demonstrated in sev-
eral reports [15, 24]. It is commonly used for the treatment
of osteomyelitis in Europe because of its favourable phar-
macokinetics. TCP has high tissue penetration owing to its
lipophilic nature, low autotoxicity and nefrotoxicity, does
not necessitate therapeutic drug monitoring, and has a long
half-life allowing single-dose administration [24, 31].

Histopathologically majority of our cases in the control
and empty microsphere groups had more intense and wide-

microsphere group, but the necro-inXammatory scores did
not have statistically signiWcant diVerences (P > 0.05).
InXammatory inWltration and vascular changes seemed to
be more related to the presence of necrotic bone fragments
than the treatment type. Displaced necrotic bone fragments
due to surgical intervention could be seen even inside the
bone. Tissue inXammatory reaction can be caused not only
by infection but also by surgical trauma. We had similar
Wndings in a previous study and comparable radiological
and histomorphological observations have been reported by
others [23, 27, 32]. In a model of chronic osteomyelitis,
inXammation and necrosis are largely replaced by Wbrosis
and new bone formation. As these can be induced by surgi-
cal trauma and foreign bodies as well as chronic osteomy-
elitis, repair scores are aVected by all of these factors.
Nevertheless, we found a signiWcant diVerence in the repair
scores of TCP–IM injection group and the control group
(P < 0.05). It seems likely that TCP–IM injections have
been eVective in minimising the bone necrosis and inXam-
matory reaction by eliminating the infection, thus reducing
the resultant Wbrosis and new bone formation. TCP–PLGA
microsphere group had also lower repair scores when com-
pared to control groups, but these were not statistically sig-
niWcant (P > 0.05). As the cases in this group had
conspicuous Wndings of surgical trauma, the tissue reaction
was probably not only against infection. Insertion of micro-
spheres was probably the cause of additional surgical
trauma leading to increased inXammation and Wbrosis,
shadowing the eVects of antibiotic treatment. Additionally,
PLGA is known to induce new bone formation [33]. We
consider these to be causes of discrepancy between colony
counts and histopathological scores for TCP–PLGA micro-
sphere group.
According to in vivo data obtained, number of MRSA
was less in the bone samples treated with TCP–PLGA
microspheres (1.7 £ 103 § 1.3 £ 103 CFU/g) than those of
IM group (5.8 £ 104 § 5.3 £ 104 CFU/g) and empty micro-
sphere group (6.2 £ 104 § 4.8 £ 104 CFU/g). These diVer-
ences between the TCP–PLGA microsphere group and other
groups were statistically signiWcant (P < 0.001). But the
diVerence between IM group and empty microsphere group
was not statistically signiWcant (P > 0.05). While one tibia
of seven animals treated with TCP–PLGA microspheres
was found to be sterile, MRSA was grown from all other
tibias. Despite low colony count compared to IM group and
control group, we could not eradicate MRSA from tibias,
thus showing the need further investigation for optimal dos-
ing of TCP–PLGA microspheres. Some other experimental
animal studies with TCP was to be given at 10–20 mg/kg
body weight per day intramuscularly [34, 35], which is com-
parable to our study, though new studies are needed for
eradication of bacteria without harming the animals with
TCP–PLGA microspheres. If this could be achieved, the
treatment of the chronic osteomyelitis would move one step
further [36]. The major limitation in our study was the
absence of the optimal dosage studies of TCP–PLGA micro-
spheres in that kind of experimental models.
It is largely assumed that the eYcacy of a drug in animal
model will correspond to that in human. Tissue damage and
inXammation animal studies, including osteomyelitis ani-
mal models, consider impact of both bacterial growth and
resultant eVects of antibiotics. However, relevance to
human infection is still uncertain [37]. Additional studies
are needed.
Conclusion
This study was conducted to investigate the use of PLGA
microspheres to deliver a clinically eVective concentration
of TCP for a prolonged period in order to eradicate MRSA
infections in chronic osteomyelitis. Previous studies had
shown that most of the antibiotic is permanently trapped in
the bone cement, resulting in poor drug release. The major
disadvantage of such non-biodegradable systems is the
need for a secondary surgery to remove the implant upon
completion of the antibiotic release. In contrast, these bio-
degradable carriers do not require post-treatment surgical
intervention to remove the spent implant. Importantly,
these biodegradable TCP–PLGA microspheres (TMS4)
implanted to proximal tibia of the rats are clinically eVec-
tive, thereby strongly supporting the exploitation of these
biodegradable delivery systems for the local treatment of
chronic osteomyelitis. Further research into the determina-
tion of optimal dosage of applied antibiotics for the eVec-
tive total eradication of MRSA and the possibility for
development of antimicrobial resistance upon prolonged
use of these delivery systems are ongoing.
