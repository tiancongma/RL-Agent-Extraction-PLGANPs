A B S T R A C T
Background: Methotrexate (MTX) is a broad-spectrum anti-cancer agent used in a variety of cancers. However, its
non-selective toxicity causes serious side effects and, consequently, a novel drug delivery system would be an
ideal way to maximize the effectiveness of MTX against tumor tissues. Aim: The objectives of the present study
were to prepare and compare the physicochemical properties, drug release, and in vitro cytotoxicity of linear and
star-shaped polymeric nanoparticles (NPs) for drug delivery of MTX.
Methods: Linear and star-shaped poly(ε-carprolactone) (PCL), and poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) were
synthesized by ring-opening polymerization and characterized using1H NMR, gel permeation chromatography,
and FT-IR techniques. MTX polymeric NPs were prepared by using double emulsion solvent evaporation method
and characterized in the case of size, morphology, zeta potential, encapsulation efficiency, loading capacity, and
drug release. The anti-proliferative effect of NPs was assessed on B16F10 cells implementing the MTT test.
Results: The optimized process and formulation parameters resulted in the homogeneous spherical population of
linear and star-shaped PCL NPs with a diameter of 56 and 48 nm, and encapsulation efficiency of 18.59 % and
34.64 % w/w, respectively. The linear and star-shaped PLGA NPs were 73 and 54 nm in diameter, with an
encapsulation efficiency of 54.99 % and 86.41 % w/w, respectively. Furthermore, the MTX release studies
showed that the cumulative release of MTX in the two star-shaped polymeric NPs was 2 times higher than their
linear analogs. Finally, the MTT assay showed that both star-shaped polymeric NPs had higher toxicity compared
to the linear ones.
Conclusion: Star-shaped PCL and PLGA NPs had better physicochemical properties, so they are preferred for drug
delivery of MTX compared to the linear analogs.
1. Introduction
Several approaches have been developed to promote targeted drug
delivery and reduce the adverse effects of anti-cancer agents. Nano­
technology is one of those methods which has many advantages in the
targeted delivery of ocular and dermal drug [1,2]. Nanoencapsulation
reduces the drug interaction with the biological environment as well as
prevents its degradation, in addition nanoparticles (NPs) accumulate in
cancerous tissues because of enhanced penetration and retention (EPR)
effect of tumor cells [3–8]. Polymers with various physicochemical
properties (e.g. solubility, polarity, biocompatibility, biodegradability,
swelling, and charge) are used to prepare polymeric NPs [9]. Biode­
gradable aliphatic polyesters, particularly poly(ε-carprolactone) (PCL),
are of great interest for environmental, medical, and pharmaceutical
applications. PCL is an inexpensive biodegradable polyester and de­
grades by hydrolysis of its ester linkages in physiological conditions;

(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) is another popular polymer in nano­

effectively deals with these two monomers, there is very minimal sys­

biomaterial applications [9,13,14]. Both PCL and PLGA polymers are
FDA approved and are therefore commonly used in medicine [15,16].
Methotrexate (MTX) is a hydrophilic folic acid antagonist with anti-
neoplastic activity, which is used to treat certain malignancies and in­
flammatory diseases [17]. Systemic use of MTX shows many side effects,
including hepatotoxic effects, alopecia, myelosuppression, oral ulcers,
and cardiotoxicity [18,19] that can either be prevented or at least
reduced by targeted drug delivery. PLGA and PCL polymeric NPs alter
the physicochemical properties of drugs and have been widely used in
targeted drug delivery [20,21]. However, the conventional linear PCL
and PLGA polymers suffer from low drug loading capacity for hydro­
philic drugs and exhibit inappropriate drug release patterns i.e.
incomplete release with huge burst release [12]. Several studies have
reported low drug loading capacity for MTX NPs which have been pre­
pared by linear PLGA or PCL polymers [22–25]. Numerous efforts have
been made to improve the drug loading capacity and drug release of PCL
and PLGA particles with the addition of hydrophilic polymers [26]. But
it should be considered that any change in the nature of the monomers
may require further FDA approval in a time-consuming and costly pro­
cess [16]. Consequently, any alteration without changing the monomers
would be ideal. Star-shaped co-polymers were introduced by Schaefgen
and Flory in 1948 [27]. These polymers possess a certain number of
arms (at least three arms) and are linked to each other by a low mo­
lecular weight core [28–31]. As observed in previous studies, the
branched and star-shaped PLGA and PCL can load higher amounts of
drugs and release them more appropriately [29,32–35]. Thus, we aim to
prepare MTX-loaded PCL and PLGA NPs using star-shaped polymers and
to compare the general physicochemical properties, drug release, and in
vitro cytotoxicity with old-style linear analogs.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Materials
MTX was donated by the Zahravi pharmaceutical company (Iran).
Caprolactone, lactide, glycolide, stannous octate (Sn(Oct)2), and poly­
vinyl alcohol (PVA) 40–88 were purchased from Sigma Aldrich (Ger­
many and Netherlands). Span80® and dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) were
bought from BDH laboratory supplies (England) and Merck (Germany),
respectively. Chloroform and dichloromethane were supplied by Dae-
Jung chemicals company (Korea). Melanoma cell line (B16F10) was
obtained from Pastor Inc. (Iran). 3-(4, 5- dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5-
diphenyltetrazolium bromide (MTT) and fetal bovine serum (FBS)
were purchased from Invitrogen Corp (USA) and Life Technologies, Inc.
(USA), respectively. Phosphate buffered saline (PBS), Roswell park
memorial institute 1640 medium (RPMI 1640) with L-glutamine and
penicillin-streptomycin were purchased from Lonza company (Basel,
Switzerland).
2.2. Preparation and characterization of nanoparticles
2.2.1. Preparation and characterization of linear and star-shaped PCL/
PLGA polymers
The polymer synthesis was performed using the ring-opening poly­
merization method. 0.05 mol of PCL monomer was placed in a two-neck
round-bottom flask under a nitrogen atmosphere and heated for 15 min
at 120 ᵒC. After melting the caprolactone, three drops of Sn(Oct)2, as a
catalyst, were added and stirred for 6 h by using a magnetic stirring bar.
For polymer purification, 100 mL of dichloromethane was added to
solubilize the copolymer and 300 mL of cool diethyl ether was added to
precipitate the copolymer. Star-shaped copolymers were prepared by
the addition of 4-arm ethane-1,1,2,2-tetrol core (0.005 mol) to the flask
and using the same method of preparation. It should be mentioned that
ethane-1,1,2,2-tetrol core was synthesized and provided by Heidarpour
et.al. [30]. To prepare the linear and star-shaped PLGA copolymers the
same synthesis method was used to polymerize glycolide (0.025 mol)
and lactide (0.025 mol) instead of caprolactone.
To investigate the chemical structure and confirm the production of
polymers from monomers, the Fourier-transform infrared (FTIR) spec­
troscopy (8400S, SHIMADZU, Japan) and proton nuclear magnetic
resonance (1H NMR) spectroscopy were used (Bruker, 400 MHz). Gel
permeation chromatography was also used for polymer molecular
weight analysis.
2.2.2. Preparation and characterization of MTX-Loaded linear and star-
shaped PCL/PLGA polymeric nanoparticles
NPs were prepared by using the double emulsion solvent evaporation
technique [36]. 50 mg of linear PCL polymers were dispersed in 5 mL of
chloroform and 150 mg span80 was used as the lipophilic surfactant of
the oil phase. 500 μL of MTX aqueous solution 2.5 % (w/v) (inner water
phase) was gently added to polymeric dispersion on a magnetic stirrer
(MR Hei –Tec, Heidolph, Germany) at room temperature, and then,
water-in-oil (w/o) nanoemulsion was prepared using a probe sonicator
(UP200H, Hielscher, Germany) for 5 min. w/o nanoemulsion was added
to 20 mL of PVA 3% (w/v) and stirred using a magnetic stirrer for 5 min.
(water-in-oil)-in-water (w/o/w) nanoemulsion was equipped by soni­
cating the emulsion for 5 min using a probe sonicator. w/o/w nano­
emulsion was converted to MTX-loaded linear PCL nanoparticle
suspension (Formulation A) after evaporating chloroform by the means
of the rotary evaporator (LABOROTA 4002, Heidolph, Germany) under
reduced pressure of 110 mbar at 35ᵒC and 80 rpm. The same method­
ology was utilized for star-shaped PCL polymers to prepare MTX-loaded
star-shaped PCL NPs (Formulation B).
For preparing MTX-loaded linear PLGA NPs (Formulation C), 25 mg
of linear PLGA polymers were dispersed in 5 mL of dichloromethane and
75 mg span80, then, 250 μL of MTX solution 2.5 % (w/v) was added to
homogenizing (Silent crusher M, Heidolph, Germany) oil phase within
7 min (w/o emulsion). The outer water phase (15 mL of PVA 3% (w/v))
was gently added to w/o emulsion throughout 15 min and then soni­
cated for 5 min to form w/o/w nanoemulsion. Finally, dichloromethane
was vaporized using the same methodology which was conducted for
PCL NPs. MTX-loaded star-shaped PLGA NPs (Formulation D) were
prepared according to the same method for star-shaped PLGA polymers.
2.2.3. Particle size distribution and zeta potential analysis
Prepared nanoparticle formulations were diluted in phosphate-
buffered saline (PBS) then, particle size distribution and zeta poten­
tials were measured by photon correlation spectroscopy (Zetasizer-ZS,
Malvern Instrument, Malvern, UK) at 25 ⁰C with measurement angel of
90◦and repeated three times.
2.2.4. Scanning Electron microscopy
Prepared nanoparticle formulations were fixed on a metal stub and
covered with gold under vacuum (100−150 A˚) in an argon atmosphere
by a direct current sputter technique (DST3, Nanostructured Coating
Co., Tehran, Iran). Then, the morphology of the prepared samples was
observed by using a scanning electron microscope (SEM) (MIRA3 TES­
CAN, UK) running at 15 kV.

capacity percent (LC%) of MTX in nanoparticle

(Ultra-15, molecular weight cutoff of 100 kDa, Millipore, Darmstadt,
Germany). The dispersion was centrifuged (Universal 320, Pole Ideal
Tajhiz, Iran) at 4000 rpm for 10 min, and then the free drug amount of

2.5. Cytotoxicity studies
The cytotoxicity of the formulations was evaluated by MTT assay.
Melanoma cell line (B16F10) was maintained in a 75 cm2 culture flask,
in RPMI 1640 medium supplemented with 10 % (v/v) of FBS and
antibiotics (100 mg/mL of penicillin-streptomycin). The cells were
incubated at 37 ◦C in a 5% CO2 atmosphere and 95 % air with more than
95 % humidity. The medium was changed every two days for cell
feeding. Briefly, when the cell population reached a 70 % confluence, it
was dissociated with 0.25 % trypsin in the PBS (pH 7.4) and centrifuged
at 900 rpm for 8 min at room temperature. Then it inoculated in 96-well
microplates with a cell density of 1 × 104 cells per well in 200 μL RPMI
1640, and incubated in a humidified atmosphere containing 5% CO2 at
37 ◦C for 24 h to allow the cells to attach to the bottom of the well. In
this context, different concentrations (50, 100, 200, and 400 μg/L) of
MTX-loaded polymeric NPs and pure MTX solution (as positive controls)
were prepared in fresh cell culture media and administered to the wells.

2.6. Data analysis
Statistical analysis was performed through a one-way analysis of
variance (ANOVA) using a Tukey honest significant difference (HSD)
test by SPSS software (version 16.0, Chicago, IL, USA). A P value < 0.05
was considered statistically significant.
3. Results and discussion
PCL and PLGA are the most widely used degradable polymers for
targeted drug delivery. But according to the low drug loading capacity
for hydrophilic drugs and inappropriate drug release patterns of con­
ventional linear PCL and PLGA [12] many attempts (including using
star-shaped analogs) have been done to overcome these problems [26,
31,32]. In this study, MTX was considered as a hydrophilic drug to
compare the important general drug delivery parameters (including
particle size, loading capacity, drug release, etc.) of NPs, prepared by
linear PCL/PLGA or their star-shaped analogs independent of the spe­
cific target tissue or route of administration.
To the best of our knowledge, this is the first study which compares

3.1. Preparation and characterization of copolymers
3.1.1. FT-IR and 1H NMR studies
Fig. 1 shows the polymer synthesis pathway and Fig. 2 represents
successful synthesis of copolymers by FT-IR and 1H NMR of linear and
star-shaped PCL and PLGA copolymers.
The FT-IR spectra of all synthesized polymers represent the
absorbance at 1200 and 1750 cm−1 which are characteristic absorptions
of the C–O stretching and carbonyl ester (C––O) stretching, respec­
tively. The peak, observed at 2850 cm−1 is attributed to the C–H
stretching of the aliphatic part. The strong −OH stretching at about
3400 cm−1 shows that the polymers are hydroxyl ended.
In the 1H NMR spectra of all polymers, different proton peaks are
indicated alphabetically. Evidently, the proton peaks of the main chains
in all synthesized polymers are consistent with earlier reports [43–46].
In 1H NMR of linear and star-shaped PLGA polymers, “d” and “e” peaks
represent protons of the terminal lactide group and the “f” peak belongs
to the proton of the terminal glycolide group. The peak which is defined
as “f” in 1H NMR spectra of linear and star-shaped PCL, shows the
caprolactone terminal −CH2 −OH.
Additionally, the “g” and “h” proton peaks in 1H NMR spectra of the
two star-shaped polymers attribute to the protons of the ethane-1,1,2,2-
tetrol core.

3.2. Preparation and characterization of nanoparticles
The double emulsion solvent evaporation method was preferred for
the preparation of nanoparticles as it provides a higher encapsulation
yield for hydrophilic drugs compared to other methods [47,48].
3.2.1. Size, distribution and zeta potential
The average particle size, polydispersity index (PDI), and zeta po­
tential of NPs are summarized in Table 1 and detailed information is
available in a supplementary file. According to the results, although the
average particle size of B and D formulations is slightly less than A and B
formulations, all prepared NPs are smaller than 100 nm and the PDI
value is less than 0.5. Thus, it could be concluded that the role of particle
size as a confounding parameter in loading capacity and drug release has
been eliminated. In addition, the zeta potential of all NPs ranges from
-10 to −20 mV which provides adequate stability for nanoparticle sus­
pensions. These results are important as particle size, PDI, and surface
properties (e.g. zeta potential) influence the bioavailability, cellular
uptake, drug release, and clearance of NPs. The previous studies have
shown that particles with approximately 100 nm diameter provide
better drug delivery pattern compared to larger particles, due to opti­
mum surface area to volume ratio and less lymphatic system clearance
[49]. Furthermore, the size of NPs can play an important role in
increasing the depth and rate of drug delivery through the skin. Previous
studies have also shown that follicular penetration growth with
decreasing the size of NPs and particles with less than 100 nm diameter
are desirable for dermal delivery of drugs, especially through the hair
follicles [50]. Therefore, each of the star-shaped or linear polymeric NPs
can be used as a drug delivery system according to the appropriate
particle size, required for delivery of the drug to different target organs.
3.2.2. Morphology analysis
Due to the importance of particle size in the distribution of NPs in the
body and on the other hand, because of the technical errors in each
particle size measurement method, studies have emphasized the use of
at least two different methods for determining particle size. It is rec­
ommended that one of these methods should be microscopic imaging
[51].
The SEM images of the synthesized NPs (Fig. 4) verify the results of
the photon correlation spectroscopy. As shown in the SEM images, all
prepared NPs are spherical, however, NPs, prepared by star-shaped
polymers are more uniform in the case of both PCL and PLGA co­
polymers. Because of the greater safety, efficacy, and stability of uniform
systems [52], star-shaped polymers appear to have excessive benefits for
drug delivery. It should be noted that the term “star-shaped co-polymer”
represents the arrangement of the monomers that form the structure of
the co-polymer, so that it does not alter the spherical morphology of
prepared nanoparticles.

3.3. Encapsulation efficiency and loading capacity of MTX in
nanoparticle
Table 2 presents EE% and LC% for polymeric NPs. The results reveal
that PLGA copolymers could load a larger amount of MTX than PCL
copolymers. Moreover, both star-shaped copolymers could load more
MTX than linear analogs (p < 0.05). The higher loading capacity of star-
shaped copolymers may be due to the higher amount of hydrophilic
ending functional groups which reduces the hydrophobicity of polymers
and offers greater benefits in the delivery of hydrophilic drugs [28,35].
3.4. In vitro drug release study
The pH and temperature of cancerous tissues have been reported to
be lower and higher than the pH and temperature of normal tissues,
respectively. Thus, the in vitro drug release assessment was performed in
the PBS (pH = 6) at T = 40⁰C to simulate the cancer cell’s environment
[53,54].
Cumulative drug release from different types of NPs (Fig. 5)
demonstrated that A and B formulations released 98.41 % ± 5.18 and
34. 09 % ± 2.0 of MTX after seven days. Cumulatively released MTX was
97.82 % ± 2.71 and 59.80 % ± 3.10 for C and D formulations, respec­
tively. In conclusion, star-shaped polymers had a higher drug release
than linear copolymers at all measured time intervals (p < 0.05),
possibly due to the lower crystallinity of star-shaped polymers [55]. As
indicated in previous studies, polymer crystallinity may be considered to
be an important parameter for drug release because of variations in
chain mobility. Polymers with less crystallinity properties have higher
chain mobility and consequently demonstrate higher drug release [56].
3.5. In vitro cytotoxicity assay
Cytotoxicity assay is crucial while introducing new synthetic poly­
mer or drug delivery formulations to prevent undesirable cytotoxic ef­
fects. Fig. 6(i) shows the cell viability of numerous concentrations for
different types of synthesized polymers (drug-free) as a measurement
tool for the biocompatibility of polymers. As expected, according to the
known biocompatible nature of Lactide, Glycolide, and Caprolactone
monomers, none of the synthesized polymers showed cytotoxicity in low
concentrations (250 and 500 μg/mL), but in higher concentration
(1000 μg/mL) cell viability was decreased for PCL polymers. Strangely,
as indicated in Fig. 6(i) cellular growth was observed in PLGA wells. This
cell growth was significantly higher (p < 0.05) for the star-shaped PLGA
in comparison to linear PLGA at 1000 μg/mL concentration, but all
polymers showed almost similar behavior in terms of cell viability, in
other concentrations (p < 0.05). There are several reasons for increased
cell viability of PLGA copolymers: some chemical compounds or parti­
cles interfere with MTT reagent or spectroscopic method; the change in
the number of cells in the control group may occur; finally, suitable
scaffold surfaces for cell growth may form by polymers. [57–60]
Although it is difficult to distinguish between these reasons, it may at
least be concluded that the synthesized polymers are biocompatible.
Fig. 6(ii) represents the cell viability of the prepared MTX-NPs with
different MTX concentrations and pure MTX solution (as positive con­
trol). The results demonstrate that almost all polymeric NPs, loaded with
MTX had higher cytotoxicity than MTX solution at all concentrations of
MTX(except for the formulation of C at high concentrations). It can
therefore be concluded that MTX polymeric NPs have a greater thera­
peutic efficacy compared to MTX solution. Additionally, more cytotox­
icity was detected for formulation D in all concentrations, and for
formulation B in lower concentrations (50 and 100 μg/L), which may be
due to the higher drug release of star-shaped polymeric NPs, in the first
24 h (Fig. 5). It should be noted that this result was statistically signif­
icant only in a concentration of 400 μg/L for PLGA and in 50 μg/L for
PCL (p < 0.05) polymeric NPs. It should also be mentioned that the
increased toxicity of nanoencapsulated MTX may lead to higher toxicity
for both tumor cells and surrounding cells. However, NPs could accu­
mulate in tumor tissues due to the EPR properties of cancerous tissues,
which is a suitable tool for targeted drug delivery through NPs ranging
from 50−200 nm. [61,62] Therefore, less toxicity is speculated for
surrounding non-cancerous cells, which do not have the EPR effect

4. Conclusion
The characterization of prepared NPs shows that star-shaped poly­
meric NPs possess desired size distribution, morphology, drug encap­
sulation, loading capacity, and drug release, as well as cytotoxicity in
comparison with linear polymers. Consequently, due to the improved
physicochemical properties of NPs prepared with star-shaped polymers,
it appears that these NPs may be used as effective drug carriers for drug
delivery of methotrexate.
