Minoxidil-loaded double emulsion nanoparticles were developed for the treatment of hair loss.

•

The nanoparticles showed high encapsulation efficiency and prevented aggregation at high concentrations.

•

The nanoparticles promoted successful hair regrowth in mice without using ethanol and propylene glycol.

Abstract

Topical administration of minoxidil (MXD) on the skin is a widely used method for the treatment of hair loss. To enhance the efficacy of MXD in human skin, the appendageal route through the hair follicle could be promising. Therefore, we developed MXD-loaded double emulsion nanoparticles (MXD-DE-NPs) using poly (D,L-lactide-co-glycolide; PLGA) and surfactants. The size of MXD-DE-NPs was 137.8 ± 0.814 nm and their encapsulation efficiency of MXD was approximately 43%. The MXD-DE-NPs showed no aggregation in aqueous conditions at 1% w/v MXD concentration without ethanol and propylene glycol. In human follicular dermal papilla cells, the NPs showed fast cellular uptake and no significant toxicity. After treatment on the skin of shaved mice, efficient skin penetration of the NPs could be observed on fluorescence imaging. NP treatment for three weeks resulted in efficient regrowth of hair in mice without ethanol and propylene glycol similar to the traditional MXD formula. These results demonstrated that MXD-DE-NPs are a promising carrier for MXD in the treatment of hair loss.

Alopecia is abnormal hair loss due to various reasons including severe stress, genetic disorders, and nutritional imbalance [1]. The frequency of alopecia is increasing worldwide; thus, the relevant market is growing every year [2], and in particular, the recent interest regarding appearance further contributes to this concern. The only two medical treatments approved by the FDA for alopecia proven to improve hair loss are finasteride and minoxidil (MXD). Finasteride is a 5-alpha reductase inhibitor and mainly used in tablet form for oral administration. The drug is effective; however, side effects, such as impotence, were reported in some patients [3]. Conversely, MXD is an antihypertensive vasodilator and generally used as a topical treatment on the skin [4].

Recently, a clinical study in which the effect of applying 5% MXD to the skin was compared with oral administration of 1 mg MXD for female-pattern hair loss patients was published [5]. When comparing the hair density 24 weeks after drug treatment, the orally administered MXD group showed a better outcome than topical administration, and side effects were not observed except for hypertrichosis. The result is notable considering the efficacy of local delivery is generally superior to systemic delivery. In topical delivery through the skin, human skin is thicker than mouse skin used in disease animal models and skin permeation experiments. Because human skin is very thick, the results of mouse experiments may not represent the results of clinical trials [6]. In human skin, the hair follicles are actively generated and localized in deep areas from the epidermis to the subcutaneous tissue. Therefore, to efficiently deliver drugs past the thick epidermal layer of human skin, an alternative strategy would be to optimize the appendageal route through the hair follicle rather than the transcellular route through the existing tonic solution of ethanol and glycerol [7].

Recently, nanoparticles (NPs) have attracted the attention of biomedical researchers as potential drug carriers [8]. Various NPs have been developed for drug delivery in numerous diseases [9]. In particular, NPs showed promising data in transdermal drug delivery resulting in efficient skin penetration mainly through hair follicles compared with drug administration without NPs (Scheme 1a) [10], [11]. However, in topical administration of MXD on the skin, the required concentration of MXD (>1% w/v) for the treatment of hair loss is very high compared with NP formulas with other drugs. Therefore, NPs for MXD delivery require high encapsulation efficiency (EE) and stability without aggregation at high concentrations, which may be why many studies containing successful hair growth data in an animal model using NP formulas of MXD have not been published [12], [13].

Herein, we developed double emulsion (DE) type NPs with poly (D,L-lactide-co-glycolide; PLGA) and surfactants for efficient delivery of MXD through the skin without using ethanol and propylene glycol. The formula was optimized and the size and shape of the resulting MXD-loaded DE-NPs (MXD-DE-NPs) were analyzed. Fluorescence microscopy was used to observe the cellular uptake in human follicular dermal papilla cells. Next, the skin penetration of the NPs was observed using real-time imaging and histology after application on the skin of shaved mice. Finally, the effect of the DE-NPs on hair growth was tested in the same mice models and compared with the MXD formula with ethanol and propylene glycol.

Materials and methods

Materials

Pluronic F-127, PLGA, sodium chloride (NaCl), polyvinyl alcohol (PVA), and sulforhodamine B (Rho B) were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, MO, USA). MXD was purchased from Tokyo Chemical Industry (Tokyo, Japan). Dichloromethane (DCM) was purchased from Daejung Chemical & Metals (Siheung, Gyeonggi-do, South Korea). Glycerol was purchased from Junsei Chemical (Tokyo, Japan). Ethyl alcohol and acetic acid were purchased from Duksan (Ansan, Gyeonggi-do, South Korea). Emulsan was purchased from Biovio (Greifswald, Germany). DMEM high glucose media was purchased from Biowest (Nuaille, France). Phenol red-free RPMI was purchased from WELGENE (Gyeongsan, Gyeongsangbuk-do, South Korea). Hoechst 33342 was purchased from Thermo Fisher Scientific (Waltham, MA, USA).

Preparation of MXD-DE-NPs

First, 30 mg Pluronic F-127 and 30 mg PLGA were dissolved in 1 mL DCM (oil phase). Then, 70 mg MXD was dissolved in 1 mL aqueous solution (distilled water, DW:ethanol = 1:1 containing 3% acetic acid (v/v)) as the first water phase. The second water phase (5 ml) used 2% glycerol (v/v in DW) containing PVA 2.5% (w/v), NaCl 0.5% (w/v), and emulsan 0.24% (w/v). The first water phase was introduced to the oil phase and emulsified using a probe sonicator (VC505, Sonics and Materials, Newtown, CT, USA) at 100 watts for 90 seconds in an ice bath. Then, the water-in-oil (W/O) emulsion was added into the second water phase and emulsified by sonication at 200 watts for 90 seconds in an ice bath. The DE solution was stirred at 65 °C for one hour in a fume hood for DCM evaporation. Next, the solution was subjected to dialysis with a 14-kDa dialysis bag in DW for one hour to remove the unloaded drugs and obtain the MXD-DE-NPs. For the control, empty NPs were similarly prepared without MXD. For imaging, NPs were prepared using 1 mg sulforhodamine B (Rho B), a hydrophilic dye, instead of MXD. NPs were lyophilized using a freeze dryer from ilSinBioBase (Korea) and resuspended when used.

Characterization of MXD-DE-NPs

The size and zeta potential of particles were measured using Zetasizer Nano ZS90 (Malvern Instruments, UK). A transmission electron microscope (TEM; JEM, JEOL, Japan) was used to examine the morphology of particles. EE and drug release from NPs were quantified using a Nanodrop 2000 spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific) or a microplate reader (Synergy H1 Hybrid Multi-Mode Reader, BioTek Inc., USA) at an absorbance of 317 nm and sulforhodamine B fluorescence was recorded at excitation 565 nm/emission 591 nm. The buffer for quantifying drugs consisted of 80% DMSO, 10% PBS, 10% DW, and 1% Triton X-100 (v/v). To measure the release of MXD from NPs, the NP solution was placed in a 14-kDa dialysis bag in 20 mL PBS at room temperature and analyzed at 1, 3, 6, and 12 hours.

Cellular uptake imaging

Human hair follicle dermal papilla cells (Promocell, Heidelberg, Germany, used at passage 3) were seeded at 3 × 104 cells/well in confocal dishes with DMEM high glucose serum medium containing 10% FBS and 1% antibiotic–antimycotic solution. After 3 days, the cells were washed with DPBS and then treated with free Rho B or Rho B-DE-NPs, both at 8 ug/mL Rho B. The treatment time and temperature applied varied among samples. Then, after washing with DPBS, the cell nuclei were stained with Hoechst 33342 for 20 minutes and washed again with DPBS. Finally, cells in phenol red-free medium containing 2% FBS were observed using confocal laser scanning microscope (LSM800, Carl Zeiss, Germany).

Transdermal uptake in C57BL/6 mice

All mouse experiments in this study were approved by the Samsung Medical Center Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (Approval code: 20210617001) and conducted according to the AAALAC International Guidelines and the United States Animal Welfare Act.

Male C57BL/6 mice (7-week-old, 20–22 g) purchased from Orient BIO (Seoul, Korea) were treated after acclimatization to laboratory conditions for 1 week. Mice were fed with standard mouse food and water and stayed in a controlled environment with 12-hour light/dark cycles, temperature, and 35–60% humidity. After one week adaptive period, 8-week-old mice were used for experiments.

Using isoflurane to maintain respiratory anesthesia, the dorsal area (approximately 2 cm in width and 4 cm in length) of mice was shaved with hair clippers.

RHO solution (25 ug in 200 uL) was applied to the shaved mouse dorsal area 1 hour and 5 hours before IVIS measurement. IVIS was observed for each group and the related optical index (ROI) value of the 1-hour image was adjusted to 1. Thereafter, mice were euthanized using CO2 asphyxiation and the skin was excised, embedded in OCT, and frozen for histological study.

Anagen induction assay in C57BL/6 mice

The mice were randomly divided into four groups (9 male mice each). All mice were anesthetized with isoflurane. The dorsal area (approximately 2 cm in width and 4 cm in length) of mice was shaved with hair clippers. Animals in the sham group did not receive any treatment except for hair removal. To prepare the solution for the positive control group, MXD was dissolved in a mixture of propylene glycol (10%, v/v) and ethanol (30%, v/v) to a content of 1% (w/w). For the 1% MXD-DE-NP group, MXD was dissolved by adding DW to obtain 1% MXD. For the empty group, the same amount of NPs was used as in the MXD-DE-NP group except MXD was excluded. Equal quantities of prepared solutions (200 uL) were topically applied to the shaved area of mice once a day for 21 days. Then, mice were euthanized using CO2 asphyxiation and the skin was excised and fixed in 10% formalin solution for histological study.

Ex vivo porcine skin permeability test

The whole skin of a pig with fat removed was purchased in size of 1 cm × 1 cm (Apures co., Gyeonggi-do, South Korea). We put 1 ml of 1xPBS and paper in a 60 mm cell culture dish, put the epidermis of porcine skin on it. We applied RHO solution (5 ug in 50 ul) to it, and wrapped it with foil while separating each sample. They were placed in an incubator at 37 °C for 1, 5, and 24 hours. After that, the treated tissues were cut and embedded in OCT so that the middle part was facing down to make a frozen block. After cryo-sectioning, the tissue slide was observed using a confocal microscope image.

Results

Development and characterization of MXD-DE-NPs

MXD-DE-NPs were developed using water-in-oil-in-water (W/O/W) solvent evaporation method (Scheme 1b) [14]. The first water phase containing MXD was added to an oil phase containing Pluronic F-127 and PLGA, and then emulsified to prepare a W/O emulsion. Then, the emulsion was added into the outer second water phase containing PVA, emulsan, and NaCl, resulting in W/O/W DE-NPs after sonication. The ratio of each component was experimentally optimized as shown in Table 1 to achieve small size, narrow size distribution, and EE. The sizes of empty DE-NPs and MXD-DE-NPs were 124.1 ± 1.343 and 137.8 ± 0.814 nm, respectively, showing similar sizes after drug loading (Fig. 1a). Both NPs showed spherical morphology in TEM images. In particular, similar to a previous report, acetic acid was used to prevent aggregation and increase EE of MXD [15] (Table 2 and Fig. 1b). When MXD was dissolved with a solvent including acetic acid during formulation, the EE was 43%; however, without acetic acid, the EE was only 25% and large aggregates of the unloaded MXD could be observed with the naked eye. These data showed that MXD could be stably prepared in water without using ethanol and propylene glycol, and the MXD-DE-NPs did not aggregate at 1% w/v MXD concentration. After loading, MXD was gradually released from MXD-DE-NPs over time in aqueous conditions (Fig. 1c). In addition, MXD-DE-NPs showed similar sizes after freeze-drying and resuspension, indicating possibility of stable storage in the dried form (Fig. 1d).

Cellular uptake and viability test of MXD-DE-NPs in dermal papilla cells

Human hair follicle dermal papilla cells were used to test the cellular uptake of the NPs. For fluorescence imaging, sulforhodamine B (Rho B) was loaded in DE-NPs instead of MXD resulting in Rho B-DE-NPs, and their cellular uptake was observed using confocal microscopy. Rho B-DE-NPs showed more efficient cellular uptake than free Rho B without NPs (Fig. 2a). The mean fluorescence intensity of Rho B-DE-NP-treated cells was approximately 1.76-fold higher than in free Rho B-treated cells (Fig. 2b). Notably, the cellular uptake mainly decreased at 4 °C showing the uptake of NPs was based on an energy-dependent process such as endocytosis [16]. In time-dependent studies, Rho B-DE-NPs showed high fluorescence intensity from 1 hour, and the intensity gap between the cells treated with free Rho B and Rho B-DE-NPs increased over time (Fig. 2c and 2d). The viability of dermal papilla cells was evaluated using the CCK-8 assay after treatment in the three groups (empty DE-NPs, MXD, and MXD-DE-NPs) for 48 hours (Fig. 2e). The cell viability in all groups did not decrease compared with untreated control cells showing the DE-NPs had negligible toxicity which may be because materials reported and regarded as non-toxic were used during formulation. Furthermore, the viability of MXD-DE-NP-treated cells increased more than of control cells and the difference was statistically significant. The results showed that MXD-DE-NPs may have enhanced cell proliferation.

In vivo skin penetration of Rho B-DE-NPs in C57BL/6 mice

To evaluate the degree of transdermal uptake of DE-NPs in vivo, IVIS, a fluorescence imaging device for live animals was used (Fig. 3a). After topical administration of samples containing Rho B, fluorescence images were obtained at 1 and 5 hours. In the case of free Rho B dissolved in water, the relative fluorescence values were 1.0 and 1.1 at 1 and 5 hours, respectively, showing that almost no skin permeation occurred (Fig. 3b). However, for Rho B-DE-NPs in water, the relative intensity value decreased to 0.25 at 5 hours, showing that approximately 80% of the Rho B dye was transmitted compared with 1 hour. A positive control with free Rho B dissolved in 30% ethanol and 10% propylene glycol showed approximately 90% of the fluorescence value decreased from 1.0 to 0.12. Notably, in the sliced tissue images, unlike the skin absorption rate, the fluorescent dye was delivered to the telogen hair follicles in the dermis after 5 hours only in the Rho B-DE-NP group (Fig. 3c). This result showed the potential of deep penetration of Rho B-DE-NPs via the appendageal route.

Effects of 1% MXD-DE-NPs on hair growth in C57BL/6 mice

To observe the effects of MXD-DE-NPs on the ratio of telogen and anagen phase in mouse hair follicles and the growth rate of hair, C57BL/6 mice experiments were performed. Statistically significant difference was not observed between the empty particle group and the untreated sham group. However, the 1% MXD dissolved in ethanol and propylene glycol group and the 1% MXD-DE-NP group showed enhanced hair growth after 14 days of skin application (Fig. 4a). These two groups showed a significant difference in score compared with the other groups; however, the difference in score between the tonic solution with ethanol and propylene glycol group and the NP group was not statistically significant (Fig. 4b). On day 21 after skin application, mice were sacrificed, and H&E staining was performed on longitudinal and transverse sections of the back skin tissue. In tissue images, an increase in hair follicles could be observed in the 1% MXD in ethanol and propylene glycol and 1% MXD-DE-NP groups (Fig. 4c). Only the number of intact hair follicles visible in the H&E tissue was counted, and hair follicles located in the dermis were classified as telogen, and hair follicles located in the hypodermis as anagen. Significant difference was not observed between the empty particle group and the untreated sham group. However, in both groups containing 1% MXD, the anagen ratio was approximately 80% and statistically significant with the sham group (Fig. 4d). When measuring the number of intact hair follicles on a slide containing a 6.0 mm tissue section, the tonic containing 1% MXD and NPs showed a more than two-fold increase in hair follicles in the empty and sham groups (Fig. 4e). These data showed application of MXD-DE-NPs resulted in enhanced hair growth similar to traditional formulas using ethanol and propylene glycol.

Ex vivo penetration test of Rho B-DE-NP in porcine skin

In order to evaluate the skin penetration behavior of DE-NPs in thicker skin than mice, an ex vivo pig skin permeability study was performed (Fig. 5a). Samples were collected 1, 5, and 24 hours after skin application. Porcine skin is a tissue with a poor distribution of hair follicles, and we obtained images around hair follicles. In the case of free Rho B dissolved in 30% ethanol and 10% propylene glycol, there was no image of the fluorescent dye moving through the appendageal route or dyeing the hair follicles at any time (Fig. 5b, upper panel). On the other hand, the Rho B-DE-NP-treated group showed weak fluorescence around the hair follicles located in the epidermis from 5 hours after treatment, and it could be clearly observed that the sample entered the hair shaft via appendageal route at 24 hours (Fig. 5b, lower panel). We could observe many hair follicles in the dermis were stained (Fig. 5c).

Discussion

In hydrophilic fluorescent dyes, water cannot pass through the skin barrier but when incorporated into NPs, the dyes can effectively penetrate the skin barrier. However, NPs with incorporated hydrophilic dye were inferior in permeability to ethanol or propylene glycol, which physically open the skin barrier [17], [18]. NPs are a method of delivering drugs by widening the gap between cells or directly opening the cell membrane. Conversely, in the case of NPs, the skin penetration mainly flows into the hair shaft and delivers the drug directly to the hair follicles [15], [19]. Although the permeability is lower than with ethanol and propylene glycol combination, the anagen ratio, number of hair follicles, and hair growth level appear to be similar.

Based on observation of hair growth in the animal model, 1% MXD-DE-NPs showed 3.3-fold difference in hair growth score, 4-fold change in anagen ratio, and 2-fold greater number of hair follicles compared with the sham and empty groups; however, the 1% MXD group did not show statistical difference from the tonic solution dissolved in 30% ethanol and 10% propylene glycol group.

The MXD-DE-NPs we designed enabled a stable formulation of MXD and efficient delivery without ethanol and propylene glycol. Although the result was meaningful, the penetration through skin and hair growth effect was only similar to but did not outperform commercialized formulas. The NPs can be further improved because they showed more efficient penetration through the skin than the free form of drugs. Reportedly, the skin penetration of NPs varies based on their components [20]. The MXD-DE-NPs in the present study were optimized to have a small size and high EE; however, the procedure was based on in vitro data. Therefore, we postulate that deeper penetration of NPs can be achieved in future studies if further optimization is performed based on animal data.

The mouse skin used in the skin permeability study was about 300 μm thick from the epidermis to the dermis, and the thickness of the porcine skin was 2–3 mm, about ten folds thicker. It might be the reason that DE-NP showed intense fluorescent signals in hair follicles after 5 hours in mice experiments, but it was delayed in porcine skin. Even though there is a difference between ex vivo and in vivo experiments, we could observe that the arrival time of the NPs to hair follicles has a strong relationship with the thickness of the skin. The porcine skin test showed delivery through hair follicles is important particularly in thick skins of pig and maybe human because extensive penetration of thick skin is very difficult. In addition, our NP showed an excellent penetration effect using the appendageal route but cannot penetrate the skin other than hair follicles. Therefore, for effective drug delivery through the skin using NPs, it will be important to rationally control the delivery and drug release pattern in consideration of the thickness of the skin and the density of hair follicles in the target area. Furthermore, additional studies using other NPs such as ethosomes and transfersomes that were reported for efficient penetration will be also helpful [20], [21].

Conclusion

In summary, MXD-DE-NPs were developed for efficient delivery of MXD for the treatment of hair loss. The MXD-DE-NPs were composed of PLGA, PVA, Pluronic F-127, and emulsan, and formulated using the optimized DE method with acetic acid. The resulting MXD-DE-NPs loaded MXD at approximately 43% drug EE without aggregation in aqueous conditions at high MXD concentration (1% w/v) without ethanol and propylene glycol. The MXD-DE-NPs showed efficient uptake in dermal papilla cells and did not significantly affect cell viability. In particular, deep penetration of MXD-DE-NPs through hair follicle was observed in mice based on real-time fluorescence imaging. Furthermore, the hair regrowth effect was evaluated in mice for three weeks. The MXD-DE-NPs resulted in vigorous regrowth of hair, which was comparable to the traditional formula of MXD with ethanol and propylene glycol. Therefore, MXD-DE-NPs have potential as an efficient carrier of MXD for transdermal delivery to treat hair loss.
