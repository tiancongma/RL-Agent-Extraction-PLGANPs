ABSTRACT: Biodegradable nanoparticles, as drug delivery paradigms,
have been extensively used for delivery of a wide range of small molecules
as well as macromolecules, such as peptides, proteins, and genes. The
morphological modification may improve the physicochemical characteristics
of the biodegradable polymers. In the current investigation, the synthesis
and characterization of linear, poly(D,L-lactide-co-glycolide) (PLGA)-poly(ethy-
lene glycol) (PEG-PLGA), star-branched b-cyclodextrin-PLGA (b-CD-PLGA),
and glucose-PLGA (Glu-PLGA) copolymers containing insulin as a model
peptide drug have been reported. Linear and star-branched copolymers of PLGA
were synthesized by bulk melt polymerization of the lactones (lactide and
glycolide) in the presence of PEG, glucose, or b-CD using Sn-octoate as catalyst.
Nanoparticles were prepared by a modified (w1/o/w2) double emulsion method.
Bovine insulin was successfully encapsulated into the linear and star-branched
PLGA nanoparticles with retention of insulin stability and the nanoparticles
preparation process was optimized to reduce the burst effect and provide in vitro

particles since they offer a suitable means of diverse delivering of
small molecular weight drugs, peptides, proteins, and genes by either
localized or targeted delivery to the desired cells/tissue [11–14].
Recently, it was shown that oral application of polymeric nanoparticles
containing insulin is useful in prolonging the duration of drug effect,
and accordingly, enhanced oral absorption of insulin was reported
upon its administration as polymeric nanocapsules [15]. However,
the most important limitation for such nanoparticles as oral delivery
systems is the requirement of sufficient absorption from the GIT.
To overcome such an obstacle, the association of the drug with
bioadhesive polymers was also examined, in which adhesion of the

induce tissue irritation. Intriguingly, uptake of hydrophobic nano-
particles seems to be higher than hydrophilic nanoparticles. Thus, an
optimal hydrophobic-hydrophilic balance is required for the controlled
delivery of drugs. Further, hydrophilic biodegradable materials are
required for incorporation of water soluble polypeptide drugs in
solution, in particular when controlled delivery of protein/peptide
drugs is desired. In the case of linear polyesters consisting of PLA
or PLGA this is partially achieved by copolymerization or adjustment
of molecular weight. However, in many cases the release of peptides
and proteins from linear polyesters is not satisfactorily controlled,
leading to undesired discontinuous or polyphasic release patterns [18].
To overcome these problems, two major modifications of the polymers
have been proposed, namely: (a) increasing the hydrophilicity of the
polymers, and (b) accelerating the degradation rate of linear polyesters
by branching PLA or PLGA. An example for the first approach is the
linear ‘ABA’ triblock copolymers consisting of the hydrophobic PLGA
and central hydrophilic poly ethylene glycol (PEG) as A and B blocks,
respectively. Such architecture appears to provide a more rapid swelling
characteristics and accelerated degradation rates [19,20]. As for the
second concept, star-branched polyesters, brush like grafted PLGA,
and PLGAs with low molecular multifunctional alcohols have been
described [21–23].

Materials
DL-Lactide (LA) and glycolide (GA) were purchased from Boehringer,
Ingelheim (Germany). They were recrystallized twice from ethyl acetate
and dried under high vacuum at room temperature before use. Bovine
insulin, poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG, Mw 1000), poly vinyl alcohol (PVA,
Mw 10000), b-cyclodextrin (b-CD), stannous octoate, and glucose were
obtained from Sigma Chemical Co. (St. Louis, MO, USA). The structure

was conducted in nitrogen atmosphere using the DSC60 (Shimadzu,
Japan). Thermograms covered a range of 20 to 2008C with heating
and cooling rates of 108C/min.
Synthesis and Characterization of PLGA-PEG Copolymers
Poly(ethylene glycol) was dried by azeotropic distillation in a flask
with toluene (3 times, 50 mL) under an atmosphere of nitrogen followed
by drying at 1308C under vacuum (5 mmHg). LA and GA monomers
(in the mole ratio of 3:1) were added to the flask followed by the addition
of stannous octoate (0.05 wt%) and the reaction mixture was heated
at 1508C under vacuum (10 mmHg). The progress of the reaction was
followed by GPC. After an appropriate time, the reaction was stopped
and the flask was cooled down to room temperature. The residue was
dissolved in cold water and heated at 808C to precipitate the polymer.
The supernatant was decanted and the polymer residue was re-dissolved
in cold water and heated to induce precipitation. The resulting polymer
was lyophilized (Christ Alpha 1-4, Germany).

Synthesis and Characterization of PLGA-glucose
Lactide (0.684 mol), glycolide (0.838 mol), and D(þ)-glucose (2.2 mmol)
were placed in a flask and heated, while stirring to 1358C in an argon
atmosphere followed by addition of stannous octoate (0.05%). After
10 min, stirring was discontinued and the brown viscous mixture was
reacted further at 130–1408C for 10 h. After cooling 50 mL of methylene
dichloride was added. The mixture was dissolved and the solvent was
separated. The dark brown solution was treated with 25 mL of 5%
aqueous HCl solution to remove the catalyst. The solution was washed
with water and diluted with methylene dichloride. The solution was
added dropwise to 500 mL methanol at 258C. The product was filtered
and dried at 408C under vacuum.

Primary water-in-oil (w/o) emulsion was prepared using a homogenizer

Finally, the particles were washed with water and freeze-dried.
Particle Size Measurement and Morphology of the Particles
The morphology of the polymeric nanoparticles was observed using
transmission and scanning electron microscopy methods. For transmis-
sion electron microscopy (TEM), a drop of drug loaded nanoparticle
suspension in aqueous solution was placed on a carbon film coated on
a copper grid for TEM and freeze-dried. Observation was performed
at 80 kV using LEO 906 TEM (Zeiss, Germany). For each batch of
nanoparticles suspension prepared, a sample was analyzed for reprodu-
cibility of particle size and distribution. Analysis was performed with
a laser diffraction particle size analyzer (Shimadzu SALD 2101, Japan).
For
the
scanning
electron
microscopy
(SEM),
the
lyophilized
nanoparticles were placed on a double stick tape over aluminum stubs
to get a uniform layer of particles. Samples were then gold-coated using
a sputter gold coater. Gold coated particle samples were cooled over
liquid nitrogen prior to SEM observations to avoid their melting under
high magnification due to the electron beam exposure (Leo Electron
Microscopy Ltd, Cambridge, UK). Prior to examination, samples were
prepared on aluminum stubs and coated with gold under argon
atmosphere by means of a sputter coater.

Definite quantity of freshly prepared insulin containing particles in
suspension
was
ultracentrifuged
at
50,000 rpm
(Beckman-Optima

un-entrapped insulin. The supernatant was removed and analyzed by

USA) using a phenomax-C18 column at room temperature with a mobile
phase consisting of a mixture of potassium hydrogen phosphate solution

adjusted to pH 3 with phosphoric acid and acetonitrile (70 : 30). The
injection volume for samples and standards were 20 mL and eluted at
a flow rate of 0.5 mL/min. The eluent was monitored by the UV detector
at 214 nm. The amount of the drug present in the nanoparticles was
determined as the difference between the total amount of drug used to
prepare the nanoparticles and the amount of the drug present in the
aqueous medium. Insulin loading efficiency of the nanoparticles was
defined as the ratio of the amount of the entrapped drug to that of the
drug used for nanoparticles preparation.
Study of the Insulin Release from Nanoparticles
Release studies were conducted by incubating certain amounts
of particles in 5 mL of 0.1 M PBS (pH 7.4) at 378C using Unimax 1010
shaker-incubator (Heidolph, Germany). At appropriate time intervals,
individual samples were taken out and centrifuged at 10,000 rpm for
30 min. The supernatant was separated and assayed by HPLC to
determine the amount of insulin released from particles and the
particles pellets were redispersed in the same volume of release medium.
Insulin Temperature Stability Studies
The thermal stability of insulin was studied according to the metho-
dology reported earlier [24]. Briefly, 1 g of insulin loaded nanoparticles
dispersion was centrifuged at 10,000 rpm for 20 min, after which the
pellet was collected and incubated at 608C. At time intervals of 1, 2, 4, 8,
and 10 h, they were removed from the oven and 1 mL of PBS was added
and stored at 48C for 24 h to extract the insulin from the nanoparticles.
The sample was then centrifuged and the supernatants were filtered
and analyzed with HPLC to determine the remaining insulin in
the sample.

Synthesis and Structure of the Copolymers
The polymer structure used to formulate the nanoparticles can
strongly affect the properties and applications of nanoparticles. For each
application and drug, thus, the properties of the system should be
optimized for a given drug delivery application.
The purpose of using cyclic polyols, such as glucose and b-CD for
preparing branched biodegradable polyester was to provide a higher

Molecular weight and molecular weight distribution of the copolymers
were determined by means of GPC (Table 1). The molecular weight of
these copolymers depended on extension of the hydroxyl groups’
esterification on the cyclic polyol. The impacts of various formulation
variables on controlled release of drugs from injectable in situ formed
biodegradable PLGA microspheres have already been investigated [26],
in which the authors showed that the drug release can be retarded with
increase in the molecular weight of the encapsulated drug.
Physicochemical Characterization of Nanoparticles
Shown in Figure 4 are the typical transmission and scanning electron
micrographs of the insulin loaded nanoparticles formulated using linear
and star-branched polyesters. The TEM and SEM images of the nano-
particles revealed relatively spherical morphology with smooth surface
and confirmed the size range of 150–300 nm (Figure 4(B), (D), and (F)).
The most common method used for the preparation of polymeric nano-
particles is the emulsion-solvent evaporation technique which has been
successfully used for encapsulating hydrophilic small drugs as well as
macromolecules including peptides/proteins [13,27–30]. Dissolving a high
concentration of high molecular weight polymer in a mixture of
dichloromethane-acetone (80: 20) resulted in the spontaneous formation
of nanoparticles. The amount of stabilizer (PVA) had an important effect
on the properties of the nanoparticles. When the concentration of the
stabilizer was too low (50.5% w/v) aggregation of the particles accrued.
Alternatively, when larger amounts of the stabilizer were used (45% w/v),
the drug incorporation was reduced. Basically, overall 2% (w/v) of PVA
was ideal for creating smaller nanoparticles (i.e., 5400 nm).

CONCLUSIONS
Bovine insulin was successfully encapsulated into the linear and star-
branched PLGA nanoparticles with retention of insulin stability and the
nanoparticles preparation process was optimized to reduce the burst
effect and provide in vitro sustained-release. PLGA-b-CD nanoparticles
resulted in maximum loading efficiency (95%) with minimal particle
size (e.g., 120–350 nm) that also displayed the slowest drug release
profile. Based on the present findings, it is proposed that these novel
PLGA nanoparticles could be used as a carrier for improved-prolonged
delivery of peptide/protein based drugs, such as insulin, in particular
when they are used for oral delivery and targeting.
