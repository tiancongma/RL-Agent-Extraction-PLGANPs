2.2.2. Preparation of curcumin-loaded PLGA nanoparticles (C-PNPs)
C-PNPs were prepared by using the single emulsion-solvent
evaporation technique reported by Khalil et al.  with slight
modifications. Briefly, 100e200 mg of PLGA polymer was dissolved
in 5 ml of dichloromethane (DCM) in a glass tube. Then 10 or 20 mg
of curcumin powder was added to the polymer/solvent mixture and
allowed to dissolve for 30 min, with intermittent vortexing. The
organic phase containing PLGA and curcumin was rapidly added
drop wise into a glass tube containing 10 ml of PVA in an aqueous
solution, while vortexing. Once all the drug/polymer mixture was
added to the PVA solution, the contents were vortexed for an
additional 10 s at a high setting. The tube contents were then
emulsified by using sonication for 7 min at 40% amplitude in an ice
- water bath by using a probe sonicator (Vibra-Cell VCX 750 sonicator, Sonics & Materials Inc., Newtown, CT, USA). The resulting fine
(O/W) emulsion was immediately poured into 30 ml of an aqueous
PVA 0.5% w/v solution under rapid stirring with a magnetic stirrer.
Dichloromethane was then evaporated from the droplets under
magnetic stirring at 800 rpm for 3 h. The nanoparticles were
collected by centrifugation at 20,000 rpm for 15 min and washed 3
times with Milli-Q water. Supernatants were collected to evaluate
the encapsulation efficiency of curcumin. Finally, the pellet of the
nanoparticles was re-suspended in 5 ml of Milli-Q water.

3.2. Experimentally determined input parameters of C-PNPs for
optimization purposes

Table 2 shows the measured values of particle size, PDI and ZP,
EE% and DR% for the eight C-PNP formulations prepared during the
optimization stage. It is clear that the particle sizes of all the C-PNPs
were in the nano-size range. The smallest particle size of 181.5 nm
was observed for C1-PNP and the largest particle size of 206.9 nm
was observed for C4-PNP. The PDI values of the C-PNPs were also
very small (i.e. between 0.010 and 0.060), which shows that the
particle size populations of the C-PNPs were very homogeneous. A
small PDI value (i.e. ~ 0.2) is often taken as an indication of a homogenous particle size population, while a larger PDI value
(i.e. > 0.3) reflects a heterogeneous particle size population.
The ZP of all of the C-PNP formulations displayed a high negative
value between 30.5 mV and 41.7 mV. A ZP value for nanoparticles in this range usually predicts that the nanoparticles are
very well dispersed in the aqueous media and that the nanosuspensions will have a very good stability and tolerance against
aggregation.
The EE%s of the C-PNPs were also very promising. A high EE% is
usually desirable for NPs because it increases the probability that
the NPs can deliver and release a sufficient amount of drug to the
target site, especially if the NPs are designed so that they have a
proper triggered release mechanism and drug release rate. The
highest and the lowest EE% of 83.2% and 58.1% were observed for
C4-PNP and C5-PNP, respectively (Table 2). The EE%s of the C-PNPs
in this study clearly correlated with the particle size, i.e. the larger
the particle size of the C-PNP the higher the EE% and vice versa.
The in vitro drug release characteristics of the C-PNPs were
determined by using a three-different pH release media (SGF pH
1.2, SIF pH 6.8 and SCF pH 7.4) approach in order to mimic the GI
tract. The in vitro release profiles of curcumin were obtained by
graphing the cumulative percentage of the drug released with
respect to the total amount of curcumin encapsulated in the C-PNPs
as a function of the time (Fig. 1). Negligible amounts of curcumin
were released in the buffer medium at pH 1.2 which indicates that
curcumin is not released from any of the C-PNPs at gastric and ileal
pH (acidic pH). Only a slight release of curcumin took place at pH
6.8 (i.e. < 5%), whereas a substantial amount of curcumin was
released at the higher colonic pH values of pH 1/4 7.4. All the C-PNPs
also displayed a sustained release of curcumin up to 24 h at colonic
pH values (Fig. 1). The highest release of curcumin after 24 h, 62.8%,
was observed for the C-PNP4 formulation (Fig. 1A, Table 2), whereas
the lowest release of curcumin, 34.4%, was observed for the C-PNP1
formulation (Fig. 1A, Table 2).

A sustained and triggered release of the drug in a delivery system is an important property closely related to its pharmacokinetics and efficacy. The in vitro release characteristics of the C-PNPs
in Fig.1 show that practically no curcumin was released from any of
the C-PNP formulations in the SGF medium (~zero), whereas a
slight release of curcumin took place in the SIF (i.e. < 5% of curcumin released). Once the pH reached neutral values (i.e. pH 7.4),
curcumin was, however, rapidly released in a biphasic manner
reaching a sustained release of 34e63% after 24 h. This behavior
differs from the one presented in the study by Xie et al.  where a
burst release of curcumin of up to 25% and a sustained release of
48% at pH 1/4 2 were reported for PLGA-based NPs for oral administration. Furthermore, Xie et al.  and Cartiera et al.  reported that curcumin-loaded PLGA-based NPs for oral delivery
released curcumin in a biphasic manner reaching a sustained
release of ~55% and ~20% after 24 h. Thus, it can be concluded that
the sustained release of curcumin from the C-PNPs in this study is
high and in good agreement with other similar studies. The fact
that negligible amounts of curcumin were released in the pH range
2e6.8 clearly shows that a triggered release of curcumin from the
C-PNPs in an environment mimicking the colon is taking place, and
that the amount of curcumin released is substantially higher in a
colonic environment than in an environment mimicking the
stomach or small intestine. This means that the main absorption of
curcumin from the C-PNP formulations should take place in the
large intestine, and not in the stomach or small intestine, unless the
C-PNPs are taken up earlier in the villi of the small intestine.
However, the uptake of the C-PNPs before they reach the colon

could possibly be minimized by a proper surface functionalization
of the NPs. Thus, the optimized C-PNP9 formulation developed here
should enable a further development of an oral formulation for
triggered, sustained, targeted and effective colon treatment with
curcumin.
The kinetic constants and mode of release obtained from the
release kinetic fits to the in vitro release profiles of the C-PNPs
(Fig. 1) are shown in Table S1. The controlled release pattern of
curcumin from the C-PNPs followed mainly the Higuchi diffusion
model with a half-life of 7e29 h. This indicates that a sustained
release of curcumin from the C-PNPs mainly takes place by diffusion from the PLGA matrix and not through extensive degradation/
erosion processes of the PLGA nanoparticles. This is in sound
accordance with the fact that degradation of PLGA can last from
weeks to months , and if the diffusion of the drug is faster than
the matrix degradation, then the mechanism of drug release should
occur mainly by diffusion . The release mechanism of the CPNPs is in good agreement with recent studies that have reported
that drug-loaded PLGA NPs exhibit a controlled release pattern
following the Higuchi diffusion model .
