Background: With the global increase in antibacterial resistance, the challenge faced by developing coun­
tries is to utilize the available antibiotics, alone or in combination, against resistant bacterial strains. We
aimed to encapsulate the levofloxacin (LVX) into polymeric nanoparticles using biodegradable polymers i.e.
Chitosan and PLGA, estimating their physicochemical characteristics followed by functional assessment as
nanocarriers of levofloxacin against the different resistant strains of bacteria isolated from biological
samples collected from tertiary care hospital in Lahore, Pakistan.
Methods: LVX-NPs were synthesized using ion gelation and double emulsion solvent-evaporation method
employing chitosan (CS) and poly-lactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA), characterized via FTIR, XRD, SEM, and
invitro drug release studies, while antibacterial activity was assessed using Kirby-Bauer disc-diffusion
method.
Results: Data revealed that the levofloxacin-loaded chitosan nanoparticles showed entrapment efficiency of
57.14%  ±  0.03 (CS-I), 77.30%  ±  0.08(CS-II) and 87.47%  ±  0.08 (CS-III). The drug content, particle size, and
polydispersity index of CS-I were 52.22%  ±  0.2, 559 nm  ±  31 nm, and 0.030, respectively, whereas it was
66.86%  ±  0.17, 595 nm  ±  52.3 nm and 0.057, respectively for CS-II and 82.65%  ±  0.36, 758 nm  ±  24 nm and
0.1, respectively for CS-III. The PLGA-levofloxacin nanoparticles showed an entrapment efficiency of
42.80%  ±  0.4 (PLGA I) and 23.80%  ±  0.4 (PLGA II). The drug content, particle size and polydispersity index of
PLGA-I were 86%  ±  0.21, 92 nm  ±  10 nm, and 0.058, respectively, whereas it was 52.41%  ±  0.45,
313 nm  ±  32 nm and 0.076, respectively for PLGA-II. The XRD patterns of both polymeric nanoparticles
showed an amorphous nature. SEM analysis reflects the circular-shaped agglomerated nanoparticles with
PLGA polymer and dense spherical nanoparticles with chitosan polymer. The in-vitro release profile of
PLGA-I nanoparticles showed a sustained release of 82% in 120 h and it was 58.40% for CS-III. Both types of
polymeric nanoparticles were found to be stable for up to 6 months without losing any major drug content.

and gram-ve bacteria, except for K. pneumonia, yet, PLGA-I demonstrated efficacy against K. pneumonia as

1. Introduction
Undoubtedly, antibiotics have been widely used by humans over
the past 60 years, either as prophylaxis or as a therapeutic option,
against various infections [1] – contributing to significantly reduced
infection-associated mortality. [2] Among others, Levofloxacin (LVX),
a third-generation fluoroquinolone, is known to be a drug of choice
in many infections including gram-negative, gram-positive, and
atypical pathogens – controlling urinary tract, respiratory tract, skin,
and soft tissue infections [3]. However, it requires frequent dosing
and exhibits diverse pharmaceutical concentrations in the blood. [4]
Additionally, in Pakistan, quinolones have shown resistance against
enterobacteria and salmonella typhi [5,6].
The use of nanocarriers to deliver antibiotics has been well-
documented to help overcome the current limitations associated
with antibiotic therapy. [7] In this context, many efforts have been
made to develop a sustained delivery system of LVX in the form of
capsulate [8], filament [9], microsphere [10], and nanoparticles. [4,7]
In these efforts, several natural polymers, such as alginate or chit­
osan (CS), synthetic polymers, such as polycaprolactone (PCL) and
poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) are extensively used. [7] PLGA,
the most widely used biodegradable and biocompatible polymer in
the preparation of nanoparticles, is approved by the Food and Drug
Administration (FDA) for human use. [11] On the other hand, chit­
osan (CS), a natural polymer, cationic in nature, water-soluble, bio­
compatibility, and low toxicity makes it an attractive choice for the
preparation of nano-carriers. [12] Numerous literature report de­
monstrated the loading of LVX in several polymeric nanoparticles,
e.g., LVX-CS nanoparticles for ocular delivery, [13], LVX-PLGA nano­
particles for antibacterial activity against gram-negative (-) and
gram-positive (+) bacteria, [14] LVX-PLGA nano-particles against
mycobacterium tuberculosis. [15] More recently, an attempt has
been made to optimize the preparation of both LVX-CS and LVX-
PLGA nano-particles using ion-gelation and double-emulsion solvent
evaporation methods, respectively [7].
Besides, both polymers, CS and PLGA, have been utilized to pre­
pare bioactive luteolin loaded CS-PLGA-NPs employing emulsifica­
tion-evaporation process demonstrating 3.19 and 2.63 folds
improvements in AUC as evidenced from permeation and pharma­
cokinetic studies, respectively [16]. Likewise, using both CS and
PLGA, forskolin and rutin loaded CS-coated PLGA-NPs were prepared
for improved ocular delivery. The prepared NPs showed sustained
release of forskolin and rutin with significantly high permeation [17].
Similarly, forskolin loaded CS coated PLGA-NPs enhanced ocular
hypotensive effects attributable to forskolin release and enhanced
permeation profile [18]. Furthermore, metal nano-composites, such
as iron, titanium dioxide and iron oxide, have been used with
fluoroquinolone antibiotics, e.g., ciprofloxacin (CIP), to further im­
prove the use of CIP against resistant bacteria, i-e., E.coli in mastitis
induced rabbit model with no changes in rabbit vital organs [19]. In a
similar attempt, CIP loaded gold (Au) nanoparticles were prepared to
test against the colonization of Enterococcus faecalis in the liver and
kidney of mice. Data suggested that CIP-Au-NPs successfully eradi­
cated Enterococcus faecalis from the host tissues [20]. These data
clearly suggest that use of both CS and PLGA significantly enhanced
the delivery of antibiotics to the target site improving the permea­
tion and pharmacokinetic profile of the drug. Studies from Pakistan
suggest that the most common isolated pathogens from the clinical
settings include, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Staphylococcus aureus,
Escherichia coli, Klebsiella pneumonia isolated from pus, tracheal se­
cretions, urine and blood [21]. In this context, the number of AMR
deaths in Pakistan is higher than deaths from neoplasms, respiratory
infections, tuberculosis, diabetes and kidney disease with greater
number of deaths related to E. coli, S. aureus, P. aeruginosa, and K.
pneumonia [22]. However, the use of LVX loaded polymeric, CS and
PLGA, nanoparticles against the resistant bacterial strains, Gram
negative; E. coli, P. aeruginosa, and K. pneumonia and Gram Positive;
S. aureus and S. pneumonia and Methicillin Resistant S. aureus, MRSA,
from patient biological samples, prevalent in a tertiary care hospital
of Lahore, Pakistan, have not been investigated before. Thus, the
present study, to our knowledge the first report, was aimed at the
development of various LVX loaded polymeric, CS and PLGA, nano-
formulations by altering polymer (CS) and surfactant (PVA) con­
centrations to examine their antibacterial activity against resistant
gram-negative and gram-positive bacterial strains form patient
biological samples.

2.2. Preparation methods
2.2.1. Synthesis of blank nanoparticles
Synthesis of blank nano-particles are described in supplementary
material.
2.2.2. Synthesis of chitosan-based nanoparticles
2.2.2.1. Levofloxacin loaded chitosan (LVX-CS) nanoparticles. The
different composition of LVX and CS for the preparation of LVX-CX-
NPs are summarized in Table S1. Different concentrations of
Chitosan gel solution (i.e. 2 mg/mL, 4 mg/mL and 6 mg/mL) were
prepared by dissolving the chitosan in 1% glacial acetic acid. These
solutions were subjected to continuous stirring overnight by
magnetic stirrer at 300–400 rpm speed until a clear gel solution
was obtained. The resultant solution pH was adjusted at 4.7 to 5.0 by
dropwise adding 1 M NaOH. The chitosan solution was then filtered
with syringe filter having pore size 0.45 µm. To aforementioned
prepared gel solution of different concentrations, constant amount
of LVX was added in three different ratios, 1:1, 1:2 and 1:3 and
stirred for 1 h. The aqueous solution of sodium tripolyphosphate was
prepared in accordance with the concentration of polymer i.e. 1:1,
1:2 and 1:3. The sodium tripolyphosphate solution was added
dropwise into the drug-chitosan solutions at rate of 12 mL/hour
with vigorous stirring, 700–800 rpm, at room temperature to obtain
CS-I, CS-II and CS-III. These solutions were subjected to probe
sonication for 5 min with amplitude of 40%. The suspensions were
then homogenized on magnetic stirring for 2 h to evaporate the
organic solvent. The resultant final suspensions (CS-I, CS-II and CS-
III) were then subjected to ultracentrifugation at 12000 rpm for
40 mins. Later, the collected pellets from supernatant were washed
with deionized water to remove free drug from pellets. The
supernatants were then analyzed further to determine the drug
entrapment efficiency. These pellets were again suspended in
deionized water by magnetic stirring and then ultracentrifuged at
speed of 12000 rpm for 40 min. The washing process repeated in
triplicate to completely remove the excess free drug.
The resultant pellets were then suspended in deionized water
and freeze dried at 0.4mbar and − 40ᵒC for 24 h. This lyophilized
levofloxacin loaded chitosan nanoparticles of different con­
centrations were stored at 2–4ᵒC for further characterization. The
two more batches were prepared with same abovementioned
technique to determine the reproducibility of the method.
[4,7,23,24]. The schematic diagram of LVX-CS-NPs synthesis I s
shown in Fig. 1A.
2.2.3. Synthesis of PLGA-based nanoparticles
The PLGA nanoparticles were prepared with and without loading
the levofloxacin by double emulsion-solvent evaporation tech­
nique [25].
2.2.3.1. Levofloxacin-loaded PLGA (LVX-PLGA) nanoparticles. LVX-
PLGA nanoparticles were prepared using varying the concentration
of PVA (1% and 2%), while keeping levofloxacin and polymer ratios
constant, i-e., 1:2 (Table S2) (Fig. 1B).
Briefly, PLGA was dissolved in 10 mL DCM by vortexing.
Levofloxacin was added in 6 mL of PVA solution with vigorous stir­
ring until a clear solution. Next, 5 mL of the organic phase was added
drop-wise in the aqueous phase on a magnetic stirrer containing
drug and stabilizer PVA to form an oil-water (o/w) emulsion. The
emulsion is subjected to probe sonication for 90 s at 40% amplitude.
The resultant o/w emulsion was added dropwise into the remaining
5 mL organic phase with constant stirring to form a double emulsion
and re-sonicated under the same conditions. This double emulsion
was added drop-wise in PVA solution to stabilize the double emul­
sion followed by sonication for 5.5 min at 40% amplitude. The or­
ganic solvent was placed on a magnetic stirrer for overnight
evaporation. Pellets were obtained by centrifugation, washed with
deionized water, and suspended again in deionized water followed
by centrifugation for 40 min at 12000 rpm. The centrifugation and
washing were done twice to remove the free drug. The supernatant
was stored The obtained levofloxacin-loaded PLGA nanoparticles
were freeze-dried at 0.4 m bar and − 40ᵒC for 24 h. The nanoparticles
were stored at 2–4ᵒC. The schematic diagram of LVX-PLGA-NPs
synthesis I s shown in Fig. 1B.

2.3.7. In-vitro dissolution profile

out using dissolution apparatus USP II with dialysis tubing method
to determine the sustained release pattern of both types of nano­
particles i.e. LVX-CS-NPs and LVX-PLGA-NPs. Briefly, the dialysis
membrane was pre-treated with distilled water for 4 h to remove
glycerol and placed overnight in a buffer solution, pH 7.4 (Sodium
chloride NaCl 0.137 M and potassium chloride KCl 0.0027 M) at 25ᵒC.
In a plastic tube, 5 mg of levofloxacin-loaded nanoparticles
equivalent to 5 mg of LVX with 2 mL PBS solution were added, the
base of which was enclosed with a presoaked dialysis membrane.
The dialysis tubing system was affixed on a paddle in a separate
dissolution flask containing 90 mL dissolution medium at pH 7.4 and
37ᵒC temperature. The sink conditions were maintained by dissol­
ving of NPs in fresh medium time to time, ensuring adequate volume

4 mL of the medium was drawn from each vessel according to pre-

and 72 h. The obtained media were then centrifuged and filtered
before assay using a UV spectrophotometer at a wavelength of

2.3.8. Stability studies
Stability studies were conducted to determine the stability of
LVX-NPs by keeping them in amber glass bottles wrapped with
aluminum foil and kept in an incubator at 37 °C and 75% relative
humidity (RH) for 6 months. The changes in appearance and drug

2.4. Biological assay
2.4.1. Biological samples
The blood samples of febrile patients were collected in Versatrek
Redox 1 (aerobic) and Redox 2 (anaerobic). The samples of febrile
patients were collected included urine, tracheal swabs, and wound
swabs. The samples were inoculated with the help of a needle/in­
oculating loop on agar culture media plates under a laminar flow
hood and were incubated for 72 h at 37ᵒC. The plates were examined
after every 24 h. The collecting colonies were identified by using
VITEK 2 identification (ID) GN, by uploading directly and after ad­
justing the turbidity of colony suspension (greater than 0.5
McFarland). [29,30].

2.4.2.1. Kirby-Bauer disc diffusion method. The biological samples
demonstrating positive growth of bacterial species were selected
for further antibacterial susceptibility studies. The prepared Muller
Hinton plates were inoculated with isolated colonies by streak
method. The desired discs i.e. impregnated with LVX-PLGA-NPs,
LVX-CS-NPs, and commercially available free-levofloxacin were
placed in the plates with the help of a needle. The commercial
antibiotic discs were used as standard and the prepared
nanoparticles were loaded onto blank discs. Plates were incubated
for the next 24 h. After 24 h of incubation, the zone of inhibition was
measured with a measuring scale in mm for each disc. [32].
2.4.2.2. Minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC). The MIC of LVX-CS-
NPs and LVX-PLGA-NPs was determined using the standard broth
serial microdilution method. The bacterial inoculums were adjusted
to the concentration of 6 × 105 CFU/mL. For every experiment
negative control (medium only) and positive control (medium and
inoculum) were included in 96-well micro-well plates. In each well
medium and test compounds were added and serially diluted
followed by the addition of bacterial inoculum and incubation at
37 °C for 18 – 24 h. The absorbance of the wells was read at 570 nm
using microtiter plate reader. The lowest concentration of
antibacterial agent that inhibits bacterial growth was considered
as MIC value.
2.4.3. In-vitro toxicological studies
The in vitro toxicological studies were conducted to determine
the safety margin of prepared polymeric nanoparticles, LVX-CS, LVX-
PLGA - 50 μg/mL, 500 μg/mL, 1 mg/mL, and 5 mg/mL in EP glass vial
for 1 h at 37ᵒC. The cytotoxic effects were estimated by employing
human erythrocytes. The fresh human blood was obtained from
Blood Bank Ittefaq Hospital Trust, Lahore. The red blood cells (RBCs)
were incubated in PBS (control), with free-LVX, LVX-CS, and LVX-
PLGA, all in triplicates. The treatment effects of nanoparticles were
compared with the control concerning erythrocyte morphology and
erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) [33].
2.4.4. Statistical analysis
Data were analyzed using GraphPad Prism version 9. Significant
differences among the groups were estimated using one way
ANOVA. An alpha value to 0.05 or more was considered statistically
significant.

As shown in Table 1, NPs synthesized using a 1:1 ratio of LVX and
CS (CS-I) demonstrated the smallest particle size of 559 nm, followed
by the particle size of 595 nm and 758 nm for 1:2 (CS-II) and 1:3 (CS-
III), respectively. The particle sizes of LVX-PLGA-I (PVA1%) and LVX-
PLGA-II (PVA2%) were 92 nm and 313 nm, respectively, keeping the
LVX-PLGA ratios constant (Table 1). The polydispersity index (PI), a
measure of heterogeneity in a sample based on size, was 0.030 and
0.057 for CS-1, and CS-II, respectively, and 0.058 for PLGA-I, sug­
gesting more of a mono-dispersion system compared to 0.1 for CS-III
and 0.076 for PLGA-II (Table 1).
3.3. Fourier transfer infrared (FTIR) spectra
FTIR spectra were performed to determine characteristic peaks of
a pure drug in synthesized nanoparticles.
3.4. FTIR spectra of LVX-CS-NPs
FTIR spectra of free-levofloxacin (LVX), CS polymer, sodium tri­
polyphosphate, and LVX-CS-NPs; CS-I, CS-II, and CS-III are docu­
mented in Table 2. The LVX spectrum exhibited characteristic peaks
at 3265.cm−1 due to COOH (aromatic), 2933 cm−1 due to C-CH3 (C-
methyl group), 1722 cm−1 due to C]O i.e., carbonyl group, 1295 cm−1
due to C-N group and 1088 cm−1 due to Fluoro group (Fig. 2C). The
FTIR spectra of chitosan and blank chitosan nanoparticles showed
characteristic peaks at 3358 cm−1 linked to an amino group (NH2)
and a hydroxyl group (OH) stretching vibration, while the peaks at
1636 cm−1 and 1526 cm−1 showed the presence of CONH2 and NH2
groups (Table 2). These peaks were shifted from 1647 cm−1 and
1558 cm−1 in CS-blank-NPs probably due to the interaction of the
NH3 group of chitosan and phosphate group of tripolyphosphate.
Another significant peak in CS-blank-NPs was at 1220.7 cm−1
showing the presence of phosphate group stretching vibration
(Fig. 2D). The FTIR spectra of CS-I, CS-II, and CS-III showed the
characteristic peak of both CS-NPs and a drug (LVX) in Fig. 2E. When
the IR bands of levofloxacin, chitosan and LVX-CS nanoparticles were
compared, a marked shift was observed in the intensity of the bands
of LVX-CS-NPs compared to CS-blank-NPs, reflecting the ionic-in­
teraction between chitosan and levofloxacin (Fig. 2D&E) (Table 2).
3.4.1. FTIR spectra of LVX-PLGA-NPs
FTIR spectra of LVX-PLGA-NPs did not show major shifts in the
peaks. The intensity of bands representing the characteristic peaks
remained similar in both reference and LVX-PLGA-NPs (Fig. 2F-H).
These data demonstrated that the functional groups were intact
with drug encapsulation in PLGA, thereby, the drug characteristic
peaks had less intensity than PLGA (Table 3).
3.5. X-ray diffractometry (XRD)
The XRD analysis was performed for LVX-CS-NPs and LVX-PLGA-NPs.
The X-ray diffraction of a pure drug showed crystalline shapes of free-
LVX and sharp peaks, characteristic of LVX crystallinity [34]. Whereas,
the chitosan showed two characteristic peaks as 2 = 10ᵒ and 20ᵒ. [35]. The
PLGA is amorphous, so no crystallinity peak was observed [35].
The XRD patterns of LVX-CS-NPs, CS-I, CS-II, CS-III, and LVX-
PLGA-NPs, PLGA-I & PLGA-II, are shown in Fig. 3A & B. In both cases,
reduced spectral peaks were observed. The comparative patterns of
both LVX-PLGA-NPs and LVX-CS-NPs are shown in Fig. 3C. These
patterns indicated the amorphous nature of both PLGA and Chit­
osan-based LVX nanoparticles.

3.7. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM)
Scanning electron microscopy was performed to determine the
morphology of PLGA and chitosan-based levofloxacin nanoparticles
at a scale of 10–100 µm.
3.7.1. SEM study of LVX-CS-NPs
The SEM data showed that LVX-CS-NPs, CS-I, CS-II & CS-III, had
irregular surfaces but few were seen as round with smooth surfaces,
though with some agglomeration (Fig. 4A-Ci-ii), which might happen
during ion-sputtering with the gold.
Morphologically, the LVX-CS-NPs appeared dense cubical, rec­
tangular, and spherical. They were attached, yet retained their shape.
The addition of surfactants can improve the morphology of nano­
particles – probably more spherical (Fig. 4A-Ci-ii).
3.7.2. SEM analysis of LVX-PLGA-NPs
The SEM analysis showed that LVX-PLGA-NPs, PLGA-I, and PLGA-
II, had smooth circular surfaces but were agglomerated. There is a
possibility that agglomeration might happen during the ion-sput­
tering with the gold (Fig. 4D-Ei-ii).
3.8. Stability study
The stability data of CS-I, CS-II & CS-III, and PLGA-I and PLGA-II
are shown in Table 4. Data suggested that there were no significant
changes in the drug content starting from 1st month till 6th month –
the difference was even less than 3% (Table 4). Likewise, no sig­
nificant changes were observed in the color in the 1st, 3rd and 6th
months (Table 4). The aggregation was observed in LVX-PLGA-NPs
with passing time.

3.10. Toxicological study
For toxicological studies, PLGA-I and CS-III formulations along
with control and free-LVX were tested against freshly isolated
human red blood cells (RBCs, n = 4). The PLGA-I and CS-III were in­
cubated with RBCs for 1 h at 37ᵒC (Fig. 6A & B).
The RBCs in the control and free-LVX samples demonstrated no
morphological changes, i-e., the RBCs retained their spherical shapes
with smooth surfaces (Fig. 6A & B). Likewise, the PLGA-I treated RBCs
showed no changes in the morphology, no interaction with the
surface of RBCs and no hemolysis (Fig. 6C).
Similarly, the CS-III samples demonstrated no vivid morpholo­
gical changes in RBCs, yet the nanoparticles adhered to the surface of
erythrocytes, probably due to their positive ion. The RBCs retained
their shapes but the CS-III adhered with RBCs. Also, no hemolysis
was observed with CS-III treatment (Fig. 6D).
4. Discussion
Despite, several available options for antimicrobial therapy, the
global increase in antimicrobial resistance is threatening the popu­
lation worldwide. [36] With the number of new antibiotics egre­
giously limited, the rising antibiotic resistance poses a continuous
threat to confront infectious diseases [37]. Thus, the dwindled an­
tibiotic conduits initiated a changeover in research stratagems to
configure the existing antibiotic molecules, such as antibiotic func­
tionalized nanoparticles and pure antibiotics engineered into na­
noscale having potential for targeted delivery [37,38]. The present
study demonstrated the synthesis, characterization, and anti­
microbial sensitivity of LVX-NPs against resistant bacterial strains
employing two different FDA-approved polymers, i-e., PLGA and
Chitosan (CS). Three different CS-based, CS-I, CS-II & CS-III, and two
different PLGA-based polymeric NPs, PLGA-I and PLGA-II, were
prepared and characterized for the particle size, physiochemical
nature, morphology, polydispersity, crystallographic structure and
drug release profiles. Data revealed that CS-III and PLGA-I demon­
strated the highest entrapment efficiency (EE) and loading capacity
(LC), while CS-I and PLGA-I had smaller particle sizes. Both the for­
mulations, CS-III and PLGA-I, had optimal drug release profiles and
stability over 6 months, and were found effective against gram-po­
sitive and gram-negative resistant bacterial strains compared to
free-LVX.
The composition of PLGA and CS nanoparticles was investigated
by FTIR analysis. Both formulations showed the characteristic peaks
of the drug and a polymer. However the few peaks of the drug were
masked and were reduced in intensity possibly due to the entrap­
ment property of the polymer. [24,39] The SEM and XRD analysis
were carried out to determine the morphology and nature of

The utility spectrum of NPs is determined by morphological
characteristics, such as particle size, polydispersity index, zeta po­
tential, surface charge, and shape. These properties affect NP’s bio­
logical activities, while preparation methods play a pivotal role in
controlling these properties. Our data suggested that a constant ratio
of LVX, yet with increasing CS ratio (1:3) resulted in large size NPs
(CS-III) and increased PDI, indicative of broad particle size dis­
tribution, and higher EE and LC. While out of three formulations, CS-I
(1:1) with equal ratios exhibited the smallest particle size, and
monodispersing system but with lower EE and LC, corroborating
previous findings [42,43]. There have been conflicting reports with
regards to the effect of mass ratios of CS and a drug on NP char­
acteristics, e.g., particle size decreases with increasing concentration
of CS [43,44], but with subsequent increase[43] and decrease [44] in
EE%. The explanation of these differences compared to data pre­
sented in this study could be the cross-linker concentration, which
was not maneuvered, and the negligible contribution of the loading
drug (LVX) as an ionic crosslinker. However, Zhang and Hu used
Alphastatin, which acted as a polyanionic cross-linker with cationic
CS [44], demonstrating enough ionic interaction that resulted in
small particle size and high EE%, nonetheless, the reduced EE% in our
case could be attributed to intermediate lipophilicity of LVX [45].
However, the encapsulation efficiency and loading capacity of the
CS-I (1:1) were among the lowest of all three formulations.

The previous report clearly demonstrated that the PLGA and PVA
concentrations have a direct impact on particle size of the NPs [46]
prepared using the double emulsion-solvent evaporation method.
However, it has also been shown that changing PLGA concentration
does not have any significant effect on the PDI and particle size of
NPs prepared using the double emulsion evaporation method. [47]
Thus, we examined only the effects of surfactant (PVA) concentra­
tion on NP characteristics keeping the drug (LVX) and PLGA con­
centrations constant. The change in PVA concentrations can be
argued based on previous findings that the increasing concentration
of PVA guarantees better stabilization, optimal size, and reduced
coalescence of emulsion [48], while increasing the concentration of
PVA above 2.5% has been argued to exist as an aggregated form ra­
ther than a single molecule. [49] We observed that by changing the
concentrations of PVA, the particle size and PDI of LVX-PLGA-NPs
increased with increasing PVA concentration, while the EE and LC
decreased significantly. The increase in particle size can be attribu­
table to the higher viscosity of the aqueous phase affecting the so­
nication shear stress that might result in the formation of large
emulsion droplets. While PVA concentration of more than 1% has
been shown to reduce the EE and LC [50]. Seemingly, the 0.5% PVA is
considered as the most optimum concentration to attain higher EE
and LC, corroborating previous findings,which may be attributable to
higher viscosity and large but fewer emulsion droplets. [50].
Additionally, particle size and charge have been shown to impact
EE and drug release. [51] We observed that by increasing CS con­
centration the drug release decreased from 61% to 51% with a de­
crease in particle size, though the pattern was bi-phasic. The drug
release from CS NPs is governed by polymer swelling, loaded drug
per se, diffusion, erosion, and degradation of the polymer.[52]
Therefore, the main mechanism of LVX release, a hydrophilic drug,
could be diffusion and polymer erosion and degradation, while
higher drug release of CS-I over 120 h could be attributable to re­
duced particle size which has been shown to exhibit larger initial
burst release and optimal polymer swelling, erosion and degradation
compared to CS-II. The slower drug release from CS-II or CS-III, due
to increased polymer ratio, could be due to several factors, such as
polymer swelling, polymer erosion or degradation and diffusion of
drug from the polymer. Notably, drug diffusion from the matrix can
be described by Fick’s law of diffusion – initial burst followed by
slow release, attributable to diffusion barrier by polymeric chains
associated with swelling or erosion [53]. While the swelling of the
polymer matrix occurs via non-Fick’s diffusion system, affected by
relaxation constant – higher value of the relaxation constant, slower
will be the drug release from the matrix [53]. When it comes to
PLGA, it has been reported that initial drug release from the PLGA
matrix was via diffusion followed by the release of a drug by both
diffusion and degradation of a polymer. [50] We observed that PLGA-
I formulation (PVA1%) demonstrated improved drug release of
∼88.58% over 120 h compared to PLGA-II (PVA2%) of 82.6% - corro­
borating previous report that cumulative drug release was higher for
the PLGA-NPs synthesized using 1% PVA compared to 2% PVA. [54].
Other plausible explanations could be the higher PVA concentration
[55] and impact of particle size on release kinetics, as small particles
may have greater initial burst release due to surface absorption and
the capacity of entrapment [56].

5. Conclusion
Taken together, these data suggested that among various for­
mulations of LVX-NPs, CS-III and PLGA-I demonstrated the highest
loading capacity and entrapment efficiency, and with a significant
effect of polymer (CS) and surfactant (PVA), on particle size. The
polydispersity was optimal for all the formulations – mono-disper­
sion system, except for CS-III and PLGA-II. Major shifts in FTIR
spectra were observed for CS-NPs due to the interaction of NH3 and
phosphate groups, while no shifts were observed for PLGA-NPs. SEM
data suggested spherical shapes of CS-NPs and circular shapes of
PLGA-NPs with some agglomeration. With formulations having an
amorphous nature and stable for up to 6 months, the highest in vitro
drug release patterns were observed for CS-I and PLGA-I after 120 h.
CS-III, having a higher polymer ratio, demonstrated better anti­
bacterial activity against resistant strains except against K. pneu­
monia whereas PLGA-I, having 1% PVA, was found superior against K.
pneumonia.
