A B S T R A C T
Staphylococcus aureus (S. aureus), particularly methicillin-resistant strains (MRSA), poses a significant threat to
global public health due to its resistance to conventional antibiotics. The urgent need for alternative treatments
has highlighted cepharanthine (CEP), a bisbenzylisoquinoline alkaloid, known for its antiviral, antibacterial, and
anti-inflammatory properties. However, the clinical application of CEP is constrained by several factors. These
include the requirement for a high therapeutic dosage, low aqueous solubility, restricted oral absorption, and a
short half −
life. In this study, we developed a chitosan-coated Poly Lactic-co-Glycolic Acid (PLGA) nano­
emulsion encapsulating CEP (CCPN) using the double-emulsion solvent evaporation method. The formulation
was optimized to achieve ideal physicochemical properties, including a particle size of 588.13 ± 31.87 nm and a
zeta potential of 48.60 ± 1.00 mV, ensuring stability and uniformity. Biological evaluations demonstrated that
CCPN effectively inhibited hemolysis, suppressed biofilm formation, disrupted mature biofilms, and displayed
potent antibacterial activity against S. aureus. In vivo studies using a murine pneumonia model revealed that
CCPN significantly alleviated lung damage, reduced bacterial load, mitigated inflammatory responses, and
improved survival rates of mice infected with S. aureus or MRSA. These findings highlight CCPN as a promising
therapeutic strategy for treating bacterial pneumonia. This novel nanoemulsion effectively tackles the key lim­
itations in antimicrobial therapy by boosting the solubility, stability, and antibacterial efficacy of CEP. It holds
great promise in the fight against antibiotic −
resistant infections and shows substantial potential for promoting
the treatment of pulmonary diseases.
1. Introduction
Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA), the emergence
and spread of antibiotic-resistant bacteria, is a common opportunistic
human pathogen. It causes various infections, including abscesses, pul­
monary infections, bacteremia, endocarditis, and osteomyelitis, while
its antibiotic resistance has contributed to its worldwide spread (Jin
et al., 2022, Wertheim et al., 2005). Acute lung injury (ALI) is charac­
terized by extensive inflammation of the pulmonary epithelium and
endothelium, often triggered by sepsis, bacterial infection (Lucas et al.,
2020), or viral infection (Zhang et al., 2020). It is typically marked by
severe hypoxemia, pulmonary edema, and excessive of neutrophils
infiltration in the lung tissue (Zhang et al., 2009). Staphylococcus aureus
pneumonia (SAP) is among the most prevalent S. aureus diseases,
comprising approximately 13.3 % of all invasive staphylococcal in­
fections, with significant morbidity and mortality, placing a significant
burden on healthcare and community environments (Klevens et al.,
2007, Rivera and Boucher, 2011). Consequently, the development of
alternative and innovative therapeutic agents is urgently required to
address this challenge (Wang et al., 2016).
Cepharanthine (CEP), a didodecyl alkaloid extracted from Stephania
cepharantha Hayata, demonstrates significant anti-inflammatory

been reported to protect against LPS-induced pulmonary vascular
damage, making it a promising candidate for treating pulmonary
inflammation. However, the clinical application of CEP is currently

A wide range of carrier polymers is available for biodegradable mi­
crospheres, including both natural and synthetic types. Most natural
polymers are derived from plants, animals, or microorganisms,
including polysaccharides (Xu et al.,2022, Balde et al., 2022, Liu et al.,
2022, Matalqah et al., 2020), gelatin (Takei et al., 2020, Zhai et al.,
2023), alginate (Severino et al., 2019, Swastha et al., 2023, Ibrahim
et al., 2022), etc. Chitosan (CS), a naturally occurring linear poly­
saccharide, is the second most abundant renewable biomaterial after
cellulose (Elgadir et al., 2015). Due to its non-toxicity, biodegradability,
biocompatibility, immune-enhancing properties, and anti-tumor, anti­
bacterial, and antimicrobial activities, chitosan is widely utilized in
biological and medical applications (Kravanja et al., 2019). PLGA, a
synthetic polymer, is widely utilized as a drug delivery system due to its
excellent biocompatibility and biodegradability (Su et al., 2021, Zhang
et al., 2022). Its safety profile is well-established and has been recog­
nized by both the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and the
European Medicines Agency (Gu et al., 2022). Additionally, PLGA is
officially classified as a pharmaceutical excipient by the FDA (Tao et al.,
2022).
In this study, PLGA was utilized to form the nanoparticle core, which
was subsequently coated with chitosan to enhance antibacterial activity
and stability. A cepharanthine-chitosan PLGA nanoemulsion (CCPN)
was constructed by encapsulating CEP into nanoparticles using a double-
emulsifying method. The stability and safety of CCPN were evaluated
through physicochemical characterization. The antibacterial efficacy
was assessed using in vivo and in vitro antibacterial assays.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Reagents and materials
Cepharanthine was acquired from Macklin Co., Ltd. (Shanghai,
China). Poly(lactic-co-glycolic) acid (50:50, 0.7–2 Mw, Carboxyl ter­
minal) was procured from the Shandong Academy of Pharmaceutical
Sciences. Chitosan (20–100 mPa⋅s) was acquired from TCI Development
Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). Acetone and ethanol were provided by
Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China), and Polox­
amer 188 (F68) came from Shanghai Yuanye Biotechnology Co., Ltd.
Horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-conjugated secondary antibody was
purchased from Wuhan Servicebio Technology Co., Ltd.
2.2. Animals
Five-week-old healthy BALB/c mice were acquired from the Animal
Care and Use Committee of Yangzhou University (Yangzhou, China).
The mice were housed in a controlled setting at 25℃ with 12 h of light
exposure and 45 % humidity. BALB/c mice were randomly divided into
6 groups with 16 mice in each group (8 mice in model group), which
were control group, Pneumonia model group, CEP treatment group,
CCPN (5, 10 mg/kg) treatment group and dexamethasone treatment
group. The ethical standards for experimental animal welfare must be
rigorously followed throughout the animal testing process.
2.3. Preparation of CCPN
PLGA (100 mg) was dissolved in 2 mL acetone, and 280 mg CEP was
added as the oil phase. In this procedure, 200 μL of deionized water was
added into the oil phase to achieve an internal water phase-to-oil phase
ratio of 1:9. Subsequently, a stable oil-in-water (O/W) emulsion was
formed by subjecting the mixture to ultrasonication at a power setting of
90 W, with a 2-second pulse interval, for a total duration of 4 min.
Concurrently, a 1 % acetic acid solution was prepared by diluting 1 mL
of acetic acid in 100 mL of deionized water, which was then used to
dissolve chitosan to a final concentration of 2 mg/mL. Poloxamer 188
(0.9 g) was added to the chitosan solution, fully dissolved, and used as
the outer water phase. The PE was then combined with the outer water
phase (outer water phase-to-oil phase ratio of 13:1) to produce a stable
water-in-oil-in-water (W1/O/W2) emulsion via ultrasonication at 120
W with 2-second intervals for 4 min. The emulsion was stirred
magnetically for 12 h to remove organic solvents and stored at 4 ◦C.
Blank chitosan PLGA nanoemulsion (BCPN) was prepared using the
same procedure without CEP.
2.4. Characterization of CCPN
2.4.1. Encapsulation efficiency
High performance liquid chromatography was used to determine
CEP encapsulation efficiency. The chromatography was performed on
DionexTM CarboPacTM PA-20 column (150 mm × 3.0 mm, 10 µm).
Chromatographic conditions: methanol: Water: triethylamine (80: 20:
0.05) was the mobile phase. The detection wavelength was 283 nm and
column temperature were 30℃. Volume flow rate: 1.0 mL/min; Injec­
tion volume: 10 μL.
2.4.2. Measurement of droplet size, zeta potential and polydispersity index
BCPN and CCPN were diluted 10-fold in PBS, and their particle size,
polydispersity index (PDI), and zeta potential were measured using a
nanoscale particle size analyzer.
2.4.3. Morphology study
CCPN and BCPN were diluted 10 times with PBS and placed on
copper net. After drying, appropriate amount of tungsten 1 % phosphate
was added to negative stain for 5 min, and then dried at room temper­
ature. CEP was dissolved with ethanol alone to make its final concen­
tration 2 mg/mL. The ultrastructure was then observed and
photographed under a transmission electron microscope (TEM).
2.4.4. Atomic force microscope (AFM)
The CCPN was dripped onto the cell crawl sheet and dried with a hair
dryer, then observed under an atomic force microscope.
2.4.5. Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR) and differential
scanning calorimetry (DSC) analysis
FT-IR spectra of PLGA, chitosan, CEP, BCPN, and CCPN were
recorded using a Thermo Electron Corporation spectrometer in the
4000–500 cm−1 range. DSC was performed to evaluate thermal stability.
2.4.6. Stability assessments
The newly prepared BCPN and CCPN were divided into glass bottles
at 4℃ and room temperature, respectively. Particle size, PDI and anti­
bacterial activity were measured at 0, 7, 14, 28 and 49 d.
2.5. Minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) and minimum bactericidal
concentration (MBC)
MIC and MBC were determined following the Clinical and Labora­
tory Standards Institute (CLSI, M100, 2019) guidelines. The bacterial
strains, including S. aureus ATCC29213 and MRSA, were obtained from
the American Type Culture Collection. To explore the potential targets
of CCPN, the MICs of CCPN against S. aureus ATCC29213 in the presence
of bacterial components were measured. The MIC of clinical isolates was
determined by 96-well plate micro-broth dilution method after cultured
at 37℃ for 16–18 h. The growth of the bacteria was observed after
continuous culture for 48 h, and the bacteria were observed by coating
the plate. The lowest drug concentration contained in the sterile growth

2.6. Growth curve
The S. aureus ATCC29213 strain was cultured in Mueller-Hinton
Broth (MHB) to achieve a bacterial concentration of approximately
106 CFU/mL. Cultures were treated with CCPN at varying concentra­
tions (1/4 MIC, 1/2 MIC, 1 MIC, 2 MIC, and 4 MIC) and incubated at
37 ◦C on a shaker at 120 rpm for 24 h. Bacterial growth was monitored
spectrophotometrically by measuring the optical density (OD) at 600 nm
at regular intervals.
2.7. Time-dependent killing curves
Time-kill assays were conducted using S. aureus ATCC29213 cultured
in a constant-temperature shaking incubator. Samples (20 μL) were
taken at specific time points (0, 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 15, 20, 30, and 45 min),
serially diluted 10-fold, and plated on Mueller-Hinton Agar (MHA).
Colony-forming units (CFU) were counted after incubation to determine
bacterial survival over time.
2.8. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM)
The S. aureus ATCC29213 strain was cultured in MHB broth obtain
approximately 106 colony-forming units (CFU)/mL, then treated with 1
× MIC CCPN in a shaking table for 4 h. Then the ultrastructural changes
of S.aureus ATCC29213 were observed by TEM.
2.9. Hemolytic analysis
The S. aureus ATCC29213 strain was cultured to 1 × 106 CFU/mL,
and CCPN was added to the bacterial solution with final concentrations
of 1/4MIC, 1/2MIC and MIC, respectively. Then the bacterial solution
was placed in a shaking table at 37℃ culturing for 4 h. 100 μL bacterial
supernatant was added to sterile PBS containing 25 μL defibrillated
rabbit blood in a total system of 1 mL, and the system was incubated at
37℃ for 15 min. The mixed system was centrifuged at 10,000 × g for 2
min, and the hemolytic activity of each sample was determined by
detecting the absorbance value of the supernatant at 543 nm. DMSO
treated samples and 1 % Triton-X 100 treated samples were used as
negative controls (0 % hemolysis) and positive controls (100 %
hemolysis).
2.10. Membrane function assay
To investigate effects of CCPN on membrane permeability, a PI-probe
was used. Overnight cultures of S. aureus were exposed to varying
concentrations of CCPN for 4 h. Following three washes with PBS via
centrifugation, the bacteria were incubated with 5 μL of 1 mg/mL PI
solution at 37 ◦C for 15 min. After washing and resuspending the stained
bacterial cells, they were fixed between slides and coverslips. Fluores­
cence was measured using a spectrofluorometer with excitation and
emission wavelengths of 535 nm and 617 nm, respectively. The fluo­
rescence intensity was analyzed using ImageJ software.
2.11. Biofilm formation measurement
Biofilm formation was measured by Crystal violet (CV) method.
Bacterial suspension with an initial concentration of 1 × 106 CFU/mL
was added to each well of the 96-well plate, and then different con­
centrations of CCPN was added respectively. Untreated group served as
positive control., After incubation for 24 h, washing with PBS for 3
times, the cells were fixed with 100 μL 10 % methanol solution per well
for 30 s. After sucking the methanol solution, add 100 μL of 10 % crystal
violet dye and leave it at room temperature for 20 min. Gently shake off
the dyeing solution, wash each hole with distilled water, and dry in the
oven at 37℃. Before the determination, 100 μL 33 % acetic acid was
added to each well for decolorization, and the light absorption was
determined at 570 nm after full oscillation. The number of live bacteria
in biofilm was calculated by plate count method. For the Atomic force
microscope imaging, the bacteria were cultured on the cell slipper for 3
days to form a biofilm, they were treated with different concentrations
of CCPN for 4 h, then dried and observed under atomic force microscope.
The height and roughness of samples were analyzed by software. At the
same time, the bacterial biofilm was observed by scanning electron
microscope after fixation, cleaning and coating.
2.12. In vivo safety evaluation
Mice were randomly assigned to six groups, with equal numbers of
males and females in each group. Following a three-day acclimation
period, the groups were administered CCPN at doses of 40 mg/kg, 20
mg/kg, 10 mg/kg, 5 mg/kg, and 2.5 mg/kg, or normal saline (control
group) via injection. Observations were recorded daily for 14 days,
including behavior, body weight, water intake, and mortality. After the
observation period, organ and intestinal tissues were collected for organ
index calculation and histopathological examination to evaluate po­
tential toxicity.
2.13. Mouse model of S. Aureus-induced pneumonia
Single colonies of S aureus were selected and inoculated into 10 mL
fresh MH broth medium overnight, and then cultured by oscillating at
37℃ and 150 r/min for 12 h (1 × 109 CFU/mL), centrifugally collected
the bacteria, washed 3 times with PBS, and finally re-suspended in 5 ml
PBS (2 × 109 CFU/mL). Each mouse was inoculated intranasally with 20
μL bacterial suspension after anesthesia. After post-infection, the mice
were intraperitoneal injected with CEP, CCPN (5 and 10 mg/kg) and
dexamethasone. After 7 days of observation, lung tissues were harvested
to detect the organ index, bacterial load, pathological change and wet
weight ratio. Blood samples were harvested to detect the red blood cell
count (RBC), white blood cell count (WBC), and neutrophil percentage
(Gran).
2.14. White blood cell count and protein content in BALF
BALF was centrifuged at 600 r/min for 5 min, and the precipitated
cells were suspended in 2 mL PBS, and the cells were counted using a cell
counting plate. In addition, the supernatant was collected, and the
protein concentration was detected using a kit.
2.15. Oxidative stress index detection
The content of myeloperoxidase (MPO) in serum was measured ac­
cording to the instructions provided in the commercial assay kit. The
results were used to assess oxidative stress levels in the study groups.
2.16. Immunohistochemistry
For immunofluorescence staining of CD177, lung tissue sections
were subjected to xylene-based dewaxing and rehydration through a
gradient ethanol series. Antigen retrieval was performed using citrate
buffer in a microwave oven at high temperature. After cooling, sections
were blocked with 10 % goat serum at room temperature for 30 min. The
samples were incubated with a primary antibody against the neutrophil
marker CD177 (dilution 1:200) at 37 ◦C for 1 h, followed by PBS
washing. Nuclei were counterstained with DAPI for 2 min. Stained
sections were observed and imaged under a fluorescence microscope at
200 × magnification.

3.2. CCPN displayed good bactericidal activity against S.aureus
CCPN had significant bacteriostatic effects, with a minimum inhib­
itory concentration (MIC) of 156.3 μg/mL, which was 4 times higher
than that of CEP (625.0 μg/mL). CCPN had a significant inhibitory effect
on bacterial growth, showing significant antibacterial effects (Fig. 3A).
Moreover, CCPN at 4MIC concentration could completely kill the bac­
teria within 10 min, while CCPN at 2MIC concentration required 30 min
to achieve complete eradication (Fig. 3B). The results indicated that
CCPN had significant bactericidal effects.
The hemolytic activity of S. aureus was notably reduced by CCPN in a
dose-dependent manner during the experiment. At a concentration of 1/
2MIC, CCPN substantially suppressed the hemolytic function of
S. aureus. with an inhibition rate reaching extremely significant level (P
< 0.001) (Fig. 3C, D).
3.3. CCPN interfered bacterial membranes and inhibited S. Aureus
biofilm formation
Previous studies suggested that bactericidal agents with rapid effects
may alter membrane permeability. To verify this, we employed TEM
analysis to observe the morphological changes in S. aureus treated with
CCPN. Compared with DMSO treated S. aureus, CCPN-treated S. aureus
exhibited damage to the cell wall and cell membrane, with the loss of
intracellular substances, contraction of the cytoplasm, and obvious
separation of the cytoplasm from the cell wall (Fig. 4A). In addition, the
fluorescence intensity of PI can reflect the degree of IM damage, as PI
cannot penetrate an intact IM. Compared with the control group, the
fluorescence intensity of PI in bacterial cells was increased under CCPN
treatment (Fig. 4B). This suggested that CCPN could damage the inner
membranes of S. aureus, which was consistent with the results of
transmission electron microscopy (Fig. 4C). The effect of CCPN on
S. aureus biofilm was evaluated by crystal violet method. As shown in
Fig. 4D, E, the biofilm clearance rate was significantly increased after 4 h
treatment with CCPN. When the concentration of CCPN was 1/4MIC, the
biofilm clearance rate was 28.82 %. When the drug concentration of
CCPN was increased to 1/2MIC and MIC, the biofilm clearance rates
were increased to 34.33 % and 43.46 % (p < 0.001), respectively. At low
magnification, it was observed that CCPN could significantly reduce the
number of bacteria on the surface of biofilm in a dose-dependent
manner. Under high magnification, CCPN was seen to damage the
bacterial structure, causing the plasma membrane to break apart and
revealing the cell’s contents. The surface morphology of S. aureus bio­
film was also observed to change with CCPN by AFM, and the height and
roughness were increased (Fig. 4G, H, I).

3.5. CCPN alleviated symptoms caused by S.aureus
The protective effect of CCPN was further evaluated in a mouse
model of S.aureus-induced SAP. This bacterial pneumonia model was
established by intranasally inoculating mice with S. aureus and allowing
the infection to progress for one week. In this model, the lungs undergo
recruitment of inflammatory cells, production of inflammatory cyto­
kines, and an increase in protein permeability, ultimately manifesting as
pulmonary edema, hemorrhage, and significant damage to the alveolar
structure. After seven days, the model group mice showed a significant
decrease in body weight (Fig. 6A) compared to the control group and
developed symptoms including decreased appetite, reduced activity,
and coughing. However, these symptoms were alleviated to varying
degrees in mice treated with free CEP, CCPN or DEX, and the weight
gradually returned to the normal level. The wet weight ratio of lung
tissue (Fig. 6B) and calculation of organ index (Fig. 6C-G) also showed
that S. aureus could cause damages such as pulmonary edema in mice.
The histopathological examination of the lungs also revealed significant
destruction of the alveolar structure in the model group, accompanied
by extensive neutrophil infiltration. After treatment with CCPN, the
alveolar structure was preserved (Fig. 6H), with minimal inflammatory
cell infiltration, suggesting that CCPN exerts a significant therapeutic
effect on SAP.
3.6. CCPN suppressed bacterial load of S. Aureus and modulated the host
immune response
Compared with the normal group, the leukocyte ratio in blood, the
number of cells in BALF, the protein concentrations, and the levels of
inflammatory cytokines IL-1, IL-6, TNF-a, and IL-10 in lung tissue were
significantly increased in the model group (Fig. 7A-G). The severity of
these parameters was reduced to varying degrees in mice treated with
free CEP, CCPN, or DEX. The presence of neutrophils in lung tissue
across the six groups was evaluated using the CD177 marker. The
findings revealed a marked rise in CD177-positive cells in the lung tissue
of the model group when compared to the control group. However, the
number of CD177-positive cells in lung tissue of mice in the CCPN
treatment group was significantly reduced compared with the model
group, but still higher than the control group (Fig. 7H), indicating that
the neutrophil recruitment in lung tissue of mice in the treatment group
was inhibited to some extent. Moreover, after 1 day of CCPN treatment,
significantly reducing the S.aureus load in the lungs (P < 0.01), and
S. aureus was basically undetectable after 7 days (Fig. 7I, J). The results
suggested that CCPN might alleviate lung tissue injury induced by
S. aureus, likely via its antibacterial and anti-inflammatory properties.

4. Discussion
In this study, we developed a drug delivery system by encapsulating
CEP within PLGA-chitosan nanoparticles, forming CCPN, and evaluated
its antibacterial effects against SAP. In vitro, CCPN inhibited bacterial
cell membrane formation and biofilm development. In vivo, CCPN
reduced the pathological changes of lung tissue in a mouse model of SAP
and down-regulated the levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines. These
findings indicate that CCPN has the potential to be a valuable thera­
peutic option for treating SAP.

new antibiotics agents, many of which exhibit significant antibacterial
and anti-inflammation properties. CEP, as an effective agent against
pulmonary inflammation, has garnered substantial attention. However,
its low bioavailability and poor solubility have hindered its clinical

bacteria depends on a variety of factors, such as electrostatic attraction,
hydrophobicity, receptor-ligand interactions, and van der Waals forces
(Wang et al., 2017). Basic research on the interactions between nano­
materials and bacteria provides important insights into the design of
novel antimicrobial agents. We encapsulated CEP in PLGA chitosan
nanoparticles to enhance its antibacterial activity and anti-
inflammatory effect. The stability and solubility of CEP are signifi­
cantly improved after being wrapped, while the particle size is also
reduced. The zeta potential of nanoparticles plays a critical role in
enhancing their antibacterial efficacy. Nanoparticles are typically posi­
tively charged, while bacterial cell membranes are usually negatively
charged. his charge differential facilitates strong electrostatic attraction,
enabling nanoparticles to adsorb onto bacterial surfaces and establish
tight binding(Pan et al., 2013). The particle size of CEP decreases and
the potential becomes positive after encapsulation, which may be the
reason for enhancing its antibacterial effect.
In the present study, we found that CCPN exerted inhibitory effects
on S. aureus by reducing bacterial growth rate, disrupting cell mem­
brane, inhibiting hemolytic activity, and slowing biofilm formation.
S. aureus codes α-, β-, γ- and δ-hemolysin, among which the best studied
staphylococcus aureus virulence factor is α-hemolysin encoded by Hla
gene, which can cause damage to a variety of host cells, including
epithelial cells, red blood cells and monocytes, and lead to cell mem­
brane damage and apoptosis (Ahmad-Mansour et al., 2021). The ability
of S. aureus to construct biofilms is closely related to the severity of
infection. By forming biofilms, the bacteria enhanced their pathoge­
nicity, confer protection and subsequently develop drug resistance
(Alarjani and Skalicky, 2021). These mechanisms significantly
contribute to the bacterium’s ability to evade host defenses and resist
antimicrobial treatments. Based on these findings, CCPN shows poten­
tial as an effective anti-S. aureus formulation. However, further studies
are required to fully elucidate its inhibitory mechanisms.
In vivo, we investigated the protective effect of CCPN on SAP induced
by S. aureus in a murine model. S. aureus can recruit and activate neu­
trophils at the site of infection (Gough et al., 2017). T cells mediate IL-17
responses, which further promote neutrophil recruitment, the release of
pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1α, IL-1β, TNF-α), and host defense
peptides. However, rapid neutrophil recruitment can exacerbate
inflammation and enhance S. aureus colonization, particularly on the
skin (Marchitto et al., 2019). Our results demonstrated that CCPN
significantly alleviated lung pathology, reduced the release of TNF-α, IL-
1β, and IL-6, and inhibited MPO activity. The instillation of pro-
inflammatory cytokines such as TNF-α or IL-1β into the lungs is
known to induce acute inflammatory injury （Meduri, 2002）. In this
study, CCPN significantly suppressed the production of TNF-α, IL-1β and
IL-6. These results suggested that the protective effect of CCPN on SAP
mice may be attributed to inhibition of inflammatory factors. MPO, the
most abundant granulocyte enzyme, is commonly used as a marker for
neutrophil accumulation in lung tissue (Sun et al., 2009). We know from
the results of the present study that CCPN can significantly reduce the
activity of MPO. The degree of neutrophil infiltration in mouse lung
tissue was detected by neutrophil marker CD177. In addition, the
number of cells in BALF of mice was significantly decreased in CCPN-
treated mice with S. aureus pneumonia. Collectively, these findings
suggest that CCPN effectively mitigates excessive inflammatory immune
responses, highlighting its potential as a therapeutic agent for SAP.
5. Conclusion
In conclusion, our study introduced a nanoemulsion co-constructed
using PLGA and CS, characterized by uniform particle size and high
stability, which effectively enhanced drug solubility. Furthermore, the
CCPN nanoemulsion exhibited remarkable antibacterial activity in vitro
and significant therapeutic efficacy against SAP in vivo. These findings
suggest that this nanoparticle system holds considerable potential for
developing advanced drug delivery systems targeting bacterial inflam­
mation and may be broadly applicable for the treatment of CEP. Further
research is needed to explore the long-term effects of CCPN in clinical
settings and the mechanisms by which they work.
