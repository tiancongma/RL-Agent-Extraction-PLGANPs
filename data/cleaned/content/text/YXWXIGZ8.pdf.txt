Abstract
Nanoparticle-based antigen carrier systems have become a significant area of research with the
advancement of nanotechnology. Biodegradable polymers have emerged as particularly
promising carrier vehicles due to their ability to address the limitations of existing vaccine
systems. In this study, we successfully encapsulated the G5-24 linear peptide, located between
amino acids 253 and 275 in the primary sequence of the rabies virus G protein, into
biodegradable and biocompatible PLGA copolymer using the double emulsion solvent
evaporation method. The resulting nanoparticles had a size of approximately
230.9 ± 0.9074 nm, with a PDI value of 0.168 ± 0.017 and a zeta potential value of
−9.86 ± 0.132 mV. SEM images confirmed that the synthesized nanoparticles were uniform in
size and distribution. Additionally, FTIR spectra indicated successful peptide loading into the
nanoparticles. The encapsulation efficiency of the peptide-loaded nanoparticles was 73.3%,
with a peptide loading capacity of 48.2% and a reaction yield of 30.4%. Peptide release studies
demonstrated that 65.55% of the peptide was released in a controlled manner over 28 d,
following a ‘biphasic burst release’ profile consistent with the degradation profile of PLGA.
This controlled release is particularly beneficial for vaccine studies. Cytotoxicity tests revealed
that the R-NP formulation did not induce cytotoxicity in fibroblast cells and enhanced NO
production in macrophages, indicating its potential for vaccine development.
Keywords: rabies, peptide delivery, PLGA nanoparticles, vaccine, nitric oxide

1. Introduction
Rabies, a viral central nervous system (CNS) infection, is
caused by the rabies virus (RABV), which belongs to the genus
Lyssavirus and the family Rhabdoviridae. This disease leads to
approximately 60 000 human deaths annually worldwide [1].
The primary source of RABV transmission is through the bites
of rabid animals, especially dogs. Contagious saliva from rabid
dogs accounts for 98% of transmission cases [2, 3].
RABV infection is difficult to diagnose for most of its
lifespan, and symptoms of neuronal dysfunction usually
emerge at the end of the illness [4]. Rabies can occur in
two distinct forms. The majority of those who contract rabies
develop the classic (furious) form, while a smaller proportion
develop the paralytic form. In the classic form, symptoms such
as hydrophobia and aerophobia are common, and the disease
progresses to coma, whereas the paralytic form begins with
muscle weakness and eventually advances to paralysis [5, 6].
Once clinical symptoms manifest, the outcome is mostly fatal.
However, human vaccinations can be used before and after
exposure to prevent/treat the RABV [7].
Five viral proteins are encoded by the RABV genome,
which is a single-stranded, (−) polarity RNA of about 12 kb
in size [8]. RABV’s ribonucleoprotein (RNP) core is made up
of N (nucleoprotein), P (phosphoprotein), and L (polymerase)
proteins, along with viral RNA. The lipid membrane is com-
posed of the G (glycoprotein) and M (matrix) proteins [9, 10].
G protein is a trimeric transmembrane protein with 524 amino
acids and three domains: ectodomain, transmembrane, and
cytoplasmic domain. The attachment of the virus to the cells
[11], spread in the CNS, and activation of both cellular and
humoral immune responses [12] are all dependent on this pro-
tein. Furthermore, the generation of a potent virus-neutralizing
antibody (VNA) is solely stimulated by this protein [13]. It
has been used extensively in vaccine development [14–16].
Studies utilizing monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) have revealed
that virus-neutralizing mAbs do not bind to epitopes on the G
protein when it is denatured. However, contrary to this rule,
the G5 antigenic region is a linear, non-conformational epitope
that is recognized by mAbs [17]. This epitope is situated in a
conserved region, indicating that peptides designed to target it
could promote VNA production and aid in the development of
synthetic peptide vaccines against RABV. G5-24, a 24-amino
acid peptide sequence (PPDQLVNLHDFRSDEIEHLVVEE)
discovered by Dietzschold et al. (1990) based on the G5 anti-
genic region, was used for this present study. This peptide is
known to exhibit immunogenic properties and induces VNA
production [18, 19].
Vaccines made from the entire organism are known for gen-
erating strong humoral and cellular immune responses, but
their high cost of production and notable side effects have
led to the emergence of subunit vaccines [20]. These vaccines
are comprised of antigenic elements of the infectious agent.
One such type, synthetic peptide vaccines, consists of frag-
ments of proteins that are recognized by the immune system
[21, 22]. Produced through chemical synthesis or biological
means, these peptides offer numerous benefits, including high
standardization [23], cost-effectiveness, enhanced stability
[24], and lack of virulence [22]. Peptide vaccines offer numer-
ous applications, including allergy treatment [25], virus infec-
tion prevention [26], cancer therapy [27], and Alzheimer’s
disease [28] management. Yet, due to their low immunogen-
icity, adjuvants, or carrier systems (such as liposomal for-
mulations, polymeric or inorganic particles [29]) must be
utilized to enhance their properties. Nanoparticulate drug
delivery systems serve as a protective mechanism to prevent
molecule degradation and promote increased bioavailability
and immune response [30].
Poly (lactic acid-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) is a biodegrad-
able polymer ester made up of lactic and glycolic acid. It
has been widely adopted as a vaccine delivery system due
to its ability to break down into its monomers in the biolo-
gical system [31]. Its safety as a pharmaceutical excipient has
been approved by the Food and Drug Administration [32].
Encapsulating peptide fragments demonstrates a self-adjuvant
feature that prompts the immune system and releases active
substances in a controlled manner. Additionally, the PLGA
nano/micro particle systems extend the plasma half-life of
peptides and provide passive targeting to cells by regulating
parameters such as size and surface charge [33]. Modifying the
monomer ratios in its structure allows for the control of proper-
ties like hydrophilicity, degradation rate, and crystallinity [34].
A study conducted by Clawson et al investigated the effect of
PLGA as a carrier system. They observed that encapsulating
an immunostimulatory peptide, Hp91, in PLGA nanoparticles
increased its capacity to stimulate dendritic cells when com-
pared to the free peptide [35].
The present research explores the loading of G5-24 pep-
tide onto PLGA nanoparticles, which can specifically bind to
the G5 antigenic region of the RABV G protein. This study
is the first investigation into the physical and chemical prop-
erties of the resulting nanoparticles, as well as their biocom-
patibility. The encapsulation of the G5-24 peptide into PLGA
nanoparticles was achieved using the double emulsion solvent
evaporation (w/o/w) method. Morphological characterization
of the nanoparticles was performed using field emission scan-
ning electron microscopy (FESEM), while functional group
analysis was carried out using Fourier transform infrared
(FTIR) spectroscopy. Peptide release was monitored over time
using fluorescence spectroscopy. The study also assessed the
formulation’s cytotoxicity and its ability to induce NO produc-
tion in vitro.
This innovative nanoparticle-based vaccine approach holds
significant potential for improving rabies prophylaxis, espe-
cially in regions with limited access to conventional vaccines.
Additionally, the findings from this study could be instru-
mental in the development of nanoparticle-based delivery sys-
tems for other peptide vaccines, enhancing the effectiveness
and stability of immunization strategies. Future benefits of
this research include the development of more efficient and
cost-effective vaccine formulations, which could be cru-
cial for large-scale immunization programs. Moreover, the

2. Materials and methods
2.1. Materials
The G5-24 peptide (H2N-WPPDQLVNLHDFRSDEIEHL
VVEE-COOH) [18] of RABV was synthesized in our laborat-
ory using the solid phase peptide synthesis method, as previ-
ously described in the literature, with good process yield and
purity (%76 w/w and >90 w/w, respectively) [36, 37]. Due to
its fluorescent property, the tryptophan (Trp-W) amino acid
was added to the N-terminal to modification of the G5-24 syn-
thetic peptide. PLGA (Poly(D, L-lactide-co-glycolide) lactide:
glycolide 50:50, Mw 38 000–54 000), poly(vinyl alcohol)
(PVA), dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO), Griess reagent (N-(1-
Naphthyl)ethylenediamine dihydrochloride), sulfanilamide,
Trypsin-EDTA (0.25%) was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich
(St. Louis, USA). Dichloromethane (DCM), ethanol, sodium
chloride, and hydrochloric acid were purchased from Riedel-
De-Ha¨en (Charlotte, USA). Sodium phosphate dibasic hep-
tahydrate, sodium dihydrogen phosphate dihydrate, and
sodium hydroxide were purchased from Fluka (Charlotte,
USA). Fibroblast cells (L929), macrophage cells (RAW
264.7), Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM/F12),
FBS (Fetal Bovine Serum), penicillin-streptomycin were
acquired from Capricorn. MTT [3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-
2yl)-2,5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide] was purchased from
Biomatik. Ultra-pure water was acquired from the Millipore
Milli Q Gradient system.
2.2. Polymeric nanoparticles synthesis
RABV antigenic peptide (G5-24) loaded PLGA nanoparticles
were prepared by the double emulsion solvent evaporation
method as described previously with minor modifications [38]
and this process is schematically illustrated in figure 1(a).
To form the first emulsion (w/o), 5 mg of antigenic peptide
was dissolved in 1 ml of ultrapure water (aqueous phase)
and dropped into 3 ml of DCM containing 20 mg of PLGA
(organic phase) on an ice bath under sonication (80 W amp-
litude, 1.5 min) using a probe sonicator (Bandelin Sonopuls,
Germany). Then, the first emulsion was added dropwise to the
aqueous phase of 4 ml PVA (3% w/v) under the same condi-
tions to form the double emulsion (w/o/w). The solution was
left to stir overnight at room temperature for evaporation of
the organic phase. The nanoparticles obtained after evapora-
tion (R-NPs) were centrifuged at 9000 rpm for 20 min, washed
three times with ultrapure water, and the pellet was lyophil-
ized while the supernatant was separated for further analysis.
Empty PLGA nanoparticles (E-NPs) without peptides were
also produced using the same methods and ratios, serving as a
control.
2.3. Characterization of nanoparticles
2.3.1. Size, polydispersity index (PDI) and zeta potential.
The particle size, PDI, and zeta potential of nanoparticles were
measured using Zetasizer Nano ZS (Malvern Instruments,
Worcestershire, UK) device, with dynamic light scattering
(DLS) and electrophoretic light scattering (ELS) methods.
Measurements were performed three times at 25 ◦C and repor-
ted as the mean of triplicates. All samples were diluted with
pure water (1:30 v/v) before each measurement [39].
2.3.2. FTIR spectroscopy.
FTIR spectra of RABV G5-24
peptide, PLGA, E-NPs and R-NPs were collected using the
ATR mode of the spectrophotometer (Shimadzu, Japan) to
study the surface chemistry of nanoparticles. The spectral ana-
lysis was conducted within the 600–4000 cm−1 range, with a
resolution of 4 cm−1 [40].
2.3.3. FESEM.
The surface morphology, shape, and size
uniformity of the prepared empty and RABV G5-24 peptide-
loaded PLGA nanoparticles were confirmed using field-
emission FE-SEM conducted with a Thermo Scientific Apreo
2 S instrument located in Waltham, MA, USA. During the ima-
ging procedure, 1 mg of lyophilized nanoparticles was posi-
tioned on a double-sided black carbon tape. FE-SEM images
were acquired employing an in-lens detector at different mag-
nifications (100 and 50 kx) and an electron voltage (1.00 kV
and 500.00 V) [41].
2.4. Encapsulation efficiency (EE), loading capacity (LC) and
nanoparticles yield (NPY)
EE and peptide LC were calculated by measuring the free G5-
24 peptide remaining in the supernatant after centrifugation.
The amount of free peptide was calculated from a calibration
curve prepared using the maximum fluorescence emission of
the peptide when the solution was excited at 280 nm [42].

2.6. Cell culture studies
L929 fibroblast cells were used for cytotoxicity testing in R-
NP and RABV groups, and RAW 264.7 macrophage cells
were used for nitric oxide analysis. Fibroblast cells were cul-
tured in DMEM/F12 medium containing 10% FBS and 1%

nitric oxide analyses. Before being used in the analysis, R-
NP groups at concentrations of 0.025, 0.05, 0.1, 0.25, 0.5,
and 1 mg mL−1 and P group at concentrations of 6.125, 12.5,

group, were examined using L929 cells, while the nitric oxide

2.6.1. Cytotoxicity analysis.
L929 cells were seeded in 96-
well plates at a density of 10 000 cells per well. Plates were
incubated for 24 h, followed by the application of designated
concentrations of experimental groups to the cells. The control
group was replaced with fresh medium. Three replicates were
performed for each concentration. Plates were then incub-
ated for 72 h, and cell viability in the wells was analyzed.
MTT method was used to determine cell viability [49]. In this
method, tetrazolium in the MTT salt is reduced to formazan
by viable cells. The formazan intensity in the wells provides
information about cell viability [50]. For cell viability ana-
lysis, 10 µl of MTT solution prepared in PBS at a concen-
tration of 10 mg ml−1 was added to each well. Plates were
incubated in the dark for 4 h. Subsequently, 100 µl of DMSO
was added to dissolve the formazan in the wells, and the plates
were kept in the dark for 30 min. Formazan intensity in the
wells was measured at 570 nm using a microplate reader. The
standard deviation was calculated from the obtained absorb-
ance data. Additionally, cell viability (%) was calculated using

2.6.2. Nitric oxide analysis.
Nitric oxide (NO) plays an
important role in the immune system. The presence and level
of nitric oxide were used to determine the stimulatory effects
of R-NP and RABV groups on the immune system. NO level
was measured using Griess reagent [51]. RAW cells were used
in this analysis. Cells were seeded in 96-well plates at a dens-
ity of 10 000 cells per well. Plates were incubated for 24 h,
followed by the addition of samples at designated concentra-
tions to the wells. The control group was replaced with a fresh
medium. Three replicates were performed for each concentra-
tion. Plates were then incubated for 96 h. Afterwards, super-
natants from each concentration were transferred to new 96-
well plates in triplicates. Simultaneously, different concentra-
tions of nitrite solutions were added to some wells for calib-
ration curve. Subsequently, 50 µl of Griess reagent was added
to the supernatant and standard wells. Plates were incubated in
the dark for 10 min. After this incubation period, plates were
read at 540 nm using a microplate reader. A calibration curve
was first plotted using the obtained absorbance data. Then,
nitric oxide concentrations of experimental groups were cal-
culated using the equation from the graph equation.
2.7. Statistical analysis
All experiments were performed in triplicate, and the results
were expressed as mean ± standard deviation. The statistical
analysis of the in vitro release and cytotoxicity investigation
involved employing one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) to
compare the findings between the control and treated groups.
Statistically, significance was determined for values with a p-
value below 0.05.
3. Results and discussion
3.1. Characterization of nanoparticles
In this study, we employed the double emulsion solvent evap-
oration method to synthesize empty NPs (E-NP) and G5-24
peptide-loaded PLGA (R-NP) nanoparticles. This method is
widely favored for its ease of application and optimal results
in active substance encapsulation [52]. It is worth noting that
the size and PDI values of nano-sized drug carriers are critical
factors that influence cellular uptake. These values are also

The sizes of empty nanoparticles without peptides (E-NP)
and loaded nanoparticles containing G5-24 peptide (R-NP)
were measured as 196.6 ± 1.652 nm and 230.9 ± 0.907 nm,
respectively. This increase in the size of the loaded nano-
particle compared to the empty one indicates that encapsu-
lation has taken place [44, 55]. For vaccine delivery, nano-
particle size plays a critical role in modulating their immune
response. Microparticles ranging from 0,5–2 µm primarily
trigger humoral responses, while smaller nanoparticles than
500 nm, such as the ∼230 nm R-NP in this study, are
absorbed by dendritic cells, promoting the induction of cel-
lular immune responses. Also, it is crucial to consider the size
of PLGA particles, as it dramatically impacts their biodistri-
bution. Usually, smaller particles are considered to be more
effective for delivery as their size enables them to pass through
biological barriers more easily [33, 34, 56].
Both synthesized nanoparticles exhibited PDI values smal-
ler than 0.2, as shown in figure 1, indicating a monodisperse
particle distribution. This narrow particle size distribution is
advantageous for drug delivery systems, enhancing the treat-
ment effect [38]. Generally, a PDI value below 0.2 is con-
sidered highly monodisperse and suitable for such applications
[57, 58].
The stability of a nanoparticle in a colloidal solution is
closely tied to its surface charge, known as zeta potential.
In general, charged particles are less prone to aggregation
due to electrical repulsion [59]. The zeta potentials of both
empty and loaded nanoparticles were measured, with values
of −14.5 ± 0.473 and −9.86 ± 0.132, respectively (as seen in
figuress 1(d) and (e)). This negative charge is likely due to the
presence of terminal carboxyl groups in the chemical structure
of PLGA [60]. The decrease in zeta potential observed in the
R-NP formulation can indeed be attributed to the screening
effect of the encapsulated peptide. The peptide sequence used
in this study (PPDQLVNLHDFRSDEIEHLVVEE), being rel-
atively negatively charged (net charge at pH 7: approximately
−5.8, as calculated using the PepCalc tool), likely influences
the overall surface charge of the nanoparticles. Similar to find-
ings reported in the literature [61] interactions between pep-
tides and the carboxyl groups of PLGA can modulate the zeta
potential. Studies have shown that positively charged peptides
exhibit strong interactions with PLGA-COOH groups, result-
ing in higher EE and significant shifts in surface charge. In
contrast, negatively charged peptides, such as the one used in
this study, may have weaker interactions with the PLGA sur-
face, leading to a more neutralized zeta potential and account-
ing for the less negative charge observed in the R-NP com-
pared to the E-NP formulation.
Certain amino acids, such as tryptophan, tyrosine, and
phenylalanine, exhibit fluorescence. Among these, tryptophan
displays the highest degree of fluorescence [62]. Incorporating
tryptophan at the end of a peptide sequence during peptide
synthesis can enable the resulting peptide to be measured
through fluorescence spectroscopy. The EE and peptide LC
were assessed indirectly [63] using fluorescence spectroscopy

3.2. In vitro peptide release
The release of peptides from PLGA is primarily facilitated by
both diffusion and erosion. As water diffuses, PLGA under-
goes both surface and bulk erosion, leading to gradual hydro-
lysis and peptide release [69–71]. Hydrolytic degradation and

RNP over 4 weeks.
release rates of nanoparticles vary depending on many proper-
ties, such as lactide/glycolide ratio, polymer molecular weight,
particle size, porosity, and surfactants used. The release of the
active substance from nanoparticles typically follows mono-
phasic, bi-phasic, or tri-phasic release profiles [34, 72]. In
our study, the synthesized peptide-loaded nanoparticles were
suspended in pH 7.4 PBS to monitor peptide release over
time. The results showed that 58.23% of the peptide was
released within the first 24 h. This initial phase of rapid release,
commonly referred to as burst release, is often attributed to
peptides localized near the nanoparticle surface or adsorbed
weakly [33, 72]. An increased surface area for smaller nan-
oparticles can lead to a larger burst release due to a greater
quantity of active substance being present at or near the surface
[73]. Following this initial burst, peptide release continued
at a steady rate, resulting in a cumulative release of 65.55%
over 28 d (figure 4). This release pattern corresponds with
the biphasic profile often reported in the literature [74–76].
Such a release profile may prove advantageous in vaccine
applications, as it can promote consistent peptide presenta-
tion to antigen-presenting cells (APCs) and facilitate a reliable
immunization strategy [77].
3.3. Cytotoxicity and NO analysis
Cellular viability rates of L929 fibroblast cells in response to
stimulation with RABV peptide, E-NP and R-NP at different
concentrations were illustrated in figure 5. The results obvi-
ously reveal that E-NP and R-NP did not induce any cyto-
toxicity on L929 cells at investigated dosages. The increased
concentrations of R-NP lead to no meaningful reduction over
cellular survival levels of fibroblasts in contrast to untreated

huge opportunity for an efficient and reliable transmission of
antigenic molecules into targeted immune cells with a lack of

of RABV has been previously enlightened well, however the
knowledge in regard to decoration of PLGA nanoparticles
by encapsulation of RABV remains missing. Therefore, in
second step of biological investigations we explored in vitro
immunostimulatory performance of peptide loaded PLGA

quantity and the most significant immunostimulatory activ-

was applied, on contrary. These outputs verify that R-NP eli-

density could trigger macrophages to produce superior NO.

R-NP upon 96 h incubation. However, it should not be forgot-

in vitro release of immunogenic molecules within short-term
incubation periods and the amount of released antigens from
nanoparticles that interact with macrophages is likely lower
than free antigens. Hence, secretion of less nitric oxide from
macrophages in response to R-NP administration is an expec-
ted result. We predict that R-NP formulation would exhibit
more remarkable immunostimulatory effect if cells could have
exposed to peptide loaded nanoparticles for a long-term incub-

system. Similarly, it would be expected that this nanoplatform
would achieve higher immune response in biological systems.

secretion in macrophages at higher dosages, indicating non-
toxic property of peptide encapsulated nanosystems and its
possible uptake by macrophages that improved the deposited
antigen concentration inside. Besides, potent immunostimu-
latory efficacy of R-NP could be also explained by adjuvant
feature of PLGA polymer, described before [80]. In another
study, it was ascertained that free PLGA nanoparticles did not
merely induce nitric oxide production within macrophages,
but it excessively raised the immunostimulatory efficacy of
ESAT-6 peptide of M. Tuberculosis upon encapsulation, sug-
gesting the perfect adjuvant ability of PLGA-based antigen
delivery platforms [42].

The production of NO in macrophages and other APCs
is a substantial defense mechanism to combat with intra-
cellular microorganisms. Following to phagocytosis, phago-
somes containing internalized microbes fuse with lysosomes
and microbial agents encounter with reactive oxygen species,
hydrolytic enzymes and NO. It is well known that NO displays
a powerful antimicrobial impact and contributes to the degrad-
ation of microbial molecules such as proteins, nucleic acids
and lipids by binding them covalently [81, 82]. Accordingly,
external stimulation of macrophages with immunogenic anti-
gens could improve the resistance of the innate immune sys-
tem to invasion of target microbial agents. Furthermore, it was
recently discovered that NO released by innate immune cells
could advance the survivability and differentiation of T lymph-
ocytes. NO might guide the conversion of naive CD 4 T lymph-
ocytes into effector Th1 cells that can regulate the immune
attack on intracellular microorganisms by secreting special
cytokines such as IFN-gamma [83]. That’s why NO is thought
to have a pivotal role in the progression of innate and adaptive
immunity. Besides, enhancement in NO production of macro-
phages triggered by an external force could be considered as a
substantial hallmark for effective vaccine candidates that pro-
mote innate and adaptive immune response. Consequently, R-
NP with an elevated immunoactivity would exert strong poten-
tial for the development of vaccine candidates for intracellu-
lar microorganisms. But further investigations deserve to be
performed.
4. Conclusion
This study successfully demonstrated the encapsulation of
the G5-24 peptide into PLGA nanoparticles using the double
emulsion solvent evaporation method, yielding nanoparticles
with desirable physical and chemical properties. The morpho-
logical and functional analyses confirmed the effective load-
ing of the peptide within the nanoparticles. Furthermore, the
controlled peptide release and the biocompatibility assess-
ments, including cytotoxicity and NO production, highlighted
the potential of these nanoparticles as a robust delivery system.
These findings suggest that R-NPs could serve as a promising
candidate for rabies vaccine development. In this study, the
primary focus was on the formulation and characterization
of R-NP. Further studies, including potency assays to evalu-
ate VNA production, are planned to assess the full potential
of the nanoparticles for vaccine development. These future
investigations will also provide a comparison with unencap-
sulated peptides, offering deeper insights into their biological
activity. Additionally, the application of this approach to other
peptide-based vaccines should be explored.
