ABSTRACT
Periprosthetic joint infection (PJI) is a severe complication of total joint arthroplasty (TJA), leading to high rates of revision
surgeries, long‐term morbidity, and mortality. Conventional antibiotic treatments often suffer from limited bioavailability and
systemic toxicity. This study explores a novel approach using vancomycin‐loaded poly(lactic‐co‐glycolic) acid (PLGA) micro-
particles (VMP) formulated via a microfluidic double emulsion method for controlled, localized drug delivery for managing PJI.
The PLGA microparticles were synthesized to achieve high loading capacity and sustained vancomycin release, aiming to
maintain therapeutic intra‐articular concentrations. In vitro characterization demonstrated optimal loading capacity (up to 28%
w/w), morphology with a homogeneous particle size distribution (49–65 µm), and sustained release profiles over 8 weeks. In
vivo efficacy was evaluated using a rat joint infection model, showing significant reductions in bacterial viability and enhanced
bone healing compared to controls. Weight‐bearing recovery assessments showed that VMP‐treated rats regained functionality
significantly earlier than controls (p < 0.05). Radiographic, histological, and immunofluorescent analyses confirmed reduced
inflammation and improved bone integrity with VMP treatment. These findings suggest that microfluidic‐synthesized
PLGA microparticles provide a promising strategy for localized, controlled release of antibiotics, potentially helping the
management of PJI and improving postsurgical outcomes. Future research should explore the long‐term effects and
scalability of clinical applications. This study lays the foundation for advancing controlled release systems in orthopedic
postoperative care.

Introduction
Periprosthetic joint infection (PJI) can be a severe complication
of total joint arthroplasty (TJA) [1, 2]. The acute condition is
characterized by pain, swelling, and tenderness in the affected
area. Although PJI occurs in only 1%–2% of TJA cases performed
at tertiary healthcare facilities, it is more common in community
hospitals and is currently the leading cause of arthroplasty fail-
ure. With a recurrence rate of about 16%, the consequences of PJI
can be severe and may require multiple resection arthroplasties,
arthrodesis, or even amputation [3, 4]. The mortality rate is also
high, partly due to the need for multiple revision surgeries [1, 2].

The glycopeptide vancomycin hydrochloride (vancomycin) is
among the antibiotics that are commonly administered intra-
venously and locally to treat these infections, especially those
caused by methicillin‐resistant Staphylococcus aureus. Its bio-
availability at the infection site is limited, especially in bone
tissue, and increased dosing can lead to serious systemic tox-
icity, such as ototoxicity or nephrotoxicity [5]. Localized bone
cement applications of antibiotics reduce systemic toxicity.
However, the release of antibiotics from the spacers is often
insufficient to treat the infections effectively [6, 7]. Controlled
localized delivery of vancomycin from poly(lactic‐co‐glycolic)
acid (PLGA) microparticles encapsulating vancomycin could
positively impact infection treatment outcomes by increasing
the local drug concentration, improving the drug's pharmaco-
kinetics, and reducing toxicity [8]. PLGA, a co‐polymer of
human metabolites glycolic and lactic acid monomers, has been
in medical use for decades and demonstrated clinical success in
long‐acting therapies for oncology, ophthalmology, and pain
management. On the other hand, achieving the desired
microparticle loading capacity and release rates for hydrophilic
drugs is challenging due to PLGA's hydrophobic structure.
Several commercial grades of PLGA, with varying ratios of lactic to
glycolic acid monomers, molecular weights, and terminal groups,
allow tuning of biodegradability and extended drug release rates [9].
Many complex PLGA‐based injectables have been cleared by the
FDA to extend the release duration of therapeutics in different
indications [10]. For example, Zilretta (Pacira, FL, USA), a PLGA
microparticle formulation encapsulating the hydrophobic cortico-
steroid triamcinolone acetonide, releases its cargo for up to 6
months. The intra‐articular injection of Zilretta in arthritic patients
has been shown to provide sustained pain relief for 3–4 months
[11]. PLGA microparticles are typically formulated using emulsion
and solvent evaporation methods, which enable the encapsulation
of drugs in the polymeric matrix formed by using single and double
emulsion techniques. However, the conventional homogenization
method used in emulsion‐based microparticle synthesis has limi-
tations, such as poor drug loading capacity and nonhomogeneous
particle size distribution, particularly for hydrophilic drug molecules
[12]. In contrast, encapsulation using microfluidic devices offers
better control over particle size distribution, higher drug loading
capacity, and greater scalability [13].
In this study, we present a new vancomycin‐encapsulating
PLGA microparticle prepared with a microfluidics‐based double
emulsion method. We hypothesize that these microparticles can
maintain vancomycin release to achieve intra‐articular vanco-
mycin concentrations sufficient to prevent or treat PJI caused
by S. aureus.

Materials and Methods
Vancomycin hydrochloride was purchased from Hospira Inc.
(Lake Forest, IL). PLGA grades used were Resomer RG 503 H
(molecular weight [MW] = 24,000–38,000 g/mol) and Resomer
RG 504 (MW = 38,000‐54,000 g/mol) were obtained from Evonik
(Piscataway, NJ). Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA), Tween 20, and sodium
chloride were purchased from Sigma (St. Louis, MO). Purasorb
PDLG 5004 (MW = 43,000 g/mol), another PLGA grade used, was
obtained from Corbion (Gorinchem, Netherlands). Chloroform
and ethyl acetate were purchased from Fisher Scientific (Fair
Lawn, NJ). All chemicals used were of analytical grade.
2.1
|
Formulation of Microspheres
PLGA
microparticles
containing
vancomycin
hydrochloride
(VMP) were fabricated using the “dilution microfluidic cartridge”
(DMC) on the NanoAssemblr Ignite microfluidic platform (Pre-
cision NanoSystems, Vancouver, Canada). Respective syringes
were filled with the first water solution (W1), organic solution (O),
and second water solution (W2) and connected to the DMC
(Figure S1). Solutions with varying concentrations (Table S1) were
used to prepare PLGA microparticles. The emulsions formed in
the DMC were collected from the outlet in a 50 mL plastic tube
containing 5–10 mL of W2 solution. The contents of the plastic
tube were poured into a beaker and placed on a shaker under the
hood for 12–16 h at 250 rpm at ambient temperature to evaporate
ethyl acetate and water. The resulting dispersion was centrifuged
at 3000 rpm and washed with DI water three times to remove any
residual vancomycin. PLGA microparticles were then dispersed in
5 mL water and subjected to freezing at −80°C for 12 h. The fro-
zen pellets were lyophilized for 48 h on a BenchTop Pro Lyophi-
lizer (VirTis, SP Scientific, Warminster, PA) and stored at −20°C
until further characterization.
2.2
|
Particle Characterization
Particle morphology was investigated using optical and scan-
ning electron microscopy (SEM). An aliquot of 30 μL of VMP
suspended in water was placed on a glass slide for imaging
using an SZX12 Optical Microscope/DP11 Digital Camera from
Olympus Life Sciences (USA). An SEC‐SEM‐12 Zeiss Gemini
360 FE‐SEM SEC operated at 3 kV was used for SEM imaging.
VMP (50 μL) was fixed onto SEM stubs with double‐sided car-
bon adhesive tape and gold sputter‐coated (20 nm) and imaged.
Loading capacity and particle size distribution experiments
(for details, see the Supporting Information) were performed
with imaged particles. Selected formulations were tested for in
vitro release, and a pharmacokinetic model was applied to the
release result to predict the in vitro vancomycin concentrations
(for details, see Supporting Information).

|
In Vivo Efficacy in a Rat Joint Infection
Model
This study was approved by the Institutional Care and Use
Committee of Massachusetts General Hospital (Approval No.
2022N000013).
Twelve
adult
male
Sprague–Dawley
rats
(350–400 g) from Charles River Laboratories were used. Rats
were randomly divided into control (n = 6) and VMP (n = 6)
groups, with ad libitum access to chow and water [7, 14]. Fol-
lowing a 3‐day acclimation, baseline measurements were taken
preoperatively. Postoperative assessments, including weight
monitoring, X‐rays, blood collection, gait analysis, and static
weight bearing, were conducted on POD 1, 3, 7, 14, 21, and 28
(Figure S2). At Day 28, ex vivo bacterial culture, micro‐CT
imaging, and histology were performed.

microparticle suspension was administered into the knee joint
using a pipette (Figure 3b). The wound was closed with 5‐0 Vi-
cryl sutures. The control group did not receive treatment. Post-
operatively, buprenorphine was given every 12 h for 72 h.
Euthanasia was performed using pentobarbital (100 mg/kg IP),
and knee tissues, tibial screws, and PE plugs were collected
aseptically for bacterial count measurement.
2.4
|
Determination of Bacterial Viability
On postoperative Day 28, femoral and tibial tissues surrounding
the implants were harvested and homogenized in 1 mL sterile
PBS using a TissueRuptor II (QIAGEN, Germany) for 1–2 min.
The tissue lysate was serially diluted, plated on tryptic soy agar,
and incubated for 16–20 h at 35°C. Viable bacteria counts were
normalized to tissue weight. Implant materials (titanium screws
and polyethylene plugs) were also harvested, sonicated in 1 mL
PBS for 40 min, and the sonicate fluid was processed similarly.
The colony‐forming units (CFU) from both tissues and implants
were compared to the initial S. aureus inoculum (ATCC 12600;
108 CFU) to determine the effect of vancomycin eluting PLGA
particles. Greater than three log reduction in the observed
bacterial count was determined as eradication.
2.5
|
Microcomputed Tomography and X‐Ray
Microcomputed tomography (micro‐CT) was used to assess
bone mass and quality in the proximal tibia. Each rat's proximal
tibia was scanned using a high‐resolution desktop micro‐CT
system (micro‐CT40, Scanco Medical AG, Brüttisellen, Switz-
erland) with a 36 µm3 isotropic voxel size, 70 kVp peak X‐ray
tube potential, 114 mAmps tube current, and 240 ms integration
time. Volumetric and mineral density analyses were performed
in a region starting 720 µm distal to the proximal growth plate
and extending 9 mm into the diaphysis (Figure S4). Manual
contouring allowed for cortex and endocortical region analysis
within transverse slices. Bone segmentation used a fixed min-
eral density of 350 mgHA/cm3, and the Scanco Evaluation
Program measured bone volume (BV, mm3), total volume (TV,
mm3), bone volume fraction (BV/TV, %), and bone mineral
density (BMD, mgHA/cm3). X‐ray imaging (lateral) was per-
formed on POD 1, 10, 14, and 28 to monitor implant migration.
The rats were anesthetized using 1%–3% isoflurane in oxygen
and placed on a high‐resolution digital X‐ray plate. The X‐ray
machine was set to a voltage of 54 kVp and an exposure time of
0.075 s. All radiographs were assessed by two blinded observers.
2.6
|
Static Weight‐Bearing
For weight‐bearing analysis, we used a custom‐designed plat-
form with total internal reflection (TIR) technology to measure
hind‐limb weight distribution in rats [15]. Rats were placed on
an elevated plexiglass platform, and their movements were
recorded and analyzed using MATLAB software to quantify
weight exerted by each hind limb. Detailed methods are pro-
vided in the Supporting Information.
2.7
|
Toe Spread
To assess recovery progression, we performed toe spread ratio
analysis of the right (surgical) versus left (nonsurgical) hind paws.
High‐speed gait videos [16] were captured at 400 frames per sec-
ond using the PROMON U750 camera, and baseline measure-
ments were taken before surgery. Gait videos were analyzed with
ImageJ software, identifying frames with complete paw contact.
Toe spreads between the 1st and 5th toes and between the 2nd
and 4th toes were measured, and the ratio of these measurements
between the surgical and control paws was calculated. Detailed
methods are provided in the Supporting Information.
2.8
|
Histology and Immunofluorescent Staining
To assess structural changes and the local inflammatory
response in tibial bone posttreatment, Hematoxylin and Eosin
(H&E) and immunofluorescent staining were performed on
tibial bone sections at POD 28. Following euthanasia with
pentobarbital, tibia samples were fixed in 4% formaldehyde,
decalcified in Versenate EDTA solution, processed histologi-
cally, and embedded in paraffin wax. H&E staining was used for
general tissue structure visualization, and sections were scan-
ned for analysis. Immunofluorescent staining identified TNF‐α
and IL‐6 to evaluate the inflammatory response, using specific
antibodies and fluorescently labeled secondary antibodies. De-
tailed methods are provided in the Supporting Information.
2.9
|
Statistical Analyses
Paired t‐tests were performed between and within control and
experimental groups on weight bearing asymmetry, toe spread
results that were collected throughout the study to compare any
changes between days. Significant differences were determined
as p‐values less than 0.05.

Particle Size Measurements
The diameter of VMP was in the range of 49–65 µm (Table 1).
Changes to the organic solvent used in F01 (acetonitrile) and
F02 (ethyl acetate), PVA concentration (F02–F04), and PLGA
molecular weight (F07–F08) resulted in relatively minor varia-

maximum loading capacity showed a SPAN value of 0.95, which
indicates a relatively homogeneous size distribution.
3.1.3

Particle Morphology
Light and electron microscopy images revealed spherical mor-
phology for VMP (Figures 1 and 2). SEM analysis of F01 showed
a smooth, spherical morphology, while that of F02 revealed a
porous appearance on the surface of the particles. SEM images
of F03 and F04 showed malformed particles with visible
grooves. The remaining batches exhibited smooth, spherical
particles with relatively narrow size distributions (Figure 2),
also supported by the SPAN values from the particle size
analysis (Table 1).

In Vitro Characterization of Vancomycin
Release
The elution profile of the tested formulations (F08–F12) ex-
hibited an S‐curve with an initial burst release of vancomycin
followed by a steady, low‐rate release and a second burst
starting around 6 weeks (Figure 3a). The fractional release
reached equilibrium around 8 weeks. The first burst release, the
release rate in the steady phase, and the cumulative fractional
release were the highest for F10.
PK modeling for the vancomycin release assumed that clearance
from the synovial liquid equals serum clearance. The estimation
for 160 mg VMP injection into the human knee (4 mL) resulted
in a Cmax of 6.98 mg/mL and a tmax of 1.28 h (Figure 3b). Simi-
larly, the vancomycin concentration in the rat knee (0.1 mL) was
estimated to remain over 1 × MIC (1 µg/mL) for the 76 days of
release duration after 10 mg VPM injection (Figure 3c) with a
Cmax of 0.43 mg/mL and a tmax of 1.20 h. The maximum local
drug concentration was reached quickly for both human and rat
models due to the burst release, followed by a sustained release.

Discussion
Microfluidics technology was used for encapsulating vanco-
mycin in biodegradable PLGA microparticles in this study. The
primary emulsion was formed by mixing of W1 and O phases
inside the chip, while the second emulsion was formed where
the W2 phase interfaced with the primary emulsion (Figure S1).
In a microfluidics system, the concentration of solutions plays a
significant role in the morphology of the resultant particles [17,
18]. We found that if the solutions were too concentrated, they
either clogged the narrow microfluidic passages in the chip or
generated fibers through the coalescence of multiple micro-
particles. The ratio of the flow rates and composition of the
formulation phases was also expected to influence the rate of
formation of the initial skin or shell layer of the particles, which
can affect the subsequent solvent removal rate as well as the
quality of the shell layer in terms of its mechanical strength,
porosity, and density. Ultimately, this can have an impact on
the drug loading and encapsulation efficiency of the micro-
particles. The precise control of the mixing process using
microfluidics and the organic (O) – water (W) phase miscibility
were crucial in the production of high‐quality PLGA micro-
particles [19–21].
In the formulation space of the study, the factors that led to
more uniform particles with high loading capacity were the use
of ethyl acetate as the organic solvent, increased PVA concen-
tration and high PLGA molecular weight (with reduced con-
centration). The solvent choice for the O phase (ethyl acetate or
acetonitrile) significantly impacted the characteristics of the
microparticles formed; the acetonitrile (F01) batch had more
fibers compared to the ethyl acetate batch (F02). The loading
capacity of F01 was found to be lower than that of F02, which
can be attributed to the higher miscibility of acetonitrile with
the aqueous phase, presumably leading to faster evaporation of
the solvent and the formation of filaments and fibers. Non-
spherical precipitation has been shown to occur when the sol-
ubility of a solvent in water is greater than 15% (w/w) [22].
Thus, the improved formation of spherical particles when using
ethyl acetate as the solvent in the O phase is attributed to its
lower miscibility with the W phase (approx. 8.3%) [22]. PVA is
added to the dilution fluid to increase emulsion stability [22, 23]
as the PVA molecules can reduce the surface tension through
hydrogen bonding between the primary emulsion and the W2
phase of the emulsified PLGA microparticles. The increase in
loading capacity with increasing PVA concentration in the
dilution fluid is likely due to increased stability, minimized

PLGA microparticle release profiles are typically tri‐phasic: an
initial burst release, followed by a period with an about constant
drug release rate, and a final rapid drug release phase leading to
complete drug exhaustion [25]. While optimizing the formula-
tions in the present investigation from F01‐ F08, the focus was
to improve the loading capacity. After achieving the desired
loading capacity, we focused on the release rate, initial burst,
and sustained release duration. The optimized batch F10
demonstrated a limited initial burst, sustained release, and a
final accelerated release during the release experiments.
While in‐vitro release profiles are commonly used to evaluate
release mechanisms and optimize formulation parameters, esti-
mation of local concentrations is necessary to evaluate possible in
vivo efficacy of the drug delivery system. The pharmacokinetic
model of F10 estimated that the vancomycin concentration
would be over 1 × MIC for 2 months, which is clinically relevant.
There are no standard guidelines for local (sustained) delivery of
antibiotics; here, we used the exposure time to the antibiotic
concentration above MIC. Systemic antibiotics are commonly
administered for 4–6 weeks during the treatment period between
the first and second stage of the revision surgeries for PJI [26],
and the local release profile here is aligned with this period.
Our previously published infection rat model [7, 14] has been
instrumental in simulating postsurgical joint infections, offering
an ideal platform for assessing the therapeutic potential of local
drug delivery systems in a clinically relevant setting. Our
comprehensive analysis in this model, encompassing bacterial
viability, radiographic, histological, and immunofluorescent
analyses, provide strong support for efficient in vivo release of
vancomycin and preliminary feasibility of the VMP in mana-
ging infections.
The complexity and outcome of PJI is often dictated by bacterial
biofilm formation as the efficient eradication of biofilm requires
rigorous treatment strategies, including mechanical debride-
ment [27]. Prevention of bacterial adhesion and colonization of
implants in the early stages of implantation can prevent recal-
citrant post‐arthroplasty infections caused by biofilm [28]. Thus,
it is desirable to increase the available tools that can sustain the
delivery of appropriate antibiotics in the local tissue, especially
at the time of surgical intervention. The bacterial viability
studies evidently demonstrated the antibacterial activity (> 3 log
reduction, Figure 4) exerted by vancomycin microparticles
showcasing the retained stability and functionality of the en-
capsulated drug. X‐ray imaging of the periprosthetic area
(Figure 5a,b), μ‐CT and histological analysis of bone loss and
bone quality (Figures 5c and 7a) were primary indicators that
the eluted drug from the microparticles significantly hindered
bacterial colonization of the implant materials and peri‐implant
tissues, emphasizing their potential for prophylactic use.
Functional assessments, as depicted by the weight‐bearing and
toe spread ratios (Figure 6), supported these findings, indicating
significant enhancements in motor function and weight‐bearing
capacity, indicative of successful recovery trajectories. These
improvements likely result from the direct antibacterial action
of vancomycin and the mitigation of inflammation‐mediated
tissue damage, as indicated by the suppressed TNF‐α and IL‐6
levels in immunofluorescent assays (Figure 7b,c). The anti‐
inflammatory effect observed might also be attributed to the
direct pharmacological actions of vancomycin beyond its anti-
bacterial properties [29]. By modulating the immune response
at the local level, vancomycin may further contribute to the
downregulation of pro‐inflammatory cytokines and chemo-
kines, promoting a shift toward a more regenerative, less
inflammatory microenvironment. This shift is supportive of the
restoration of bone integrity, as excessive inflammation can
impede bone remodeling processes and delay healing [30].
Microfluidics technology can significantly enhance drug delivery
system development by enabling the precise manipulation of the
physicochemical properties of the microparticles [31]. In this
study, we were able to formulate a high loading capacity for
vancomycin using the optimization of microfluidics technology,
overcoming the challenges of traditional particle technology for
loading hydrophilic drug molecules into this polymer matrix [32].
In addition, we showed stability of the particles for in‐vivo
application, showing feasibility for scalability. Appropriate release
profiles were obtained for the prophylactic use of these micro-
particles to prevent the progression of postsurgical infections. Our
findings highlight the potential of microfluidics‐engineered PLGA
microparticles for controlled intra‐articular drug delivery.
Our study has some limitations. In rat models of infection, it is
necessary to use a high inoculum for bacteria to establish and
maintain an infection [7, 14]. The number of contaminating
bacteria is not relevant clinically and the dosing required to
eradicate this higher level of bacteria is also higher (along with
other differences in metabolism which may change the phar-
macodynamics/pharmacokinetics profiles of the drug). How-
ever, in studies where in‐vitro antibacterial efficacy has been
correlated with in vivo treatment success, it has been shown
that a log reduction of 1.5 in vitro could be sufficient for
increased efficacy in vivo [33]. The results of this study can be
interpreted as we have outlined as a preliminary confirmation
that the vancomycin release was sufficient to significantly
hinder infection progression. Further, this animal model was
limited to a 28‐day timeframe; longer‐term studies are needed
to evaluate the sustained effects of microparticle exposure on
bone remodeling and implant integration. The study also fo-
cused on a single antibiotic and bone injury model, which may
not represent the variety of clinical scenarios in orthopedic
surgery. While microfluidics offers precision in particle for-
mulation, future studies should assess the cost‐effectiveness and
regulatory compliance of this technology to facilitate its tran-
sition from laboratory to clinical settings.
