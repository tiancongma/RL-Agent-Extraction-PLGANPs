Abstract
The encapsulation of hydrophilic drug in polymeric nanoparticles with high loading remains a
challenge due to the rapid penetration of the drug to the external aqueous phase. In order to
improve the encapsulation efﬁciency of daunorubicin (DNR) in poly(D,L-lactic-co-glycolic
acid (PLGA) and poly(D,L-lactic acid) (PDLLA) nanoparticles, we fabricated a series of
DNR-loaded nanoparticles using a modiﬁed double-emulsion solvent evaporation/diffusion
method, which introduced a partially water-soluble organic solvent into the particle formation.
The inﬂuence of various preparation parameters was investigated systematically, such as the
ratio of organic solvent, the type of surfactant, the type of polymers and the molecular weight.
Results showed that regular spherical PLGA nanoparticles with diameters of 200–300 nm
could be produced with a remarkably high DNR encapsulation efﬁciency (>80%) and loading
(6.5% (w/w)). Upon encapsulation, the sustained release of DNR could be controlled over
2 weeks. The results of FT-IR and DSC analysis indicated that the encapsulated DNR in
polymeric nanoparticles was inclusion, not absorption. Furthermore, optimized DNR/PLGA
nanoparticles showed a signiﬁcant enhancement of cellular uptake, higher cytotoxicity against
HL-60 cells compared with free DNR. These results were potentially useful for the
nanoparticle formulation of hydrophilic chemotherapeutic drugs that require efﬁcient delivery
to cancer cells as well as sustained release at the speciﬁc site.

1. Introduction
Acute myeloid leukemia (AML) is the most common form
of acute leukemia in adults, particularly in individuals over
60 years of age; it also accounts for 15–20% of cases
in childhood [1].
At present, chemotherapy remains the
standard treatment of AML [2, 3].
Daunorubicin (DNR)
(scheme 1), an anthracycline antibiotic, has been extensively
used in the treatment of AML [4, 5]. DNR is active against

various hematological malignancies and solid tumors due to
its intercalation of the planar aromatic ring system between
the DNA base pairs.
However, its efﬁcacy is restricted
by dose-limiting toxicity, multi-drug resistance (MDR) and
several drug-associated side effects, especially cardiotoxicity
and myelosuppression [6–8].
Hence, we propose the use
of biodegradable polymeric nanoparticles, in which DNR
is encapsulated, in a controlled release manner, reducing
the toxic effects against normal cells whilst increasing its
therapeutic activity.

Among the drug delivery systems, the nanoparticles
of
biodegradable
polymers
such
as
poly(D,L-lactic-co-
glycolic acid) (PLGA) and poly(D,L-lactic acid) (PDLLA)
(scheme 1) have been considered as promising carriers for
anticancer agents [9, 10]. PLGA and PLA are biocompatible,
biodegradable materials that have been widely used for the
drug delivery system and approved by the FDA for certain
clinical uses. Their degradation can be varied from days to
years by altering the type of polymer, the polymer molecular
weight or the structure of the nanoparticles [11]. Due to the
existence of the EPR (enhanced permeation and retention)
effect in tumor tissues, nanoparticles containing an anti-
cancer agent can be targeted passively to the tumor cell, and
thus, improve the speciﬁcity of the pharmacodynamic action
[12–16]. It was reported that nanoparticles could reverse the
MDR of the cancer cell, which was one of the key factors
leading to the failure of chemotherapy [17].
This ﬁnding
is of great importance for the particular case of DNR, since
acquired resistance to the drug has already been reported [18].
Therefore, PLGA or PLA nanoparticles appear to be a useful
approach for overcoming certain kinds of drug resistance.
The performance of the polymeric nanoparticles depends
on
several
factors
such
as
polymer
nature
(polymer
composition and molecular weight), organic solvents, type and
concentration of surfactant, etc [19]. The choice of a suitable
nanoparticle preparation technique is largely dependent on
the physicochemical properties of the drug and polymers.
Commonly utilized techniques for nanoparticle fabrication
are the nanoprecipitation technique [20, 21], the single
or double emulsion solvent evaporation [22, 23] and the
solvent diffusion technique [24]. For hydrophilic compound
encapsulation, double emulsion solvent evaporation is the
most popular technique among these preparation methods.
However, the difﬁculty of the encapsulation of hydrophilic
molecules into PLGA or PDLLA nanoparticles remains
a problem [25–27].
To address this problem, a regular
double emulsion technique was modiﬁed by introducing water
immiscible organic solvent dichloromethane (DCM) and water
partial miscible solvent acetone (ACE) with a certain ratio
during the polymeric nanoparticle fabrication process.
We
hypothesized that combining the double emulsion evaporation
and diffusion technique at the same time could result in a
better encapsulation efﬁciency of hydrophilic molecules in
nanoparticles.
The aim of this study is to fabricate and optimize the
DNR-loaded PLGA or PDLLA nanoparticles in order to
obtain a controlled DNR delivery system with high drug
loading and encapsulation efﬁciency, and enhanced anti-
tumor activity.
The polymeric nanoparticles containing
DNR were prepared using a modiﬁed simultaneous double-
emulsion (W/O/W) solvent diffusion/evaporation method.
The inﬂuence of various preparation parameters, such as the
ratio of organic solvent, the type of surfactant, the type of
polymer and its molecular weight, the level of drug loading
on the characteristics of the nanoparticles and drug release,
was investigated to optimize the nanoparticle formulation. In
addition, we also investigated the drug release kinetics, in vitro
the cellular uptake and cytotoxicity of DNR-loaded polymeric
nanoparticles.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Materials
PDLLA (Mw = 190 000) was synthesized in our laboratory;
PLGA (LA/GA ratio 50:50, Mw 50 000 and 100 000) was
obtained from the Chengdu Institute of Organic Chemistry,
Chinese Academy of Sciences. DNR (Pharmacia and Upjohn
S.p.A) was provided by Tongji Medical College, Huazhong
University of Science and Technology (HUST). DCM, ACE,
gelatin, Pluronic F68, polyvinyl alcohol (PVA), Tween-80 and
the other reagents were of analytical grade and purchased from
Aldrich Chemical Co.
The human leukemic HL-60 cell line was obtained from
Tongji Medical College, HUST and cultured in an RPMI 1640
medium supplemented with 10% of fetal calf serum (FCS) at
37 ◦C in a humidiﬁed atmosphere containing 5% CO2.
2.2. Preparation of DNR-loaded nanoparticles
DNR-loaded nanoparticles were prepared using a modiﬁed
simultaneous double-emulsion (water-in-oil-in-water) solvent
evaporation/diffusion technique. Brieﬂy, 100 mg of PDLLA
or PLGA was dissolved in 10 ml organic mixture of DCM
and ACE with various ratios (from 5:
0 to 2:
1, v/v)
containing Tween-80 (5%, v/v) as an emulsiﬁer. DNR was
dissolved in 2 ml of distilled water, and then emulsiﬁed in
the polymer solution through homogenization for 1 min ×
2. The primary W/O emulsion was further added to 40 ml
of external water with homogenization (3 min) to achieve the
stable double emulsion (W/O/W). The resulting emulsion
was dropped gradually into 200 ml of the aqueous solution
with different surfactants (Pluronic F68, PVA, and gelatin)
under steady stirring to make the nanoparticles solidify. The
residual organic solvents were evaporated under negative
pressure and the nanoparticles suspending in emulsion were
collected by ultracentrifugation at 12 000 rpm and washed with
distilled water three times. Finally, the products were dried by
lyophilization and stored at 4 ◦C.
2.3. Particle size and size distribution
The mean particle diameter and size distribution of the
nanoparticles were measured by a dynamic light scattering
(DLS)
method
using
a
Zetasizer
Nano
ZS
(Malvern
Instruments, UK).

2.6. DSC analysis of DNR-loaded nanoparticles
A differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) thermal analysis
(Perkin Elmer, DSC-7, USA) was employed to investigate
the physical status of the DNR inside the nanoparticles.
In the DSC measurement, pure DNR, PDLLA, PLGA,
DNR/PDLLA nanoparticles and DNR/PLGA nanoparticles
were respectively heated from room temperature to 300 ◦C
under a nitrogen atmosphere with the heating rate of 10 ◦C
min−1, and then, the DSC thermal curves of those samples
were obtained.
2.7. Fourier transform infrared measurements
A Fourier transform infrared spectrophotometer (FT-IR,
Bruker, VERTEX 70, Germany) was employed to investigate
the interactions between DNR and polymeric materials.
DNR, PDLLA, PLGA, DNR/PDLLA nanoparticles and
DNR/PLGA nanoparticles were scanned in the IR range from
500 to 4000 cm−1.
2.8. In vitro release proﬁles
The rate of the DNR release from the nanoparticles was
evaluated in the phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) solution.
Brieﬂy, 10 mg of dried DNR-loaded nanoparticles were
suspended in 10 ml of 0.01 M PBS (pH 7.4). The samples were
placed in a thermostatic water bath at 37 ◦C with continuous
agitation at about 100 rpm. At various preset intervals, the
supernatant was withdrawn after centrifugation at 10 000 × g
for 5 min, and replaced with an equal volume of the release
medium. The amount of DNR released was measured by the
spectroﬂuorometer.
2.9. Cellular uptake assay
An HL-60 cell line was used as a model cancer cell for the
examination of endocytosis. HL-60 cells, at a density of 1 ×
106 cells ml−1, were cultured in a 96-well plate and after 6 h,
DNR-loaded PLGA nanoparticles and free DNR were added
to the culture medium, both of which had the same DNR
concentration of 0.5 μg ml−1. After various lengths of time,
ranging from 30 to 180 min, cells were washed twice in ice-
cold PBS and resuspended in 0.5 ml ice-cold PBS. The cells
were then viewed and imaged by a ﬂuorescence microscope
(Olympus, IX71).
To obtain the accurate quantiﬁcation of
DNR, the intracellular drug was determined for each scheduled
time using a spectroﬂuorimeteric analysis [28]. Brieﬂy, the
cells were incubated with DNR for various lengths of time and
then washed twice with RPMI 1640 supplemented with 10%
of FCS. Afterward the cells were centrifuged immediately at
4 ◦C (1000 rpm for 5 min) and the pellets were suspended and
lysed with 1.5 ml PBS containing 50 μg ml−1 proteinase K
and 1% sodium dodecyl sulfate, followed by a 12 h shaking at
37 ◦C. At the end of this period, the cell extracts were
centrifuged at 3000 rpm for 10 min.
The ﬂuorescence of
the extracts was measured according to the method described
in section 2.5.
The results obtained were expressed as
a percentage of the total amount of nanoparticles or free
DNR found in the solubilized cells to the total amount of
nanoparticles or free DNR applied to the cells.
2.10. Cytotoxicity assays
Cytotoxicity of free DNR and DNR-loaded nanoparticles was
determined by measuring the inhibition of the cell growth
using a tetrazolium dye (MTT) assay [29, 30]. HL-60 cells
harvested in a logarithmic growth phase were seeded on a
96-well plate at a cellular density of 7 × 105 cells ml−1.
Following that, the cells were incubated for 24 h or 48 h
with various concentrations (0.02–5 μg ml−1) of free DNR
and DNR-loaded nanoparticles. Cell viability could then be
determined using the MTT assay. MTT absorbance, which
is related to the number of metabolically active cells, was
measured using a microplate reader (Molecular Devices). The
IC50 value (drug concentration resulting in the 50% inhibition
of growth compared to controls) was directly determined from
the dose-response.
2.11. Statistical analysis
Statistical tests were performed with a two-sided Student’s
t-test.
A P-value of 0.05 or less was considered to be
statistically signiﬁcant.

3. Results and discussion
3.1. Effect of preparation conditions on the characteristics of
the DNR-loaded nanoparticles
The common double emulsion techniques for encapsulating
hydrophilic drugs into polymeric nanoparticles suffer from
low encapsulation efﬁciency, which is due to the drug rapidly
penetrating from the inner aqueous phase to the external
aqueous phase.
In order to improve the encapsulation
of hydrophilic drugs, we used a modiﬁed double-emulsion
solvent diffusion/evaporation technique in which acetone
was introduced into the emulsion process as a co-solvent
(scheme
2).
The
properties
of
DNR-loaded
PLGA
nanoparticles prepared by the modiﬁed technique utilizing
different
solvent
ratios
are
summarized
in
table
1.
Nanoparticles prepared with only DCM showed the largest
particle size (over 400 nm) and widest size distribution, lowest
drug loading (2.1%) and encapsulation efﬁciency (26.3%).
In contrast, the nanoparticles prepared with DCM: ACE
mixture solvent at the ratio 4:1 exhibited the highest drug
loading (6.5%) and encapsulation efﬁciency (81.3%).
The
explanation of this result is that the partial water-miscibility
of acetone diffuses freely to the external aqueous phase and
creates regions of local supersaturation near the interface,
which induce polymer precipitation and solidiﬁcation once
the critical concentration is reached, which could eventually
prevent the rapid partitioning of hydrophilic drug content to
the external aqueous phase [19, 31, 32]. It is also found that the
size of PLGA nanoparticles decreased (from 416 to 206 nm)
with increasing ACE ratio; this ﬁnding can be explained by
the different nanoparticle formation mechanisms. ACE used
in the modiﬁed particle preparation is partially water miscible,
so the organic solvent can diffuse freely to the aqueous phase
to further decrease the size of the emulsion droplet, whereas
DCM does not diffuse extensively through the aqueous phase
and maintains each droplet to form a single particle. It was
also understandable that the drug loading and encapsulation
efﬁciency were decreased with the increase of the ACE ratio
of 4:1, since DNR rapidly penetrated the external aqueous
phase while a large amount of ACE diffused out.
The inﬂuence of the type of surfactant was also
investigated.
The particle size, polydispersity index (PDI),
drug loading and encapsulation efﬁciency of the DNR-loaded
PLGA nanoparticles are illustrated in table 2. The particle
size and PDI were signiﬁcantly affected by the surfactant.
For example, a small particle size (247 nm) with a small
PDI (0.119) was obtained when the gelatin solution was used
as an aqueous surfactant compared with the Pluronic F68
batch where the particle size and PDI were 437 nm, 0.425,
respectively. The drug loading and encapsulation efﬁciency of

that increasing polymer molecular weight leads to increasing

There was a clear trend of drug loading
and encapsulation efﬁciency reduction when the GA/LA ratio
was decreased.
For example, the nanoparticles prepared
with PLGA exhibited a higher encapsulation efﬁciency of
81.3% compared with PDLLA, which had the encapsulation
efﬁciency of only 38.8%. This may be due to the fact that the
decrease of the GA unit in the polymer backbone decreased
the hydrophilicity of the polymer, which reduced interaction
between the DNR and polymer matrix [33]. Moreover, the
low molecular weight of PLGA showed high drug loading and
encapsulation efﬁciency.
Figures 1(a)–(c) show the surface morphology of
nanoparticles prepared with different surfactants. There were
no signiﬁcant differences in morphology among the three
formulations. Most nanoparticles were of regular spherical
shape with smooth surfaces and without any aggregation or
adhesion. It was noticeable that the nanoparticles fabricated
with PVA and gelatin as surfactant were more homogeneous
in size and distribution, which was consistent with DLS data.
Some of the nanoparticles prepared with Pluronic F68 had
a hollow structure, which can be observed from ﬁgure 1(a).
This may be due to the fact that Pluronic F68 produced a large
amount of foam after the homogenization of double emulsion;
then the vaporization of the solvent and air bubbles inside
the nanoparticles causes the disruption of the polymer ﬁlm
[34, 35]. These results further indicated that gelatin was better
than Pluronic F68 in particle formation.

actually particles internalized by the cells and not those bound
to the cell surface.
3.6. Cytotoxicity assay
To examine whether PLGA-encapsulated DNR maintains its
anticancer activity, the drug-loaded nanoparticles were applied
on the cells to investigate their anti-tumor effect on cancer
cells. Figure 7 shows the survival curves of HL-60 cells after
exposure to DNR, either in solution or in nanoparticles for
24 h and 48 h, respectively. The cytotoxicity was observed
after 24 h exposure and increased in all cases with longer
(48 h) exposure. The amount of DNR required to achieve 50%
of growth inhibition (IC50) was much higher in nanoparticles
than in the solution after 24 h (DNR-loaded nanoparticle:
IC50 = 2.05 μg ml−1; free DNR: IC50 = 0.37 μg ml−1).
A possible explanation of this phenomenon is that DNR, as a
widely utilized clinical medicine to inhibit leukemia, has a high
sensitivity on the HL-60 cell [44–46], while the cytotoxicity
of DNR-loaded nanoparticles is determined by a sustained
release of DNR from nanoparticles after internalization within
cells [42, 47].
By comparing the survival curve in ﬁgure 7(a) with that in
ﬁgure 7(b), it could be observed that at the DNR concentration
of 0.5 μg ml−1, the cell viability dropped from 45% in 24 h
to 40% in 48 h when exposed to free DNR, while the viability
when exposed to DNR nanoparticles witnessed a sharp decline
from 71% in 24 h to 35% in 48 h.
Moreover, with the
increase of incubation time, the IC50 of DNR nanoparticles
decreased from 2.05 to 0.18 μg ml−1 after 48 h exposure,
whereas the IC50 of free DNR decreased from 0.37 to
0.11 μg ml−1. This is clear evidence that the DNR nanoparticle
controlled release system is able to provide sustained drug
concentration and results in long-term enhanced cytotoxicity.
Furthermore, we noted that less than 50% of DNR was released
from PLGA nanoparticles during the ﬁrst 48 h according to
the in vitro drug release data (ﬁgure 4), suggesting that the
drug-loaded nanoparticles are even more potent on a per drug
released basis.
4. Conclusions
In
this
study,
the
modiﬁed
double
emulsion
solvent
evaporation/extraction with the co-solvent technique was
used for the encapsulation of hydrophilic drug DNR into
PLGA and PDLLA nanoparticles, which resulted in improved
formulation characteristics including smaller size and PDI,
higher drug loading and encapsulation efﬁciency. Compared
to free DNR, optimized DNR-loaded PLGA nanoparticle
formulation showed a controllable drug release proﬁle, a
signiﬁcant enhancement of cellular uptake, higher cytotoxicity
along with prolonged incubation against HL-60 cells.
The
results of the present study indicate that optimized polymeric
nanoparticle formulation could be potentially used for
hydrophilic drug or nucleic acid delivery systems in cancer
therapy.
