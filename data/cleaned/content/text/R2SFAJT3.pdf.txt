A B S T R A C T
Polymer-based nanoparticles have become an appealing carrier for improving vaccine delivery efficiency. In this
study, we investigated an interesting approach based on PLGA nanoparticles encapsulating Cerastes cerastes
venom as an intranasal vaccine delivery system for snake envenomation prevention. Particles were synthesized
by double emulsion solvent evaporation method and characterized for their size, morphology, distribution, and
venom-nanoparticles interactions. An immunization trial was performed in mice by the intranasal route to
evaluate the immune response, the reactogenicity, and the protective effect of this nanovaccine.
The physicochemical and structural characteristics of Cc-loaded PLGA NPs revealed that the particles
exhibited a spherical shape with a diameter of 370 nm, and a negatively charged surface with a zeta potential
value of 19,9 mV.
The immunization with Cc-PLGA NPs can induce a systemic innate and humoral immune response and confers
protection against Cerastes cerastes venom (Cc) over than 6 LD50 with a cross-protection against Vipera lebetina
venom (Vl) over than 5 LD50. Nano-encapsulation of Cc venom reduced its toxicity and the induced tissue
alterations.
Our results confirm that the nano-formulation Cc-PLGA NPs is a potent adjuvant system that improve the
humoral immune response and provide protection against high lethal doses of viper venoms.
1. Introduction
Envenomation caused by snakebite is a serious public health issue in
many parts of the world. It is known as a neglected tropical disease that
affect over 5 million occurring worldwide each year, resulting in 2.5
million envenomation and 81,000 to 138,000 deaths, according to the
World Health Organization (WHO) [1–3]. Snakebite mortality is
strongly associated with poverty, poor investment in health by national
governments, and limited access to healthcare [3,4]. The emergency
caused bay snake envenomation and limited access to treatment must be
considered by each country and by the World Health Organization
(WHO) [5]. Since June 2017, snakebite envenomation is finally recog­
nized by WHO and re-included in the neglected tropical diseases list
(NTDs) [6]. Snake venom is a complex mixture with diverse and varied
biochemical and toxicological profiles [7,8]. After envenomation,
several symptoms such as signs at the site of the bite, hemorrhage,
myonecrosis, dermonecrosis, hemostatic abnormalities, and inflamma­
tory reactions (edema, leukocyte infiltration, pain, and release of several
mediators) are observed in bitten patients by Viperidae snakes [9–13].
Vipers are among the potentially dangerous snakes given to their rela­
tively frequent bites in North-Africa [14]. Right now, immunotherapy is
the only specific therapy for snakebite envenoming [15]. However,
immunotherapy effectiveness is limited by many factors such as the
inappropriate delay in its administration, its availability, and other
adverse effects that cause complications [14–16]. Several strategies
have been undertaken to improve the efficiency of antivenom against
snakebites. In our team, we showed that the gamma irradiation of snake
venom can decrease the toxicity and leads to an enhancement of
neutralizing ability of antibodies [17,18]. Furthermore, to reduce the
deleterious effects caused by snake venom, therapeutic strategies
implying L-arginine and Heparin combined or not with polyvalent an­
tivenom have been used to improve tissue regeneration and minimize
local tissue damage [13,19–22]. Vaccination has been considered as the
most effective way of protection against many infectious diseases [23].
Some new vaccines using subunit antigens and recombinant proteins
poorly immunogenic often needing adjuvants and effective delivery
systems to improve the safety and efficiency of vaccine [24,25]. Ad­
vances in nanotechnology, especially in nanovaccinology, have

composition, size, shape, and surface properties for medicinal applica­
tions [26]. Nanoparticles (NPs) are solid and spherical structures with a
size ranging from 10 to 1000 nm, developed from natural or synthetic
polymers [27]. NPs promote the uptake of antigens by APCs and lead to
boost the immune response [28,29]. Polymer-based particulate systems
has been widely investigated for vaccine development [30,31]. Among
the delivery systems developed by our team, calcium alginate and chi­

delivery, which conferred a protective immune response against the
scorpion venom and its toxic fractions [32,33]. PLGA (poly-d,L-lacti­

polymers in nanotechnology because its hydrolysis conducts to biode­
gradable metabolite monomers, lactic acid, and glycolic acid [34].
Several studies have shown that PLGA nanoparticles were used as de­
livery systems for antigens and/or adjuvants to modulate immune re­
sponses. In addition, these particles are efficiently internalized by APCs
and facilitate the proper processing and presentation of antigens to T
cells, and also promote the prolonged availability of the antigen in the
blood that leads to a higher proliferation of B cells and thus more
memory cells [28,35–38]. In recent years, the nasal route has become
extremely useful in vaccine delivery to enhance their efficiency and
promote nasal delivery [39]. The encapsulation of Hepatitis B surface
antigen (HbSAg) into PLGA/poly (L-lactic acid) (PLA) particles
improved systemic immune response following nasal delivery [40].
Bovine serum albumin encapsulated in particles induced a higher anti­
body response compared with free antigen after intranasal administra­
tion [41].
The aim of this study is to evaluate the effectiveness of nanovaccine
based on Cerastes cerastes-loaded PLGA nanoparticles delivered through
the intranasal route against envenomation. The physicochemical prop­
erties of nanovaccine were firstly characterized. An immunization
schedule was then undertaken in mice to assess the safety, adjuvant
capacity, and immunoprotective potential.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Materials
2.1.1. Venoms
The lyophilized Cerastes cerastes and Vipera lebetina venoms were
obtained from our Laboratory of Cellular and Molecular Biology, Faculty
of Biological Sciences, University of Sciences and Technology Houari
Boumediene (USTHB) and were provided from Pasteur Institute of
Algeria. Before use, the venom (20 mg/ml) was dissolved in 0.15 M NaCl
and stored at – 20 ◦C. As already described, the lethal doses (LD50) of
Cerastes cerastes and Vipera lebetina venoms correspond respectively to
48 μg/20 g and 50 μg/20 g of mouse body weight [17,42].
2.1.2. Chemical reagents
PLGA (Polylactic-co-glycolic acid, 50:50 lactide–glycolide ratio, Mw
25000), PVA (polyvinyl alcohol, Mw 15 KDa), O-dianisidine hydro­
chloride, O-phenyldiamine, phosphotungstic acid, Coomassie brilliant
blue G-250, ethyl acetate, and antimouse IgG1, IgG2a antibodies have
been purchased from Sigma–Aldrich, St. Louis, USA. All other chemicals
and reagents used were of analytical grade.
2.1.3. Animals
Female NMRI mice (20 ± 2 g, 6–8 weeks age) were obtained from
Pasteur Institute of Algeria. They received water and food ad libitum
under controlled environmental conditions. The experimental protocols
were performed according to the European community rules of ethical
committee for animal welfare.
2.2. Methods
2.2.1. Preparation of PLGA-based nanoparticles
Snake venom loaded PLGA nanoparticles were prepared by double
emulsion solvent evaporation method as previously reported with
modifications [43]. In the first emulsion (W/O), an aqueous solution of
the native antigen was emulsified with 1 ml of ethyl acetate containing
5% (w/v) of PLGA using a homogenizer, ultra-turrax (T25, IKA) for 2
min at 12000. The acquired water in oil emulsion was emulsified with 2
ml of an aqueous solution containing PVA. The double emulsion was
added to 25 ml of PVA solution under stirring at room temperature. The
external phase underwent stirring for 3 h at constant room temperature
to evaporate the ethyl acetate. The nanoparticles were collected by
centrifugation at 35000g for 10 min. The nanoparticles were
freeze-dried and stored at −20 ◦C.
2.2.2. Characterization of the nanoparticles
2.2.2.1. Particle size and zeta potential measurements. The particle size,
polydispersity (PDI), and zeta potential of the nano-formulations were
measured using a Malvern Instruments ZS Zeta sizer Nano in distilled
water at 90◦scattering angle and a temperature of 25 ◦C based on the
Dynamic Light Scattering technique (DLS). Zeta potential (ZP) mea­
surements were performed using the same equipment. Zeta potential
determinations were based on electrophoretic mobility of the nano­
particles in dilute aqueous suspensions.
2.2.2.2. Morphological characterization. The morphology of the nano­
particles was explored by Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM)
(ZEISS LEO 900, Germany). The samples were placed on coated copper
grids and negatively stained with 2% (w/v) phosphotungstic acid
solution.
2.2.2.3. Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy. The chemical
structure of the nanoparticles and the interaction between the polymer
and antigen were analyzed by FTIR. FTIR spectra were obtained using an
Alpha Bruker (single refection diamond ATR) spectrometer. Trans­
mission spectra were recorded in the spectral range 4000–400 cm‾1.
2.2.2.4. Encapsulation efficiency assay. The encapsulation efficiency
was estimated by quantification of free Cc venom in the supernatant
after centrifugation of samples at 15000 rpm for 30 min at 4 ◦C. The
protein concentration of each supernatant was analyzed using the

Immunizations were performed in mice (five mice per group) by the
intranasal route on day 0 (prime immunization) and on day 15 (boost
injection). The first group of mice was immunized with encapsulated
venom of Cerastes cerastes in PLGA NPs (Cc-PLGA NPs) (20 μg per 20 g of
mice) in 20 μl of saline buffer in small droplets to the tip of the nose, then
boosted at day 15 later. The second group received a sublethal dose of
Cerastes cerastes venom (20 μg per 20 g of mice) (Cc). The third and
fourth groups received free PLGA nanoparticles (PLGA NPs) and saline
buffer (NaCl 0.9%) respectively. Blood samples were collected 6 and 24
h and on days 7 and 14 post-immunization. The samples were centri­
fuged (3000 g, 10 min) and the resulting sera were pooled for cell count
analysis and enzymatic activity. Organs were collected for histological
study 24 h after the boost immunization.
For antibody analysis, blood was collected 24 h, and 7, 14, 30 and 45
days post last immunization, specific anti-venom IgG, IgG1, IgG2a
antibody in serum were determined by ELISA.
2.2.4. Evaluation of immune response during the immunization
2.2.4.1. Inflammatory response
2.2.4.1.1. Neutrophil count. The determination of circulating neu­
trophils was realized on the blood smears stained with May–­
Grunwald–Giemsa. The slides were examined under common light
microscopy at × 1000 magnification. The percentage of cell sub­
populations was calculated based on a count of 100 cells.
2.2.4.1.2. Measurement of eosinophil peroxidase (EPO) activity. The
eosinophil peroxidase (EPO) activity in sera was measured according to
the method based on the oxidation of o-phenylenediamine (OPD) in the
presence of H2O2 as reported by Van Oosterhout and collaborators [45].
The substrate solution corresponds to a mixture of 10 ml of Tris-HCl
buffer (0.05 M) containing O-phenyldiamine (OPD) and H2O2 (30%
w/w). The substrate solution (100 μL) was added to the sera (50 μL) in
duplicate in a 96-well microplate. The absorbance was measured at 490
nm using a microplate reader (EL800 Bio-Tek Instruments) after incu­
bation for 1 h at 37 ◦C in the dark.
2.2.4.1.3. Measurement of myeloperoxidase (MPO) activity. The
myeloperoxidase (MPO) activity was determined by the orthodianisi­
dine method [46]. Samples were mixed with orthodianisidine (0.167
mg/ml) and H2O2 in phosphate buffer. The absorbance was measured at
460 nm.
2.2.4.2. Adaptive immune response
2.2.4.2.1. Titer determination of IgG subtypes antibodies. The specific
IgG antibody titers were determined by ELISA. Briefly, venom antigens
(5 μg/ml) in 0.1 M carbonate buffer pH 9.6 were adsorbed on 96-well
microplates for 24 h, at 4 ◦C. After three washes with PBS-Tween 20,
the non-specific sites were saturated with skim milk (5%) in PBS buffer
for 1 h at 37 ◦C, and thrown away by washing. Serum samples were
successively diluted in PBS (0.1 M), added to the wells and incubated for
1 h at 37 ◦C. After another washing step, plates were incubated with
anti-mouse IgG1 and IgG2a conjugated with peroxidase (Sigma–Aldrich,
St. Louis, USA) for 1 h at 37 ◦C. The O-phenyldiamine (OPD) solution
(10 mg/ml in phosphate buffer, pH 7.4) containing H2O2 was added to
the wells (100 μL/well). The reaction was stopped by adding 50 μL of
H2SO4 2 N. The absorbance was read at 490 nm using a microplate
reader (EL800 Bio-Tek Instruments).
2.2.4.2.2. IgG1 and IgG2a isotypes titers. The IgG1 and IgG2a iso­
types titers were evaluated by ELISA to determine the immune response
profile.
2.2.5. Evaluation of protective and cross-protective effect of vaccine
formulation
Immunized mice with Cc-PLGA NPs were challenged individually by
the subcutaneous route with increasing lethal doses of Cerastes cerastes
and Vipera lebetina venoms two months after the boost immunization.
Mortality was recorded 24 h after injection.
2.2.6. Histological analysis
Organs (liver, lungs, and heart) were collected from immunized and
non-immunized mice after 24 h of the immunization and have been
formalin-fixed. The tissues were subsequently dehydrated and
embedded in paraffin. Then, they were sectioned at 5 μm and stained
with hematoxylin and eosin for microscopic analysis (Motic Digital
Microscope PAL system).
2.2.7. Evaluation of lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) activity
Lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) was assayed in sera using Spinreact
Commercial Kit. The enzyme values were expressed in International
Units (IU/l) and represent the mean of three replicates determinations
(mean ± SD).
2.2.8. Statistical analysis
Results are expressed as mean ± standard error of the mean (SEM).
The results were analyzed by one way ANOVA and Tukey’s multiple
comparison test. P-values less than 0.05 was considered statistically
significant.
3. Results
3.1. Characterization of venom loaded PLGA NPs
3.1.1. Particle size and zeta potential measurements
The particle size, the PDI, and zeta potential, were characterized by
Zeta sizer (Fig. 1; Table 1). Dynamic light scattering showed that the
Polydispersity index of the free NPs and Cc-PLGA NPs were respectively
0.243 and 0.308. The hydrodynamic particle sizes of free NPs and Cc-
PLGA NPs were respectively 227 nm and 370 nm.
Obtained results showed that the Cc-PLGA NPs were distributed in
relative homogeneous size, giving an achievement of a single charac­
teristic peak.
The size of the nanoparticles encapsulating the venom increased up
to 370 nm, showing an encapsulation of the venom. The zeta potential
measurements showed that free NPs and Cc-PLGA NPs presented a
negatively charged surface with a Zeta potential of 20.5 mV and 19.9 mV
respectively.
3.1.2. Morphological characterization of nanoparticles
The Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM) analysis shows the
particle size and surface morphology of venom loaded PLGA nano­
particles (Fig. 2). The TEM images revealed that Cc-PLGA NPs present a
spherical shape and a smooth surface.
3.1.3. Encapsulation efficiency evaluation (EE)
The entrapment efficiency of vaccine formulation based on PLGA
nanoparticles encapsulating Cerastes cerastes venom was estimated at
84.1%.
3.1.4. Fourier transformed infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy
FTIR analysis visualized the possible interactions between the
encapsulated Cc and PLGA polymer matrix. The spectrum of PLGA is
characterized by carbonyl C––O stretching at 1746 cm−1, CH at 2960
cm−1, and C–O stretching (1050-1250 cm−1). In Cc venom spectrum, a
strong and widepeak at 3300 cm−1wavenumber is related to OH
stretching, a vibration band at 1650 cm−1 is attributed to C––O
stretching, and CH stretching at 630 cm−1. In Cc loaded PLGA nano­
particles spectrum the Cc venom signals can be observed at 3300 cm−1,
1650 cm−1, and 630 cm−1. The signals in the FTIR spectra confirm the
incorporation of Cc venom in the PLGA nanoparticles (Fig. 3).

3.2.2. Specific immune response
To investigate the effect of nanovaccine on antibody response, the
levels of IgG and IgG subclasses were measured using ELISA analysis.
The results showed that the immunization with Cc-PLGA NPs
generated a humoral response characterized by a high antibody titer
from day 14–45 after the boost immunization compared to controls

The nanovaccine is able to exhibit a high level of the IgG1 isotype

results indicate that the humoral response with Th2 profile was pre­
dominately activated with the encapsulated venom.
The comparison of the humoral immune response induced by Cc-
loaded PLGA NPs with that induced by non-encapsulated Cc venom,
revealed an immunoadjuvant effect of PLGA NPs (Fig. 5 B and C).
Our results suggest that PLGA, as a safe polymer, may have an
adjuvant potential to enhance the immune response by stimulating the
innate and adaptive responses of the immune system.
3.2.3. Analysis of histological alterations during immunization
The intranasal administration of Cc-PLGA NPs induced low
alterations in myocardium, pulmonary, and hepatic parenchyma
compared to animals that received the non-encapsulated venom (Fig. 6).
These alterations were associated with the antigenic property of
nanovaccine.
Tissue analysis of the pulmonary parenchyma of the immunized
animals Cc-PLGA NPs was similar to the lung tissue control. However,
lung parenchyma of immunized animals with non-encapsulated venom,
revealed hemorrhage, pulmonary edema, thickening of interalveolar
septa, and cell infiltration. The myocardial tissue of immunized mice
with non-encapsulated venom, revealed a degeneration of myocardial
fibers, interstitial edema, and hemorrhage compared to the immunized
mice with Cc-PLGA NPs. The structure of hepatic parenchyma of
immunized animals with Cc-PLGA NPs is similar to the controls; how­
ever, the hepatic parenchyma of immunized mice with non-
encapsulated venom presented marked hepatocyte degeneration. The
tissue analysis of animal controls revealed normal architecture.
3.2.4. Evaluation of lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) activity
The evaluation of histopathological alterations was correlated with
the LDH activity. The LDH activity in sera of mice immunized with Cc
venom entrapped in PLGA nanoparticles revealed no significant varia­
tion, whereas a significant increase in LDH activity was observed in sera
of mice received non-encapsulated Cc venom compared to control ani­
mals and those received free PLGA NPs (Table 2).

4. Discussion
Snake envenomation represents a major yet neglected public health
problem in terms of incidence and severity [2,47]. For over 120 years,
immunotherapy using antivenom preparations from animals is always
the primary treatment for snakebite envenoming. Because of all the
limitations of the current treatment, an alternative approach offering
preventive therapy to protect the populations at risk is of interest.
Vaccination could potentially be used as a preventive approach against
snake envenomation. Adjuvants are crucial to obtain a protective and
lasting response by generating significant titers of antibodies with high
affinity [48]. Adjuvants offer the stability, safe usage, biodegradability,
cost-effectiveness, and easy use of vaccines [49,50].
Various studies have explored the efficiency of nanoparticles as an
enticing way to improving the conventional adjuvant system [51–53]. In
the last years, research focused on the discovery of new carriers for
vaccine delivery with an aim to develop better vaccine systems and
minimize the frequency of immunization. In our team, nanoparticulate
systems were used to enhance the immunogenicity of venom antigens
and enhancing long-lasting immunoprotective effect [32,33].
The present study was designed to assess the effect of a new carrier-
based on polymeric nanoparticles for vaccine delivery against snake
envenomation through the intranasal route. The native venom of
Cerastes cerastes was encapsulated into nanoparticles based on PLGA, a
biodegradable and biocompatible polymer.
PLGA nanoparticles showed vaccine adjuvant potential and may
induce long-lasting adaptive immune responses due to their biocom­
patibility, stability, and slow release of encapsulated antigens [25,54,
55]. Alternative routes of administration of particulate systems have
become extremely attractive. The intranasal route offers the advantage
of non-invasive ease of administration and limited metabolism in the
nasal cavity which is characterized by high vascularity and permeability
[56,57].
The particle size and surface properties appear to be important in
vaccine formulations using nanoparticles; these properties promote
their cell uptake and nasal permeation [58,59]. In this study, the size of
nanovaccine is about 400 nm. In a previous study, researchers have
shown that smaller particles of 200–500 nm could be up-taken by DCs
and thus generate a stronger immune response compared to vaccine
alone [60].
It is noteworthy that the intranasal administration of Cc-PLGA NPs
formulation elicited high serum IgG responses resulting in a high level of
IgG1 isotype compared to that of IgG2a isotype, showing a

Our results showed that the administration of non-encapsulated
Cerastes cerastes venom via the intranasal route seems to induce a sig­

alterations due to the cytotoxic effects of venom resulting in systemic
inflammation that affect most of the organs.
The assessment of the reactogenicity of nanovaccine showed that Cc-
PLGA NPs leads to a slight increase in neutrophil count, EPO, and MPO
activities compared with non-encapsulated venom.
The histological analysis of organs (liver, heart, and lungs) of
immunized animals revealed that the nanovaccine is able to decrease the
deleterious effects caused by the snake venom especially inflammation,
edema, and hemorrhage. This analysis was correlated with the LDH
activity diminution; the results showed that the encapsulation of Cc
venom reduced the alteration of membrane integrity.
Several studies reported that viper venom injected by diverse routes
causes local effects and systemic manifestations, characterized by an
important inflammatory response that leads to multiple vital organs
deficiencies and alteration of membrane integrity [10,12]. These results
suggest that the PLGA NPs entrapping venom were more immunogenic
and have the adjuvant potential to boost the immune response corre­
lated with low reactogenicity.
The encapsulation of Cc into PLGA nanoparticles confers an immu­
noprotection against over 6 LD50 of Cc native venom and over 5 LD50 of
Vl native venom, showing that vaccines based on PLGA nanoparticles
are efficient as carriers for vaccine delivery system [55]. The surviving
mice may have developed antibodies against venoms, and thus may be
able to withstand venom challenge. However, the immunized mice with
non-encapsulated venom could not withstand high doses of native
venoms. The protection of mice against Vipera lebetina challenge sug­
gests that the antibodies generated by immunization with a specific
snake venom can cross-react to certain toxins present in heterologous
venoms and neutralize their toxic effects [63,64].
These results would indicate that PLGA nanoparticles lead to a slow
and gradual release of the antigen while sustaining a continuous stim­
ulation of antigen-presenting cells (APCs) [37,65].

5. Conclusion
The immunization of mice with the developed nano-formulation can
reduce the toxicity of native venom and improve the specific immune
response, which confers protection of immunized mice from lethal doses
of snake venom. These characteristics make this delivery system a
promising vaccine to prevent the high-risk population, mainly children
in areas with important snakebites incidence and limited access to
health care.
