Abstract
The objective of our study was to prepare nano- and microparticles economically considering some practical parameters such as size and
encapsulation efﬁciency as well as ability of particle recovery. Bovine serum albumin (BSA) model protein was encapsulated by poly(d,l-lactide-
co-glycolide) (PLGA) using a multiple water-in-oil-in-water emulsion-solvent evaporation technique. The effect of three surfactants: polyvinyl
alcohol, poloxamer, and polyvinyl pyrrolidone, used in the outer water phase, on the properties of particles was investigated. The emulsiﬁer/PLGA
mass ratio played an important role in the preparation procedure of the particles. This ratio was found to be approximately 1 for polyvinyl alcohol
(PVA) if the aim was to formulate nanoparticles with narrow size distribution (<220 nm), high yield and good encapsulation efﬁciency (>90%).
Although, a ratio of 2:1 was sufﬁcient to produce submicron particles by poloxamer with high yield, more than 70% and 90% encapsulation
efﬁciency required minimum 4 and 10 emulsiﬁer/PLGA mass ratio, respectively. Five times more PVA and 10 times more poloxamer than the
PLGA mass were necessary to obtain nanoparticles which were easy to redisperse after centrifugation. Microparticles released more BSA than
nanoparticles prepared by PVA, however, the situation was reverse with poloxamer. Microparticles formulated by polyvinyl pyrrolidone (PVP)
showed the fastest in vitro release.

1. Introduction
Biodegradable nano- and microparticles can be useful
delivery devices for various active agents. Poly(d,l-lactide-
co-glycolide) is one of the most common biodegradable and
biocompatible polymer used for the controlled delivery of drugs
[1]. Bovine serum albumin is an often applied material to model
highly water soluble protein-type agents [2]. PLGA–BSA com-
posite nano- and microparticles can be fabricated efﬁciently by
the water-in-oil-in-water (w/o/w) double emulsion method [3].
Particle size, encapsulation efﬁciency and structure are the
most important formulation properties. Size and structure inﬂu-
ence the protein release kinetics and other pharmaceutical
and cellular uptake features of the particulate delivery system

and polypropylene glycol) is another promising surface active
agent [8], however, according to our knowledge its inﬂuence
on encapsulation efﬁciency, size and structure of nano- and
microparticles was not studied thoroughly till now. Polyvinyl
pyrrolidone (PVP), which is also a popular emulsiﬁer in the
pharmaceutics, was examined in even less paper [6]. It must
be noted that poloxamer, PVA or PVP are generally deﬁned as
emulsiﬁers in similar systems, although emulsiﬁers are used for

surface adsorption but by increasing the viscosity of water. The
sufﬁciently high viscosity prevents emulsiﬁed multiple drops
from interﬂowing.

and poloxamer and that of microparticles prepared by PVP was
also studied during a week in order to improve particles having
relatively fast release.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Materials
PLGA (50:50, Mw = 8000, Resomer® RG 502H) with free
carboxyl end groups was obtained from Boehringer Ingelheim,
Germany.BSAwasapresentfromTrigonBiotechnologicalLtd.,
Hungary. PVA (Mw = 30 000–70 000) and phosphate-buffered
saline (PBS, pH 7.4) were obtained from Sigma. Poloxamer
(Mw = 8350, BASF, Ludwigshafen, Germany, Lutrol® F68)
was a kind gift from Pharmaceutical Technology Depart-
ment, University of Szeged, Hungary. PVP (Mw = 350 000) was
obtained from Serva, Heidelberg, Germany. Dichloromethane
was obtained from Spektrum-3D, Hungary. The micro-BCA
protein assay reagents were purchased from Pierce, Bonn, Ger-
many.
2.2. Preparation of nano- and microparticles
Nano- and microparticles containing BSA were formu-
lated by a double emulsion-solvent evaporation technique [10].
Brieﬂy, BSA solution (20–100 mg/ml) in 0.25–1 ml phosphate-
buffered saline (PBS, pH 7.4) was added to 2.5–10.0 ml
dichloromethane containing 100–200 mg PLGA. This two-
phase system was emulsiﬁed for 15–60 s with a probe sonicator,
Model W-220 (Heat Systems-Ultrasonics, Inc.) at setting #6 in
an ice bath. The resulting water-in-oil emulsion was further
homogenized into 20 ml of an aqueous solution of emulsiﬁer
(PVA, poloxamer or PVP) for 30–180 s in an ice bath. The emul-
siﬁer concentration was changed in a wide range: 0–5% (w/v).
This second homogenization was achieved by a sonicator at set-
ting #8 or by a magnetic stirrer at 800 rpm. The dichloromethane
was evaporated under magnetic stirring at 800 rpm for 2 h. The
nano- and microparticles formed were isolated by centrifugation
(Beckman Optima Max-E) for 25 min at 50 000 and 30 000 × g,
respectively. Finally, the particles were washed with distilled
water to remove the residual surfactant and then lyophilized
(Leybold-Heraeus Lyovac GT2).
2.3. Determination of BSA loading
BSA loading was determined by analyzing both the super-
natantfortheproteinthatwasnotencapsulatedandtheentrapped
protein in the particles. The protein content of the particles
was measured by a Biuret method relying on the reduction of
Cu2+ by proteins in alkaline solution at 20 ◦C. As we know, this
method was not used for this purpose before. Brieﬂy, 20 mg of
the lyophilized particles was dissolved in 3 ml of 1N NaOH.
For the calibration, unloaded PLGA particles were prepared
and hydrolysed under the same conditions for 2 h and known
quantities of BSA was added to each of them. One milliliter
of Biuret reagent (30 mM KI, 100 mM K–Na–tartarate, 30 mM
CuSO4, 3.8 M NaOH) [11] was added to each 3 ml extract and
the absorbance was quantiﬁed spectrophotometrically at 546 nm
(Pharmacia LKB, Biochrom 4060) after 15 min at room temper-
ature.
The amount of protein, that had not been encapsulated, was
measured in the supernatant after centrifugation by the micro-
BCA (bicinchoninic acid) protein assay (Pierce Biotechnology,
Inc.) [12]. In practice, 400 l sample containing protein and
emulsiﬁer, and 400 l reagent were mixed and incubated for 1 h
at 60 ◦C. The corresponding solvent without protein was used
as a blank. After cooling the mixtures to room temperature for
20 min, the absorbance of it was evaluated at 562 nm.
2.4. Particle size distribution
Particles were sized by laser scattering using a Mastersizer
2000 (Malvern Instruments, Malvern, UK) at 20 ◦C. The average
particle size was expressed in volume mean diameter.
2.5. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM)
Lyophilized particles redispersed in distilled water were
dropped onto the grid and dried under room temperature. Then,
they were vacuum-coated for 3 min with a mixture of gold
and palladium and examined for morphology with a Philips
XL-30 environmental scanning electron microscope (ESEM) at
25 kV.
2.6. In vitro release of protein
Approximately 40 mg of nano- and microparticles were incu-
bated with 8 ml of phosphate-buffered saline with 0.03% (w/v)
sodium azide as preservative aliquots in a 15-ml Falcon tube.
The temperature of air-bath incubator was maintained at 37 ◦C
with continuous agitation at 170 rpm. After an hour, 4 ml of each
sample was centrifuged at 50 000 × g and the clear supernatant
of the release medium was withdrawn and the pellet was resus-
pended by fresh phosphate-buffered saline. This procedure was
repeated every day with 1 ml of the samples. The supernatant

3. Results and discussion
Several batches of PLGA–BSA particles were prepared by
the use of PVA, poloxamer and PVP emulsiﬁers. With the ﬁrst
twoagentsbothnano-andmicroparticleswereformulated,while
with PVP only microparticles were obtained.
3.1. Encapsulation efﬁciency
In order to determine the encapsulation efﬁciency of the par-
ticles, both the entrapped BSA and the fraction that remained in
the supernatant were measured. With this method the measuring
errors could be almost completely excluded.
The amount and distribution of protein associated with PLGA
particles prepared by the w/o/w method had been shown to
depend critically on formulation conditions such as the concen-
tration of protein, polymer and external aqueous phase surfactant
[6]. Lamprecht et al. [13] found that the encapsulation efﬁ-
ciency increased when the BSA concentration of the internal
aqueous phase decreased, and the optimal encapsulation efﬁ-
ciency reached 85% (at 25 mg/ml BSA concentration) by using
PVA for the preparation of PLGA–BSA nanoparticles. In our
experiments nanoparticles were formulated as well, in order
to investigate the change of entrapment efﬁciency as a func-
tion of initial BSA loading. When the BSA/PLGA mass ratio
was not higher than 0.1, our results show even higher efﬁcien-
cies (Fig. 1a), what can be explained by our lower initial BSA
amount. For example, Panyam et al. [10] used 100 mg/ml BSA
concentration in the inner water phase and got about 90% of
encapsulation efﬁciency, while in the work of Lamprecht et al.
[13] lower than 70% was found at the same BSA concentration.
This and our results suggest that the ratio of BSA with respect to
the PLGA is more important parameter than the protein concen-
trationintheinneraqueousphase.Itisalsostatedthatiftheinitial
BSA is not higher than 10% (w/w) of the initial PLGA amount,
higher than 90% of BSA can be encapsulated by using PVA as
an emulsiﬁer, when the PVA amount is suitable (see later).
In the case of poloxamer lower amount of BSA, maximum
7.5% (w/w) with respect to the PLGA mass could be encapsu-
lated efﬁciently. Encapsulating 10% (w/w) of BSA resulted in
about 20% decrease in entrapment efﬁciency compared to the
7.5% (w/w) of BSA.
The encapsulation efﬁciency was studied also maintaining
constant all parameters except for the emulsiﬁer (PVA) concen-
tration in the external water phase (Fig. 1b). The initial BSA
was 5% (w/w) of the initial PLGA amount and the PLGA was
1% (w/v) related to the external water phase. This latter param-
eter is extremely important considering the fact that when the
PVA concentration was only 0.25% (w/v), but the volume of the
outer water phase was doubled; this means that the PLGA con-
centration was only 0.5% (w/v) with respect to the external water
phase; the encapsulation efﬁciency was found to be as high as
in the case of 0.5% (w/v) PVA and 1% (w/v) PLGA both related
to the external water phase. That is to say, half of the PLGA

3.2. Size of particles
The size of the particles inﬂuences substantially their abil-
ity to act as drug delivery vehicles. 0.1-m-sized nanoparticles
had about 10-fold higher intracellular uptake in an in vitro cell
culture model compared to 10-m-sized microparticles [14].
<0.1 m nanoparticles have about 27-fold higher transfection
compared to >0.1 m size nanoparticles [15]. Nanoparticles
have a further advantage over microparticles, because they are
better suited for intravenous delivery. Since the smallest capil-
laries in the body are 5–6 m in diameter, particles distributed
into the bloodstream must be much smaller than 5 m and they
must not form aggregates [16]. A not negligible beneﬁt of parti-
cles, which are smaller than 220 nm, is that they could be easily
sterilized by ﬁltration as the size of bacteria and viruses is bigger.
PVA has been the most commonly used emulsiﬁer for the for-
mulation of PLGA nanoparticles, because the particles formed
using this emulsiﬁer are relatively uniform and smaller in size
[17]. This was also conﬁrmed by our measurements. Table 1
summarizes the size variation of particles as a function of the

Several studies emphasized that the nanoparticles pre-
pared by PVA emulsiﬁer are easy to redisperse in aqueous
medium [17,18]. Nevertheless, it must be noted that Lemoine
and Preat [18] applied PVA of different molecular weight
(Mw = 13 000–23 000) which could inﬂuence the properties of
the particles formed. On the contrary, in the beginning our
examinations showed that the particles were strongly adhered
to each other and even after ultrasonic treatment, coarse aggre-
gates maintained (Fig. 2). This was observed up to 2.5% (w/v)
PVA concentration; however, using 5% (w/v) PVA the nanopar-
ticles were really easy to redisperse. This means that huge excess
of PVA is necessary to gain nanoparticles that can be redispersed
after centrifugation. This can be also concluded from the referred
papers. Although, the authors of the review article [17] used only
2.5% (w/v) PVA in the external water phase, their PLGA con-
centration was much smaller (0.375%, w/v) than ours (1%, w/v)
[10]. Lemoine and Preat [18] worked only with 0.4% (w/v) PVA,
but their PLGA concentration (with respect also to the external
water phase mass) was very low: about 0.1% (w/v). It can be
stated from these studies and ours, minimum four to ﬁve times
mass excess of PVA is needed to form particles that are easy to
redisperse. It is noted that centrifugation of the samples, thus,

3.3. Release properties
As the particle size is reduced, surface area to volume ratio
increases, resulting in a large surface area, which is expected
to result in higher in vitro release of the encapsulated therapeu-
tic agent. However, PLGA nano- and microparticles formulated
using PVA as an emulsiﬁer generally tend to have a certain
amount of residual PVA associated with the particle surface
and this PVA is unwashable [19]. The amount of PVA released
into the incubation medium was higher for 0.1 m particles
compared to the other sizes. The higher amount of PVA asso-
ciated with 0.1 m particle could inﬂuence the degradation of
the polymer and also the release of the encapsulated protein.
Surface-associated PVA could form a barrier to the penetration
of the solvent into the particle leading to lowered degrada-

For example, faster polymer degradation should theoretically
enhance the release of the encapsulated therapeutic agent.
However, faster polymer degradation could also result in the
accumulation of acidic monomers and oligomers that could lead
to protein aggregation and non-release of the protein. Simi-
larly, smaller size of nanoparticles presents a large surface area
for the protein to diffuse out more rapidly compared to that
from microparticles. However, smaller-sized particles have been
found to contain more surface-associated PVA, which could
present a signiﬁcant barrier to the outward diffusion of the
encapsulated protein [10].

4. Conclusions
BSA-loaded PLGA nano- and microparticles were prepared
economically by using different amounts of PVA, poloxamer

or PVP as emulsiﬁers of external aqueous phase in the w/o/w
method. PVA and poloxamer were efﬁcient in synthesizing both
submicron sized- and microparticles, however, only PVA was
usefultoproduceparticlesexclusivelysmallerthan220 nm.PVP
was suitable only for microparticles formation. PVA was found
to be the most efﬁcient emulsiﬁer considering both the encap-
sulation efﬁciency and the size reducing effect. Preparation of
nanoparticles, which are easy to redisperse after the removal of
the surfactant, demanded high excess of PVA or poloxamer. As a
consequence of these results, it can be also concluded that from
the studied three surfactants only PVA could be suitable to pre-
pare nanoparticles with sufﬁciently small size for intravenous
administration without further size separation.
Microparticles showed higher BSA release than nanopar-
ticles formulated by PVA. Probably, the release proﬁle was
determined primarily by the structure of the particles. The sit-
uation was reversed by poloxamer emulsiﬁer, which might not
inﬂuence the protein release of nanoparticles adsorbing on the
surface. Particles fabricated by PVP released most of the encap-
sulated BSA during 1 week.
It is established that the circumstances adjusted in this paper
canbeusedforpreparingPLGAparticleswithotherhighlywater
soluble protein than BSA, leastways for getting particles with
similar size and encapsulation efﬁciency, as these properties are
mostly inﬂuenced by the emulsiﬁer. Surely the release proﬁle
of each protein needs distinct investigations. The results of this
paper can substantially facilitate the selection of conditions for
preparing protein containing composite particles.
