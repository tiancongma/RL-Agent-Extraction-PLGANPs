A B S T R A C T
Recent advances in understanding AlzheimerвҖҷs disease (AD) suggest the possibility of an infectious etiology, with
Porphyromonas gingivalis emerging as a prime suspect in contributing to AD. P. gingivalis may invade systemic
circulation via weakened oral/intestinal barriers and then cross the blood-brain barrier (BBB), reaching the brain
and precipitating AD pathology. Based on the proposed links between P. gingivalis and AD, a prospective
approach is the development of an oral nanovaccine containing P. gingivalis antigens for mucosal delivery.
Targeting the gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT), the nanovaccine may elicit both mucosal and systemic
immunity, thereby hampering P. gingivalis ability to breach the oral/intestinal barriers and the BBB, respectively.
The present study describes the optimization, characterization, and in vitro evaluation of a candidate chitosan-
coated poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA-CS) nanovaccine containing a P. gingivalis antigen extract. The
nanocarrier was prepared using the double emulsion solvent evaporation method and optimized for selected
experimental factors, e.g. PLGA amount, surfactant concentration, w1/o phase ratio, applying a d-optimal staВӯ
tistical design to target the desired physicochemical criteria for its intended application. After nanocarrier
optimization, the nanovaccine was characterized in terms of particle size, polydispersity index (PdI), О¶-potential,
encapsulation efficiency (EE), drug loading (DL), morphology, and in vitro release profile, as well as for
mucoadhesivity, stability under simulated gastrointestinal conditions, antigen integrity, in vitro cytotoxicity and
uptake using THP-1 macrophages.
The candidate PLGA-CS nanovaccine demonstrated appropriate physicochemical, mucoadhesive, and antigen
release properties for oral delivery, along with acceptable levels of EE (55.3 Вұ 3.5 %) and DL (1.84 Вұ 0.12 %).
The integrity of the encapsulated antigens remained uncompromised throughout NPs production and simulated
gastrointestinal exposure, as confirmed by SDS-PAGE and Western blotting analyses. Furthermore, the nanoВӯ
vaccine showed effective in vitro uptake, while exhibiting low cytotoxicity. Taken together, these findings unВӯ
derscore the potential of PLGA-CS NPs as carriers for adequate antigen mucosal delivery, paving the way for
further investigations into their applicability as vaccine candidates against P. gingivalis.
1. Introduction
AlzheimerвҖҷs disease (AD) is a neurodegenerative disorder characВӯ
terized by progressive cognitive decline, usually beginning with
impairment in the ability to form recent memories, but inevitably
affecting all intellectual functions and leading to complete dependence
for basic functions of daily life, and premature death (AlzheimerвҖҷs AsВӯ
sociation, 2023). Ranked as the seventh-leading cause of death in the
USA in 2021, AD accounts for 60вҖ“80 % of all cases of dementia
(AlzheimerвҖҷs Association, 2023), being the most prevalent neurodeВӯ
generative disorder in the world, with a tendency to increase (Gauthier
et al., 2022). It is not only a public health problem but also an economic
burden, with no world-accepted and effective disease-modifying or
curative treatment yet established (AlzheimerвҖҷs Association, 2023).
Although the mechanisms leading to AD are not completely understood,
evidence suggests the disease is caused by a combination of genetic,
lifestyle, and environmental factors that affect the brain over time
(Livingston et al., 2020). One such factor suggests an infectious etiology

for AD (Seaks and Wilcock, 2020). Possible mechanisms involve infilВӯ

AD. Pathogens may cross a weakened oral/intestinal barrier to the
vascular circulation and then overcome the blood-brain barrier (BBB),
reaching the brain and causing neurological damage by eliciting neuВӯ
roinflammation, or, alternatively, cause low grade chronic inflammation
and subsequent neuroinflammation from the periphery (Panza et al.,
2019). Multiple animal and human studies have shown an association
between periodontitis and AD (Choi et al., 2019; Ilievski et al., 2018;
Hao et al., 2022), and demonstrated that periodontitis patients present a
significantly higher risk of AD, compared with those without periВӯ

isms that synergistically influence the progression of periodontitis
(Arora et al., 2014), Porphyromonas gingivalis has been particularly
implicated as a probable contributor for the development of AD (Dominy
et al., 2019; Tang et al., 2021). Featuring a vast array of virulence facВӯ
tors, e.g., arginine- (RgpA, RgpB) and lysine-specific (Kgp) gingipains,
lipopolysaccharide, fimbriae, outer membrane vesicles, etc., P. gingivalis
is endowed of particular potency to influence AD pathophysiology by
those mechanisms (Olsen and Singhrao, 2019), activating specific
pathways to produce the full spectrum of AD-related pathological feaВӯ
tures in the brain, including neuroinflammation, diffuse and neuritic
extracellular beta-amyloid plaques, and intracellular neurofibrillary
tangles accompanied by reactive microgliosis/astrogliosis, and neuroВӯ
degeneration (Ilievski et al., 2018).
Therefore, a new framework for the prevention and treatment of AD
has emerged, underscoring the importance of managing periodontitis to
mitigate the onset and progression of AD (Panza et al., 2019; Harding
et al., 2017). In this context, we hypothesize that vaccination against
P. gingivalis may be an innovative, feasible approach to break the link
between periodontitis and AD. While all vaccines proposed to date
against P. gingivalis have exclusively targeted periodontitis (Jong and
van der Reijden, 2014; OвҖҷBrien-Simpson et al., 2016), the suggested
mechanisms for P. gingivalis penetration in the systemic circulation and
the brain are an opportunity for developing a prophylactic vaccine
against AD. Our proposal focuses on developing an oral nanovaccine
containing P. gingivalis extract antigens for mucosal delivery exploiting
the mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue (MALT) components, particuВӯ
larly the gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT), for eliciting protective
mucosal and systemic immune responses (Gaspar et al., 2016). With this
purpose, we will trigger immunity by oral vaccination attempting to
break not only P. gingivalis ability to cross the BBB (systemic humoral
and cellular response), but also to avoid the crossing of the weakened
oral/intestinal barriers (mucosal immunity).
An effective delivery system for P. gingivalis antigens should preserve
antigen integrity, enhance its own permeation across the mucosal barВӯ
rier, and ensure optimal uptake by antigen-presenting cells (APCs) in the
GALT. In this study, biodegradable/biocompatible polymers such as
poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) and chitosan (CS) were used to
design the intended nanovaccine. PLGA is an aliphatic polyester charВӯ
acterized by its hydrolytic instability, which promotes timely degradaВӯ
tion upon contact with biological fluids (Frizzell and Woodrow, 2020;
Amin and Boateng, 2023). PLGA nanoparticles (NPs) can be coated with
CS, a naturally occurring cationic polysaccharide with hydrophilic,
mucoadhesive, and permeation-enhancing properties (Sangnim et al.,
2023), to promote enhanced adhesion and penetration through the
negatively charged, hydrophilic mucus layer lining the intestinal
epithelium, thereby resisting removal by the continuous mucus flow in
the gastrointestinal tract (Ensign et al., 2012). These properties allow
CS-coated PLGA (PLGA-CS) NPs to access the M cells of the GALT and be
translocated from the gut lumen to the underlying APCs (Borges and
Borchard, 2013). PLGA and CS degrade into lactic and glycolic acid, and
glucosamine, respectively, which are naturally metabolized by the body,
minimizing long-term toxicity concerns (Makadia and Siegel, 2011;
Kean and Thanou, 2010). Biodegradation ensures controlled release and
eventual clearance of the delivery system, enhancing safety and
effectiveness (Makadia and Siegel, 2011). Biodegradable micro-/Вӯ
nanosystems, including PLGA-CS NPs, have been thoroughly investiВӯ
gated as vaccine adjuvants to elicit local and systemic immune responses
via mucosal immunization (Azevedo et al., 2006; HF Florindo et al.,
2009; Caetano et al., 2017; Jin et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2014), funcВӯ
tioning on the principles of antigen protection from proteolytic enzymes,
improved access to MALT, efficient phagocytosis, and sustained antigen
release with a depot effect suitable for a prolonged stimulation of the
immune system (Azevedo et al., 2006; Jin et al., 2019). Multiantigenic
nanovaccines based on these polymers have proven to be safe and
effective for a variety of diseases (Azevedo et al., 2006; HF Florindo
et al., 2009; Caetano et al., 2017; HF Florindo et al., 2009; Florindo
et al., 2008; Heng et al., 2023), taking advantage of their intrinsic
immunoadjuvant (Jin et al., 2019) and immunomodulating properties in
terms of Th1/Th2 balance (HF Florindo et al., 2009; Caetano et al.,
2017; HF Florindo et al., 2009; Florindo et al., 2008). Antigens adsorbed
or encapsulated in NPs induce strong protective immunity against
experimental infection (Azevedo et al., 2006) with a clear advantage
over the molecular adjuvants so far proposed for gingipain-based vacВӯ
cines (Jong and van der Reijden, 2014; OвҖҷBrien-Simpson et al., 2016).
Furthermore, the use of nanovaccines based on a bacterial extract
associated to polymeric NPs has shown to be a cost-effective approach
compared to recombinant subunit vaccines (Azevedo et al., 2006; HF
Florindo et al., 2009; HF Florindo et al., 2009; Florindo et al., 2008).
This approach, yet unexplored for P. gingivalis, may generate immune
responses against multiple bacterial targets, thus increasing efficacy in
preventing infection.
The present study aimed at the optimization, characterization, and
preliminary in vitro evaluation of a candidate PLGA-CS nanovaccine for
oral delivery of P. gingivalis extract antigens to the GALT, combining the
protective and controlled release properties of PLGA with the mucosal
targeting ability of CS. The proposed nanocarrier was prepared by
double emulsion solvent evaporation method and optimized for selected
formula parameters following a Design-of-Experiment (DoE) approach
to target NPs with suitable physicochemical characteristics for oral deВӯ
livery and subsequent antigen encapsulation. Physicochemical and in
vitro characterization studies were performed, including mucoadheВӯ
sivity, antigen in vitro release profile, stability under simulated gastroВӯ
intestinal
conditions,
antigen
integrity,
biocompatibility
and
internalization of the nanovaccine in a target cellular environment.

2.1.2. Preparation of P. gingivalis extract antigens
P. gingivalis strain DSM 20,709 (ATCC 33,277), obtained from the
German Collection of Microorganisms and Cell Cultures (DSMZ, GerВӯ
many), was grown under anaerobic culturing conditions in Wilking
Chalgreen Medium II (Condalab, Spain) supplemented with 1 g/L soВӯ
dium bicarbonate, 0.5 g/L of l-cysteine, 0.25 g/L sodium thioglycolate,
and 0.5 mg/L of resazurin as redox indicator at 37 в—ҰC to attain a cell

dehyde 1 % (v/v) for 24 h at 37 в—ҰC with agitation. Cells were harvested
by centrifugation at 10,000 Г— g, 4 в—ҰC for 20 min, washed twice with
sterile 50 mM NaHEPES 200 mM NaCl buffer (pH 7.0), and the pellet
from 500 mL cell culture broth was resuspended in 10 mL of the same
buffer and frozen at вҲ’20 в—ҰC until further use. The frozen suspension of
inactivated bacterial cells was thawed at room temperature (RT), then
10 mL were transferred to a flask and sonicated in an ice bath using a
Branson 250 Analog Sonifier at 70 % amplitude and 30 % duty cycle.
The sonication was performed for 2.5 min and repeated six times with 1-
min intervals. After sonication, the cell lysates were centrifuged at
12,000 Г— g, 4 в—ҰC for 20 min, the supernatant collected, and its protein
concentration determined using the MicroBCAв„ў Protein Assay Kit
(Pierce, USA). This supernatant, containing the P. gingivalis antigens
extracted from the lysate, was then diluted to a concentration of 3 mg/
mL and stored in aliquots at вҲ’20 в—ҰC until needed.

2.1.5. Preparation of P. gingivalis extract-loaded chitosan-coated PLGA
NPs
PLGA-CS NPs containing encapsulated P. gingivalis extract antigens
(extract-loaded PLGA-CS NPs) at different concentrations were prepared
by adjusting the double emulsion (w1/o/w2) solvent evaporation
method previously applied to the encapsulation of the model protein
PAP (detailed in Section 2.1.3.) to the final optimized conditions derived
from the DoE-based experiments: PLGA amount 30 mg, PVA concenВӯ
tration 1.25 % (w/v), w1/o phase ratio 0.3. NPs were prepared across
PLGA:antigen ratios ranging from 120:1 to 30:1 (w/w), adding four
different initial amounts of extract antigens (250, 500, 750, and 1000

harvesting by centrifugation, the supernatant was preserved for quanВӯ
tification of the non-encapsulated extract antigens. NPs in the pellet
were resuspended with filtered purified water and freeze-dried. Each
formulation was prepared in triplicate. Controls consisted of empty
PLGA-CS NPs produced under identical conditions.
2.1.6. Nanoparticle characterization
Particle size and PdI were measured by dynamic light scattering
(DLS) using a Malvern Nano S (Malvern Instruments, UK) with the
average size being determined by cumulative analysis. The О¶-potential of
the NPs was measured by electrophoretic light scattering (ELS) using a
Malvern Nano Z (Malvern Instruments, UK). Each sample was
appropriately diluted with filtered purified water in a proper cuvette
and then measured in triplicate at 25 в—ҰC.
To assess preliminarily the mucoadhesivity of the NPs, additional
О¶-potential measurements were conducted upon NPs dilution 1:1 with
water (control) or with mucin dispersion (2.0 mg/mL).
The morphological analysis of NPs was performed by transmission
electron microscopy (TEM). The samples were prepared by mounting a
drop of the NPs dispersion onto a carbon-coated copper grid, where they

3.1.1.2. Response surface contour plotting
Information derived from the regression models was graphically
expanded to three-dimensional (3D) response surface contour plots,
providing a visual representation of the combined effects of the formula
parameters on the particle size, PdI, and О¶-potential responses (Fig. 1).
Considering the coefficients of the models it is noted that all the studied
formula parameters significantly affected the different physicochemical
characteristics of PAP-loaded PLGA-CS NPs.
Specifically, PLGA amount and w1/o phase ratio positively and linВӯ
early affected both NPs size (Fig. 1A and B) and PdI (Fig. 1C and D)
implying that within the tested range, an increase in these parameters
tends to produce larger NPs with a broader size distribution. These
findings are consistent with the literature and may be explained by an
increased viscosity of both the organic phase and intermediate primary
emulsion, which hinders their dispersibility with the aqueous phases
during emulsification, hence promoting the formation of larger, less
uniformly sized NPs (Ramalho et al., 2021).
On the contrary, PVA concentration exerted a negative effect on
particle size and PdI, in line with earlier published results (Ramalho
et al., 2021; FeczkВҙo et al., 2011). As a surfactant, PVA enhances emulВӯ
sion formation and stabilization by reducing the interfacial tension beВӯ
tween the two immiscible phases, leading to smaller emulsion droplets
with a more uniform distribution. In addition, the higher viscosity of the
aqueous phase due to increased PVA concentration prevents emulsified
droplets from coalescing and becoming larger (Tadros, 2013). Although
both mechanisms provide a clear explanation of the effect of PVA conВӯ
centration on particle size, there are nuances to consider. While at lower
PVA concentrations each molecule of PVA might be crucial in stabilizing
the NPs, as the concentration increases, the particle surface might get
saturated with PVA molecules. After this saturation point, the excess
PVA may not be as effective in further reducing the particle size, leading
to a non-linear relationship between PVA concentration and particle size
(Tadros, 2013). This relationship was indeed observed in this study вҖ“ as
evidenced by a positive quadratic coefficient of 14.046 вҖ“ with similar
PVA concentration ranges and curvature patterns of previous reports
(FeczkВҙo et al., 2011; Chen and Wang, 2019), showing a decrease in NPs
size from 0.5 to ~1.4 % PVA, followed by an increase from 1.4 to 2 %.
The observation of an actual increase in NPs size, rather than a plateau,
could be attributed to an increased viscosity of the aqueous phase at
elevated PVA concentrations. Although high viscosity can stabilize
emulsified droplets post-emulsification, it might also hinder the initial
disintegration of larger droplets into smaller ones during the emulsifiВӯ
cation process itself (Tadros, 2013), particularly when combined with
high levels of PLGA amount and w1/o ratio, as reflected by the interВӯ
action terms identified in this study.

CS NPs, a moderate yet positive О¶-potential was detected. This was
anticipated given the positively charged amino groups of CS, thus

3.1.1.3. Prediction and validation of optimal formula parameters
When considering the desired physicochemical features of NPs for
optimal mucosal delivery, particle size emerges as a critical attribute
determining their ability to penetrate mucosal barriers and interact with
underlying cells. Smaller NPs are generally more effective at penetrating
the mucus layer and being taken up by M cells and enterocytes (He et al.,
2012). A PdI value below 0.3 is often regarded as desirable for ensuring
consistent behavior of the NPs in terms of uptake, distribution, and
release (Rafiei and Haddadi, 2019). In the context of the PLGA-CS
nanocarrier, a moderately positive О¶-potential may be desirable, as it
confirms the presence of CS on the surface of the NPs, while accounting
for colloidal stability. Surface charge can affect mucoadhesion, with
positively charged particles adhering better to the negatively charged
mucosal layer, thereby promoting retention and subsequent uptake by
the epithelium (Ensign et al., 2012). When assessing interactions with
APCs, a balance is to be considered: while highly positively charged NPs

physicochemical characteristics for their intended application.
The NPs resemble the morphological attributes of similar systems
(Aldawsari et al., 2020; Pandit et al., 2017), showing a reasonably hoВӯ
mogeneous size distribution вҖ“ more evident at 5 kx magnification вҖ“ and a
spherical shape under TEM analysis (Fig. 3). No noticeable differences
were noticed between empty and extract-loaded PLGA-CS NPs. Particle
size observed in the micrographs (223.5 Вұ 68.3 nm for empty PLGA-CS
NPs and 242.2 Вұ 70.2 nm for extract-loaded PLGA-CS NPs) fairly
aligned with the DLS measurements, confirming the NPs meet the
desired size for oral delivery.
The EE and DL were determined across PLGA:antigen ratios ranging
from 120:1 to 30:1 (w/w), revealing a consistent increase in DL with
higher initial antigen amounts. This indicates that, within the tested
amounts, the NPs have not reached saturation (Table 5 and Fig. 4).
While DL could be further optimized by adjusting production parameВӯ
ters for improved EE, the current DL values may be sufficient when
considering the practical aspects of oral immunization studies.

Besides size and DL specifications, the extract-loaded PLGA-CS NPs
were also designed to facilitate their interaction with the mucosal surВӯ
faces, taking advantage of the well-acknowledged mucoadhesive and
permeability-enhancing properties of CS (Ilium, 1998). CS-coated PLGA
NPs were produced by dissolving CS in the external aqueous phase in
which NPs formation occurs. This approach ensures CS molecules anВӯ
chor to the surface of the PLGA NPs through physical entanglement
between polymer chains, which is further enhanced by the ionic interВӯ
action between the negatively charged PLGA matrix and the positively
charged CS (Vila et al., 2002). So, the mucoadhesive capability of CS
coating was evaluated using mucin, an essential glycoprotein compoВӯ
nent of mucus. For that purpose, О¶-potential measurements were perВӯ
formed on empty and extract-loaded PLGA-CS NPs after dilution with
either water or mucin dispersion. As detailed in Table 6, a marked
reduction in О¶-potential was observed in both formulations exclusively
upon incubation with mucin, suggesting mucin adsorbed onto the NPs
due to the charge dynamics between the positively charged amino
groups of CS and the negatively charged sialic acid residues in mucin
(Silva et al., 2017). Such interactions are likely to enhance NPsвҖҷ
adherence to the intestinal mucus layer, improving the chances of upВӯ
take across the epithelium (Ensign et al., 2012). Nevertheless, these are
preliminary observations derived from a simple experimental setup and
further investigation is needed using more detailed mucoadhesion and
permeation studies (e.g., intestinal permeability in co-cultures of Caco-2,
Raji B, and HT29-MTX cells (De Lima et al., 2018)).
3.1.3. In vitro release study of P. gingivalis extract antigens
The release profile is characterized by a biphasic pattern, revealing
an initial fast release with 19.9 Вұ 1.5 % of the total P. gingivalis extract
proteins released within the first 24 h, followed by a more sustained and
slower release phase, reaching a cumulative release of 32.8 Вұ 3.5 % over
28 days (Fig. 5), corresponding to a total of 30.5 Вөg of antigens released.
The observed antigen release is likely adequate to elicit the expected
immune response, as it aligns with the mucosal immunization schedule
reported elsewhere (4 Вөg antigen, 3 times at 2-week intervals) for a
flagellin-adjuvanted gingipain-based recombinant subunit vaccine,
inducing both local secretory IgA and systemic IgG responses (Puth
et al., 2017).
While drug release profiles from PLGA-CS NPs vary widely in the
literature, largely due to the numerous variables influencing biodegraВӯ
dation (Makadia and Siegel, 2011), previous studies using PLGA with
analogous molecular weights and molar ratios of the lactic and glycolic вҖ“
factors known to significantly impact the biodegradation rate вҖ“ fairly
match the rate and extent of drug release observed in this study
(Azevedo et al., 2006; DurВҙan-Lobato et al., 2015). PLGA NPs typically
exhibit an initial fast release within the first few hours (often вүӨ24 h),
mainly due to the release of drug molecules located at their surface,
followed by a more controlled and sustained release of the drug moleВӯ
cules entrapped within the polymeric matrix (Ramalho et al., 2021).
When used to coat PLGA NPs, CS can provide additional control over
drug release, acting as an additional physical barrier to drug diffusion
(De Lima et al., 2018), or even promoting stronger interactions between
the NPs surface and protein drugs (HF Florindo et al., 2009). This effect
of CS coating may, however, be advantageous for preventing premature
antigen release in the gastrointestinal tract, ensuring that a higher
amount of antigen is retained within the NPs until they interact with the
APCs in the GALT. Therefore, the sustained protein release profile is
suitable for mucosal vaccination, including a prolonged stimulation of
the immune system.

3.1.5. In vitro cell viability studies
The in vitro biocompatibility of empty and extract-loaded PLGA-CS
NPs was evaluated using differentiated THP-1 cells, a recommended
model for APCs (Chanput et al., 2014), subjecting both M0 and M1
macrophages to a series of assays performed consecutively within the
same plate: PI uptake assay to assess membrane integrity, comВӯ
plemented by resazurin and MTT reduction assays to evaluate cellular
metabolic activity. The PI uptake assay on both macrophage phenotypes
revealed significant differences between the negative control and NPs
formulated with higher initial amounts of P. gingivalis antigens, particВӯ
ularly 500, 750, and 1000 Вөg, suggesting a potential cell membrane
compromise in the presence of NPs, yet less severe than in cells treated
with SDS (positive control), an anionic surfactant known to induce cell
lysis (Fig. 7A and B). Furthermore, no significant differences were disВӯ
cerned between groups with different antigen amounts. On the other
hand, the resazurin and MTT reduction assays (Fig. 7C-F) showed that
both macrophage phenotypes retained high levels of cell viability (>80
% in relation to the negative control) after 24 h incubation with all NPs
samples, which is consistent with previous cell viability studies on
PLGA-CS NPs (De Lima et al., 2018; DurВҙan-Lobato et al., 2015) and
aligns with standard definitions for non-cytotoxicity (Faqi, 2017).
Furthermore, the incorporation of extract antigens into PLGA-CS NPs at
different initial amounts of antigens did not affect cell viability in
comparison to the blank formulation, implying a lack of cytotoxic effects
attributable to the antigens.
The apparent variations in results between PI uptake and resazurin/
MTT assays emphasize the importance of employing complementary
assays when evaluating NPs cytotoxicity (Kamiloglu et al., 2020). While
PI can only bind and label DNA from cells with compromised memВӯ
branes, being therefore an indicator of membrane integrity (Rosenberg
et al., 2019), both resazurin and MTT are only reduced to their respecВӯ
tive optically detectable end-products in the presence of different active
cellular oxidoreductase enzymes, thereby providing a broad perspective
on the metabolic activity of cells (Stockert et al., 2018; Lavogina et al.,
2022). In this study, the results from PI uptake assay suggest that THP-1
cells experienced some level of membrane perturbation in the presence
of NPs. Yet, the maintenance of metabolic activity, as demonstrated by
the resazurin and MTT results, indicates the cells remained functionally
active despite possible membrane stress instigated by the NPs. ConsidВӯ
ering that numerous factors have been reported to influence the outВӯ
comes of PI uptake assays, often leading to an underestimation of cell
viability (Rosenberg et al., 2019), one might hypothesize about the
potential influence on such outcomes of transient changes in membrane
integrity during active NPs uptake. However, it is important to note that
persistent membrane compromise, if not transient or repairable, evenВӯ
tually leads to cell death (Zhang et al., 2018). To differentiate better
between transient cellular responses and genuine cytotoxic events,
future investigations should focus on extended observation periods and
the inclusion of additional cytotoxicity markers. Nevertheless, after this
preliminary analysis and recognizing the superior sensitivity of resaВӯ
zurin and MTT assays over dye exclusion methods for assessing cell
viability (AslantГјrk, 2018), the evidence herein presented suggests a
favorable biocompatibility profile for both empty and P. gingivalis
extract-loaded PLGA-CS NPs.

4. Conclusions
The present study elucidated the potential of the DoE approach to
effectively optimize a PLGA-CS nanocarrier for the subsequent encapВӯ
sulation of P. gingivalis extract antigens, resulting in a stable, reproВӯ
ducible nanovaccine with suitable physicochemical/mucoadhesive
properties and unaltered antigenic integrity. The in vitro release profile
of antigens from the optimized PLGA-CS NPs demonstrated a sustained
release profile over a period of 28 days. Furthermore, in vitro assays on
differentiated THP-1 macrophages confirmed the ability of the proposed
nanovaccine to interact with APCs, demonstrating effective in vitro upВӯ
take, while displaying minimal cytotoxicity.
To the best of our knowledge, this study is the first attempt for
developing a vaccine based on the encapsulation of P. gingivalis antigens
in polymeric NPs. While the proposed nanocarrier exhibited favorable
attributes for an adequate antigen mucosal delivery, complementary
qualitative uptake studies may provide further information regarding
the effectiveness of the nanocarrier for the delivery of P. gingivalis
mucosal vaccine. Further studies with this carrier are underway in
relevant peripheral APCs for characterization of uptake mechanisms,
intracellular traffic within phagocytic cells, and antigen presentation, to
understand immunity in terms of Th1/Th2 responses.
