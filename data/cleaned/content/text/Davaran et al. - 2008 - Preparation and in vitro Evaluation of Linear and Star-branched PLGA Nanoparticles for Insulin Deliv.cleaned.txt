of the biodegradable polymers. In the current investigation, the synthesis
and characterization of linear, poly(D,L-lactide-co-glycolide) (PLGA)-poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG-PLGA), star-branched b-cyclodextrin-PLGA (b-CD-PLGA),
and glucose-PLGA (Glu-PLGA) copolymers containing insulin as a model
peptide drug have been reported. Linear and star-branched copolymers of PLGA
were synthesized by bulk melt polymerization of the lactones (lactide and
glycolide) in the presence of PEG, glucose, or b-CD using Sn-octoate as catalyst.

To overcome these problems, two major modifications of the polymers
have been proposed, namely: (a) increasing the hydrophilicity of the
polymers, and (b) accelerating the degradation rate of linear polyesters
by branching PLA or PLGA. An example for the first approach is the
linear 'ABA' triblock copolymers consisting of the hydrophobic PLGA
and central hydrophilic poly ethylene glycol (PEG) as A and B blocks,
respectively. Such architecture appears to provide a more rapid swelling
characteristics and accelerated degradation rates . As for the
second concept, star-branched polyesters, brush like grafted PLGA,
and PLGAs with low molecular multifunctional alcohols have been
described .
In the current investigation, various PLGA based nanostructures
possessing linear and star-branched architecture were synthesized.

Synthesis and Characterization of PLGA-PEG Copolymers

Poly(ethylene glycol) was dried by azeotropic distillation in a flask
with toluene (3 times, 50 mL) under an atmosphere of nitrogen followed
by drying at 1308C under vacuum (5 mmHg). LA and GA monomers
(in the mole ratio of 3:1) were added to the flask followed by the addition
of stannous octoate (0.05 wt%) and the reaction mixture was heated
at 1508C under vacuum (10 mmHg). The progress of the reaction was
followed by GPC. After an appropriate time, the reaction was stopped
and the flask was cooled down to room temperature. The residue was
dissolved in cold water and heated at 808C to precipitate the polymer.
The supernatant was decanted and the polymer residue was re-dissolved
in cold water and heated to induce precipitation. The resulting polymer
was lyophilized (Christ Alpha 1-4, Germany).

Synthesis and Characterization of PLGA-glucose

Lactide (0.684 mol), glycolide (0.838 mol), and D(th)-glucose (2.2 mmol)
were placed in a flask and heated, while stirring to 1358C in an argon
atmosphere followed by addition of stannous octoate (0.05%). After
10 min, stirring was discontinued and the brown viscous mixture was
reacted further at 130-1408C for 10 h. After cooling 50 mL of methylene
dichloride was added. The mixture was dissolved and the solvent was
separated. The dark brown solution was treated with 25 mL of 5%
aqueous HCl solution to remove the catalyst. The solution was washed
with water and diluted with methylene dichloride. The solution was
added dropwise to 500 mL methanol at 258C. The product was filtered
and dried at 408C under vacuum.

Study of the Insulin Release from Nanoparticles

Release studies were conducted by incubating certain amounts
of particles in 5 mL of 0.1 M PBS (pH 7.4) at 378C using Unimax 1010
shaker-incubator (Heidolph, Germany). At appropriate time intervals,
individual samples were taken out and centrifuged at 10,000 rpm for
30 min. The supernatant was separated and assayed by HPLC to
determine the amount of insulin released from particles and the
particles pellets were redispersed in the same volume of release medium.

Insulin Temperature Stability Studies

The thermal stability of insulin was studied according to the methodology reported earlier . Briefly, 1 g of insulin loaded nanoparticles
dispersion was centrifuged at 10,000 rpm for 20 min, after which the
pellet was collected and incubated at 608C. At time intervals of 1, 2, 4, 8,
and 10 h, they were removed from the oven and 1 mL of PBS was added
and stored at 48C for 24 h to extract the insulin from the nanoparticles.
The sample was then centrifuged and the supernatants were filtered
and analyzed with HPLC to determine the remaining insulin in
the sample.

Synthesis and Structure of the Copolymers

The polymer structure used to formulate the nanoparticles can
strongly affect the properties and applications of nanoparticles. For each
application and drug, thus, the properties of the system should be
optimized for a given drug delivery application.
The purpose of using cyclic polyols, such as glucose and b-CD for
preparing branched biodegradable polyester was to provide a higher

affinity to insulin by maximizing complexation between polymer and
drug , upon which the loading efficiency of insulin may be favored.

by bulk melt polymerization of the lactones (lactide and glycolide) in
the presence of the PEG, glucose, or b-CD using Sn-octoate as catalyst.

Physicochemical Characterization of Nanoparticles

Shown in Figure 4 are the typical transmission and scanning electron
micrographs of the insulin loaded nanoparticles formulated using linear
and star-branched polyesters. The TEM and SEM images of the nanoparticles revealed relatively spherical morphology with smooth surface
and confirmed the size range of 150-300 nm (Figure 4(B), (D), and (F)).
The most common method used for the preparation of polymeric nanoparticles is the emulsion-solvent evaporation technique which has been
successfully used for encapsulating hydrophilic small drugs as well as
macromolecules including peptides/proteins . Dissolving a high
concentration of high molecular weight polymer in a mixture of
dichloromethane-acetone (80: 20) resulted in the spontaneous formation
of nanoparticles. The amount of stabilizer (PVA) had an important effect
on the properties of the nanoparticles. When the concentration of the
stabilizer was too low (50.5% w/v) aggregation of the particles accrued.
Alternatively, when larger amounts of the stabilizer were used (45% w/v),
the drug incorporation was reduced. Basically, overall 2% (w/v) of PVA
was ideal for creating smaller nanoparticles (i.e., 5400 nm).

## Conclusion
Bovine insulin was successfully encapsulated into the linear and starbranched PLGA nanoparticles with retention of insulin stability and the
nanoparticles preparation process was optimized to reduce the burst
effect and provide in vitro sustained-release. PLGA-b-CD nanoparticles
resulted in maximum loading efficiency (95%) with minimal particle
size (e.g., 120-350 nm) that also displayed the slowest drug release
profile. Based on the present findings, it is proposed that these novel
PLGA nanoparticles could be used as a carrier for improved-prolonged
delivery of peptide/protein based drugs, such as insulin, in particular
when they are used for oral delivery and targeting.
