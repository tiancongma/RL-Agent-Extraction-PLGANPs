Collection and preparation of plant extract
The Fagonia indica plant was collected from the village of Khushab, Pakistan. The aerial part of the plant was
washed three times with distilled water and shade-dried at room temperature. After drying, it was crushed into
a fine powder using an electric grinder and kept in an airtight bottle for future processing. To prepare the plant
extract, 30 g of powdered sample of FI were subjected to extraction using 300 ml of 100% ethanol (1:10) for
72 h. The mixture was stirred with a sterilized glass rod every 24 h and filtered using Whatman filter paper. This
filtrate was then concentrated using a SENCO(r) rotary evaporator and dried at 50 degC in an oven. The FI extract
was weighed and kept at 4 *C in glass vials for further use. On the day of the experiment, a series of working

2

concentrations  ug/ml were prepared from the stock solution (10 mg/ml) using twofold serial
dilution in 2% fetal bovine serum25.

Preparation of blank and drug-loaded PLGA nanoparticles
PLGA nanoparticles were prepared under optimal conditions using a single emulsion solvent evaporation
method26 The solubility parameters of PLGA and PVA were considered to optimize the nanoparticle formulation.
PVA is a hydrophilic polymer immediately soluble in water due to its ability to form hydrogen bonds. Acetone
was selected due to its ability to dissolve PLGA efficiently due to its intermediate polarity and ester affinity,
rapidly evaporates during nanoparticle formation, compatibility with the emulsification process using PVA27,28.
Additionally, acetone is widely accepted in pharmaceutical formulations due to its safety and ease of removal26.

Briefly, the organic phase was prepared by adding 50 mg PLGA in 3 mL acetone and then sonicated for
1 min. Next, the aqueous phase was prepared by adding 1.5% PVA in 10 ml of distilled water under stirring at
25 *C until a clear solution was obtained. The organic phase was added dropwise at a 0.5 mL/min rate into the
aqueous solution at room temperature under continuous stirring (400 rpm) until the organic solvent completely
evaporated. The PLGA NPs were obtained by centrifugation at 15,000 rpm and 4 *C for 15 min (Thermo Scientific,
Sorvall RC 6 Plus Centrifuge). The pellet was dispersed in the distilled water and washed 3 times to clear any
residual surfactant. The synthesized nanoparticles were stored at 4 *C for future use. To prepare the drug-loaded
PLGA NPs, PLGA (50 mg) and FI extract (10 mg) were added to 3 ml of acetone to get the organic phase29. Other
steps are the same as the above procedure. A 10 mg dose of the plant was selected after preliminary experiments
using various drug amounts (5, 10, 15 mg) to evaluate nanoparticle formulation (Supplementary file 1, Table S1).
A lower drug amount (5 mg) resulted in reduced drug loading and encapsulation efficiency, while a higher drug
amount (15 mg) led to an increase in particle size and PDI, making it less suitable for efficient drug delivery.
The 10 mg drug provided an optimal balance, yielding nanoparticles with appropriate particle size, PDI, and
encapsulation efficiency, making it the most suitable choice for the current study.

Qualitative phytochemical screening
The phytochemical analysis of FI extract was performed to identify secondary metabolites. Different
phytochemicals (alkaloids, saponins, flavonoids, phenols, reducing sugar, glycosides, proteins, and fats) were
estimated qualitatively by using standard procedures26.

Cell culture
A ductal breast cancer (BT-474) cell line was obtained from Khyber Medical University, Peshawar, and cultured
in a 25 cm2 tissue culture flask containing DMEM-F12 medium supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum
(FBS) and Penicillin/Streptomycin. The cell culture was incubated for 24 h in an incubator (5% CO2, 37 degC) to
ensure proper adhesion to the substratum. Once the cells reached 80-90% confluency, they were seeded into a
96-well plate and incubated further. When the cells in the plate acquired 80-90% confluency, the growth media
was discarded, and the cells were treated with varying concentrations of FI extract and FI-PLGA NPs. All cell
culture procedures were performed under sterile conditions in a Class 2 laminar flow hood.

Quantitative cellular uptake of FI and FI-PLGA NPs
The quantitative cellular uptake of FI and FI-PLGA NPs was examined by using a microplate reader (Bio-Tek,
ELX 800 model). Briefly, BT-474 cells were grown in 96 well plates with cell density 5 x 103 cells/well in a growth
medium and incubated for 24 h at 37 degC. After incubation, cells were treated with FI and FI-PLGA NPs at
different concentrations  ug/ml and incubated for different intervals  h. The free drug
residues were then removed by washing the cells 3 times with PBS. The optical absorbance was measured at
630 nm using a microplate reader to quantify the drug in the cells35.

Cellular uptake study by confocal microscope
The cellular uptake of free FI extract and FI-PLGA NPs was evaluated in BT-474 cells using confocal microscopy.
Briefly, BT-474 cells were grown on glass coverslips in a 6-well plate. Cells were then exposed to 25 ug/mL of FI
extract and FI-PLGA NPs and incubated for 2 h. Following incubation, cells were washed with PBS and fixed
with 2% paraformaldehyde (PFA) for 18 min in the dark. After washing with PBS, cells were stored in cold PBS at
4 degC until imaging. Confocal microscopy was performed using a 63 x oil immersion objective, with an excitation
wavelength of 417 nm36.

added30. The MTT solution was discarded, and DMSO (extracting solution) was added to each well after 4 h of
incubation. The yellow colour of the MTT solution is reduced to purple-coloured formazan by viable cells. The

Statistical analysis
All tests were analyzed in triplicate, and Microsoft Excel and Origin Pro 2023 were used to generate the charts
and graphs. All data from 3 independent experiments are shown as the mean +- standard deviation. The mean

Characterization of nanoparticles
The nanocarrier's size is a key factor that affects the treatment efficacy. The hydrodynamic size of prepared NPs
was measured using dynamic light scattering (Fig. 2). Blank PLGA NPs displayed a particle size of 192 nm,
PDI 0.087, and a negative charge of -21.7 mV. The nanoparticles exhibited a negative zeta potential, which is
attributed to the terminal carboxylic groups present in the polymer. The particle size, PDI, and zeta potential of
FI-PLGA NPs were measured as 217 nm, 0.093, and -30.5 mV, respectively. The increase in particle size, PDI, and
zeta potential attributed to FI extract successfully encapsulated into PLGA NPs.

within the PLGA matrix. The amount of free FI extract was determined by measuring the absorbance at 272 nm,
and the UV spectrum is provided in the Supplementary File 3 (Figure S1). A standard calibration curve was

The absorption spectra of blank, free FI extract, and FI encapsulated PLGA NPs were observed in the range

of Fagonia indica. FI extract showed characteristic peaks at 3298 cm-1, corresponding to -OH stretching, peaks

The XRD technique was used to characterize the structural properties of PLGA NPs, FI-PLGA NPs, and
FI extract. The XRD pattern of PLGA NPs is depicted in Fig. 4(b), in which no distinct peak was observed,
indicating the amorphous nature of the polymer. Furthermore, FI-PLGA NPs also showed no characteristic peak
due to the amorphous nature of the drug. A reduction in spectrum intensity was detected as compared to the FI
extract, possibly due to the entrapment of the extract in the PLGA matrix.

Drug release study
The FI extract's release from PLGA NPs was assessed in PBS (pH = 7.4) (Fig. 5a). The pH 7.4 was selected to
evaluate the release profile of the nanoparticles in a relevant biological environment. This helps in predicting
the drug behavior in systemic circulation before reaching the tumor microenvironment. After 96 h, a maximum
release of 99.08% FI extract was achieved. An initial burst release of 28.8% FI extract from PLGA nanoparticles
occurred within the first 12 h, likely due to drug molecules present on or near the nanoparticle surface. This
surface-bound drug rapidly diffused into the surrounding medium upon adding nanoparticles. Following the
initial burst, a slower and more sustained release was obtained, attributed to the gradual diffusion of FI extract
encapsulated within the nanoparticle core. Moreover, 44.6%, 63.2%, 86.7%, and 99.08% of FI extract were
released by degradation of the polymer matrix during 24, 48, 72 and 96 h.

In-vitro cytotoxicity
The cytotoxicity of FI extract and FI-PLGA NPs was assessed using the colorimetric assay MTT against the BT474 cell line (Table 1). The viability of BT-474 cells after being incubated for 24 and 48 h at several concentrations
 mg/ml of FI and FI-PLGA NPs is depicted in Fig. 7. Increasing the drug concentration from
25 to 200 mg/ml led to a corresponding reduction in the cell viability. Notably, a more pronounced decrease in
cell viability was observed after 48 h compared to 24 h, suggesting that prolonged incubation enhances cellular
inhibition. These results demonstrated that both higher drug concentrations and extended incubation periods
lead to a marked decrease in cell viability (Fig. 7 a-d).

The MTT data further corroborated the microscopic observations, presenting significant morphological
changes in treated cells at 24 h and 48 h treatment groups compared to the controls (untreated cells) (Fig. 8 and
Fig. 9). An increase in the concentration of extract showed significant changes in cell density and morphology.

In-vitro Phototoxic study
The photocytotoxicity of FI extract and FI-PLGA NPs at several concentrations  mg/ml was
assessed. The cytotoxicity against BT-474 cells dramatically increased upon light activation (Table 2, Fig. 10).
These results indicate that increasing both drug concentration and light dose (from 6 to 12 J/cm2) led to a marked
reduction in cell viability. Additionally, FI-PLGA NPs showed superior phototoxicity compared to the free FI
extract, likely due to enhanced cellular uptake and improved bioavailability.

Morphological investigation of cancer cells also revealed a noticeable reduction in cell viability upon laser
light exposure (Fig. 11). This reduction is likely due to the presence of photoactive molecules in the FI extract
that initiate phototoxicity.

The intracellular reactive oxygen species (ROS) levels were examined using fluorescent dye to determine
oxidative stress produced by FI extract in combination with laser treatment. A significant increase in fluorescence
intensity was visualized in the cells exposed to the FI-PLGA NPs followed by laser irradiation, compared to the
FI extract. Notably, cells treated at 12 J/cm2 fluency indicated the most considerable ROS production compared
to 6 J/cm2, as shown in Fig. 12. These findings imply that the plant extract produces enough reactive oxygen
species (ROS) in response to light stimulation which causes oxidative stress and leads to cell death.

## Discussion
Cancer is the second foremost cause of death, and the available therapeutic modalities have adverse side effects.
Natural products, especially plants, are a real hope due to their low toxicity, safety, and targeted gene regulation
at cell signaling level. Due to poor water solubility of plant metabolites, biocompatible modifications are required
to deliver optimal and effective concentrations at target site20 . Various studies have explored the plant extract
loaded PLGA NPs to enhance bioavailability and therapeutic effects. In present study, FI extract loaded PLGA
NPs successfully synthesized to improve the solubility, bioavailability, and therapeutic effect against BT-474
cell line. Previously, an EEPS-encapsulated PLGA NPs shown to improve the cellular uptake and cytotoxicity
compared to the free EEPS29.

The size, PDI, and zeta potential of FI-PLGA NPs were 217 nm, 0.093, and -30.5 mV, respectively. LNEOloaded PLGA NPs by single emulsion method reported by Ercin and colleagues exhibited a particle size of
211.4 +- 4.031 nm and PDI 0.068 +- 0.01647. Previously published research data showed that a high zeta potential
value of around -25 mV provides a sufficient energy barrier to stabilize nanosuspensions26. Similarly, in this
investigation, the extract's encapsulation resulted in an increase in the negative charge from -21.7 mV to
-30.5 mV, suggesting that the FI-PLGA NPs possess an optimal surface charge for stability. The increase of
negative charge may contribute to the presence of some compounds, i.e. hydroxyl groups, phenols, carboxylic
groups, etc., in FI extract with a possibility of adsorption of these compounds on the surface of PLGA NPs43. The
FTIR spectrum further validated the existence of these compounds. The particle size, zeta potential, and PDI
values obtained in our study are compatible with the previous research.

The drug encapsulation and loading efficiency were measured to be 86% and 16.6%, respectively. In an earlier
study, 64.16 +- 2.538, 70.86 +- 1.457, and 72.53 +- 2.914% encapsulation efficiency of fagonia cretica extract loaded
silver, albumin, and liposomes NPs were determined, respectively20. These encapsulation values are lower than
those obtained in our study.

The examination of phytochemicals helps to find chemical constituents in plant extract that may lead to
locating the pharmacologically active phytocompounds44. The FTIR analysis confirmed the existence of
functional groups of several phytochemicals, such as phenols, alkenes, alkanes, alkynes, aldehydes, alcohols,
ketones, esters, amines, ethers, and carboxylic acids, which may contribute to the plant's pharmacological
activities40. The characteristic peaks of these phytochemicals were also exhibited in FI-PLGA NPs with a slight
shift of bands, it was probably due to the entrapment of bioactive compounds in the PLGA matrix. The FTIR
spectrum of rutin-loaded IL polymer NPs indicated a change in the intensity of C-H and C = O bands due to the
addition of rutin peaks at the same wavenumber. This observation demonstrated the efficient encapsulation of
rutin in the nanoparticles45. In another investigation, a slight shift and reduction in peaks were observed, which
indicated that nisin is successfully encapsulated in PLGA NPs46. The FTIR results of our study are consistent
with these findings.

The release profile of FI extract from PLGA NPs was evaluated in PBS (pH = 7.4). The release behavior is
influenced by multiple parameters, such as the type of nanoparticle, release medium, temperature, and pH47.
After 12, 24, 48, 72 and 96 h, 28.8%, 44.6%, 63.2%, 86.7%, and 99.08% of FI extract were released, respectively.
An initial burst release was up to 28.8% within 12 h; after that, the release was slower and sustained. The same
phenomenon has been reported for naringin-loaded PLGA nanospheres, quercetin-loaded PLA NPs, and
oridonin-loaded PLGA NPs48,49.

To date, the FI extract loaded PLGA NPs into cancer cells has not been explored. Cytotoxicity evaluation
revealed that both FI extract and FI-PLGA NPs showed cytotoxic effects. However, the same concentration
of FI extract in NPs demonstrated significantly higher cytotoxicity compared to the free extract. The blank
PLGA NPs did not show cytotoxic effects, indicating that nano-encapsulation significantly enhanced the
cytotoxic potential of FI extract. It was probably due to the small size of the particle, optimal cellular uptake
of FI-PLGA NPs, and sustained release of FI from PLGA NPs. The tailored size, surface charge, and controlled
release characteristics of nanoparticles allow them for longer retention and improved interaction with cellular
membranes, which is responsible for the higher intracellular accumulation of drugs50. Previous investigations
have shown that nanoparticles between 100 and 400 nm in size are optimally internalized faster by cells. This size
range is large enough to avoid macrophage capture in the reticuloendothelial system and prevent rapid leakage

10

into capillaries51. FI extract exhibited cytotoxicity against the breast cancer (BT-474) cell line might be attributed
to the existence of bioactive compounds, including flavonoids, polyphenols, glycosides, alkaloids, and saponins.
These phytochemicals play an important role in targeting multiple signaling pathways (ICAM-1, VEGF, and
NF-kB) involved in cancer progression. It inhibits cell cycle regulation and triggers apoptosis by activating p53
and FOXO3a in breast cancer16.

Cellular uptake and drug release are key factors in optimizing treatment efficacy. The in-vitro cellular
uptake of free FI and FI-PLGA NPs were examined by confocal microscopy, utilizing the intrinsic fluorescent
property of FI. This investigation confirmed the significantly higher intracellular internalization of FI-PLGA
NPs compared to free FI extract after 2 h of incubation. These findings are aligned with previous findings, which
reported intrinsic fluorescent property of the plant extract (FCe) to investigate the intracellular accumulation
of free FCe and FCe-loaded NPs. Nano-encapsulated FCe showed better cytotoxic effects against than free FCe
against TFK-1 cell line36 . Similarly, enhanced cytotoxic potential of NOE loaded PLGA/PEG NPs against lung
cancer (A549) cells were observed than free drug. It was indicated that controlled and sustained release of NOE
from NPs, enhance the therapeutic effects due to prolong intracellular accumulation. Whereas free NOE quickly
diffused across lipid bilayers and was cleared more rapidly51. Several studies reported that PLGA-based drugloaded nanocarrier's cellular uptake was observed through endocytosis52.

Interestingly, FI extract not only demonstrated cytotoxic effects but also presented phototoxic properties.
Two prominent peaks of FI extract at 613 nm and 671 nm were observed, corresponding to the red region of
the UV-visible spectrum. The light penetration at this wavelength in tissues is high enough to produce reactive
oxygen species (ROS). In PDT, the absorption bands of the compounds in the 650-850 nm range can reach
appropriate excited states when exposed to laser light. These states then interact with biomolecules, producing
reactive oxygen species that initiate PDT action53. A similar effect has been noted in previous studies, which
reported that Ficus carica extract-loaded PLGA NPs had a higher phototoxicity than free extract36.

and a reduction in cell density. Apoptotic cells are evident in the treatment groups. Our findings are in line

## Conclusion
In this study, FI extract encapsulated PLGA NPs (FI-PLGA NPs) were successfully synthesized to enhance the
drug solubility, bioavailability, and therapeutic efficacy. FI-PLGA NPs showed a higher cytotoxic and phototoxic
effect against the BT-474 cell line than free FI extract, possibly due to their sustained drug release and improved
cellular uptake. Notably, cells treated at a fluence of 12 J/cm2 exhibited the highest ROS production compared to
6 J/cm2 and leads to the cell death.

Further research is required to identify the specific compounds responsible for these effects and evaluating
the in-vivo anti-cancer potential of FI-PLGA NPs to support clinical applications.
