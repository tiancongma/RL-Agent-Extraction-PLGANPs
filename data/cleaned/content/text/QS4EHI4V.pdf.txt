Abstract
Finasteride is an orally active testosterone 5-alpha-reductase inhibitor that is used for the treatment
of benign prostatic hyperplasia as a surgical alternative. The aim of this work was to improve
finasteride levels in plasma and prostate through the formulation of biodegradable finasteride
nanoparticles and to quantify finasteride levels using near-infrared application. Finasteride
nanoparticles were prepared by emulsion solvent evaporation method utilizing the biodegradable
polymers poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) and poly-ε-caprolactone. The prepared nanoparticles were
characterized by particle size, zeta potential, and encapsulation efficiency. The selected finasteride-
biodegradable formula was examined in vivo, and both plasma and prostate levels of finasteride
were quantified utilizing near-infrared technique. Results revealed that the prepared finasteride
nanoparticles size range was from 231 ± 78 to 956 ± 224 nm with finasteride–poly-ε-caprolactone
nanoparticles showing larger particle sizes compared with finasteride–poly(lactic-co-glycolic
acid) nanoparticles. The encapsulation efficiency ranged from 68.89% ± 2.99% to 99.15% ± 4.32%.
The selected formula of finasteride nanoparticles showed improved levels of finasteride in both
plasma and prostate of the investigated rats. The realization of sustained release of biodegradable
finasteride nanoparticles for possible oral or parenteral application could improve the activity

Introduction
Finasteride (FIN; Figure 1) is pharmacologically classified as a 5-alpha-reductase inhibitor that
prevents the formation of dihydrotestosterone (DHT) synthesized in the prostate gland. DHT is
responsible for prostate size enlargement. DHT reaches 35% of its serum concentration after 1 day
with a single dose of 1 mg of FIN. Accordingly, testosterone level is increased by about 15%.1 FIN
is used for the treatment of benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH), reduces the acute urinary retention,
and reduces the need for surgery including transurethral resection of the prostate.2,3 FIN is absorbed
orally with Cmax from 1 to 2 h, T½ from 4 to 7 h, widely distributed, bind to plasma protein (about
90%) and metabolized essentially by liver (about 65%) and about 35% excreted through the kidney.
FIN undergoes extensive hepatic metabolism to essentially inactive metabolites.4 DHT is found in
the skin and the scalp with large concentrations, so adverse effects include alopecia (hair loss),
impotence, and breast tenderness observed after accumulation of FIN after multiple dosing.5
Chemically, FIN is sparingly soluble in water and aqueous solutions, freely soluble in ethanol
and dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO),6 not affected by food,7 and classified as class 2 drug in biophar-
maceutical classification system (BCS), which means it has low solubility and high permeability.8
Low solubility may be the reason for its moderate bioavailability (65%) as the drug shows low
first-pass metabolism.9 Several trials were carried out to enhance its solubility through formulation
as liposomes and niosomes10 in the form of solid lipid nanoparticles (NPs) for transdermal patches.
Also, decreasing particle size could increase the surface area and enhance lymphatic and paracel-
lular uptake, which improve the bioavailability.11

that absorb certain characteristic frequencies causing changes in their inherent vibrational or rota-
tional molecular motion. The variation in the intensity of particular wavelength absorbed, that is

work aimed to quantify rat plasma and prostate levels of FIN utilizing NIR application. The
achievement of this goal will lead to a decrease in FIN-dosing frequency and reduction of its
adverse effects.
Materials and methods
FIN was gifted from EIPICO Pharmaceutical Company (Cairo, Egypt). Poly(lactic-co-glycolic
acid) (PLGA), Resomer® RG 503, 50:50, ester terminated, Mw 24,000–38,000 and poly-ε-
caprolactone (PCL) Mn 70,000–90,000 were obtained from Sigma–Aldrich (St Louis, MO, USA).
All other chemicals were of analytical grade.
Preparation of FIN biodegradable NPs
Finasteride–poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (FIN-PLGA)-loaded and finasteride–poly-ε-caprolactone
(FIN-PCL)-loaded NPs were prepared by emulsion solvent evaporation method. The selected pol-
ymer and FIN (50 mg) were weighed and dissolved in 2 mL of chloroform by water bath sonication
for 30 s, then added to 10 mL polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) solution 1% w/v, and then sonicated with a
probe sonicator for 3 min. After sonication, the formed emulsion was poured into 20 mL distilled
water and homogenized using magnetic stirrer and left on the stirrer for 3 h; after that, the remain-
ing chloroform was evaporated by rotary evaporator at 25°C for 45 min. The formed NPs were
separated by centrifugation at 15,000 r/min for 45 min and then washed twice with distilled water.
The collected NPs were then lyophilized overnight. All NP batches were produced in triplicate.
FIN formulations were prepared at drug:polymer (D:P) ratios of 1:1, 1:2, and 2:1 and the amount
of FIN was fixed at 50 mg in all formulations.
Characterization of the prepared FIN NPs
Particle size analysis and zeta potential measuring.  The prepared NPs were evaluated for their particle
size, size distribution, and zeta (ζ) potential by laser diffraction, using Zetatrac analyzer (Microtrac
Inc., Montgomeryville, PA, USA). A sample of 2 mL was vortexed, placed into analyzer chamber,
and measured. Each determination was made in triplicate.
Drug encapsulation efficiency
A known weight of formulated FIN NPs was dissolved in chloroform and sonicated and then fil-
tered. FIN content was analyzed by high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC)12 and encap-

Surface morphology
The NPs morphology such as shape was examined by scanning electron microscope (SEM) Jeol
JSM 7600f field emission SEM (Chiyoda-ku, Tokyo, Japan). The lyophilized NPs were mounted
on metal stubs coated with gold under vacuum and then examined.
Drug release study from the prepared NPs
Drug release studies from FIN-loaded NPs were carried out in comparison with raw FIN powder
using automated Franz cell instrument (Franz diffusion, Hanson Research, MicroettePlus,
Chatsworth, CA, USA) as described previously.17 Briefly, a synthetic nylon membrane with pore
size of 0.2 µm (PALL Corporation, NY, USA) was used as diffusion membrane. The prepared NPs
were placed in donor part above the diffusion membrane to allow FIN diffusion to the receptor
compartment. Phosphate buffer saline (pH 7.0) was used as a diffusion medium in the receptor
chamber stirred at 300 r/min. Samples were withdrawn by the autosampler at 0.5, 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10,
and 12 h. Withdrawn samples were analyzed by HPLC.12 Each sample was carried out in
triplicate.
In vivo study
NIR quantification of FIN concentration in rats’ plasma and prostate
Sample preparation.  All samples were frozen at −80°C until analysis. NIR spectra were collected
using Nicolet IZ 10 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA). Spectra range was from
4000 cm−1 to 10,000 cm−1 with average 16 scans with a wavelength increment of 0.4 nm. The
absorbance spectra of free plasma, raw-FIN, and polymeric plasma-FIN samples were scanned and
the NIR frequency region was selected by the apparatus for the best calibration model setting.
Chemometric analysis.  All chemometric spectral data were analyzed utilizing TQ Analyst EZ soft-
ware (Version 9.4.45, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA). The analysis depends on
partial least squares regression (PLS), different pre-treatment measurements, standard normal vari-
ant (SNV), and multiplicative scatter correction (MSC), and first and second subordinates were
trialed. SNV and MSC scientific change strategies utilized mostly to decrease the variation between
spectra, after method development the instrument is capable of predicting the required samples
upon calibration model. The calibration coefficient (R2) and root mean square error of cross valida-
tion (RMSECV) were used to select the appropriate number of principal component (PLS) factors
for each model. A good model should have high calibration coefficient (R2) and RMSECV
values.
Pharmacokinetic study of FIN in rats’ plasma and prostate.  This study was conducted using 250–350 g
male Swiss Wistar rats, supplied by the Animal Breeding Laboratory, King Fahd Medical Research
Center, King Abdulaziz University, Jeddah, Saudi Arabia. Animals were acclimatized for 8 days
before the experiment in our animal facility. They were maintained on a 12-h light–dark cycle
without any stressful stimuli. The room temperature was kept at 22°C ± 2°C. Animal handling and

experimental protocol were approved by the Unit of Biomedical Ethics, King Abdulaziz University
(approval date: 15 December 2015). The selected formulae were subjected to pharmacokinetic
study in plasma of normal rats. Raw-FIN, FIN-PCL (F1), and FIN-PLGA (F4) NPs were sus-
pended each in 1-mL distilled water and used in a single oral dose of 10 mg/kg animal body weight.
In total, 63 rats were divided into three groups. The first group was given raw-FIN NPs while the
second group was administered FIN-PLGA formula and the last one was administered FIN-PCL
NPs. Three animals from each group were lightly anesthetized with ether and subjected to blood
sampling at 0, 1, 2, 4, 8, 12, and 24 h after ether treatment. Animals were sacrificed at the assigned
time points immediately after blood withdrawal, and prostates were rapidly dissected out. Blood
was drawn from the retro-orbital plexus before sacrifice. Blood samples were collected in heparin-
ized vacutainer tubes and centrifuged at 250g at 4°C for 8–10 min. Plasma and prostate samples
were kept at −80°C for evaluations of FIN concentrations. For FIN analysis, all samples were
directly subjected to measurement by NIR.
Pharmacokinetic calculations and statistical analysis.  Pharmacokinetic calculations were performed
using the WinNonlin, Standard Edition, Version 1.1 (Pharsight Corp., Mountain View, CA, USA)
by the non-compartmental method. Data are expressed as mean ± standard error of the mean. Sta-
tistical comparisons between groups for the pharmacokinetic parameters were made by one-way
analysis of variance (ANOVA) followed by a post hoc test (Tukey–Kramer method). All statistical
analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism software, version 6 (GraphPad Software, Inc., La
Jolla, CA, USA). The level of significance was set at p < 0.05.

with FIN-PLGA NPs (Table 1). It was reported that PLGA plays a role as a surfactant.18–20 Sakai

the smaller-size PLGA NPs compared with PCL-based NPs could be attributed to PLGA potentiating
the emulsifying effect of PVA resulting in smaller particles formed after emulsification.
In addition, results of particle-size characterization for FIN NPs showed that the increase in the
polymer (PLGA or PCL) concentration will increase the particle size of the prepared formula. This

The EE% of FIN NP formulae is illustrated in Table 1. The results revealed that FIN-PLGA
NPs have more EE% values compared with FIN–PCL NPs. This could be attributed to the sur-
factant effect of PLGA as described in the particle-size characterization part.25 The emulsifica-
tion effect of PLGA potentiates the emulsifying effect of PVA that allowed more FIN
(water-insoluble drug) to be entrapped in the formed globules compared with PCL polymer. In
addition, drug EE% improved with the increase in polymer concentration that can be rational-
ized by two factors. First, the increase in polymer concentration causes increase in the viscosity
of organic phase. Viscous forces oppose diffusion of drug molecules to aqueous phase from the
organic phase. Second, increase in polymer concentration leads to increase in encapsulation
area so increases the amount of drug entrapped.21 However, FIN formulae (PLGA or PCL) with
D:P ratio (2:1) showed improved drug EE% compared with D:P ratio (1:1), that is, same
polymer content that could be attributed to reduced polymer concentration in relation to drug
amount that produce more porous particles that allow FIN to fill these pores and adhere to its
surfaces.26
Surface morphology of NPs
The external structure properties of FIN formula of FIN-PCL (F1) and FIN-PLGA (F4) were char-
acterized by SEM technique. SEM images (Figure 2(a) and (b)) showed spherical shape for the
prepared NPs. FIN-PCL (F1) formula showed mean particle size of 448 ± 187 nm (Figure 2(a)).
FIN-PLGA (F4) showed mean particle size of 231 ± 78 nm (Figure 2(b)) as measured by the
Zetatrac nanosizer. FIN NPs were regularly arranged in a detached manner as a result of complete
solvent evaporation followed by lyophilization.27 Particle size is an essential factor affecting gas-
tro-intestinal tract (GIT) absorption.28,29

Drug release study from the prepared NPs
Figure 3 shows the percentage of cumulative FIN released from the prepared FIN NPs. Results
exposed a biphasic drug release pattern during the 12 h release study. The fast initial release could
be attributed to FIN entrapped on the surface of the NPs, while the sustained release pattern of FIN
may be related to the drug particles deeper entrapped in the polymer matrix.27 FIN release is con-
trolled by parameters that affect drug release kinetics from the polymeric matrix. These parameters
are the rate of medium uptake, drug release, and dissolution rate, as well as the particle size and
polymer matrix erosion.30,31 In addition, the solubility behavior of the biodegradable polymers
(PCL and PLGA) could delay water penetration and may impede the diffusion of FIN into the
release medium. It is also worth to notice that PCL NPs showed improved FIN release behavior
compared with PLGA NPs (Figure 3). This could be attributed to the matrix-type characters and
porosity of PCL polymer.32,33

Pharmacokinetic study of FIN in rats’ plasma and prostate
As shown in Tables 2 and 3, the incorporation of FIN into PLGA and PCL polymers had been
thought as a way of increasing its oral bioavailability in order to improve its therapeutic effective-
ness.34 The absorption from the intestine is a well-known process affected by a number of factors
of which particle size is essential factor.35 Pharmacokinetic studies were carried out to evaluate
the potential influence of particle size and use of polymer on FIN bioavailability. The increased
Tmax values observed for all the polymeric NPs can be explained by the slow diffusion of the drug
from the polymeric carriers. As shown in the supplementary material, significant increase in poly-
meric FIN area under the plasma concentration curve (AUC0–∞) as a result of improved FIN extent
of absorption which may be achieved by one of three possible uptake mechanisms in oral absorp-
tion: (1) paracellular pathway uptake, (2) via transporters in epithelial cells and intracellular
uptake, and (3) lymphatic uptake via the M cells and Peyer’s patches.36 As known, smaller parti-
cles could increase the surface area subjected to the intestinal mucosa, an increase in surface area
could increase drug contact to mucosa, which allows sufficient release of FIN to the blood.

Moreover, coating FIN with the polymeric matrix could protect the drug from rapid metabolism
by the liver. In addition, the delay of the peak time (NP groups) compared with raw FIN could be
attrebuted to the ingestion of NPs through the lymphatic uptake pathway by M-cells of the Peyer’s
patches that clear up the reliance on molecule properties, for example, size, surface properties,
and role of hepatic first-pass effect as reported previously.37 Another possibility suggesting the
delay of the peak time (NP groups) may have been attributed to the slow release of surface bound
FIN from NPs in the lumen of the intestine prior to ingestion of NPs by lymphatic uptake pathway
that slows the overall release of FIN from NPs . For prostate analysis, the results showed signifi-
cant increase in AUC0–∞ for both FIN-PCL and FIN-PLGA formulae (Table 3). This could be
attributed to the improved bioavailability of FIN and longer plasma circulation of FIN-loaded
NPs. PCL is a semicrystalline polymer; PCL’s slower rate of degradation compared to polylac-
tides has made it a better candidate of choice for use in drug delivery and long-term implantable
devices.38 This fact may illustrate the improved prostate FIN concentration compared with FIN-
PLGA NPs. Consequently, accumulation of FIN in the prostate lead to high mean residence time
(MRT0–inf_obs) that reaches 27 h for FIN-PCL compared with 7 and 4 h for FIN-PLGA and raw FIN,
respectively.
Conclusion
This study was able to achieve biodegradable FIN nanoparticulate formulation with sustained
release characteristics compared with raw FIN powder. In addition, application of NIR was suc-
cessful as a simple, fast, and non-destructive tool for quantitative estimation of FIN in plasma and
prostate. As a result, BPH activities could be improved for a longer time with lower dosing fre-
quency that improves patient tolerability and compliance.
