Abstract
This paper presents an experimental study on preparation of tramadol
hydrochloride (TrH) loaded nanoparticles, and stability determination in
various infusion solutions. In this study, various nanoparticle preparation
parameters based on w/o/w emulsification solvent evaporation method,
including stabilizer type, stabilizer concentration, polymer concentration,
homogenization speed and initial drug amount were systematically
tested to verify their versatility for preparing nanoparticles. Initially both
particle size and encapsulation efficiency of nanoparticles were changed
significantly with the change in surfactant and polymer ratio (p<0.05).
However, homogenization speed only changed particle size (average
size 339.3±1.8 nm for 15000 rpm, 318.9±6.4 nm for 20000 rpm and
237.2±7.8 nm for 25000 rpm) (p<0.05) and initial drug concentration is
only affected the encapsulation efficiency (34.2±0.7% for 4 mg/mL and
33.2±0.9 for 1.6 mg/mL) (p<0.05). Storage at room temperature for 3
months resulted in an increase in particle size and polydispersity index.
Prepared nanoparticles showed the best stability after storage at – 20 °C
for in 3 months. Finally, storage of nanoparticles in various infusion
solutions resulted an undesirable changes for 6% Hydroxyethyl starch in
0.9% sodium chloride injection, 10% Dextran 40 and 4% Succinyl gelatin

solutions. It was shown that an appropriate delivery of TrH loaded PLGA
nanoparticles as infusion can be prepared only in water for injection,
20% Mannitol, 0.9% Sodium chloride and 5% Dextrose solutions.

Introduction
The pharmacotherapy of pain contains use of several analgesics. In case
of moderate to severe pain, opioid analgesic drugs are the first drug of
choice [1]. Because of this reason, most of the patients that are suffering
chronic pain have a treatment with opioids. However, the treatment of
pain using opioids may be controversial as a result of adverse effects of
opioids such as addiction, abuse, respiratory depression and
gastrointestinal effects [2]. Opioid use for treating chronic pain may be
acceptable only in patients who have not responded to any other

therapy, because of long term effects of clinical and excessive use of
opioid drugs can affect nearly every organ system of the body [3].
Opioid use can be categorized into short-acting and long-acting opioids.
Short-acting opioids were characterized by a fluctuations in plasma drug
levels. Even if they are suitable for the treatment of acute pain, they can
also be used for chronic pain as controlled release formulations [3].
However not all of these drugs are suitable for controlled release in every
case. For instance, long term morphine administration is not first choice
due to the effects in patients with renal failure. Also, the fluctuation of
plasma short acting opioid levels results inconsistent pain relief and the
fluctuation can be prevented using controlled release drug delivery
systems [4].
Tramadol hydrochloride (TrH) is a synthetic opioid agonist analgesic
drug [5, 6] which has an analgesic potency less than morphine and
fentanyl [7], equal to meperidine and higher than codeine [8]. Its
analgesic action is driven by opioid and non-opioid mechanisms. It was
shown that 30% of its action can be antagonized by naloxone and major
part of its effect is mediated through non-opioid mechanisms [9]. TrH
shows similar effects with morphine in severe pain, but it has lower side
effects such as respiratory depression when compared to opioid drugs
which has similar effects. It has a very low abuse potential. Moreover
TrH does not have prostaglandin inhibition-mediated adverse effects
which is commonly seen in nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug use.
However the use of TrH is still limited due to its historical concerns and
lack of knowledge [10].

In spite of the fact that intravenous (i.v.) and oral TrH drugs are the first
choice in treatment, subcutaneous (s.c.) infusions can be used in the
treatment of patients whose pain and symptoms cannot be managed
otherwise pharmacologically due to the difficulty of accessing i.v.
administration and inadequacy to tolerate oral drugs. Although the
needle application site and the thickness of the patient's adipose tissue
affect the absorption of the active substance, s.c. infusion is not
contraindicated in cachectic patients [11]. However studies showed that
the tolerability and stability of s.c. infusion of TrH is better in isotonic
solutions [12].
In the clinical setting, the use of infusion bag for systemic administration
of opioids are very simple and convenient practice. Among the other
drugs, TrH has been using for the treatment of pain due to its proven
clinical efficacy, safety and drug abuse and dependence are of minor
clinical relevance, contrary to other opioids [11].
There is a lack of studies regarding compatibility and stability of TrH
when encapsulated into nanoparticles in infusion solutions and in which
the particles are assayed at several conditions to simulate those used in
practice. There is justone study which was carried out to demonstrate the
stability of lyophilized TrH loaded nanoparticles was chemically stable in
storage conditions.
Although TrH nanoparticles remain a hot topic in the literature, the
compatibility and stability of the nanoparticles for infusions have not been
scientifically validated. Making use of clinically relevant concentrations of
TrH, this study was aimed to prepare long-acting TrH loaded PLGA

nanoparticles to reduce the dosing frequency and thus tolerate tramadol-
related side effects and determine the stability of these nanoparticles
diluted in several isotonic i.v. or s.c. infusion solutions commonly utilized
in hospital or combat zones for controlled analgesia [13, 14]. In this
study, the preliminary one factor at a time (OFAT) variation experiments
were studied to detect ranges of the formulation factors which result
reasonable formulation in case of physicochemical and stability
properties and search the possibility for administration of the prepared
formulations by infusion. OFAT variations show the effect upon the
studied results, in order to obtain extra data for the factors of influence
and responses of interest, evaluation of the cut point levels, obtain the
space of possible factor setting to adjust the experimental plan and to
prevent unnecessary experiments during the following optimization
process [15]. Moreover, experimental operational conditions were
selected in relation to effect upon nanoparticle characteristics and
limitations because of the performance of the laboratory equipment
generally used for nanoparticle preparation.
Materials and Methods
Materials
TrH, Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) (Mowiol 4-88) and PLGA copolymer
(lactide:glycolide 50:50, ester terminated, Mw:30000-60000) was a
purchased from Sigma (USA). Sodium cholate was obtained from Serva
(Germany). Ethyl acetate and acetonitrile (HPLC grade) were purchased

from Merck. D-α-Tocopherol polyethylene glycol 1000 succinate (TPGS)
was obtained from BASF (Germany). All other chemicals used were of
analytical grade.
Preparation of TrH Loaded PLGA Nanoparticles
Double emulsification solvent evaporation method was used to obtain
nanoparticles as previously described using PLGA polymer [16]. Briefly,
different amounts of TrH was dissolved in solution containing stabilizer.
The solution was emulsified in 2.5 mL ethyl acetate containing PLGA
polymer by homogenization at different ratios in an ice bath for 20 min
(IKA, T 25, Germany). Then, this first emulsion was added to the
stabilizer solution (12.5 mL) and homogenized at same speed in an ice
bath for 20 min. The Ethyl acetate was rapidly removed by using
Rotavapor under reduced pressure (Rotavapor, Buchi, Switzerland).
Finally, the nanoparticles were centrifuged at 10000 rpm for 15 min
(Beckman Model J2-21) and washed three times with water before
lyophilization. For lyophilization, prefreezing of samples was done at –
40°C for 24 h, then samples were put into freeze-drier (Christ Gamma
2-20, USA) under vacuum (- 80 °C). The process was continued till free-
flowing powder was obtained.
Various formulation variables like PLGA ratio (2,4 and 6 mg/mL),
stabilizer type (PVA, sodium cholate or TPGS) and PVA ratio (1.0, 2.0
and 3.0% w/v), homogenization speed (15000, 20000 and 25000 rpm)
were varied and the effect on particle size and distribution, zeta potential
and encapsulation efficiency were studied. Every series of experiments,

only one formulation parameter was altered to understand the change.
Nanoparticle Characterization
TrH loaded nanoparticles were checked for size and distribution, zeta
potential, morphology and encapsulation efficiency. Mean particle size,
size distribution, and zeta potential of TrH loaded nanoparticles were
determined by dynamic light scattering (Z3000, Nicomp, Port Richey, FL,
USA). Samples were diluted with ultrapure water (1:200) and measured
in triplicate.
The encapsulation efficiency was found by calculating the ratio of the
active substance trapped in the nanoparticles over the total amount of
active substance. For this purpose, the amount of unloaded active
substance was measured from the supernatants obtained in the
centrifugation process of TrH nanoparticles by HPLC (Agilent 1100,
California, USA). These amounts were calculated by subtracting the
initial amount of active ingredient added to the formulation and the
encapsulation efficiency (%) values were obtained [17, 18]. If the HPLC
method is summarised briefly, The C18 revers phase column (ACE5 150
× 4.6 mm, 5μm, UK) was used at 25°C at a flow rate of 1,0mL/min, with a
UV detector 220 nm wavelength, with an injection volume of 20 μL. The
analysis time for each sample was set at 8 mins. As mobile phase, 0,1 M
pH 7,4 potassium phosphate buffer and acetonitril 70:30 (v/v) were used
[19].
In Vitro Drug Release

Drug release study of TrH loaded nanoparticles versus free active
ingredient was performed by dialysis bag method. Briefly, TrH loaded
nanoparticles (1 mL) were poured inside a dialysis bag (cut-off 12-14
kDa) and both ends were closed to prevent any leakage. The same
amount of TrH solution was poured into a dialysis bag and dialysis bags
were prepared using the same procedure. Each bag was located inside a
beaker that contain 30 mL of PBS (pH 7.4) as release medium. Then, the
medium was stirred at room temperature at 200 rpm. At specific time
intervals, 1 mL of aliquot was taken from the incubation medium to
evaluate the release rate of the TrH. The removed solution with the same
content of fresh buffer was replaced and then for each sample, amount
of TrH was determined using HPLC analysis.
Infrared Spectroscopy (FT-IR) Analysis
For the infrared spectrum analysis of the drug, polymer and prepared
nanoparticles, FT-IR analysis was performed with the device (Spectrum
400, Perkin Elmer, USA) in the 450-4000 cm-1 range.
Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC) Analysis
Thermal analysis of drug, polymer and the prepared nanoparticles were
carried out with a Differential Scanning Calorimeter (DSC) (Jade, Perkin
Elmer, USA). Samples were evaluated between 25-300˚C on an
aluminum pan. The aluminum blank pan is used as a reference. Analysis
was performed at 20˚C / min temperature increase and 20 mL / min
nitrogen flow rate.

SEM Analysis
The morphology of optimal formulation was investigated using a
scanning electron microscope (SEM). Surface images of lyophilized
nanoparticles were observed SEM (Zeiss Evo 40, Jena, Germany) after
covering with gold. A drop of the formulation were placed on 200 mesh
copper grids and excess of formulation was taken with filter paper. The
sample was stained with a 2% uranyl acetate solution for 2 min, they
were dried at under vacuum and visualized at 120 kV.
Stability Studies
For determination of the storage stability, nanoparticles were stored in
various temperatures and particle size, polydispersity index and zeta
potential of nanoparticles were evaluated [20]. For this purpose, the
lyophilized nanoparticle formulation was stored at room temperature, 25
°C, + 4˚C at refrigerator and -20˚C in deep freezer for 3 months, and
then particle size, polydispersity index and zeta potential values were
checked.
Moreover, in order to examine the stability of the nanoparticles in the
infusion solutions, formulations were placed in 0.9% sodium chloride, 5%
dextrose, 20% mannitol, 10% dextran, 6% hydroxyethyl starch in 0.9%
sodium chloride and 4% succinyl gelatin solutions for 24 hours. Particle
size, polydispersity index and zeta potential of formulations were
evaluated after incubation time.
Statistical Analysis

All experiments were carried out in triplicate. Data were presented as
mean ± standard deviation. GraphPad Prism 6 software was used for
statistical analyses. ANOVA and post hoc Tukey’s tests were applied to
compare the results. p<0.05 were considered to be statistically
significant.

method, numerous process parameters i.e., stabilizer type, stabilizer,
polymer and drug ratio and homogenization speed, were considered for
achieving optimal formulation parameters. Every series of experiments,
only one formulation parameter was changed.

Effect of Stabilizer Type and Concentration
In emulsification solvent evaporation method, the stabilization of droplets
is essential to avoid coalescence and the agglomerate formation.
Stabilizers adsorb at interface to decrease the system energy and
prevent coalescence after formation of interface [21].

Different stabilizers were chosen based on literature such as TPGS,
Sodium Cholate and PVA were checked with the same method.
Stabilizer type and ratio did not affected the size of particles (p>0.05)
[17]. However, nanoparticles prepared using PVA formed particles with
lower PDI and particle sizes (Figure 1a). Hence PVA was chosen as
stabilizer in this study.

Figure 1b shows the effect of stabilizer concentration on TrH loaded
PLGA nanoparticles. The mean particle size was changed with the
change in the range of PVA and increasing concentration of stabilizer
resulted in a reduced mean particle size of nanoparticles (p < 0.05). This
results confirm similar results obtained by Kwon et al. using PVA as
stabilizer by w/o/w emulsification method [21].
To reduce the interfacial tension at the oil/water interface, PVA can be
used at high concentrations and it leads to the formation of smaller
emulsion droplets [22, 23]. Hence, the average diameter of the
nanoparticles with increasing amount of PVA concentration decreased.

However, increasing PVA concentration is increased the external
aqueous phase's viscosity, which has led to enhance the size of particles
because of the reduction in the net shear stress [24]. Changes in the
average diameter of particles with the PVA concentration was mainly a
result of the decrease in interfacial tension which are dominant with
increasing viscosity. High concentrations of PVA were adequate to
completely cover emulsion droplets. Consequently, during the seperation
of the organic solvent, PVA can prevent the coalescence of the droplets
and it was resulted smaller sized nanoparticles. After elimination of the
organic solvent, more PVA molecules can be physically integrated into
the nanoparticle surface [23], and then multiple hydroxyl groups
extending into the external phase can be hydrated, so a hydrated
segment can be formed on the interface to prevent nanoparticle
aggregation [24].
The entrapment efficiency of TrH was not changed significantly with
increasing PVA concentration as defined in Figure 1b (p > 0.05).
Decreasing particle size was expected with the increasing PVA
concentration [25]. However an increase in concentration of surfactant
beyond critical micellar concentration may not help for altering the
characteristics of nanoparticles [26].
Even though increasing PVA concentration from 1% to 2% did not show
effect on zeta potential significantly (p>0.05), the effect of increasing the
PVA concentration from 2% to 3% on zeta potential became significant
(p<0.05). At high concentrations, PVA was enough to cover the emulsion
droplets totally and some of PVA molecules can be physically integrated

into the nanoparticle surface. Due to its negative charge, the
incorporation may result in a change in zeta potential [25].
Effect of Polymer Concentration
For the evaluation of the effect of polymer concentration, stabilizer and
drug were used at a fixed concentration for PVA 3 % (w/v%) and for TrH
0.8 mg/mL. PLGA amounts were changed from 2 mg/mL to 6 mg/mL.
Figure 1c demonstrates the effect of PLGA concentration on the particle
size of TrH loaded nanoparticles. Concentration of polymer was an
important parameter for the nanoparticle’s sizes, as the polymer
concentration increased, bigger particles were formed (p<0.05). Similar
results were found by Schlicher et al. [27]. The organic phase diffuses
into the water which contains PLGA molecules. As the PLGA
concentration increases, the amount of PLGA depends on organic phase
enhances. Hence higher particle size can be observed at a fixed other
condition. Moreover, as PLGA amount increases, organic solution’s
viscosity increases and this high viscosity during preparation of
formulation hinders the nanoparticle formation because of resistance to
the shear forces [21].
The present study indicated that even if PLGA did not show significant
effect on the zeta potential and PDI in given PLGA ratios, the
encapsulated TrH was increased with increasing amount of PLGA
(p<0.05). The encapsulation efficiency of TrH showed a rise significantly
(p < 0.05) when the PLGA concentration increased to 6 mg/mL. This is
possibly a result of the increased viscosity. Increasing the PLGA

concentration could increase the viscosity which shows higher resistance
the diffusion of TrH into the aqueous phase and so improves the drug
integration into nanoparticles.
Furthermore, bigger nanoparticles had higher drug encapsulation
efficiencies. When PLGA concentration was 2 mg/mL, the encapsulation
efficiency of TrH was lowest (30.1 ± 0.2%) and encapsulation efficiency
increased to 31.0 ± 0.2 % and 34.3 ± 0.1 % for 4 and 6 mg /mL,
respectively [25].
Effect of Homogenizer Speed
Agitation are speed-limiting steps in reducing particle size. High-speed
homogenization reduces particle size, producing both mechanical cutting
and hydraulic cutting. In the current study, homogenization rate has a
significant effect on particle size, while PDI has less impact on zeta
potential and drug encapsulation. The most important effect on zeta
potential is the rate of homegenization. Change in the homogenization
speed did not have significant effect on zeta potential (p > 0.05).
It was mentioned that the size of the nanoparticles in the formulation
based on the droplet size during the emulsification process [21]. PLGA
nanoparticles was obtained by using the PVA at a concentration of 3.0
(w/v %) and also constant homogenization times were used at 20 min.
The results are given in Figure 1d. Unsurprisingly, particle size of
nanoparticles was decreased with higher homogenizer speed (p<0.05).
As given in Figure 1d, all the formulations obtained showed particle size

in a nanometer range and according to the size distribution results 0.01
to 0.05, all the formulations could be defined as monodisperse and. The
mean particle size of nanoparticles decreased with the increasing the
homogenization speed and the TrH loaded nanoparticles prepared at
25,000 rpm (237.2 ± 7.8 nm) had lower mean sizes than those obtained
at 15,000 rpm (339.3 ± 1.8 nm) and 20,000 rpm (266.6 ± 2.6 nm)
significantly (p < 0.05). It could be a result of inappropriate dispersibility
of the disperse phase into the continuous phase. Because of this reason,
bigger particles were formed due to low shear forces during the
emulsification [28].
It was shown that the homogenization speed had positive impacts on the
encapsulation efficiency [29]. However, in our study, the effect of
homogenization speed on encapsulation efficiency was not significant
(p>0.05). Generally, the greatest effect in encapsulation efficiency for
obtained PLGA nanoparticles was observed by PLGA concentration,
then the PVA concentration, and lastly homogenization speed showed
the minimal effect [29]. Figure 1d shows that the highest encapsulation
efficiency (35.4 ± 0.3 %) were obtained at 25,000 rpm of homogenization
speed. In the meantime, lower encapsulation efficiencies (31.0 ± 0.2 %
and 33.1 ± 0.2 %) were obtained at lower speeds (20,000 and 15,000
rpm), respectively.
Initial Drug Amount
Particle characteristics can be modified by altering the agitation speed,
volume of continuous phase like drug/polymer ratio [30]. The effect of

first amount of TrH on nanoparticles size was given in Figure 1e. Initial
TrH amount in nanoparticles didn’t show any significant effect on the
mean particle size that was comparable with similar earlier studies with
nanoparticles prepared by emulsification solvent evaporation method
[31].
While a fixed ratio of PLGA (4 mg/mL), PVA (3 %), and homogenization
speed (20,000) were applied, the quantity of TrH changed from 0.24 to 4 
mg/mL (Figure 1e). The increase in the initial TrH ratio in inner water
phase came out witha significant increase in encapsulation efficiency of
TrH especially in higher doses, which was in accordance with the
previous studies [25].
It has been reported that drug-polymer interaction tends to the
enhancement of encapsulation efficiency [31]. Panyam et al. explained
that higher drug polymer miscibility leads to higher drug encapsulation at
their study with flutamide and this shows the importance of drug
miscibility in the polymer for hydrophobic drug [32]. In another study by
Budhian et al. reported that the enhancement in polymer-drug interaction
increases the drug content of the particles so it was resulted higher
encapsulation efficiency [24]. Therefore, it was possible to mention that
with higher initial amounts of TrH, higher amounts of drug molecules
could interact with PLGA, resulting in an improvement in the
encapsulation efficiency of TrH.
All formulations showed a net negative charge according to zeta
potential values between -16.1 ± 0.4 to -2.6 ± 1.0 mV and PDI values
ranging from 0.01 to 0.04. Lower TrH amounts containing nanoparticles

showed lower zeta potential values however the change in the zeta
potential with the drug amount alteration was not significant (p>0.05).
Regarding the formulations prepared with the different amounts of TrH,
drug amount did not affect the PDI of the nanoparticles prepared with the
emulsification solvent evaporation method. However, all of the prepared
formulations showed homogeneity in size distribution with a low PDI.
In order to optimize the TrH loaded PLGA nanoparticles, different
experimental conditions were evaluated. As can be observed, all of the
selected variables had an effect on nanoparticle characteristics.
Encapsulation of TrH was enhanced by increasing initial drug and
polymer amount and particle size was decreased by increasing
homogenization speed and stabilizer ratio. Therefore, an optimized
parameters was selected to prepare a new formulation (F14) and further
experiments were done using F4, F9 and F14 formulations.
Determination of Drug Release
TrH release profiles from free TrH solution and formulated drug loaded
polymer nanoparticles were shown in Figure 2. The drug release
analysis of free TrT showed a burst release around 90% within the first
two hours. All nanoparticles showed slower drug release rate compared
to drug solution. The release rate was influenced by increasing the
partial amount of PLGA in the nanoparticles. (Figure 2). The burst
release at the first stage is a moderately normal fact in numerous drug
release systems [32]. However, the initial burst release was observed in
all formulations between only 0-0.5 h time zone and burst release was

decreased to 15-35 % and a sustained release was gotten for at least 30
h owing to the entrapment of TrH into PLGA matrix in the nanoparticles.
The unloaded water soluble drug may not be moved away from
nanoparticles surface during washing step. It is assumed that
nanoparticles prepared by the evaporation method of emulsification
solvent will present an initial burst release due to the drug adsorbed on
the surface [33]. Burst release can be useful to enhance the penetration
of drug while sustained release delivers the drug over extended period of
time. It should also be kept in mind that all nanoparticles show a slow
release, but increase the partial amount of PLGA and decrease the
homogenization rate, increase the drug release rate [29]. Formulations
F4, F9 and F14 showed similar release patterns. When the first 24 hours
were compared, less first-pass effect was observed in the F14
formulation compared to free tramadol and the F4 formulation that
releases the same amount of active ingredient. F14 formulation was
considered suitable in terms of release.

Infrared Spectroscopy (FT-IR) Analysis
Compared with FT-IR spectrum of drug and PLGA with the nanoparticle
formulations, no apparent changes were observed after preparation of
the nanoparticles (Figure 4a-c) [30]. Extended FT-IR spectra of TrH
presented nearly sharp bands at 1288 cm−1 due to C–O– stretching,
1607 cm−1 due to aromatic chain stretching, 2600 cm−1 owing to
ammonium N–H stretching, and around 3000 cm−1 due to C–H aliphatic
stretch [34]. All the nanoparticles showed the characteristic bands
verifying no chemical interactions between TrH and PLGA (Figure 4a-c).
These results are also were found at earlier similar studies like naproxen
loaded PLGA nanoparticles [30].

Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC) Analysis
DSC thermograms of pure TrH, PLGA and all the nanoparticle
formulations are given in Figure 4d-f. TrH showed a sharp melting
endothermic peak at 180 °C. This endothermic peak related that the TrH
was in crystalline anhydrous state [35]. When the nanoparticles
thermograms were checked, the wide-going peaks in the lower
temperatures were found. In any case, the wide-going endotherms
proposed to be disappeared for the nanoparticles so that the endotherm
couldn't be found in the nanoparticles with higher extents of polymer.
The nanoparticles portrayed no particular peak in the DSC thermograms
inferable from the diminished crystallinity in the formulations and
additionally drug solvation in the amorphous carrier just as solid state
interaction instigated by heating. The glass transition temperature of the
PLGA was found to be 55 °C which is in accordance with the studies
[33].
Stability Studies
Stability of lyophilized nanoparticles were evaluated after incubation of
nanoparticles for 3 months at given temperatures (Table 2) [36]. It was
determined that there was no significant difference between the particle

size of the formulation kept at -20 °C and +4 °C (p>0.05), while the
particle size and the PDI of the formulation increased after incubation at
25˚C for 3 months (p <0.05). It was observed that aggregation occurs
and widespread polydisperse distribution is formed. Lalani et al. found
similar results in their study about PLGA nanoparticles in 2013 [37]. The
high stability of nanoparticles at -20 °C and +4 °C may be the storage
conditions of PLGA and results were found similar with the earlier similar
studies. The stability of PLGA at different temperatures was examined
and it was determined that polymer degradation was minimum at -16˚C
after one year [38, 39]. It has been determined that at 25°C, particles
tend to aggregate under the effect of temperature, and therefore particle
size and distribution increase [40]. When stability studies were
examined, it can be seen that zeta potentials were very close to each
other at -20°C and 25°C in 3 months. Due to the low zeta potential,
particles may tend to aggregate. When the particle size in the 3-month
stability study was examined, it was determined that the particles
aggregated after 3 months at room temperature (25°C). In this case, it
was thought that keeping the particles at -20°C would be more
appropriate for their stability.

obtained and administered to patients, hence drugs which are stored for
a while and did not show any significant differences were selected for
stability studies for drug administration.

The particle size of nanoparticles incubated in 10% Dextran 40 and 4%
Succinyl gelatin solutions and zeta potential of nanoparticles incubated in
6% Hydroxyethyl starch in 0.9% sodium chloride injection increased (p >
0.05) after 24 hours; so it was evaluated that the nanoparticles were not
stable in these solutions. Hydroxyethyl starch, Dextran 40 and Succinyl
gelatin are high molecular weight polymer which can be used to
encapsulate drugs [41,42,43]. Moreover, these polymers can form
nanoparticles and microparticles together with PLGA [44,45,46]. Surface
coating of PLGA nanoparticles with these polymers generally occurs by
incubating nanoparticles in these solutions [45]. Hence it can be
mentioned that polymers adsorb on the surface of the particle surface
and forms microparticles. Since no significant differences were detected
in particle size and polydispersity indexes of nanoparticles in water for

injection, 20% Mannitol, 0.9% Sodium chloride and 5% Dextrose
solutions (p< 0.05), these solutions can be used for the infusion of TrH
nanoparticles.
Conclusion
The obtained results demonstrated that the emulsification solvent
evaporation technique can be used for the encapsulation of the TrH.
Nanoparticles with proper size for parenteral administration can also be
obtained. The stability studies for obtaining storage conditions suggested
that prepared nanoparticles can be stored at least 3 months at -20°C.
However, the administration of nanoparticles as infusion must be
carefully. The incubation of prepared nanoparticles in 6% Hydroxyethyl
starch in 0.9% sodium chloride injection, 10% Dextran 40 and 4%
Succinyl gelatin solutions resulted an unwanted size increase due to
adsorption of polymer in the solution. In other words, it was shown that
an appropriate delivery of TrH loaded PLGA nanoparticles as infusion
can be prepared only in water for injection, 20% Mannitol, 0.9% Sodium
chloride and 5% Dextrose solutions.
