Abstract

Aeromonas hydrophila, an opportunistic warm water pathogen, has always been a threat to aquaculture, leading to substantial economic losses. Vaccination of the cultured fish would effectively prevent Aeromoniasis, and recent advancements in nanotechnology show promise for efficacious vaccines. Oral delivery would be the most practical and convenient method of vaccine delivery in a grow-out pond. This study studied the immunogenicity and protective efficacy of a nanoparticle-loaded outer membrane protein A from A. hydrophila in the zebrafish model. The protein was over-expressed, purified, and encapsulated using poly lactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA) nanoparticles via the double emulsion method. The PLGA nanoparticles loaded with recombinant OmpA (rOmpA) exhibited a size of 295 ± 15.1 nm, an encapsulation efficiency of 72.52%, and a polydispersity index of 0.292 ± 0.07. Scanning electron microscopy confirmed the spherical and isolated nature of the PLGA-rOmpA nanoparticles. The protective efficacy in A. hydrophila-infected zebrafish after oral administration of the nanovaccine resulted in relative percentage survival of 77.7. Gene expression studies showed significant upregulation of immune genes in the vaccinated fish. The results demonstrate the usefulness of oral administration of nanovaccine-loaded rOmpA as a potential vaccine since it induced a robust immune response and conferred adequate protection against A. hydrophila in zebrafish, Danio rerio.

Introduction

Aquaculture is one of the important food sectors globally that is expected to address food and nutritional security. There has been exponential growth in the sector due to the increased demand for fish coupled with dwindling capture fisheries (Zhang et al. 2021). According to Food and Agricultural Organization statistics, global aquaculture production reached 122.6 million tonnes in 2020 (FAO 2022). Intensive practices that involve increased stocking density induce stress in the cultured species, resulting in disease outbreaks and consequent economic losses (Maiti et al. 2020). Bacterial diseases are one of the leading causes, and autochthonous bacteria in the aquatic environment, such as Aeromonas, Edwardsiella, and Flavobacterium, become major opportunistic pathogens of fish (Plebanski et al. 2013; Shaalan et al. 2016) Though antibiotics would be the preferred choice for treating bacterial diseases, they should be the least preferred due to the issues of antibiotic resistance developing and residues in the food fish. Consequently, this practice contributes to developing and spreading antimicrobial resistance in the aquatic environment (Nayak et al. 2023). Clinical signs are shared in many diseases caused by agents other than bacteria. Most often, antibiotics tend to be used empirically as it takes more than 48 to 72 h to culture, identify and study the antibiogram of the pathogen in question. Hence the maxim that ‘prevention is better than cure ‘works best in aquaculture settings. The most cost-effective and ethical disease control approach would be vaccinating the cultured species against target pathogens (Maiti et al. 2020). However, the challenge of an efficient vaccine and its delivery through a practical mode such as oral administration remains challenging (Wang et al. 2015; Zheng et al. 2016; Zhang et al. 2021). Although vaccine development has evolved significantly from a whole cell, heat-killed to use single proteins/peptides as antigens. Yet, the issue of appropriate delivery for maximum protective efficacy of the vaccines remains a challenge (Smith et al. 2015) It is now being increasingly recognized that the bacterial outer membrane proteins are potential vaccine candidates because of their exposed epitopes on the outer cell surface, which are highly conserved in nature and immunogenic (Vàzquez-Juárez et al. 2004; Maiti et al. 2009, 2012, 2022; Neema and Karunasagar 2018). Oral administration of the vaccine, though a method of choice, experience failure due to degradation in the gastrointestinal tract by the digestive enzymes with the consequent loss of their functional immunogenic potential. Hence, there is an urgent need to develop a proficient drug delivery system for site-specific action. A suitable delivery system necessitates activating the antigen-presenting cells and induction of a strong protective immune response (Mody et al. 2013) by minimizing antigen degradation, increasing bioavailability, and presenting the antigen to target immune cells (Gregory et al. 2013; Zaman et al. 2013). Polymeric nanoparticles have emerged as effective drug-delivery vehicles in fish immunization due to their ability to encapsulate and protect the bioactive molecules from degradation caused by hydrolysis and enzymatic processes (Maiti et al. 2023; Liu et al. 2023). In recent years, nanoparticle-based vaccines have been developed for use in aquaculture to enhance drug delivery and immune protection (Shaalan et al. 2016; Smith et al. 2015). Nanoparticles with the desired size promote endocytosis by the cells, increasing antigen absorption and presentation (Shaalan et al. 2016).

In this study, the outer membrane protein A (OmpA) of Aeromonas hydrophila was cloned and expressed, and the purified protein was encapsulated in poly lactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA) nanoparticles that were characterized and evaluated in vitro for their tolerance to the digestive enzymes, release efficiency and enhancing the immunogenicity of the antigen. Zebrafish were initially given the encapsulated vaccine orally through their feed, followed by a subsequent challenge with pathogenic A. hydrophila to study the level of protection. The oral delivery of the nano-encapsulated vaccine afforded protection.

Materials and methods

Bacterial cultures and molecular confirmation

A confirmed pathogenic strain of A. hydrophila (Aero B) (Nayak et al. 2023) stored in the institute repository in an ultra-deep freezer (-80°C) was used for the experimental study. For culture activation, it was subcultured into the nutrient broth and incubated at 30°C for 24 h, and subcultured on nutrient agar. The genus-specific confirmation was done by PCR using primers targeting lip gene (Cascon et al. 1996), aer gene (Santos et al. 1999), and ompW gene (Maiti et al. 2009). Primers are listed in Table 1. The reference strain of A. hydrophila used in the study was ATCC 49,140.

PCR amplification of ompA of A. hydrophila

The complete genome sequence of A. hydrophila, accession no GU196148.1, from GenBank, was used for retrieving the sequence of ompA gene to help design three different primers, namely F1, F2, and R (Table 1). The primers F1 and R were utilized for full-length gene amplification, cloning, and DNA sequencing. Primer F2 was designed to exclude the signal peptide-encoding region and, with primer R, was used for the cloning and expression of the ompA gene. PCR amplification was performed in 30 µL reaction volumes, comprising 1X Taq amplification buffer (HiMedia), 10 mM dNTPs (2.5 mM of each of the four dNTP), 20 pmol of each primers, and 1 U of Taq polymerase (HiMedia). A template ranging from 200 to 400 ng was added to the reaction, while nuclease-free water was negative control. The PCR reaction was carried out in a programmable thermocycler (Bio-Rad T100, USA) that included an initial denaturation at 95 °C for 5 min, followed by 30 cycles of denaturation at 95 °C for 1 min, annealing at 52 °C for 1 min, extension at 72 °C for 1 min, and a final extension at 72 °C for 10 min. The resultant PCR products were electrophoresed in 1.5 to 2% agarose gel, depending on the expected size of the product, stained with ethidium bromide (5 µg/mL), and images captured in a gel documentation system (Bio-Rad, USA).

Cloning and sequencing of ompA

The PCR product of the full-length open reading frame (ORF) of the ompA gene amplified using F1 and R primers, was purified using the Qiagen PCR purification kit (Qiagen, Germany) and cloned using the PCR cloning kit of Qiagen by following the manufacturer’s protocol (Qiagen, Germany). Positive colonies were identified through blue-white screening on Luria Bertani (LB)-ampicillin-X-gal plates containing 100 µg/mL ampicillin and 2% X-gal. The selected white colonies were confirmed by PCR using F1 and R primers. The inserts in the PCR-positive recombinant plasmids were outsourced for sequencing (M/S Eurofins Genomics India Pvt Ltd, Bengaluru, India). The nucleotide sequence was obtained and analyzed for similarity using the basic local alignment search tool (BLAST) program of NCBI.

Bioinformatics-based analysis of OmpA

The SignalP-5.0 web server (services.healthtech.dtu.dk) was employed to detect the presence of signal peptide, and VaxiJen v2.0 server (http://www.ddg-pharmfac.net) to calculate the OmpA antigenicity prediction score. Linear β-cell epitopes within the protein sequence were identified using the BepiPred-2.0 web server (Jespersen et al. 2017) which combines the hidden Markov model and propensity scale method. The number of antigenic sites in OmpA was determined using the immune medicine group web tool (http://imed.med.ucm.es/Tools/antigenic.pl). For the two-dimensional (2D) structure analysis of OmpA, the PRED-TMBB online server (Bagos et al. 2004) was used. The Swiss-Model program was employed for predicting the 3D topology through protein homology modeling.

Cloning and over-expression of recombinant OmpA (rOmpA)

The ompA gene, excluding the signal peptide, was amplified using F2 and R primers. The purified PCR product was ligated to the commercial pBAD expression system (ThermoFisher, USA) at 37 °C for 2 h and introduced into chemically competent Escherichia coli BL21 cells through the heat shock method. The transformed cells were selected on LB agar plates containing ampicillin (100 µg/ml), and confirmation of clones containing the recombinant plasmid was by PCR using F2 and R primers. The recombinant clones of OmpA were cultured in LB broth supplemented with ampicillin (100 µg/ml) at 37 °C with constant agitation. When the OD600 reached 0.5–0.8, the cells were induced with 0.2% arabinose. Protein samples were prepared from whole cell lysates, and the expression of the recombinant protein was visualized by running the samples in a 12% sodium dodecyl sulfate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) system following the protocol of (Laemmli et al. 1970).

Purification of rOmpA and Western blotting

The purification of the rOmpA protein was done by nickel-nitrilotriacetic acid (Ni-NTA) affinity chromatography (Qiagen) with six histidine tags (His6), following the protocol described earlier (Khushiramani et al. 2008; Maiti et al. 2009). The purity of the protein was assessed by 12% SDS-PAGE gel, and the concentration using the bicinchoninic acid (BCA) protein assay kit (Sigma-Aldrich, USA) and spectrophotometer (Multiskan SkyHigh microplate spectrophotometer, ThermoFisher, USA). Western blotting was done to characterize the purified rOmpA (Towbin et al. 1979). Mouse anti-His antibody (diluted 1:1000 in PBS, Biolegend, USA) was used as the primary antibody to detect the protein of interest. Protein bands that interacted with the antibody on the nitrocellulose membrane (Pall Corporation, USA) were detected after incubation with an anti-mouse horseradish peroxidase conjugate (diluted 1:2500 in PBS, Promega, USA). The chemiluminescent reaction was developed using enhanced chemiluminescence substrates (ECL Western blotting substrates, Bio-Rad, USA), and the bands were visualized using a chemiluminescence documentation system (MINI HD9, UVITEC, UK).

Encapsulation of rOmpA in PLGA NP and characterization

The rOmpA was encapsulated in PLGA NPs (PLGA Mn: 7000–17,000 MW, Sigma-Aldrich) using the W1/O/W2 double emulsion solvent evaporation method. In this process, 10 mg of the protein was dissolved in a buffer solution (100 mM NaH2PO4, 10 mM Tris-Cl, 8 M Urea, pH 4.5) and emulsified in the organic phase, which consisted of 0.9 mL of dichloromethane (DCM), 5 mg/mL PLGA, and 0.5 mg/mL Pluronic F-127 in 3 mL of polyvinyl alcohol (25 mg/mL PVA). The primary emulsion was created by sonication for 20 min on ice using a sonicator (Qsonica, USA). The outer aqueous phase was then added, and sonication was continued for an additional 20 min in ice chill condition to form the secondary emulsion. The resulting double emulsion was added to a 40 mL solution of 25 mg/mL PVA and stirred for 20 h at ambient temperature (27°c) using a magnetic stirrer until DCM completely evaporated. Protein-loaded PLGA NPs were isolated and washed twice with ultracentrifugation (Beckman Coulter, USA) at 25,000 rpm for 30 min at 4 °C (Baumann et al. 2009; Pakulska et al. 2016). Finally, the obtained pellets were dispersed in 1X PBS and subjected to lyophilization using a lyophilizer (VaCo 2, Zirbus Technology, Germany). To determine the size (hydrodynamic diameter) and polydispersity index of the NPs, a zeta sizer (SZ-100V2, Horiba Instruments Ltd, Japan) was used. The lyophilized samples were appropriately diluted with PBS (pH 7.4), and aliquots from each batch were examined in dynamic light scattering (DLS) cuvettes. The NPs were analyzed for their polydispersity index and size distribution using DLS at a scattering angle of 90° and a temperature of 25 °C. Diameter and polydispersity index measurements were performed in triplicate for each preparation, and the standard deviation was calculated. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) was used to study the morphology of the NPs. The SEM imaging service was provided by the Central Research Facility of the National Institute of Technology Karnataka, India, using a SEM (ZEISS Gemini, Australia).

Encapsulation efficiency measurement and in vitro protein release study

To confirm the integrity of the protein and to determine its encapsulation efficiency, the encapsulated particles containing rOmpA were dissolved in a solution of 0.5% w/v SDS (pH 7.4), which was prepared by dissolving SDS in 0.1 M NaOH solution (Dubey et al. 2016). This mixture was incubated for 24 h, followed by centrifugation at 12,000 rpm. The concentration of rOmpA in the supernatant was determined using a BCA protein assay kit (Pierce™ BCA protein assay kit, ThermoFisher), enabling the measurement of the free antigen concentration. The amount of protein encapsulated within the nanoparticles was calculated by subtracting the concentration of free protein present in the supernatant from the total protein loaded during the NPs preparation, employing the formula described by Rauta et al. (2015). Encapsulation efficiency (%) = (Total amount of protein - Free protein) / (Total amount of protein) x 100. To study the protein release from the nanoparticles over time, protein-loaded nanoparticles were incubated in 1X PBS, and samples were collected at various time points (0, 3, 6, 9, and up to 48 h). Following centrifugation at 12,000 rpm for 10 min, 500 µL of the supernatant was collected, and an equal volume of PBS was added (Hori et al. 2005; Dubey et al. 2016). The released rOmpA protein in the collected samples was quantified using the BCA method (Pierce™ BCA protein assay kit, ThermoFisher). Western blotting was performed to validate the protein’s native conformation per the previously described protocol.

Fish and bacterial strains

Zebrafish (Karnataka strain) with a standard length of 3.0 ± 1 cm and weighing around 1 ± 0.5 g were used for the experiments. Zebrafish were bred at the sex ratio of two males and one female; the following day, eggs were collected, and hatched embryos (after three days of spawning) were reared. The water parameters were measured regularly throughout the experiment to ensure optimal water quality. The water temperature was maintained in the range of 25–28 °C. The fish were randomly allocated to experimental groups consisting of sixty fish each, with three replicates per group (twenty fish per tank), and continuous aeration was provided.

Vaccine preparations for oral delivery

For preparing the PLGA NPs rOmpA (NP-rOmpA) vaccine intended for oral immunization, the commercial feed for zebrafish was ground and sieved to achieve a fine texture. The NP-rOmpA vaccine was blended with the feed, to form a homogeneous dough. The moist vaccine-feed mixture was pelletized by extruding it through a hand extruder, air-dried at room temperature (32°c), and stored at 4 °C until use.

Vaccination and challenge study

Examination of the health status of fish was based on clinical observation by checking the surface morphology, pathological signs, clinical examination, and abnormal swimming behaviour. In addition, four fish were sacrificed to examine the pathological changes. The zebrafish were randomly divided into four groups (60 fish per group) (Fig. 1). Group 1 was the control group fed with commercial feed and unvaccinated; Group 2 was fed with empty NPs without rOmpA protein; Group 3 was fed with NPs coated rOmpA protein at a high dose of 8 µg/g of fish body weight and designated as the high dose NPs (HDNP) group; Group 4 was fed with a low dose of 4 µg/g of fish body weight and designated as the low dose NPs (LDNP) group (Dubey et al. 2016). The experimental trials were for a period of 51 days, and the fish were fed twice daily. After two weeks of immune stimulation, the fish were challenged by immersion with a pathogenic strain of A. hydrophila. From the PBS resuspended stock culture, 10 ml of the culture was added to 1 L of water to get a final concentration of 1.6 × 109 CFU/ml. Fourteen days post-challenge, mortality, and clinical signs were recorded, and protection was estimated using Kaplan Meyer’s survival analysis. The relative percentage survival (RPS) of the zebrafish was registered for 14 consecutive days and calculated according to the formula (Kole et al. 2019).

Expression of immune-related genes in the vaccinated fish

For gene expression analysis, tissue samples were collected from the vaccinated fish on days 0, 10, 30, and 51 post-vaccinations. Total RNA extraction was performed using the TRIzol (Invitrogen, USA) reagent method. The extracted RNA samples were diluted in RNase-free water, and the concentration and purity of RNA were determined using a spectrophotometer (NanoPhotometer®, Implen, USA). To study the expression levels of IFN-α, IL-1β, TLR-2, TNF-α, and IL-10 genes in the tissues, quantitative polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR) was carried out. The one-step TB Green PrimeScript RT-PCR kit (Takara, Japan) was used for RT-qPCR, and the reactions were performed on a CFX Opus 96 Real-Time PCR System (Bio-Rad). Gene-specific primers listed in Table 1 were used to quantify gene expression, and the ELF gene served as an internal control. The relative gene expression of each group was calculated using the formula: Relative expression = 2^(-ΔΔCt), where ΔCt represents the difference between the sample Ct value and the ELF Ct value, and ΔΔCt indicates the difference between the ΔCt value of the immunized group and the control group. The study utilized triplicate data points for analysis (Zhang et al. 2021).

Statistical analysis

The results obtained from the RT-qPCR were analyzed using two-way ANOVA to determine the statistical significance. Results were analyzed using GraphPad Prism, version 8.4.3. (GraphPad, CA, USA).

Results

PCR identification of ompA of A. hydrophila

The gene ompA was confirmed by amplifying the target sequence with F1 and R primers through PCR; the amplified product showed a prominent single band of 1032 base pairs. Further, the exclusion of the signal peptide region was confirmed by visualizing the PCR-amplified product of 960 bp (Fig. S1).

Bioinformatic analysis of ompA

Bioinformatic analysis revealed the signal peptide in the initial frame from 1 to 21 amino acids in the precursor OmpA. The protein displayed a pI value of 7.4 and a molecular weight of 52.41 kDa. Its antigenic nature was confirmed by a comprehensive vaxiJen score of 0.7079. Upon analyzing the OmpA sequence, eleven β-cell epitopes and twelve antigenic sites were identified. The predicted 2D structure indicated that the four external loops were located on the outer side of the protein. Additionally, the 3D model of OmpA revealed the presence of four β-barrel strands (Fig. S2).

Cloning and expression of rOmpA

The DNA sequence of the ompA gene product was analyzed, deposited in the GenBank, and assigned accession number OM912661. The optimal expression of the rOmpA occurred four hours post-induction with 0.2% arabinose. The molecular weight of the resulting OmpA sequence was 52.41 kDa in 12% SDS-PAGE (Fig. 2). The purified recombinant protein exhibited high purity on SDS-PAGE, and a distinct band was obtained in the Western blot analysis, confirming the presence of protein OmpA.

Encapsulation and characterization

The size distribution analysis of PLGA NPs encapsulating rOmpA had a size range of 209 ± 36.1 nm for the empty PLGA NPs (Fig. 3A). In comparison, the rOmpA-encapsulated NPs exhibited a larger size range of 295.5 ± 15.1 nm (Fig. 3B). The polydispersity index was determined to be 0.292 ± 0.07. The SEM presented the spherical structure of the formulated NPs (Fig. 3C).

Encapsulation efficiency and in vitro protein release study

The encapsulation efficiency of rOmpA in PLGA NPs was found to be 78.52%. In vitro release studies showed that approximately 53% of the encapsulated protein was released within 48 h of incubation (Fig. 4A). The presence of a prominent band in 12% SDS-PAGE gel indicated that the native structure of the protein was intact, indicating retention of its structural integrity. A sample containing PLGA-encapsulated rOmpA was assessed for the native structure of the protein. Western blotting confirmed the integrity of the protein, resulting in a distinct band corresponding to the purified protein (52.41 kDa). The structural integrity of the native rOmpA remained intact throughout the encapsulation process (Fig. 4B).

Challenge study

There was a significant difference in post-challenge survival proportions (PCSPs) between the vaccinated and control groups. In the control and empty nanoparticle groups, mortality started two days before the vaccinated group. There was a significant difference in the PCSPs between the control and vaccinated groups. There was a dose-dependent effect on PCSPs with the HDNP group (8 µg/g of fish body weight), which had a significantly higher PCSP than the LDNP (4 µg/g of fish body weight) group. Regarding RPS, the HDNP group showed RPS of 77.7, followed by the LDNP group of 64.4. The empty NP showed a low RPS of 28.6 (Fig. 5).

Expression analysis of immune genes

Post-vaccination, RT-qPCR was performed to analyze the expression levels of the immune genes in the fish body tissues. There was significant upregulation of the IL-1β gene in the LDNP and HDNP groups in 30 and 50 days, respectively, post-vaccination, compared to the ENP group. On the 50th day of post-vaccination, IL-10 was regulated in the HDNP group as compared to the ENP group, whereas TNF-α upregulation was observed in the ENP group on the 50th day. IFN-γ was significantly upregulated in both the LDNP and HDNP groups on the 50th day post-vaccination. TLR-2 showed a significant upregulation in the LDNP nanoparticle group on the 30th day, but was downregulated on the 50th day (Fig. 6).

Following the challenge with the pathogen, a significant upregulation of the IL-10 gene was observed in the HDNP group. TNF-α gene upregulation was detected in both the HDNP and LDNP groups. IFN-γ gene was upregulated in the ENP group. There was a significant upregulation in the TLR-2 and IL-1β gene expression in the HDNP group. (Fig. 7). The data analyzed by 2-way ANOVA (GraphPad Prism) Error bar represents the ± SEM. P values are < 0.0001 for (****) extremely significant,0.0001 (***) extremely significant, 0.001 to 0.01 for (**) very significant, 0.01 to 0.05 for (*) for significant, and ≥ 0.05 for non-significant values.

Discussion

Vaccination is one of the best approaches to prevent disease and its spread in aquaculture farms. The most practical method of administration would be by oral route. It is referred to because it is the least stressful and efficient in terms of time, cost, and suitability for fish of different sizes (Silin et al. 2007; Nakajima et al. 2014; Salgado-Miranda et al. 2013). However, oral vaccines are susceptible to degradation in the enzyme-rich gastrointestinal environment and may not reach the target sites for effective uptake by immune cells. Encapsulating the vaccine in an appropriate nanoparticle would offer protection from degradation in the stomach and intestinal tract and further enhance the immune response of the oral vaccine over a prolonged period (Zhang et al. 2021).

The PLGA nanoparticles generated in this study could encapsulate the 52.41 kDa OmpA protein of A. hydrophila. Following encapsulation, an increase in size (295 ± 15.1 nm) was observed compared to empty nanoparticles. Several studies have demonstrated that particles with a size of < 500 nm can be successfully endocytosed in the body without causing toxicity (Champion et al. 2007; Baumann et al. 2009). Dubey et al. (2016) reported good protection against A. hydrophila infection by orally administered OmpW vaccine with a particle size of 370–375 nm. The PDI has a distribution range of 0.000 to 0.500, and values > 0.5 indicate particle aggregation (Baumann et al. 2009; Behera et al. 2013). The polydispersity index (PDI) of 0.292 ± 0.07 for the protein-loaded PLGA nanoparticles in this study confirmed their monodisperse nature. The SEM images showed the PLGA-rOmpA particles as being spherical, regular, and with isolated morphology, consistent with previous studies of Rauta et al. (2015) and Behera et al. (2013).

The encapsulation efficiency of 78.52%, reported in this study, was higher than the encapsulation efficiency reported previously (Danhier et al. 2012; Dubey et al. 2016). Higher encapsulation efficiencies are desirable as they enhance the ability of nanoparticles to deliver the protein in vivo (Estey et al. 2006).The release study showed that 55% of rOmpA was released within 48 h. The in vitro release kinetics of rOmpA followed the common trend of an initial burst release in the first 3 h, gradually increasing until it reached a plateau after 46 h. As reported in earlier studies, the PLGA-rOmpA nanoparticles exhibited a high initial burst release (Rauta et al. 2015; Dubey et al. 2016), which could be attributed to the diffusion of the protein through water-filled pores in the PLGA matrix, facilitated by water uptake from the release medium (Hamod et al. 2012). Despite the high initial burst release and incomplete release observed in this study, the stability of the encapsulated protein was confirmed by Western blotting. The rOmpA protein did not undergo any conformational changes following encapsulation which is evident from the western blotting. Band size observed around 52.41 kDa, from this evidence of the fact that neither the encapsulation process nor the in vitro release from PLGA caused any structural damage or degradation of the protein. It is worth noting that certain procedures involved in NP syntheses, such as the use of organic solvents, probe sonication, forceful shaking, and lyophilization, can potentially inactivate proteins (Baumann et al. 2009; Estey et al. 2006).

The finfish immune system comprises innate and adaptive immune systems (Vinay et al. 2018; Zhang et al. 2021). After exposure to the pathogen, innate mechanisms come into play quickly, resulting in a series of immune responses (Laing et al. 2004; Zhang et al. 2021). The intestinal mucosa has epithelial cells which help deliver antigens to the dendritic cells and initiate immune response during oral administration (Fredriksen et al. 2011; Zhang et al. 2021). Enriched antigens may stimulate a robust immune response resulting in altered expression of immune-related genes (Zhang et al. 2021). When encapsulated in the PLGA nanoparticle, the immune components stay protected and thus improving the immune effect of the oral vaccine (Zhang et al. 2021). According to Das et al. (2011), early stages of A. hydrophila infection were reported to generate pro-inflammatory cytokines in the anterior kidney of Puntius sarana. Antibody-mediated humoral immunity plays a significant role in fish during bacterial infections, caused by Vibrio anguillarum, A. hydrophila, Flavobacterium psychrophilum, Streptococcus agalactiae, and Streptococcus iniae, leading to enhanced protection.

An interesting observation in this study was the expression of some of the cytokines in fish vaccinated with the nanoparticle-encapsulated rOmpA. IL-10 is considered an anti-inflammatory cytokine, intended to reduce inflammation (Zhang et al. 2005). By reducing pro-inflammatory effects and blocking chemokine receptors, IL-10 can reduce host cell damage. It is most likely connected to the immunoglobulin generated by B cells (Hummelshoj et al. 2006). There was a progressive upregulation in the IL-10 expression level in the HDNP group till 50 days of vaccination. This observation corroborates with an earlier study by Zhang et al. (2013), where the vaccinated groups showed an increase in the expression level of the IL-10 gene at three days post-vaccination (dpv). Upregulation of IL-10 plays a crucial role in maintaining a balance between pro and anti-inflammatory responses (Jiang et al. 2016) and helps regulate the ratio of cells involved in pro to anti-inflammatory responses (Jiang et al. 2016). Several immunostimulants have been reported to increase IL-10 mRNA expression levels in fish, contributing to transcriptional changes in innate immune cells (Harikrishnan et al. 2021). In the HDNP nano vaccine group, the highest expression of IL-10 was observed on the 14th-days of post-vaccination. This observation indicates a favourable response induced by the nanovaccine, highlighting its effectiveness in enhancing the production of IL-10 and potentially contributing to regulating inflammatory processes. IFN-γ, as an immunoregulatory molecule, plays a crucial role in both innate and adaptive immunity (Zhang et al. 2021). In this study, IFN-γ gene expression was upregulated in the LDNP group on the 50th day of vaccination, suggesting that the PLGA-rOmpA vaccine may induce both adaptive and innate immune responses. This induction potentially promotes the activation of immune-related macrophages and inflammatory response processes (Siegrist et al. 2011; Pereiro et al. 2016). The initial vaccination in these fish possibly triggered an immune response, which, upon subsequent pathogen challenge, resulted in a decreased level of IFN-γ in the HDNP group observed on the 14th-day post-challenge.

In the LDNP group, TLR-2 expression was upregulated on the 30th day of vaccination, followed by a decrease on the 50th day, possibly indicating the neutralization of inflammation or a reduction in bacterial counts. These findings suggest the antigenic potential of rOmpA of A. hydrophila, as evidenced by the expression patterns of the pro-inflammatory cytokine in the nano-vaccinated group.

The TLR network system is known to induce the production of reactive nitrogen, oxidative radicals, and cytokines (Palti et al. 2011). Therefore, TLR expression may depend on both the specific molecule and the pathogen involved. TNF-α, on the other hand, stimulates the production of E-selectin and other CC and CXC chemokines in endothelial cells, which aid in the recruitment and activation of phagocytes during infection (Yadav et al. 2018). The upregulation of TLR-2 levels in the HDNP group following the post-challenge period was observed in this study. The initial vaccination in these fish possibly triggered an immune response, which increased the gene expression upon subsequent pathogen challenge. Previous studies have reported varying levels of TLR-22 expression at different time intervals, such as 3 h, 12 h, and on day 28, following immunization with rOmpF in fish tissue (Zhang et al. 2013). Zhang et al. (2013) also demonstrated a transient increase in the expression of multiple TLR genes, including TLR-22, following the administration of heat-killed A. hydrophila in yellow catfish, with the expression levels returning to basal levels later. The findings suggest that the expression of TLR genes may be specific to the molecule or pathogen involved, indicating a context-dependent regulation.

Studies have analyzed the role of IL-1β (Interleukin-1β), a typical pro-inflammatory cytokine and a major player in the immune response in fish (Secombes et al. 1996). In the present study, IL-1β upregulation was seen on the 30th and 50th days post-vaccination. The upregulation of IL-1β cytokine expression levels in the intestine, spleen, and head kidney of the immunized group at 28 days of immunization has been observed earlier. The results of this study observed the activation of monocytes or macrophages, which multiplied and produced immune effectors (e.g., IL-1β) and exerted immunomodulatory effects (Lo et al. 1999; Gordon et al. 2010). A significantly higher IL-1β level in the LDNP group was observed 14 days post-challenge. Higher level of IL-1β and TNF- α expression in that group, up to 48 h post-challenge, shows that these cytokines are activated immediately after infection. The subsequent decline in their levels may signify a reduction in the bacterial load or the neutralization of the inflammation (Dash et al. 2017).

TNF- α was significantly overexpressed on the 50th day of vaccination, and even in the challenge study, on the 14th day, there was an increase in the expression level of this gene in the HDNP group. Zebrafish vaccinated with a live attenuated strain of Edwardsiella tarda demonstrated upregulation in the expression of TNF-α and IL-8 during the initial phases of the challenge Dash et al. (2017). In another study by Rojo et al. (2007), an increase in the expression levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines following infection was observed.

Several researchers have investigated changes in gene expression in fish following vaccination or infection. However, understanding the immune response in vaccinated fish following a bacterial challenge remains limited (Harun et al. 2011). In the A. hydrophila challenge study, upregulation of IL-1β, IL-10, IFN-γ, TLR-2, and TNF-α genes in the vaccinated group was observed, indicating that PLGA-rOmpA could induce innate and adaptive immune responses by activating macrophages and triggering an inflammatory response. Similar results were observed in Larimichthys crocea fish vaccinated with the MSN-DLDH@HP55 oral vaccine against Vibrio alginolyticus. Notably, the expression levels of IL-1β, IL-10, IFN-γ, TLR-2, and TNF-α in the tissues showed peak levels at different time points, potentially associated with the route of vaccination by oral administration, as the fish were vaccinated for 51 days through feeding. These findings suggest that innate immunity plays a significant regulatory role during oral administration in fish. Similar results were reported by Zhang et al. (2021) in their experimental study of oral vaccine administration.

The immune system of fish is known for its complexity, and there are limited studies focusing on the regulatory mechanisms of immune factors through oral vaccination. This study contributes valuable data on the immune factors associated with nano-oral vaccines in fish. It confirms that incorporating protective antigens into nanoparticles can effectively stimulate durable innate and adaptive immune responses. Interestingly, the rOmpA-PLGA vaccine developed in this study demonstrated superior efficacy in providing protection. When challenged with a pathogen, the RPS of zebrafish reached 77.7, indicating promising outcomes.

Conclusions

In conclusion, the developed nano vaccine delivery offers several advantages, including high antigen loading capacity and improved biocompatibility. The ability to elicit a durable immune response and the protection against Aeromonas species in zebrafish utilizing the PLGA delivery system is evidence of the commercial potential of an oral vaccine. The obtained RPS results show promise as it demonstrates the efficacy of the orally administered rOmpA-PLGA vaccine against pathogenic A. hydrophila. The vaccine trials can be extended to the field, and target different fish species, where Aeromonas epizootic frequently presents a threat. However, optimization of the vaccine dose, formulation ratio for oral delivery, and determining the appropriate immunization dose intervals for field application needs to be standardized for the individual fish species being cultivated.
