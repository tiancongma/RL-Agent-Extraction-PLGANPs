Supercritical emulsion extraction (SEE) is proposed as a green and effective strategy for the fabrication of
chitosan-covered poly-lactic-co-glycolic acid (chi-PLGA) injectable microcapsules for the controlled
release of teriparatide (THA) and teriparatide/gentamicin sulfate (THA/Gen). These formulations can be
used for locally bone pathologies treatment or in complex fracture healing of aged patients. Several
oil-water (o-w) and water-oil-water (w-o-w) emulsions were processed by SEE to produce multifunc-
tional microcapsules containing hydroxyapatite (HA) within a poly-lactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA) matrix
(up to 24 mg/g) and with both THA (0.45 mg/g) and Gen (up to 9 mg/g). Chitosan coating was also
successfully added, as external layer (0.4 mm). SEE-fabricated microcapsules showed good encapsulation
efﬁciency (up to 90%) for all the drugs tested and a mean size ranging between 1.4 (±0.4) mm and 2.2
(±0.5) mm. Different drug amounts loaded and microcapsules compositions assured a controlled drug
release over a wide range of times and concentrations, as in vitro monitored in PBS medium at 37C for
15/20 days. HA embedded into the biopolymer structure delayed the THA release proﬁle; chitosan
coating strongly reduced the initial drug “burst” release. In addition, the coencapsulation of both THA
and Gen, which have very different water solubility, accelerated the release proﬁle of the less water-
soluble drug. No drugs degradation was also monitored after the SEE manufacturing. Apparent drug
diffusivities (D) were calculated by ﬁtting of the release proﬁles. In the case of Gen, D ranged between
2.9  108 and 1.6  109 cm2s1 if the drug was entrapped in simple PLGA or in the chitosan-coated
microcapsules, respectively. In the case of THA, the calculated values ranged between 8.1  109 and
7.4  1010 cm2s1 when the drug was entrapped in PLGA/HA microcapsules or in the chitosan-coated
ones, respectively. These mass transfer values are consistent with the different release behaviors
observed and conﬁrmed the possibility of multicomponent microcapsules fabrication by SEE.

Introduction
Osteoporosis affects more than 75 million people in USA, Europe,
and Japan,1 with a consequent increase in bone fragility and
susceptibility to fracture at any skeletal site,2,3 and an enormous
economic
burden
on
health
care
systems
in
Europe
and
worldwide.
Teriparatide
(THA)
is
currently
Food
and
Drug
Administrationeapproved for the treatment of osteoporosis and
osteoarthritis4 and may strongly improve fracture healing or repair
bone defects.5 THA has been reported to accelerate and improve
healing at the standard available dose of 20-40 mg/day, especially for
aged patients with a fracture.6-8 THA is the recombinant 1-34 amino
acid segment of the parathyroid hormone (PHT) with a molecular
weight of 4117.72 g/mol. THA binds to the receptor for PHT present in
bone and stimulates the G-protein-related production of cyclic
adenosine monophosphate.9 During treatment, an increase in
trabecular connectivity and cortical thickness is observed, with
consequent improvement of the microarchitectural strength of
bone10; THA may also stimulate mesenchymal stem cell proliferation
and stem cell maturation in the musculoskeletal lineage.11

Despite the demonstrated clinical efﬁcacy of THA, patient
acceptance and compliance is limited by the need for daily sub-
cutaneous injections. The development of an equally efﬁcient
formulation not requiring daily injections would signiﬁcantly
expand the present market.12,13 Moreover, among all the possible
drug administration, a local therapy, if indicated, has many po-
tential advantages such as circumventing possible adverse side
effects resulting from systemic administration, decreasing dose or
number of dosages, and maintaining local agent levels within a
desirable range.14 Several devices have been already explored for
THA (and PHT)-controlled delivery such as, multipulse subcu-
taneous implantable devices15,16 or microchip,17 coated micro-
needle patch system,18 or implantable biopolymer scaffold.19 Eli
Lilly recently has launched a new THA formulation in the injectable
form and Daiichi and West Pharmaceuticals are working on nasal
formulations.
Poly-lactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA) excellent biocompatibility
and biodegradability makes this biopolymer an appropriate carrier
for drug delivery; in addition, PLGA has been approved by the Food
and Drug Administration as a safe biomaterial for clinical applica-
tions. The intraarticular and local injection of microcarriers, fabri-
cated with PLGA and able of controlled and local THA delivery may
be an interesting option.20 Recently, it was reported a successful
suppression of osteoarthritis progression in rats replacing the
single-dose injection, required every 3 days, by using PLGA (65:35)
microspheres able to release the drug for 19 days with a concen-
tration range of 5-100 nM (covering 10 nM) at 37C. The in vivo
study showed after intraarticular treatment with either 3 days/in-
jection or PLGA microspheres (15 days/injection) for 5 weeks a
similar
effect
on
suppressing
papain-induced
osteoarthritis
changes, by decreasing production of GAG and Col II and increasing
Col III in rat knee cartilage.21
An engineered size distribution, as well as, excellent drug
encapsulation efﬁciency are very important fabrication parameters
for microcapsules industrial production to ensure a controlled and
reproducible drug sustained release formulation. In this sense,
supercritical ﬂuid technologies may offer an improved control over
the morphology and composition of microdevices and nanodevices
coupled with good fabrication process robustness.22-25 The basic
idea of use supercritical carbon dioxide (SC-CO2) as the extracting
agent of the oily phase of an emulsion, leading solvent-free mi-
crospheres was proposed in 200626; the original process was per-
formed using a batch operative layout, then was later improved by
other
authors
and
named
supercritical
emulsion
extraction
(SEE).27-29 After these pioneering articles, new SEE continuous
process layout was proposed using a countercurrent-packed tower
to reduce the emulsion processing time (few minutes of residence
time),
improve
the
encapsulation
efﬁciency
(especially
if
double
emulsions
are
treated),
and
assured
higher
process
reproducibility.30
In this work, we will explore the SEE technology (continuous
layout) to fabricate an engineered, versatile, and multiloaded PLGA
microdevice for THA sustained release. Particularly, hydroxyapatite
(HA) will be embedded into the microcarrier structure because it is
widely used in bone tissue engineering and is reported to be good
matrix for bone cell differentiation and mineralization.31 Indeed,
in vitro and in vivo studies of biopolymer/HA composites found that
osteoblast cells adhered to HA preferentially, leading to subsequent
cell proliferation, and the composite implants displayed good bone
integration and osteoconductivity in rabbit models.32,33 Artiﬁcial
defects induced in the osteoporotic bone of the rat mandible were
also successfully reconstructed following implantation of HA
nanoparticles coated with cholecalciferol-loaded PLGA devices,34
whereas hollow HA microspheres loaded with clinically safe
doses of bone morphogenetic proteine2 could provide promising
implants for healing nonloaded bone defects.35 Chitosan (Chi) will
be also chosen because it was already used to repair critical bone
defects36-38; some authors also indicated that Chi/PLGA micro-
sphere combined with appropriate drug delivery can achieve
accelerated bone healing both in vitro and in vivo and were able to
guide bone formation in a rabbit ulnar critical-sized-defect
model.39 In our hypothesis, the basic inorganic component of HA
could interfere with the acidic degradation products of PLGA
polymer, affecting the drug release, whereas the chitosan coating
would additionally delay the drug release. Gentamicin (Gen) is
largely used in the treatment of osteomyelitis,40 and it will be also
coencapsulated in the microcarriers to understand SEE versatility
in producing
microcapsules
with a simultaneous release of
different molecules.
Different oil-in-water (o-w) and water-in-oil-in-water (w-o-w)
emulsions will be processed by SEE and the fabricated microcap-
sule (1) morphology will be observed using scanning electron mi-
croscopy, (2) particle size distribution (PSD) and zeta potential
measured by a laser scattering. HA loading and its dispersion in the
biopolymer matter will be measured by thermogravimetric anal-
ysis (TGA) and energy-dispersive X-ray analysis (EDX), respectively,
whereas drugs loading and encapsulation efﬁciency will be moni-
tored by chemical analysis. The effect of HA embedding and chi-
tosan coating on drugs release will be both explored by monitor the
in vitro drug release proﬁles obtained in PBS medium at 37C. THA
and Gen apparent diffusivities will be calculated by ﬁtting the
release proﬁles obtained from the different microcapsules.
Materials and Methods
Materials
CO2 (99.9%) was purchased from SON (Naples, Italy). Polyvinyl
alcohol (mol wt: 30,000-55,000), polysorbate (Tween80), ter-
iparatide (THA, lyophilized powder, purity 99.9%; water solubility
0.40 mg/mL), gentamicin sulfate (Gen, powder, purity 99.9%; water
solubility 50 mg/mL) were received from Sigma Aldrich Co. (Milan,
Italy). Hydroxyapatite nanoparticles (HA, nanopowder, mean size
100) were supplied by Sigma-Aldrich (PLGA 50:50, RESOMER®
RG 504H, Mol wt: 38,000-54,000) was obtained from Evonik In-
dustries (Essen, Germany). Ethyl acetate (EA, purity 99.9%), acetone
(purity 99.9%), 2-propanol, and methanol were supplied by Carlo
Erba
Reagents
(Milan,
Italy).
Sodium
borate,
ortho-
phthaldialdehyde, and 2-mercaptoethanol were used for the
preparation of the reagents in the analytical method and were all
supplied by Sigma-Aldrich Co. HPLC grade acetonitrile and tri-
ﬂuoroacetic acid were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. Ultrapure
water was obtained using Milli-Qs UF-Plus (Millipore) system.
Emulsion Preparation and Supercritical Emulsion Extraction
Apparatus
Different o-w emulsions (ratio 20:80 w/w) were used to load HA
into PLGA. The oily phase was always prepared by dissolving 2 g of
PLGA in 20 g of EA, whereas the amount of HA was varied between
100 and 50 mg to reach nominal loadings of 25-50 mg/gPLGA,
respectively. HA was dispersed as solid nanoparticles in the oily
phase by sonication at 40% of the nominal power (mod. S-450D,
Branson Ultrasonics Corporation, Danbury, CT) for 1 min. The oily
phase was then added into 80 g of EA-saturated aqueous Tween80
solution (0.6% w/w in water) by high-speed stirring (mod. L4RT,
Silverson Machines Ltd., Waterside, CheshamBucks, UK) for 3 min
at 3200 rpm. Several w-o-w emulsions (ratio 1:19:80 w/w) were
used to load THA and Gen into PLGA or HA/PLGA microcapsules.
One mL of polyvinyl alcohol solution (0.06% w/w) with a ﬁxed

with the oily phase (with or without HA) to form a primary w1-o

Branson Ultrasonics Corporation) for 1 min. The primary emulsion
was, then, immediately added into the external water phase.
The emulsions were processed by SEE immediately after prep-
aration. The continuous layout apparatus consisted of a high
pressure column (100 cm height; 133 cm3 internal volume) formed
by 3 stages of stainless steel cylindrical elements packed with
stainless steel packing (1889 m1 speciﬁc surface; 0.94 of voidage;
ProPak, Scientiﬁc Development Company, State College, PA). The
apparatus was thermally insulated by ceramic cloths, and its tem-
perature proﬁle was controlled by 6 temperature controllers.
SC-CO2 was fed at the bottom of the column by a high-pressure
diaphragm pump (model Milroyal B; Milton Roy, Pont Saint-
Pierre, France) at a constant ﬂow rate, whereas the emulsion was
fed from the top at a constant ﬂow rate using a high-pressure piston
pump (model 305; Gilson, Villiers le Bel, France). Operative pres-
sure and temperature conditions used in the present work were 8
MPa and 38C, respectively, with a SC-CO2 ﬂow rate of 1.4 kg/h and
a liquid/gas ﬂow rates ratio (L/G) of 0.1 on mass based.30 Micro-
sphere suspension was also continuously collected at the bottom of
the extraction column and washed with water before its lyophili-
zation. The drug not encapsulated was removed during the washing
procedure.
Droplet and Microsphere Morphology
The droplets formed in the emulsion were observed using an
optical microscope (OM, mod. BX 50 Olympus, Tokyo, Japan)
equipped with a phase contrast condenser. Microsphere shape and
morphology were investigated by ﬁeld emissionescanning elec-
tron microscopy (mod. LEO 1525, Carl Zeiss SMT AG, Oberkochen,
Germany) coupled with an EDX (INCA Energy 350, UK). Samples of
powder were placed on a double-sided adhesive carbon tape pre-
viously stuck to an aluminum stub and coated with a thin gold ﬁlm
(layer thickness 250 Å) using a sputter coater (mod.108 A; Agar
Scientiﬁc, Stansted, UK).
Microcapsule Size Distributions and Suspension Z-Potential
PSDs were measured using a Mastersizer S apparatus (mod.
Mastersizer S, Malvern Instruments Ltd., Worcestershire, UK),
based on dynamic light scattering. Analyses were performed
immediately after the preparation of the emulsions and of the
microsphere suspensions, using several milligrams of each sample,
appropriately diluted with distilled water. A Zetasizer (mod.
NanoZS Malvern Inc., Wocherstershire, UK) equipped with a 5.0-
mW He-Ne laser operating at 633 nm was used for zeta-potential
measurements. Samples were analyzed in glass cuvettes at 25C
and scattering angle of 173; all the results are based on an average
of 3 measurements, which was calculated on an average of 10 runs.
Zeta-potential value expressed in mV indicates the potential dif-
ference between the dispersion medium and the stationary layer of
ﬂuid attached on the dispersed particle and is a key indicator of the
dispersion stability.
Solid-State Characterization
The HA encapsulation efﬁciency was calculated by TGA, as the
dry residue remained after the total combustion of the polymer.
Thermal Analyser (mod. Pyris 1; Perkin Elmer, Shelton, CT)
measured the amount of weight change of a material as a function
of increasing temperature in an atmosphere of nitrogen, helium, air,
other gas, or in vacuum. In our case, air was used. The sample was
accurately weighted (5 ± 0.02 mg) into an alumina crucibles and
heated up to 600C at a rate of 10C/min, under a constant air ﬂow
of 50 Ncm3/min.
Chitosan Coating
To obtain chitosan-coated microdevices (chi-PLGA), the micro-
capsules, as recovered after the SEE process, were washed by
ultracentrifugation, resuspended in a chitosan chloride solution
(0.3 mg/mL, pH 3.4) and incubated for 1 h at room temperature.
After incubation, the uncoated chitosan was removed by ultra-
centrifugation and washing with pure water, repeated twice.
Drug Loading
Encapsulation of Gen and THA into PLGA microparticles was
measured by dispersing accurate weighed amount (approximately
20 ± 0.2 mg) of microparticles in 1.5 mL of NaOH 1N with shaking at
room for 1 h to hydrolyze the polymer use. The experiments were
performed in triplicate. In the case of chitosan-coated micro-
devices, an additional step of soaking in 1.5 mL of acid chloride
solution (pH 3.4) for 1 h was added before the NaOH treatment.
Drug incorporation was expressed as actual drug loading (weight of
drug in microspheres/weight of microspheres, mg/g) and encap-
sulation efﬁciency (%) represented by actual drug encapsulated/
theoretical drug encapsulated  100.
Gen was assayed photometrically at 332 nm with a spectro-
photometer (model 1200 series; Agilent Technologies Inc.) after
derivation with o-phthalaldehyde reagent solution. o-Phthaldial-
dehyde reagent was prepared by adding 2.5 g of o-phthaldialde-
hyde, 62.5-mL methanol, and 3 mL of 2-mercaptoethanol to
560-mL 0.04M sodium borate in deionized water solution. The
reagent was stored in a brown bottle in darkness and settled for at
least 24 h before use.41 Gen solution, o-phthaldialdehyde, and iso-
propanol (to avoid precipitation of the products formed) were
mixed in similar proportions and stored for 30 min at room tem-
perature to promote reaction of Gen amino groups into chromo-
phoric products.42
THA was assayed by HPLC-UVvis method with an Agilent LC
system (1100 and 1200 series) equipped with an injection valve
(quaternary), 210 UV detector and ChemStation software was used
for HPLC method. A reversed-phase C18 column (2.1 mm ID_100
mm L, porosity 300Å, particle size 3 mm) with guard column
(reversed-phase C18 column of 2.1 mmID_12.5 mm L, porosity
300Å particle size 5 mm) was used for separation. Good separation
results were obtained using a Mobile phase “A” consisting of 0.1%
(v/v) TFA in MilliQ water and mobile phase “B” consisting of 0.1% (v/
v) TFA in acetonitrile with a ﬂow rate of 0.3 mL/min and column
temperature of 60C. The gradient program for mobile phase was
optimized using a timed gradient program T (min)/mobile phase A
(%): 0/80, 6/80, 26.1/45, 28/0, 31/0, 31.5/80, and 40/80.43 In these
conditions, THA retention time was of 21.9 min.
In Vitro Release Study
In vitro release data were obtained by suspending 20 mg of
microcapsules in 3 mL of PBS solution (10), placed in an incubator
at 37C, and stirred continuously. Every 24 h, the samples were
centrifuged at 2 g for 15 min, and the supernatant was completely
removed and replaced with fresh media to maintain sink condi-
tions. Drug concentrations from collected samples were then
assayed as described previously. Release experiments were per-
formed in triplicate; error bars are also reported to better describe
the data. The release proﬁles were monitored for 4 weeks; after this

Diffusion Coefﬁcient Calculation
The mathematical approach used to describe drug release rates
assumes that the release is governed by diffusion and, therefore,
the mass transfer process can be quantiﬁed using Fick's second
law45 (see Eqs. 1 and 2 in Annex I). It is known, from the literature
that PLGA degradation follows pseudo ﬁrst-order kinetics, it is
inﬂuenced by (1) polymer molecular weight (2), microsphere
size44,46 and not affected by (3) encapsulated molecule.47 The
degradation mechanism in an aqueous environment can be
described through cleavage of its backbone ester linkages and the
reaction can be kinetically quantiﬁed as a heterogeneous hydrolytic
degradation.48,49 Therefore, we suppose water imbibes into the
microparticles, after exposure to the release medium, leading to an
autocatalytic reaction (see Eq. 3 in Annex I) that produces a
reduction of the polymer macromolecular weight (Mw).50 A kdeg
value of 0.46 weeks1 at pH 7.4 and 37C was calculated and used.47
The drug diffusivity (D) increases as the molecular weight (Mw) of
the polymer decreases, as a consequence of the polymer degrada-
tion (see Eq. 4 in Annex I). The constant (k) that characterizes the
dependence
of
the
diffusivity
on
the
molecular
weight
of
the polymer has a value of 5.9 1011 cm2/kDa/s, as reported in the
literature.51 The ﬁtting procedure for D calculation was based on
the application of least squares method.
Results and Discussion
SEE Processing of Emulsions
In the SEE process, the scope of the packed column is to provide
a very large surface for contact between the falling liquid phase and
the going up gas phase (liquid emulsion and supercritical extracting
agent, respectively); as a consequence, SC-CO2 rapidly diffuses into
the oily phase of emulsion, causing the formation of an “expanded”
oily phase droplet in where the solvent is rapidly extracted. A fast
polymer hardening is obtained with the formation of solid micro-
spheres (see SEE process illustration in Fig. 1). Water is not miscible
with the carbon dioxide at the operative conditions selected, and
the microspheres can be continuously recovered at the bottom of
the column after only 4 min of residence time into the apparatus.
For all the emulsion processed, a recovery of 98% of the loaded
biopolymer was always obtained. Different emulsion formulations
were tested for multifunctional microcapsules production. All the
results obtained in terms of microdevices composition, size and
distribution, encapsulation efﬁciency, or drug loading are listed in
Table 1.
HA Embedding in PLGA Microdevices: Loading and Size and
Morphology Optimization
Different droplet shrinkages between 18 and 3% were observed
and were correlated to the absence and presence of suspended HA
(see nanoparticles SEM image reported in Fig. 2a) into the oily
phase. Indeed, ﬁxing the amount of PLGA in the oily phase, the
presence of HA dispersed within reduced the droplet shrinkage to
the 3%, whereas the droplet shrinkage was of 18%, when only the
polymer was loaded. This observation suggests an effective nano-
particles entrapment into the biopolymer matrix. An optical mi-
croscope image of the o-w emulsion is reported in Figure 2b,
whereas SEM image of the spherical microcapsules produced by
SEE is illustrated in Figure 2d when 25 mg/gPLGA of HA were loaded.
An good dispersion of HA embedded into the biopolymer matrix
was obtained in this case, as conﬁrmed by the EDX analysis (see
Fig. 2c) that showed the presence of X-ray emitted by phosphor
atoms.
PSD of PLGA/HA microparticles fabricated using different HA
nominal loadings of 25 mg/gPLGA and 50 mg/gPLGA, respectively,
were also illustrated in Figure 3a; the microspheres showed an
averaged mean size between 1.5 (±0.4) mm and 1.7 (±1.0) mm,
respectively, but the narrowest distribution was obtained when the
amount of 25 mg/gPLGA was embedded within. TGA measurements
were used to measure the real HA loading into the microcapsules;
the resulted traces are reported in Figure 3b. TGA curves indicated
an encapsulation efﬁciency of 99%, when 25 mg/gPLGA were loaded,
whereas only 74% of the loaded HA was encapsulated when the
nominal loading of 50 mg/gPLGA was used. In addition, when HA
amounts higher than 25 mg/gPLGA were added in the oily phases,
often irregular and not spherical microspheres shapes were pro-
duced after SEE processing of the emulsion; thus, to ensure a well
spherical shape of the fabricated devices and the best encapsula-
tion efﬁciency, the HA loading of 25 mg/g was used in all the
microcapsule formulation described as follows.
Chitosan Covering of PLGA Microdevices
In addition to particle morphology and size, the surface charge is
considered one of the most important parameters governing
microcapsule versus tissue interactions. Zeta potential is a useful
indicator of surface charge, and it can be used as an index of the
stability of the suspension. The resulting zeta potentials for
chitosan-coated microparticles were compared to those of the
uncoated ones at pH 5.5 and an inversion from 6 to 22 mV was
measured (see Table 1). In accordance with Kim et al.,38 the
observed zeta potential inversion toward positive values indicates a
successful coating, on account of an immobilization of cationic
chitosan on the microsphere surfaces, which suppressed the orig-
inal negative charge of the PLGA. The covered microcapsules
showed a spherical shape (see Fig. 4a), and the increase in mean
size was used to estimate a chitosan layer of 0.4 mm deposited onto
the PLGA microsphere surface (see Fig. 4b).
Gentamicin and Teriparatide Loading
Gen loading was obtained by processing several w-o-w emul-
sions by SEE. Its encapsulation efﬁciency varied with the amount
loaded in the internal water phase of the emulsion; in detail, ﬁxing
the amount of PLGA in the oily phase, and varying the nominal drug

loading from 10 to 25 mg/gPLGA, the lowest value of encapsulation
efﬁciency (35%) was obtained when the highest drug/polymer ratio
was used (25 mg/gPLGA). When 10 mg/gPLGA was loaded, a drug

efﬁciency, and microsphere coated with chitosan showed the best
encapsulation performance (see data reported in Table 1). The
higher Gen entrapment obtained when nominal loading of 10
mg/gPLGA was used, can be explained by considering the fact that
the ultrasonication of inner water and oily phases induces very
small and homogeneous drop size of inner water phase, but
probably reduced the overall amount of drug that can be loaded
within (i.e., more drug loaded more loss). In contrast, the presence
of HA in the oily phase improved the entrapment efﬁciency because
it should increase the viscosity of the oily phase, reducing the
possibility of internal water phase migration to the external water
phase. In addition, the very short residence time of the emulsion
into the high pressure column prevented additional water internal
phase diffusion into the external aqueous medium during the oily
phase extraction. It is worth of note that the entrapment efﬁciency
described in this work are very difﬁcult to be obtained when using a
high water-soluble drug, such as gentamicin; indeed, encapsulation
efﬁciency between 45% and 13% were commonly described when
spray drying or emulsion plus solvent evaporation methods were
used, respectively.42 The ultrasonication method in emulsion
formulation may improve the encapsulation efﬁciency of genta-
micin up to 95% but only when methylene chloride as oily phase
solvent is used.40 Because our process is proposed as green tech-
nology, we prefer to do not use chlorinated solvents in all micro-
sphere fabrication steps, and only EA is proposed in the present
work.

Release Study
The cumulative amount of Gen released (%) versus time (days)
from uncoated and chitosan-coated PLGA microspheres is illus-
trated in Figure 5. Experimental points over the complete time
assay are presented in the scatter plot with the standard deviation
(3 replicates were performed); the dash-dot line represents the
curve ﬁtting. Almost 30% of loaded Gen was released in the ﬁrst 2
days. This “burst” effect (useful in the case of an antibiotic thera-
peutic effect) is followed by a sustained release of 4%/day for the
following 10 days, with complete release obtained in 20 days. The
chitosan coating reduced to one half the drug “burst” release and
delayed the Gen overall release. Indeed, only 10% of the loaded
molecule was released in the ﬁrst 3 days, followed by a slower Gen
release of 2%/day for the other 25 days. The amount of Gen released
from the coated system was almost one half per day if compared to
the uncoated one and, therefore, the total release time was
doubled; indeed, after 20 days, the overall amount released was
only 50% of the total drug loaded.
Gen release proﬁles from PLGA microcapsules loaded with HA
(25 mg/g) are reported in Figure 6, for coated and uncoated
microcapsules. The presence of HA further delayed the Gen release;
indeed, when HA was embedded into the PLGA microcapsules, only
18% of loaded Gen was released in the ﬁrst 2 days, followed by a
sustained release of 2%/day for the subsequent 20 days, with 70% of
the total release obtained in 30 days. This “HA effect” can be
explained by an antagonist effect between the basic inorganic
component of HA and the acid degradation products of PLGA52;
indeed, as also observed by Ho et al.,53 the presence of OH can
delay the biopolymer degradation promoted by an acid environ-
ment, resulting in a delayed drug release. Chitosan coating addi-

calculated diffusion coefﬁcients (D) and R2 values for the different

smaller compared to one of the uncoated devices (2.9  108

drug diffusion by generation an additional barrier. When HA was
added into the microcapsules, the measured diffusion coefﬁcient
for coated microcapsules was of 1.1  109 cm2s1: this value was a
smaller, but of the same order of magnitude if compared to one of
the coated devices, but without HA embedded within. These data
indicated that chitosan coating may have a strong inﬂuence in
controlling the drug diffusion. On the other hand, a higher D value
of 7.1  109 cm2s1 was measured for the uncoated PLGA/HA/Gen
microcapsules, as expected.
In the case of THA release, the measured diffusion coefﬁcients
were consistent with the observed release proﬁles. The D value for
the coated microcapsules was reduced to 7.4  1010 cm2s1,
whereas for the uncoated ones, the calculated value was 8.1  109
cm2s1; that is, 1 order of magnitude higher, and of the same order
of magnitude of the one measured for Gen entrapped into PLGA/HA
microcapsules. THA has a higher molecular weight compared to
Gen; however, when Gen was coentrapped with THA in the same
PLGA/HA microcapsules, the release proﬁles of THA were acceler-
ated, and the measured diffusion coefﬁcient became very close to
the ones measured for the coencapsulated drug. Probably, higher
microcapsule porosity produced by the additional load and release
of the high water-soluble Gen55 facilitates THA diffusion through
the capsules and accelerates its release.
The initial “burst” (when present) is well described by the
mathematical model, whereas the subsequent constant drug
release is less well described; indeed, the burst effect should be
controlled purely by diffusion, and, generally, shows a very high
drug release rate at the beginning, given the small diffusion path-
ways at the early time points. Later, other phenomena additionally
contribute to the release mechanism, and probably the modeling
approach used is not suitable to describe the drug release rates over
the entire period, especially in the later phases. The underestima-
tion of the drug release at the late time points might result from the
beginning of the breakdown of the polymeric structure, which is
partly neglected in the present model. However, the time depen-
dence of the drug diffusivity in the proposed approach gave a good
description of the experimental data because on polymer degra-
dation, diffusivity increases; therefore, the higher D values were
estimated compared to the ones calculated by Equation 2 (see
Methods section). Moreover, as the existence of “nanopores”
cannot be excluded, D represents an apparent diffusivity, taking
into account drug transfer through the polymer itself, as well as,
possible drug transfer through water-ﬁlled “nanopores.”
Conclusions and Perspectives
SEE conﬁrmed to be a green, ﬂexible, and robust technology to
fabricate multiloaded microcapsules, coupling the efﬁciency of
continuous operation to easy process scalability. Both Gen and THA
molecules were not chemically modiﬁed or degraded by the
encapsulation technology and should maintain their biological
activity. The emulsion formulations explored, coupled with the
supercritical technology, can be used to fabricate versatile micro-
devices, able to control the drug release over a wide range of time
and concentrations, depending on speciﬁc applications. Moreover,
the “burst” effect was largely reduced when chitosan coating was
added. Good encapsulation efﬁciency and high reproducibility
mean sizes will also ensure efﬁcient and reliable drug release
proﬁles, as well as, the possibility of delay or accelerate the drugs
delivery in dependence of the required target.
A perspective of this work will be the sustained delivery of THA
or THA/Gen for 15-20 days directly at the site where its biological
action is required, instead of a systemic and daily dose injection.
Alternatively, because PLGA microspheres were recently proposed
as a spacer into the apatite calcium phosphate cement to induce
macroporosity56,57 or provide acid degradation products that
seemed to accelerate calcium phosphate cement degradation,58 the
described microcapsules may be also used in orthopedical surgery
formulations as multipurpose microﬁllers not only able to generate
macropores but, additionally, to deliver THA or Gen/THA in situ
enhancing bone ingrowth, especially in aged patients. Future
studies will involve the in vivo testing of the proposed formulations
in a rat model of fracture healing.
