A B S T R A C T
Chitosan-based nanoparticles (chitosan nanoparticles (ChNps), chitosan gold Nps (ChAuNps), and chitosan gold
Nps functionalized with poly lactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA) (ChAuNps/PLGA)) were prepared as nanocarriers for
insulin to improve its oral uptake. The emulsion solvent diffusion method was employed to functionalize the Nps
with PLGA. TEM, SEM, DLS, and zeta potential were conducted to characterize the Nps. The morphological
analysis confirmed the formation of spherical Nps with hydrodynamic particle sizes of 138±23, 16±2.2, and 50
±9.3 nm for ChNps, ChAuNps, and ChAuNps/PLGA, respectively. Zeta potential measurements indicated two
types of Nps, regardless of insulin entrapment, positively charged, (ChNps (+36 ± 4.2, +31 ± 2.2mv)) and
ChAuNps (+37 ± 4.3, +33 ± 2.5mv) and negatively charged (ChAuNps/PLGA (−31 ± 2.7, −26 ± 2.1 mv)). The
in vitro studies were assessed by measuring the entrapment efficiencies (EE%) and the release profiles of insulin at
different pH values. EE% for ChNps, ChAuNps, and ChAuNps/PLGA were 97 ± 1.5, 98.4 ± 1.9, and 99 ± 1.2%,
respectively. At an acidic medium, a significant level of insulin retention was observed (96 ± 0.08%) for
ChAuNps/PLGA. While a high amount was released at higher pH values over an extended period of time. In vivo
studies, diabetic rats treated with insulin-loaded Nps had reduced blood glucose level (BGL) (38 ± 2.8, 35 ± 6.5,
and 27 ± 5.6%) for ChNps ChAuNps and ChAuNps/PLGA, respectively. The pharmacological availability (PA%)
and bioavailability (FR%) for insulin-loaded ChAuNps/PLGA were 15.8 ± 0.71% and 7.7 ± 0.93%, respectively.
Altogether, emphasize the role of biocompatible Nps and their efficiency in the convenient delivery of insulin,
thus lowering the BGL in a safe condition.
1. Introduction
Diabetes mellitus (DM) has spread over the globe, affecting millions
of people, and is anticipated to become the leading cause of death by
2030 [1]. With its various kinds, DM is induced by an inability to
manage blood glucose levels, resulting in high blood glucose levels [2].
DM complications can vary from one to another, and it is readily
detected when a patient exhibits characteristic hyperglycemia symp­
toms and has a random blood glucose level of 200 mg/dl or above [3].
Controlling blood glucose levels in diabetic patients with insulin
insufficiency necessitates exogenous insulin. Nowadays, the most com­
mon treatment for DM is regular insulin injections into the skin [4].
Numerous and frequent insulin injections, on the other hand, can cause a
variety of problems, including local infection, fat deposits at injection
sites, stress, and insulin resistance, leading to inpatient discomfort, poor
compliance, and ineffective treatment [5]. Insulin (5800 Da peptide
hormone) consists of 51 amino acids arranged in two peptide chains (A
and B chains) linked by disulfide bonds. It regulates carbohydrate, fat,
and protein metabolism by boosting cellular uptake of peripheral
glucose and synthesizing hepatic glycogen, fatty acids, and proteins [6].
Oral administration is the preferred route for drug delivery. How­
ever, the efficacy of the drug delivery continues to be challenging for
many reasons, including the drug’s physicochemical properties. To
overcome such problems, nanoscale delivery methods, including natural
and synthetic polymeric nanoparticles, liposomes, solid lipid nano­
particles, nanoemulsions, and inorganic nanoparticles, have been
developed for insulin administration [7]. Natural polymeric nano­
particles, such as chitosan and poly(Lactic acid-co-Glycolic acid)

(PLGA), were found to have superior biocompatibility and biodegrad­

When designing efficient insulin-loaded nanoparticle systems,
physiological barriers in the GI tract, including chemical, enzymatic,
and physical barriers, should be considered [9]. These systems should
have high stability when passing through a wide range of gastrointes­
tinal tract pH, including a sudden drop in pH from 6.8 (mouth) to 1.2
(stomach), accompanied by a change from a highly acidic environment
to a slightly basic environment (pH 6.5 to 8.0) in the small intestine,
which could induce deprotonation or protonation of proteins and
polysaccharides-based nanoparticles [10].
Surface characteristics play an active role in nanoparticle absorption
via the transcellular pathway. Positively charged nanoparticles,
including chitosan and hydrophobic nanoparticles, like PLGA, are well
known for their high affinity to interact with the negatively charged
mucoid epithelial cell membranes [11].
Chitosan (Ch) is a biodegradable, non-toxic linear polysaccharide
with mucoadhesive physicochemical features and generated from its
main source chitin by a deacetylation reaction and used for different
trends [12–17]. It has been applied as a surface of the nanoparticles to
act as a coating layer and improve the chemical, enzymatic stability, and
mucoadhesion properties [18]. Chitosan reacts with insulin when
negatively charged, acting as a polyanion to form nanoparticles with a
size of 210 nm [19]. In addition, Ch was also reported as a reducing and
stabilizing agent for the gold ions, generating a ruby-red solution of
insulin-loaded gold nanoparticles for nasal and oral insulin delivery
[20].
Due to their numerous deep-rooted benefits, gold nanoparticles
(AuNps) have recently gained much attention. AuNps can be obtained in
various shapes and sizes by utilizing many different methods such as
chemical, physical, or environmentally friendly biological processes
[21]. For such preparation, gold ions (Au3+) are reduced into zerovalent
nanogold (Auo) by various reducing agents, and citrate ions are the most
commonly used [22]. Because AuNps are biodegradable and biocom­
patible, they were conjugated to many biomaterials and employed as
drug delivery systems for anticancer drugs, nucleic acids, and biological
proteins [23]. AuNps having surface plasmon absorption offer good
optical properties that can be used in imaging, labeling, and sensing
[24]. The characteristics of nanoparticles used as drug-delivery systems
can be improved when coated with polymeric materials [25]. PLGA is a
type of polymer useful in medicine and is approved by the Food and
Drug Administration (FDA) for drug administration because it is
biodegradable, biocompatible, and degrades in vivo to non-toxic prod­
ucts [26]. Elodie Czuba et al. designed PLGA nanoparticles loaded in­
sulin utilizing the double emulsion diffusion method and illustrated that
the negatively charged nanoparticles are not-toxic, extra effective for in
vitro and in vivo studies, and at the same time, demonstrate greater
performance with a diabetic rat in comparison to the positively charged
nanoparticles such as ChNps [27].
Insulin is generally delivered by injection, which is undesirable and
painful for patients. So, the current research aims to prepare and char­
acterize chitosan-based nanoparticles (ChNps) and investigate the con­
ditions for the sufficient entrapment of insulin for oral administration.
ChNps were designed and used as a reducing agent to obtain AuNps and
then functionalized with PLGA to exhibit different surface charges to
improve their size, stability, and loading efficiency. The in vitro drug
release was evaluated, and the efficiency of the as-prepared nano­
materials for the in vivo ability to reduce blood glucose levels with a
sustained release in diabetic rats was investigated.
2. Experimental and techniques
2.1. Materials and animals
Chitosan (degree of deacetylation ≥75%, Mwt:190,000 Da). Poly
(Lactic acid-co-Glycolic acid) (PLGA), Gold(III) chloride hydrate,
Polyvinyl alcohol (PVA, Mwt: 31,000 Da), Sodium tripolyphosphate
anhydrous (TTP, Mwt: 367.86) were purchased from Sigma Aldrich Co.
(USA). Act rapid insulin (1 mL containing 100 IU human insulin) was
obtained from Novo Nordisk, Egypt. Streptozotocin was obtained from
mp-bio, USA. A blood glucose monitoring system (Glucometer, Check
Gold) was purchased from SD Biosensor, Germany. Glycosylated He­
moglobin (HbA1c) was purchased from Biomed diagnostic, Egypt.
Human insulin and Rat C-peptide ELISA kit were purchased from
Bioassay Technology Laboratory, China. ELISA performed on Fully
Automated Immunoassay System (stat Fax), Awareness Technology,
USA. Sixty adult male swiss albino rats weighing 120–150 g were ob­
tained from the animal hospital, Abo Rawash, in Egypt. According to
Good Laboratory Practice (GLP), all animals were housed for about 14,
under standard conditions (temperature of 23 ◦C ± 2, relative humidity,
and a 12/12 h light/dark cycle), before being experimented.
2.2. Preparation of insulin loaded chitosan nanoparticles (ChNps/insulin)
ChNps/insulin was prepared through the ionic gelation/poly­
electrolyte complexation method [28] but with some minor modifica­
tions: follows: 0.5 g of Ch was dissolved in distilled water (98 mL)
completely blended with 2 ml of glacial acetic acid. The final pH was
4.5. After complete dispersion and dissolution, 10 mL of the prepared
tripolyphosphate (10%) with (pH = 8.8) was added drop by drop to the
Ch solution while mechanical stirring was performed to ensure the
complete mixing and further dispersion. The pale yellow solution of
ChNps was developed at the end of the addition. The excess acetic acid
was allowed to evaporate during the rapid stirring, and the generated
nanoparticles were collected using an ultracentrifugation method. A
calculated volume of act rapid insulin was added dropwise to the
redispersed ChNps, followed by complete blending and homogeneity for
60 min before incubation at 2–8 ◦C for 24 h. Then, ultracentrifugation at
30,000 rpm for 30 min was performed for pellets precipitation. The
pellets were then removed from the supernatant solution and re-
dispersed in distilled water for future use and application.
2.3. Preparation of insulin loaded chitosan reduced gold nanoparticles
(ChAu Nps/insulin)
0.5 g of Ch was dissolved in 100 mL distilled water (80 mL) acidified
with glacial acetic acid (2 mL) while mechanically stirred at room
temperature. After complete dissolution, the temperature was raised to
50 ◦C, then 3 ml of HAuCl4 (1 mM) was added dropwise until the so­
lution turned ruby red, indicating the reduction of Au3+ by Ch molecules
into AuNPs [20]. After evaporating the excessive solvents, nanoparticles
were collected by ultracentrifugation. The known volume of insulin was
introduced dropwise under stirring for one hour, followed by incubating
the fabricated nanoparticles for 24 h at 2–8 ◦C. Ultracentrifugation was
carried out for 30 min at 30.000 rpm, and the red powder was then
removed from the supernatant solution and redissolved in distilled
water before being used.
2.4. Preparation of insulin loaded chitosan reduced gold nanoparticles
that functionalized with PLGA (ChAuNps/insulin/PLGA)
The preparation was carried out via the emulsion solvent diffusion
method as previously reported [28] with slight modification. The first
water phase was formed by adding 10 mL of insulin (predetermined
concentration) to 10 mL of ChAuNps colloidal solution under stirring
dropwise, then adding 1% PVA solution (10 mL; 1%). The second water
phase was obtained by dissolving 0.5 g of PVA in 50 mL of distilled at 80
◦C while stirring. The oily phase was produced by dissolving 100 mg of
PLGA in 10 mL ethyl acetate. Finally, the first water phase was added
dropwise to the solution of PLGA under sonication in an ice bath. Then,
for 60 min at ambient temperature, 50 mL of the second water phase
(PVA, 1%) was added to the previous mixture (first water and oil phase).

2.5. Physical and biological characterization of the prepared
nanoparticles
A drop of nanocomposite suspensions was placed onto carbon-coated
copper grids, wiped, cleaned, negatively stained with 2% (w/v) phos­
photungstic acid (2% w/v), air dried, and then studied using a Phillips
CM-12 transmission electron microscope running at 120 kV. Scanning
electron microscopy (SEM) was used to examine the morphologies of the
produced nanocomposites. Under vacuum, nanoparticle samples were
gold coated before being analyzed on a JEOL JSM-840 SEM (10 kV,
Japan). Dynamic light scattering was used to determine the average
hydrodynamic size of the produced nanocomposites using (DLS; Nano
ZS, Nanoseries, Malvern Instruments, UK). Regarding zeta potential
measurements, the evaluation for each sample was taken in triplicate,
each with a sample duration of 40 s, and the results were averaged.
Thus, the zeta potential of nanocomposites loaded with and without
insulin was evaluated using (Nanoseries, Malvern Instruments, UK).
2.6. Insulin encapsulation efficiency (EE%)
The percentage of insulin entrapment efficiency (EE) in the designed
nanoparticles was calculated using an indirect approach of detecting the
insulin concentration in the supernatant after ultracentrifugation at
30,000 rpm for 30 min at 271 nm with a UV–visible double beam
spectrophotometer (Jasco V-630, Japan).

2.7. In vitro release profile of insulin at different pHs
The release of insulin from the carrier was investigated in simulated
gastrointestinal media without enzymes. The dialysis method created
simulated gastric fluid (SGF; 80 mM HCL, pH = 1.2) and intestinal fluid
(SIF; duodenum; 50 mM KH2PO4, pH = 6.8 and ileum;10 mM KH2PO4,
pH = 7.4). Ultracentrifugation was used to separate the nanoparticle
pellets, then re-dispersed in the release medium. One milliliter of each
sample was placed in an activated dialysis system (Dialysis Membrane-
50, MWCO:14 kDa, HiMedia Laboratories Pvt. Ltd., India) [29]. The
device was submerged in 20 mL of the release medium and incubated at
37 ◦C. At predetermined intervals, one milliliter was removed from the
external media and replaced with equal quantities of the release me­
dium. A UV–visible spectrophotometer was used to evaluate the samples
collected at 271 nm.
2.8. In vivo study: pharmacodynamics and hypoglycemic effects
Diabetes was induced in Swiss albino rats weighting (120–150 g) by
a single intraperitoneal injection of streptozotocin (STZ, 60 mg/kg) so­
lution dissolved in cold 0.1 M citrate buffer (pH 4.5) as previously
described [30]. Rats were allowed to drink 5% glucose solution over­
night to overcome hypoglycemia caused by STZ administration. Dia­
betes was confirmed after 48 h of STZ administration by measuring
fasting blood glucose concentration from the tail vein using blood
glucose monitoring strips. Only rats with fasting blood glucose levels
above 250 mg/dl were considered diabetic. Healthy control rats
received an equivalent amount of buffer. To ensure that diabetic rats
depend on free human insulin injected subcutaneously or loaded on
nanoparticles induced orally, Rat C-peptide Concentrations (ng/ml)
were performed.
Rats were divided into ten groups (each with six rats) as follows: A
healthy control group, a diabetic group subcutaneously treated with
insulin (8 IU/Kg), a diabetic group treated orally by intragastric gavage
with (insulin solution, 60 IU/Kg), ChNps, ChNPs/insulin, ChAuNps,
ChAuNPs/insulin, ChAuNps/PLGA, and ChAuNPs/insulin/PLGA.
The biological activity of insulin loading nanoparticles was evalu­
ated by the pharmacological activity (PA), and bioavailability (FR)
associated with subcutaneous administration of insulin was calculated
using the trapezoidal method. The area above and below the curve of
blood glucose levels of serum insulin concentrations was coded as AAC
and AUC. AAC and AUC are represented by the change in initial blood
glucose level and insulin level after treatment with time. The percentage
of PA and FR were calculated according to the following equations:
PA% = AAC × dose sc

3. Result and discussion
3.1. Characterization of composites
Three Ch-based nanomaterials were prepared, characterized, and
loaded with insulin. ChNps were prepared using TPP, acting as a
crosslinking agent to form a network of nanoparticles of Ch, employing
the ionic-gelation method. The pH of TPP was adjusted to 8.8 to improve
the dissociation of its functional groups to ensure the formation of the
negatively charged TPP molecules, enabling the electrostatic attraction
to the positively charged chitosan (pH = 4.5), producing ChNps posi­
tively at neutral pH. To prepare the second nanocomposite (ChAuNps),
AuNps solution was prepared using Ch as both a reducing and stabilizing
agent, taking advantage of the urgent need for clean chemistry in the
preparation and the application of Ch as a medicinally used polymer. Ch
has a reducing power enough to convert gold ions into nanoparticles,
and the high content of hydroxyl groups promotes the stabilization of
the nanoparticles against agglomeration. The third material, ChAuNps/
PLGA, was prepared by the functionalization of ChAuNps with PLGA.
The three nanocomposites were encapsulated with insulin and then
examined for the in vitro release profile of insulin at different pH con­
ditions, and were also applied for in vivo study (Scheme 1).

very small size than that of ChNps, indicating that the formation of
AuNps helps split Ch chains, keeping them apart from each other, which,
in turn, leads to the formation of small-sized nanoparticles. ChAuNps/
PLGA (Fig. 3c) has an average size that is very close to 50 ± 9.3 nm,
confirming the insignificant impact of PLGA on the nano state of the
material [35]. These findings are in accordance with TEM data.

evaluate their stabilities in solution. Thus, Zeta potential was assessed to
evaluate the surface charge of the as-prepared materials. Fig. 4 shows
the surface charge of the Nps before and after loading with insulin in
terms of zeta potential. As established from previous studies, zeta
potential with a value above +30 or −30 provides good stability of

The zeta potential values for the prepared ChNps, ChAuNps, and
ChAuNps coated PLGA are +36 ± 4.2, +31 ± 2.2, +37 ± 4.3, and after
loading with insulin, the zeta values became +33 ± 2.5, −31 ± 2.7, and
−26 ± 2.1 mv, implying the well stability of the produced Nps [36]. The
high zeta value +36 indicates good stability of the as-synthesized
nanoparticles related to the positive charge of Ch [37]. This positive
charge supports transporting the nanoparticles easily to the cell mem­
brane. The attachment of Ch and AuNPs in the colloidal solution elec­
trokinetic potential was +31 ± 2.2 owing to wrapping or coating AuNPs
on the surface of cationic Ch.
On the contrary, the charge of ChAuNps, after being coated with
PLGA, was switched into negative either before or after insulin loading,
and this negative charge is due to PLGA hydrolysis in an aqueous me­
dium [8,38].
Insulin has an isoelectric point (PI) of 5.4; at which the net surface
charge is zero; at pH values lower than or greater than PI, insulin surface
would become either positively or negatively charged, enabling elec­
trostatic attraction of opposite charges. The encapsulation of insulin,
negatively charged at the investigated pH, onto ChNps and ChAuNps,
which are positively charged, resulted in partial charge neutralization.
Accordingly, the zeta potential values would decrease. In the case of
insulin-ChAuNps/PLGA, insulin was added to the positively charged
ChAuNps before being coated with PLGA.

high insulin entrapment efficiency, as low one could postpone the
introduction of polymeric delivery techniques. EE% values of ChNps,

3.3. In vitro release profile of insulin at different pHs
Based on the PI value of insulin (5.4), the net surface charge would
vary from positive to negative, depending on the pH of the medium.
Accordingly, insulin release profiles from the as-prepared Nps were
assessed using simulated gastric fluid (SGF) (pH = 1.2) and simulated
intestinal fluid (duodenum; pH = 6.8, and ileum; pH = 7.4) without
enzymes [41]. Fig. 6 displayed the release profile of insulin from the
solutions of free insulin and insulin encapsulated Nps (ChNps, ChAuNps,
and ChAuNps/PLGA, respectively) in SGF (pH = 1.2), demonstrating
that insulin is safe from degradation in an acidic medium after encap­
sulation into the as-prepared Nps, which is in agreement with other
studies [42]. Approximately 60 ± 0.22% (3.6 ± 0.22 IU/ml) of free
insulin solution was released after 4 h of incubation at 35 ◦C. However,
the release of insulin from ChNps (5.82 IU/ml), ChAuNps (5.9 IU/ml),
and ChAuNps functionalized by PLGA (5.94 IU/ml) was 20 ± 0.09%
(1.1 ± 0.09 IU/ml), 7 ± 0.08% (0.4 ± 0.08 IU/ml), and (4 ± 0.06)%
(0.23 ± 0.06 IU/ml), respectively. The protonation of Ch in an acidic
environment could be the reason for increasing the insulin release from
ChNps [43]. At lower pH, ChAuNps coated by PLGA can provide
significant insulin protection and enhance the controlled release
[39,44].
In SIF (pH = 6.8) and (pH = 7.4), free insulin solution was released in
the uncontrolled release manner (fast release). The highly protected
insulin in ChAuNps/PLGA displayed considerable insulin release in SIF,
as about 35 ± 0.09% (2.08 ± 0.09 IU/ml) and 48 ± 0.21% (2.88 ± 0.22
IU/ml) were released at pH = 6.8 and pH = 7.4, respectively. At an
acidic pH, about 96 ± 0.08% of the loaded insulin on ChAuNps/PLGA
wasn’t released, indicating a significant level of insulin retention. While
a large amount of insulin was observed at a higher pH over an extended
period of time. In an in vivo context, this pH triggers nanoparticles’
release form, making oral insulin administration much easier [45].

versus the time profile obtained serves as a baseline for subsequent in­
vestigations using nanoparticles-based insulin formulations. This rapid
decrease in blood glucose subcutaneously administered insulin has been
unable to reach this portal-systemic insulin concentration gradient,
reducing hepatic glucose regulation [46]. Nevertheless, insulin con­
centrations throughout meals may be high, leading to hypoglycemia.
Subcutaneous or intramuscular insulin injection can generate peripheral
hyperinsulinemia, leading to diabetic microvascular problems [47].
Even after 5 h, the oral delivery of free insulin solution could not
significantly decrease BGL. This is due to inadequate insulin absorption
and enzymatic breakdown by gastrointestinal enzymes. Although the
oral route of medication administration has become the most popular
and widely acknowledged, several nanoparticles are employed in insulin
loading to manage blood glucose levels because of their low cost and
convenience of administration. Fig. 7b shows how blood glucose levels
changed over time after different insulin nanoparticle formulations were
administered. Free insulin was given orally, and blank nanoparticle
formulations did not affect BGL. Within 3 h, Ch-loaded insulin nano­
particles reduce the BGL by 38 ± 2.8% in diabetic rats. In addition, The
BGL was decreased by 35 ± 6.5% when the diabetic rats were treated
with insulin-loaded ChAuNps. Meanwhile, insulin-loaded ChAuNps
functionalized with PLGA reduced the BGL by 27 ± 5.6%; after 7 h, BGL
came to be about 67 ± 2% of its baseline, ensuring the effect of sustained
release of insulin by nanoparticles.
3.4.3. Hypoglycemic effects by monitoring glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c)
Mucus-penetrating nanoparticles have interesting properties that
might help with oral insulin administration, particularly when paired
with biocompatible and mucoadhesive polymers like chitosan and
PLGA, which cause the nanoparticles to adhere to the intestinal mem­
brane [48]. This makes it easier for nanoparticles to engage with mucoid
membranes, prompting tight junctions to change completely and then
transfer the linked insulin along the paracellular route [49]. Rats orally
administered insulin loaded ChAuNps/PLGA showed a highly signifi­
cant change in HbA1c level (4.03 ± 0.50) (Fig. 8) when compared to
subcutaneous insulin administration (7.07 ± 0.34), insulin loaded
ChAuNps (4.4 ± 0.42), and insulin loaded ChNps (5.76 ± 0.4) after a
month of oral administration for all prepared Nps.

4. Conclusion
This study aimed to develop and characterize insulin-loaded Nps
systems using ChNps, ChAuNps, and ChAuNps/PLGA. AuNps were
prepared using a facile, cost-effective chemical reduction method using
Ch, which played the dual role of reducing and stabilizing AuNps.
Successful preparation was fully investigated in terms of particle shape,
surface morphology, DLS, and their surface charge (zeta potential). The
EE%, of insulin, in vitro release at different pH values, and the in vivo
studies using diabetic rats were also investigated. The findings illus­
trated that the as-prepared Ch had a positive surface charge with a size
around 130 nm. In addition, ChAuNps have a very small size (15 nm)
and are positively charged due to chitosan as a stabilizing agent.
After functionalization with PLGA, the particles kept their nano-sized
structure but with a negatively charged surface owing to the coating
effect of PLGA. Insulin was efficiently entrapped into all the prepared
Nps. Furthermore, the release of the incorporated insulin from the as-
prepared Nps directly correlates to a physical process and/or adsorp­
tion phenomena in addition to electrostatic interactions.
Regarding the in vivo studies, the results proved that the function­
alization of ChAuNps with PLGA exhibited significant effects in lowering
blood glucose levels within 6 h of oral administration. Also, after 4 h of
oral administration of insulin-loaded ChAuNps/PLGA, serum insulin
level gradually increased to 64 ± 4.8, compared to a sudden decrease to
17 ± 1.2, noticed after subcutaneous administration of free insulin so­
lution. Additionally, the results show that the value of HbA1c for the
treated diabetic rats with the functionalized ChAuNps/PLGA is very
close to that of the healthy rats. From these obtained promising data, it
can be concluded that the functionalization of ChAuNps with PLGA is
feasible and effective for the oral delivery of insulin to treat diabetic
disease.
