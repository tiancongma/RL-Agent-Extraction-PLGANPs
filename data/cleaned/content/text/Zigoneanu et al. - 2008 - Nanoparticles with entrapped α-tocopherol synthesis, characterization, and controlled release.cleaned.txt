## Abstract
An emulsion evaporation method was used to synthesize spherical poly(DL-lactide-co-glycolide)
(PLGA) nanoparticles with entrapped a-tocopherol. Two different surfactants were used:
sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) and poly(vinyl alcohol) (PVA). For SDS nanoparticles, the size
of the nanoparticles decreased significantly with the entrapment of a-tocopherol in the PLGA
matrix, while the size of PVA nanoparticles remained unchanged. The polydispersity index
after synthesis was under 0.100 for PVA nanoparticles and around 0.150 for SDS nanoparticles.
The zeta potential was negative for all PVA nanoparticles. The entrapment efficiency of
a-tocopherol in the polymeric matrix was approximately 89% and 95% for nanoparticles with
8% and 16% a-tocopherol theoretical loading, respectively. The residual PVA associated with
the nanoparticles after purification was approximately 6% (w/w relative to the nanoparticles).
The release profile showed an initial burst followed by a slower release of the a-tocopherol
entrapped inside the PLGA matrix. The release for nanoparticles with 8% a-tocopherol
theoretical loading (86% released in the first hour) was faster than the release for the
nanoparticles with 16% a-tocopherol theoretical loading (34% released in the first hour).

2.2.2. Synthesis of nanoparticles with PVA as a surfactant.
Synthesis of nanoparticles with entrapped a-tocopherol using
PVA as a surfactant was accomplished following the guidelines
presented in figure 1.
The organic phase was formed by
dissolving 50 mg of PLGA in 2 ml dichloromethane.
Two
a-tocopherol concentrations were studied, 8% and 16% w/w
(reported to PLGA); a-tocopherol at these concentrations was
dissolved in dichloromethane. An aqueous phase was formed
with 0.3% PVA in nanopure water. The organic phase was
added in droplets to the aqueous phase, and the emulsion was
formed under homogenization (Ultra Turrax T 18, IKA Works
Inc., Wilmington, NC, USA) for 1 min at 10 000 rpm. The
emulsion was sonicated in an ice bath at 4 *C with a probetype sonicator set at 750 W (VC505, Sonics & Materials
Inc., Newtown, CT, USA) for 2 min, in pulse mode at 38%
amplitude.
Dichloromethane was evaporated under vacuum
(40 mm Hg) and nitrogen flow (100 ml min-1) for 10 min
in a rotoevaporator (Buchi R-124, Buchi Analytical Inc.,
New Castle, DE, USA) (Mainardes and Evangelista 2005).
Unloaded nanoparticles were prepared following the same

and they were used as a control system. Following synthesis,
nanoparticles were purified by diafiltration to remove the
excess a-tocopherol and surfactant. A GE Healthcare MidJet
labscale membrane separations system with MidGee HOOP
Cartridge H22LA (Amersham Biosciences, Westborough, MA,
USA) was used for this purpose. The purification process was
achieved during 1 h at 23 psi inlet pressure and 18 psi outlet
pressure. After purification, nanoparticles were kept for 2 h
at -80 *C and freeze-dried at -41 *C under 110 mm Hg of
vacuum for 48 h in a Freezone 4.5 (Labconco Corp., Kansas
City, MO, USA). Trehalose was added to the nanoparticles
suspension before precooling at a ratio of 1:1 (w/w) relative to
the amount of nanoparticles. Finally, the lyophilized samples
were stored in desiccators placed in the refrigerator for further
use.

2.3.2.
Determination of entrapment efficiency.
The entrapment efficiency of a-tocopherol in the PLGA nanoparticles was measured by reverse-phase (RP)-HPLC. A sample of 2 mg lyophilized nanoparticles was dissolved in 2 ml
acetonitrile:water (95:5). One milliliter of each sample was
centrifuged with an Allegra 64R centrifuge (Beckman Coulter, Fullerton, CA, USA) at 30 000 rpm for 15 min in order to separate any solid residual. The entrapment efficiency
of a-tocopherol was determined by injecting 25 ml of previously prepared sample into the HPLC column.
The mobile phase used was acetonitrile:water:acetic acid in a ratio
of 95:5:0.01 at a flow rate of 1.5 ml min-1 and a total run
time of 20 min/sample. The column used was a Discovery
C18, 25 cm x 4.6 mm, 5 mm (Supelco, Sigma-Aldrich), and
the detector was a Waters 474 Scanning Fluorescence Detector (Waters Corporation, Milford, MA, USA). The excitation
wavelength was set at 290 nm and the emission wavelength at
320 nm. A standard curve was prepared under the same conditions. Chromatograms were recorded and processed using
Millennium 32 chromatography software. a-Tocopherol entrapment efficiency was expressed as the percentage of the atocopherol entrapped in the nanoparticles reported to the initial
amount of a-tocopherol used for nanoparticle preparation.

3.3. Zeta potential of nanoparticles

Zeta potential is another important parameter in nanoparticle
characterization. A highly negative or positive zeta potential
indicates high repulsive forces and implicitly low probability
for nanoparticles to agglomerate. Under these circumstances
long-term stability of the nanoparticles can be expected.
The zeta potential of the nanoparticles was negative for
unloaded nanoparticles and for nanoparticles with entrapped
a-tocopherol (table 3). This negative value was expected based
on the presence of PVA at the surface of the nanoparticles.
With the entrapment of a-tocopherol in the polymeric matrix,
the zeta potential became less negative.
It increased
significantly from -28.67 mV for unloaded nanoparticles to
-13.56 mV for nanoparticles with 8% a-tocopherol theoretical
loading.
By further increasing the a-tocopherol theoretical
loading to 16%, a not significant increase in zeta potential to
-12.66 mV was noticed.

The residual PVA associated with the nanoparticles has an
important significance for the stability of nanoparticles, by
preventing the nanoparticles from aggregating; the residual
PVA also affects the release profile of the drug from the
polymeric particles.
The residual PVA associated with the

nanoparticles) for unloaded nanoparticles. For nanoparticles
with 8% a-tocopherol theoretical loading the residual PVA
was 6.80%, and for 16% a-tocopherol theoretical loading the

The burst effect can be explained by the

By increasing the amount of a-tocopherol entrapped from

the polymeric matrix.
This may explain the differences in
the release profiles observed for the nanoparticles with 8
and 16% a-tocopherol theoretical loading; for a-tocopherol
released from nanoparticles with 16% a-tocopherol theoretical
loading, a longer time was needed to fully release the vitamin,
as compared to that required for the release of a-tocopherol
entrapped in nanoparticles with 8% theoretical loading.
The 0.1 M SDS/0.1 M NaCl was chosen as a release
medium because it met the sink condition criteria required for
the a-tocopherol release study. The selected release medium
is not a perfect model for in vivo applications, and hence the
release findings cannot be extrapolated to in vivo conditions.

3.6. Release profile of a-tocopherol from PLGA matrix

The release profile was studied for nanoparticles loaded with 8
and 16% a-tocopherol theoretical loading and prepared with
PVA as an emulsifier.
The release profile of a-tocopherol
from PLGA matrix in 0.1 M SDS/0.1 M NaCl at 37 *C was
biphasic, showing an initial burst followed by a slower release
of the drug entrapped inside the PLGA matrix. The release
profile showed an initial burst effect in the first hour for
both 8 and 16% a-tocopherol theoretical loading (figure 4).
The burst effect was higher for 8% a-tocopherol theoretical
loading, when approximately 86% was released in the first
hour followed by a constant release in the next hours.
In
24 h approximately 97% of a-tocopherol was released with
100% released in 48 h.
The burst effect for 16% atocopherol theoretical loading was lower compared with that
for 8% a-tocopherol theoretical loading.
The burst effect
noticed in the first hour was approximately 34% of the atocopherol determined at time zero.
After the burst effect,
the release profile for 16% a-tocopherol theoretical loading
showed a uniform release in time. After 7 h approximately
67% of a-tocopherol was released from nanoparticles with
approximately 82% and 90% a-tocopherol released after 24
and 48 h, respectively.
