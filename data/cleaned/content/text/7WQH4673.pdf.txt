In this work is reported a novel and promising approach for the preparation of C-glycosylﬂavonoid enriched frac-
tion of Cecropia glaziovii (EFF-Cg) loaded PLGA nanoparticles (NP) with antiherpes properties. The purpose of this
study was to evaluate and to compare the effect of two nonionic surfactants (poloxamer 188 (PLU) and polyvinyl
alcohol (PVA)), and also an emulsion stabilized by solid particles of cellulose nanocrystal (CNC) in place of
surfactants. The characterization of these nanoparticles was in terms of size, polydispersity index, zeta potential,
morphology, thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), loading capacity and percent yield. Since TGA analysis revealed
thermo stability especially for NP-PLU, this formulation was selected for the evaluation of drug release proﬁle,
cytotoxicity and antiherpes activity. The drug delivery proﬁle demonstrated a sustained release through the
polymer structure and a signiﬁcant reduction of the polymer molecular weight at 21-day period. The cytotoxicity
of these nanoparticles was determined on Vero cells, and the selected formulation did not exhibit cytotoxicity
even at the highest tested concentration. The results demonstrated a potential antiherpetic effect of the EFF-Cg
loaded NP at 48 h of testing. In summary, EFF-Cg loaded NP exhibited a promising system for the effective
drug delivery in the treatment of herpes infections.

1. Introduction
Cecropia glaziovii has several pharmacological properties described
in the literature such as anxiolytic [1], hypotensive [2,3], antiasthmatic
[4], antidepressant [5], antacid/antiulcer [6], hypoglycemic [7], anti-
inﬂammatory and antioxidant [8] effects. Beyond that, Silva et al.
(2010) demonstrated the antiherpes effects of the crude aqueous ex-
tract obtained from C. glaziovii leaves and their fractions, n-butanol
fraction and enriched ﬂavonoid fraction. The results showed that the
EFF-Cg presented the most promising antiviral activity against Herpes
Simplex Virus (HSV) types 1 and 2, when compared to the other tested
samples. The chemical major constituents of EFF-Cg include the C-
glycosylﬂavonoids isoorientin and isovitexin [9]. These compounds
may be the ones responsible for the pharmacological activities observed
from this fraction [10].
In general, extracts and fractions of the herbal products are complex
mixtures of different phytochemicals that can generate instability
during the period of use [11]. On the order hand, these compounds
could present limitations associated to bioavailability, permeability, sol-
ubility, oxidation and hydrolysis reactions that could affect the delivery
and absorption of the active compounds [11,12]. To overcome these
limitations, herbal extracts are successfully encapsulated using drug de-
livery systems technologies since the encapsulation could bring some
desirable effects as controlled delivery, separation of the incompatible
compounds and improve the ﬁnal product quality [13]. Some studies
have demonstrated the possibility to extend drug delivery systems to
herbal medicines. For instance, Mathew et al. (2012) developed a
PLGA coated-curcumin nanoparticles coupled with Tet-1 peptide [14]
able to destroy amyloid aggregates. Moreover, the system so exhibited
anti-oxidative properties that could be beneﬁcial in treatment of
Alzheimer's disease. Argenta et al. (2014) optimized a topical
nanoemulsion containing genistein, an isoﬂavone, for skin retention
[15]. The experimental design showed that the formulation increased
the retention of the genistein in epidermis and dermis, and presented
antiherpetic activity in vitro against herpes simplex virus 1 and 2. Fur-
thermore, an injectable nanoparticulate system was developed for en-
capsulation and sustained release of four compounds in Ginkgo biloba
extract. The nanoparticles showed adequate encapsulation efﬁciency,
and the sustained and synchronized release of the four compounds
demonstrated to be a promising carrier system of herbal medicines

[16]. However, the potential of plants as biological materials for the syn-

The most studying device to encapsulate and delivery bioactive
compounds are natural [18] or synthetic [19] polymeric nanoparticles.
Considering these characteristics, the poly (lactide-co-glycolide acid)
polymer (PLGA), a biodegradable polymer approved by Food and Drug
Administration (FDA) [20,21], was chosen to prepare polymeric nano-
particles. PLGA has been widely investigated for drug delivery applica-
tions due to a number of advantageous features [22,23], the most
important is that its degradation rate can be tailored to obtain con-
trolled delivery of drugs [24].
In order to improve the biopharmaceutical proﬁle of the EFF-Cg,
employed in this work, a nanoparticulate system was developed using
solvent emulsiﬁcation-evaporation method based on a w/o/w double
emulsion technique. A previous solubility test of EFF-Cg in water and
dichloromethane demonstrated that the fraction presents a hydrophilic
proﬁle [25]. The escape to the external aqueous phase of the hydrophilic
compounds during the nanoparticles preparation is the higher
challenge in the nanoparticulate system development [26]. The stabili-
zation of the particles was tuned by varying the surfactant type in the
formulation. In general, a surfactant is added to the formulation in
order to stabilize the emulsion formed during particle preparation
[27]. However, these surfactants can also inﬂuence the properties of
the particles formed [28,29]. Hence, the main aim of this study was to
evaluate and compare the physicochemical characteristics and
composition of the nanoparticles obtained with two different anionic
surfactants (PLU and PVA) and a Pickering emulsion using cellulose
nanocrystalline instead surfactant. Furthermore, the goal of this work
is also an evaluation of the efﬁciency of EFF-Cg loaded PLGA nanoparti-
cles as a potential antiherpes system.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Materials
Biodegradable polymer poly (lactic-co-glycolic acid) Resomer
PLGA 503 H (LA:GA 50:50, Mw 24,000–38,000; inherent viscosity:
0.32–0.44 dLg−1; end group: free carboxylic acid) was purchased
from Boehringer Ingelheim (Ingelheim am Rhein, Germany) and
stored in a freezer prior to use. Freeze-dried enriched ﬂavonoid
fraction of Cecropia glaziovii Snethl (EFF-Cg) was used as a water-
soluble drug. Sorbitan monooleate (SPAN 80, Merck, Darmstadt,
Germany) was used as a surfactant in the organic phase and
Poloxamer 188 (PLU) (Basf, São Paulo, Brazil) and Polyvinyl alcohol
(PVA) (31,000–50,000 g mol−1, 87–89% hydrolyzed) (Sigma Aldrich,
St. Louis, USA) and cellulose nanocrystal (CNC) (sample synthesized
and kindly provided by Dr. Elena Fortunati, University of Perugia,
Italy) were used in the external aqueous phase. Isoorientin
(3′,4′,5,7-tetrahydroxyﬂavone-6-glucoside, ≥98.0%) was purchased
from Sigma Aldrich and used as chemical marker for the quantiﬁca-
tion in HPLC. Other reagents acetonitrile and acetic acid were pur-
chased from Sigma Aldrich (St. Louis, USA) and were of analytical
grade. Ultrapure water was obtained from a Milli-Q apparatus
(Merck Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany).
The cell line used was ﬁbroblast of green monkey kidneys (VERO-
ATCC:CCL81) (Department of Clinical Virology, Göteborg University,
Sweden). Eagle's minimum essential medium (MEM), and penicillin G,
streptomycin and amphotericin B were purchased from Cultilab (São
Paulo, Brazil); fetal bovine serum (FBS) was obtained from Gibco
(Grand Island, New York). Carboxymethylcellulose (CMC), naphtol
blue-black, MTT and acyclovir were obtained from Sigma Aldrich (St.
Louis, USA). Dimethylsulfoxide (DMSO) was purchased from Merck
(Darmstadt, Germany). HSV-1 (KOS strain) was kindly provided by
Faculty of Pharmacy, University of Rennes I, France. Virus stock was pre-
pared, titrated and stored at −80 °C until use.
2.2. Quantiﬁcation of the EFF-Cg
The EFF-Cg was quantiﬁed in terms of content of chemical marker
isoorientin using chromatography conditions validated by Costa et al.
(2011) [9]. The chromatographic analysis of the EFF-Cg and nanoparti-
cles was performed on Waters Alliance 2690 Chromatograph Separa-
tions Module equipped with a multiple-UV-wavelength photo-diode
array detector (Model 996, Waters). PerkinElmer Brownlee Choice
C18 column (5 μm, 150 mm × 4.6 mm) was used. The gradient elution
consisted of acetonitrile (A) acetic acid 1.0% (B) with a ﬂow rate of
1 mL min−1 and was programmed as follows: 5–20% A (0–30 min)
and isocratic 20% A (30–37 min). Detection was at 340 nm.
2.3. Preparation of EFF-Cg loaded PLGA nanoparticles
PLGA nanoparticles were prepared using a modiﬁed solvent
emulsiﬁcation-evaporation method based on a w/o/w double emulsion
technique [29]. Brieﬂy, 40 mg of EFF-Cg was dissolved in 2 mL of ultra-
pure water (internal aqueous phase, w1) and added to 4 mL of dichloro-
methane containing 100 mg of PLGA and 100 mg of sorbitan
monooleate (organic phase, o). The water-in-oil (w1/o) emulsion was
homogenized for 15 min, using a tip sonicator (Vibracell 75043,
750 W, Bioblock Scientiﬁc). This primary emulsion was poured onto
40 mL of surfactant solution (5% (w/v) PLU or 2% (w/v) PVA or 2% (w/
v) CNC) (external aqueous phase, w2) and mixed using the same sonica-
tion conditions to obtain the double emulsion (w1/o/w2). For solvent
extraction, the double emulsion was evaporated under reduced pres-
sure at 40 °C. The obtained particles were collected by centrifugation
at 11000 rpm for 10 min and washed out two times with ultrapure
water and then lyophilized. The nanoparticles formulations were desig-
nated using abbreviations as NP-PLU (nanoparticles prepared with
poloxamer 188), NP-PVA (nanoparticles prepared with poly(vinyl alco-
hol)), and NP-CNC (nanoparticles prepared with cellulose nanocrystal).
2.4. Determination of nanoparticle size, polydispersity index and zeta
potential
Particle size and polydispersity index were determined by dynamic
light scattering and zeta potential by laser Doppler anemometry using
a Zetasizer Nano ZS (Malvern Instruments, Malvern). The measure-
ments were made at 25 °C after sample dilution in ultrapure water
and placed in appropriate cell for each evaluation. All measurements
were performed in triplicate.
2.5. Scanning electron microscope
The morphology of the nanoparticles was observed by scanning
electron microscope (SEM, XL30 Philips). Each sample was dispersed
with ultrapure water and a drop of the sample was deposited onto a
cover glass. The dried sample was coated with an ultrathin coating of
gold deposited on the sample by high-vacuum metallization.
2.6. Thermogravimetric analysis
The NP-PLU, NP-PVA and NP-CNC were performed using a quartz
rod microbalance (Seiko Exstar 6300) in the following conditions: sam-
ple weight, 6 mg; nitrogen ﬂow 250 mL min−1; temperature range
25–600 °C and heating rate 10 °C min−1.
2.7. Percent yield of the nanoparticles formulation
Nanoparticles formulations were centrifuged at the end of prepara-
tion and the supernatant were removed. Afterwards, the nanoparticles
were washed, and centrifuged twice to obtain a pellet. The pellets of
nanoparticles formulations were lyophilized and weighed and the per-
cent yield was calculated using the following equation:

Percent yield = (weight of the lyophilized nanoparticles / total

2.8. Estimation of loading capacity by HPLC method
Freeze-dried nanoparticles (20 mg) were dissolved in 4 mL of di-
chloromethane, then 4 mL of water was added and the suspension
was stirred overnight. The concentration of the EFF-Cg in the aqueous
phase was quantiﬁed by HPLC. The percent drug loading capacity for
each formulation was expressed as mg of EFF-Cg/100 mg nanoparticles.
2.9. In vitro drug release
In vitro release proﬁle of NP-PLU formulation was observed using
phosphate buffer solution (pH 7.2) and then incubated at 37 °C at
70 rpm. At pre-determined time intervals, an aliquot of 1 mL was with-
drawn for analysis. The medium was replaced with fresh dissolution
medium at each interval. The samples were then ﬁltered and analyzed
by HPLC method as described above. The results obtained in triplicate
were expressed as a percentage of the drug release. The morphology
of the nanoparticles was systematically analyzed by scanning electron
microscopy. Furthermore, the pH value of the surrounding medium
was measured using an Orion 420A pH Meter. The measurement was al-
ways done on a volume of solution equal to 10 mL.
2.10. Nanoparticles degradation study
To determine the degradation rates of the PLGA, NP-PLU was incu-
bated under the same conditions as the in vitro release. At different in-
tervals, the nanoparticles sample was centrifuged to remove buffer
solution and lyophilized. The lyophilized nanoparticles were dissolved
in tetrahydrofuran (4 mg mL−1, w/v). The molecular weight of the sam-
ples was determined by means of gel permeation chromatography
(GPC) using a refractomer index detector Waters 2414. For the determi-
nation of their molecular weight, the samples were referenced to poly-
styrene standards between 4000 and 400,000 g mol−1. Before
measurements, the samples were ﬁltered using a PTFE ﬁlter. The molec-
ular weight averages (Mw) calculated is related to different physical
properties and processing parameters of the polymer.
2.11. Cytotoxicity assay
Cytotoxicity of free EFF-Cg, EFF-Cg loaded PLGA nanoparticles and
blank PLGA nanoparticles, prepared using PLU, was evaluated by MTT
assay [30]. Vero cells (2.5 × 104 cells per well) were cultured in 96-
well plates in MEM supplemented with 5% FBS and maintained at
37 °C and 5% CO2. Before the cytotoxicity assay, EFF-Cg dissolved in
MEM was ﬁltered through a 0.22 μm sterile ﬁlter, and EFF-Cg loaded
PLGA nanoparticles and blank PLGA nanoparticles were sterilized
under UV light for 30 min. Conﬂuent cells were exposed to different
concentrations of these samples for 48 h. Cells exposed to MEM or
DMSO without samples were used as controls. After the exposure peri-
od, MEM was replaced by MTT solution (1 mg mL−1) and plates were
further incubated for 4 h. Formazan crystals were dissolved by addition
of DMSO and the optical densities were read at 540 nm (Spectra MD2,
Molecular Devices). For the determination of CC50 values, nonlinear re-
gression of concentration-response curves was used. The 50% cytotoxic
concentration (CC50) was deﬁned as the concentration that reduced cell
viability by 50% when compared to untreated controls.
2.12. Antiherpes activity
Antiherpes activity evaluation of free EFF-Cg, EFF-Cg loaded PLGA
nanoparticles and blank PLGA nanoparticles (using PLU as surfactant)
was performed by viral plaque number reduction assay [10]. Firstly,
Vero cell monolayers (2.5 × 105 cell per well) were infected with
HSV-1 suspension (100 PFU/well) for 1 h at 37 °C, except in cell controls.
The non-attached viral particles were removed and overlaid with MEM
containing 1.5% CMC in the presence or absence of different non-
cytotoxic concentrations of the samples, and incubated for 48 h. Cells
were ﬁxed and stained with naphthol blue-black, and viral plaques
were counted by using a stereomicroscope (Olympus). The concentra-
tion of each sample that reduced viral replication by 50% (IC50), when
compared to untreated controls, was estimated by nonlinear regression
of concentration-response curves. Acyclovir was used as a positive
control.
2.13. Statistical analysis
Statistical analysis was performed using SPSS software version 17.0
(SPSS, Chicago, IL, USA). Differences between the test sites were tested
for their statistical signiﬁcance using the ANOVA test for paired data
or Kruskal-Wallis test for not normally distributed non-paired data.
The signiﬁcance level was set at p ≤0.05. All measured and calculated
parameters are reported as arithmetic mean and standard deviation
(mean ± S.D.).
3. Results and discussion
3.1. Preparation of EFF-Cg loaded PLGA nanoparticles
EFF-Cg has been encapsulated in PLGA nanoparticles using double
emulsion method. A study of nanoparticles formation was performed
evaluating two nonionic surfactants as poloxamer 188, and poly(vinyl
alcohol) and nanocrystalline cellulose as particles for stabilize emulsion
droplets. In this case, nanocrystalline cellulose was used as solid nano-
particles in place of surfactants for preparation of the double emulsion
by Pickering emulsion [29]. Colloidal properties of the nanoparticles
were evaluated as particles size, polidispersity index, zeta potential,
loading capacity, yield formulation, morphology and thermal analysis.
In second time, the release proﬁle and polymer degradation study of
the chosen formulation had been performed. Moreover, cytotoxicity
assay and antiherpes activity were used to evaluate this formulation.
3.2. Characterizations of the nanoparticles
3.2.1. Nanoparticle size, PDI, zeta potential, loading capacity and scanning
electron microscope
For nanoparticles preparation, drug/polymer ratio, volumes of inter-
nal and external aqueous phase were kept constant. The values obtain of
the effective particles size for NP-PLU and NP-PVA were 276 ± 3 and
227 ± 5 nm, while it was 330 ± 1 nm for particles prepared with CNC
as surfactant. The particles obtained from PLU and PVA have an
unimodal distribution with PDI of 0.12 ± 0.03 and 0.07 ± 0.03, respec-
tively. The presence of CNC led to the formation of a bimodal population
distribution and a PDI of the 0.41 ± 0.02. Measurements of zeta poten-
tial values for the NP-PLU, NP-PVA and NP-CNC formulations were
−15.6 ± 0.3, −0.2 ± 0.0 and −26.6 ± 0.6 mV, respectively, conﬁrming
the negative surface charge attributed to the presence of carboxyl end
groups of the polymer [31–33].The yield of nanoparticles was estimated
by the percent weight of the lyophilized nanoparticles relative to the
total weight of PLGA used for the preparation. The formulations pre-
sented different percent yield results, 67 ± 2%, 26 ± 1% and 73 ± 4%
for NP-PLU, NP-PVA and NP-CNC, respectively. These results showed
that in each washing steps there was a loss of surfactant and smaller
nanoparticles that after centrifugation still remained suspended in the
supernatant. NP-PLU and NP-PVA presented similar loading capacity
around to 0.6 and 0.7%, while NP-CNC demonstrated a lower loading ca-
pacity, 0.1%. From a technical point of view, these values suggest a difﬁ-
culty of hydrophilic drugs loading into PLGA nanoparticles. This fact can
be explained because during particle preparation, the drug leaks from
the internal aqueous phase into external aqueous phase [34]. Moreover,

in the low loading capacity values [35]. Another limiting factor is the fact
that the herbal extracts contain a complex blend of phytochemicals,
which may lead to different chemical interactions and thus decrease

The SEM images for dried nanoparticles prepared using different
surfactants are shown in Fig. 1, nanoparticles formation was visually ob-
served in SEM images. After preparation of the NP, there was the total
formation of particles in the case of NP-PLU and NP-PVA formulations.
These nanoparticles show a spherical shape, without particles aggrega-
tion for NP-PLU and with slight aggregation for NP-PVA. In the case of
NP-CNC, the full formation of the nanoparticles was not observed, prob-
ably due to a high concentration of CNC in the external aqueous phase,
which inhibits the particle formation, as reported in the literature. Che-
valier et al. (2013) reported that the solid particles used in preparation
of the Pickering emulsion have a partly hydrophobic surface and can ag-
gregate in presence of the water. Thus, an excess particle in the aqueous
phase reduces the concentration of available particles to stabilize the
emulsion [37].
3.2.2. Thermal analysis
Thermal stability of the nanoparticles was determined by TGA
analysis. The corresponding TGA and DTG curves are plotted in Fig. 2.
It was observed that the thermal degradation proﬁle was inﬂuenced
by the different emulsion stabilizer on the formulation. NP-CNC showed
two regions of weight loss, 290 and 387 °C, which were different from a
single mass loss for the NP-PLU and NP-PVA at 345 °C and 285 °C,
respectively. A higher thermal stability was observed in the NP-PLU
compared to the other formulations.
The results obtained from the physicochemical study were taken
into consideration in order to determine the optimal formulation for
further studies. In general, NP-PLU demonstrated adequate particles
size, narrow particles distribution and total formation of the particles.
Also, TGA data shows that NP-PLU was more thermally stable compared
to NP-PVA and NP-CNC. Moreover, considering the proﬁle of emulsion
stabilizer, the fact that poloxamer 188 is approved by the FDA for
pharmaceutical and medical applications and has a good safety proﬁle
administration [38,39]. NP-PLU was the suitable system to study the
in vitro drug release, the cytotoxicity and antiherpes activity of the
next sections.
3.2.3. In vitro drug release and nanoparticles degradation study
The cumulative percentage release proﬁle of EFF-Cg in terms of
isoorientin (chemical marker) from NP-PLU was evaluated at pH 7.2
PBS buffer during 21 days at 37 °C (Fig. 3). EFF-Cg was continually re-
leased from nanoparticles for 21 days until the total content of drug
was released from the particles. The modeling of dissolution data was
determined using DDSolver program, a menu-driven add-in program
for Microsoft Excel written in Visual Basic for Applications. A wide vari-
ety of mathematical models have been developed to ﬁt the drug release
data, most of that are presented as nonlinear equations [40]. The most
appropriate kinetic model was chosen according to the highest adjusted
coefﬁcient of determination (R2
adjusted) and coefﬁcient of determination
(R2) provided by the program, 0.7561 and 0.7748, respectively.
In the initial release phase, a burst of 23% was observed in 30 min, a
characteristic associated with hydrophilic drugs. Usually drug particles
are encapsulated in form of small clusters on the surface or within the
polymer matrix just below the surface of the particles, resulting in a
high initial burst release [34]. Next, EFF-Cg release became slower due
to association of drug diffusion and polymer erosion as mechanisms of
drug release, as conﬁrmed by the release exponent (n) value of the
Korsmeyer–Peppas equation. The n was equal to 0.15, so, according
with this model if n ≤0.43, drug release occurs by diffusion process
through the polymer wall (Fickian diffusion) [41,42].
The nanoparticles degradation proﬁle was evaluated according mo-
lecular weight (Mw) values of the nanoparticles during release test. As
shown in Fig. 4, at all-time the Mw of EFF-Cg loaded nanoparticles de-
creased throughout the experiment. Within an incubation period of
21 days, a signiﬁcant Mw reduction of the nanoparticles occurred com-
pared with the original Mw (51%, p = 0.000). The rate of Mw reduction
may depend of the different factors including for example, physico-
chemical parameters (pH, temperature, and ionic strength of the envi-
ronment), and polymer composition (the presence of the ester groups
closer to the surface that may be more susceptible to degradation)
[43,44].
The ﬁndings of the GPC were used to elucidate the underlying degra-
dation mechanism of the polymer and explain the EFF-Cg release be-
havior. The degradation process monitored by GPC was plotted and
the molecular weight proﬁles as a function of incubation time are repre-
sented in Fig. 4. Nanoparticles presented a gradual decrease over the
14 days, and between the 7-day and 14-day did not show appreciable
change in weight-averaged molecular. After, the PLGA nanoparticles
showed rapid degradation between 14 and 21 days of incubation as es-
timated from the decrease in peak molecular weight from 20,305 to
14,998. Thus, the “relative burst release” observed between 14 and
21 days (Fig. 3) is believed to be related to degradation of the polymer.
Moreover, the FESEM image (Fig. 5) during the period (14–21 days)
shows the NP morphology completely change and the spherical shape
are lost.
SEM micrographs also revealed the morphology of nanoparticles in
the latter stages of the degradation test (7, 14 and 21-day). Images
showed that there was a total agglomeration of the nanoparticles and
a loss of initial spherical shape (Fig. 5). After 21-day period, it was ob-
served a variation of the pH of medium from 7.2 to 6.8. This may
occur due to the accumulation of the degradation products, such as car-
boxylic acid end groups and acids oligomers that induce a decrease in
pH values [45,46].

release (21 days), the antiviral assay performed for 48 h did not allowed
EFF-Cg total release, therefore, probably the effective antiviral drug con-
centration will be reached during more prolonged period of time.

4. Conclusion
One of the drawbacks of the bioactive EFF-Cg is the limitation asso-
ciated to low bioavailability, permeability and solubility as well as the
easy oxidation and hydrolysis reactions that could affect the delivery
and absorption of the active compounds. Considering these characteris-
tics, PLGA, approved by FDA, was chosen to prepare polymeric
nanoparticles.
EFF-Cg loaded in PLGA nanoparticles has been prepared using a
double emulsion technique and two different anionic surfactants (PLU
and PVA) and CNC. The NP prepared with the two surfactants present
similar characteristics concerning size, PDI and loading capacity while
TGA analysis revealed an improvement of thermo stability especially
for NP-PLU which made this formulation suitable for the evaluation of
drug release proﬁle, polymer degradation study, cytotoxicity and anti-
herpes activity.

free EFF-Cg and a potential activity for the EFF-Cg loaded PLGA

system seems to be promising for the effective drug delivery in the
treatment of herpes infections. In the future could be interesting the
development of nanocomposite systems for the delivery drug directly
on infection tissue.
