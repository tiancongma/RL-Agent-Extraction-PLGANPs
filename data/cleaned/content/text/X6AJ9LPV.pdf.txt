Abstract
Amphotericin B (AmB) is one of the most
used drugs for the treatment of systemic fungal
infections; however, the treatment causes several
toxic manifestations, including nephrotoxicity and
hemolytic anemia. Chitosan-coated poly(lactide-co-
glycolide) (PLGA) nanoparticles containing AmB
were developed with the aim to decrease AmB toxicity
and propose the oral route for AmB delivery. In this
work, the antifungal efﬁcacy of chitosan-coated
PLGA nanoparticles containing AmB was evaluated
in 20 strains of fungus isolates from patients with
vulvovaginal candidiasis (01 Candida glabrata and 03
Candida albicans), bloodstream infections (04 C.
albicans and 01 C. tropicalis) and patients with
urinary tract infection (04 Candida albicans, 02
Trichosporon asahii, 01 C. guilhermondii, 03 C.
glabrata) and 01 Candida albicans ATCC 90028.
Moreover, the cytotoxicity over erythrocytes was
evaluated. The single-emulsion solvent evaporation
method was suitable for obtaining chitosan-coated
PGLA nanoparticles containing AmB. Nanoparticles
were spherical in shape, presented mean particle size
about 460 nm, positive zeta potential and encapsula-
tion efﬁciency of 42%. Moreover, nanoparticles
prolonged the AmB release. All the strains were
susceptible to plain AmB and nanostructured AmB,
according to EUCAST breakpoint version 8.1 (resis-
tant [ 1 lg/mL), using broth microdilution method.
In C. albicans (urine, blood, and vulvovaginal secre-
tion isolates, and 1 ATCC), the MIC value of AmB-
loaded nanoparticles varied from 0.25 to 0.5 lg/mL
and EUCAST varied from 0.03 to 0.5 lg/mL. In urine
and vulvovaginal secretion isolates of C. glabrata, the
MIC value of AmB-loaded nanoparticles varied from
0.25 to 0.5 lg/mL and EUCAST varied from 0.03 to
0.015 lg/mL. In urine isolates of C. guilhermondii,
the MIC value of AmB-loaded nanoparticles was
0.12 lg/mL and EUCAST was 0.06 lg/mL. In blood
isolates of C. tropicalis, the MIC value of AmB-
loaded nanoparticles was 0.5 lg/mL and EUCAST
was 0.25 lg/mL. Finally, in urine isolates of T asahii,
the MIC value of AmB-loaded nanoparticles was
1 lg/mL and EUCAST varied from 0.5 to 1 lg/mL. In
the
cytotoxicity
assay,
plain
AmB
was
highly
hemolytic (100% in 24 h) while AmB-loaded chi-
tosan/PLGA
nanoparticles
presented
negligible
hemolysis.

Introduction
Fungal diseases are considered a serious health
problem worldwide, and consequently, effective treat-
ments and management strategies are needed to reduce
fungal infections [1, 2]. The risk of serious systemic
infections has increased, particularly in immunocom-
promised individuals, resulting from transplant, hema-
tologic
malignancies,
oncologic
therapy,
HIV
infection, hemodialysis, or due to the use of intra-
venous catheters or immunosuppressive drugs. One of
the most important groups of fungi that cause invasive
infections is the Candida genus [3]. However, not only
Candida albicans species [4], but other non-albicans
Candida species, such as C. parapsilosis, C. glabrata,
C. krusei, and C. tropicalis, have changed the
epidemiology of systemic fungal infections [5]. Also,
Cryptococcus, Trichosporon, and Blastoschizomyces
are important species causing serious invasive fungal
infections [6]. In recent years, the rate of fungal
infections has increased [7] and leads to resistance to
antifungal agents [8].
The antifungal drugs are limited to few classes, and
susceptibility, toxicity, and resistance are important
issues to be highlighted in the choice of the treatment.
Amphotericin B (AmB) is a macrolide which belongs
to polyenes class, and it is produced by Streptomyces
nodosus [9, 10]. AmB is one of the most chosen
antifungals to treat invasive fungal infections since it
presents a broad spectrum of action and low incidence
of resistance [11]. AmB has amphipathic characteris-
tics possessing both hydrophobic and hydrophilic
domains in the lactonic ring [12]. AmB interacts with
ergosterol in fungus membrane, producing channel-
like structures, altering the membrane permeability,
resulting in the depletion of ions and metabolites
[13, 14]. Also, cholesterol-forming membranes can be
affected by AmB cellular mechanism, leading to many
adverse effects, highlighting the nephrotoxicity and
hemolytic anemia [15].
Nanotechnology is an emerging alternative to solve
drug drawbacks, such as high toxicity, low bioavail-
ability, and efﬁcacy [16]. Several nanostructured
devices for drug delivery have been developed and
bring new pathways for more speciﬁc and less toxic
treatments. Polymeric nanoparticles present unique
and valuable physicochemical and biological proper-
ties [14]. The small size associated with a high surface
area, superﬁcial charge, and ability to anchor moieties
to nanoparticles surface make these systems innova-
tive drug carriers, allowing improved drug transport
and favoring the entry in tissues and cells [17–19].
We
propose
the
development
of
polymeric
nanoparticles as AmB carriers with the aim to reduce
the AmB toxicity and be applied to fungal infections
by the oral route. PLGA was used as a polymer for
nanoparticles due to its biodegradability and biocom-
patibility, and chitosan was used as a coat on
nanoparticles surface, due to its mucoadhesive prop-
erties. This study was designed to evaluate the in vitro
antifungal activity of chitosan-coated PLGA nanopar-
ticles containing AmB against 20 strains of fungus.
The hemolytic potential of AmB-loaded nanoparticles
was evaluated as well.
Materials and Methods
Nanoparticles Materials
Amphotericin B (AmB), polyvinyl alcohol (PVA,
31 kDa, 88% hydrolyzed), poly(D,L-lactide-co-gly-
colide) (PLGA, 50:50, 30 - 60 kDa), and chitosan
(medium molecular weight, 75–85% deacetylated)
were acquired from Sigma-Aldrich (USA). Dimethyl
sulfoxide, ethyl acetate were acquired from Biotec
(Brazil), dichloromethane from Fmaia (Brazil), and
acetonitrile and acetic acid HPLC grade from J.
T. Baker (USA). Ultra-puriﬁed water was obtained in
Milli-Q Plus system (Millipore). All other chemicals
were of analytical grade and of the highest commer-
cially available purity.
Strains
A total of 19 clinical isolates and one Candida
albicans ATCC 90028 were tested. The isolates were
collected from non-HIV infected patients with vulvo-
vaginal candidiasis (01 Candida glabrata and 03
Candida albicans), bloodstream infections (04 C.
albicans and 01 C. tropicalis) and patients with
urinary tract infection (04 Candida albicans, 02
Trichosporon asahii, 01 C. guilhermondii, 03 C.
glabrata). All strains were stored in brain heart
infusion broth (bioMe´rieux) with 20% glycerol at –
70 C. To ensure optimal growth conditions and
purity, all the strains were subcultured in Sabouraud
dextrose agar (Difco, Detroit, USA) before each test.

Obtaining Chitosan-Coated PLGA Nanoparticles
Containing AmB
PLGA nanoparticles containing AmB coated with
chitosan were prepared by the emulsion solvent
evaporation technique, as previously described by
our group [22], with some modiﬁcations. Brieﬂy,
PLGA (50 mg) was dissolved in 1 mL dichloro-
methane and AmB was dissolved in ethyl acetate
(5 mg/mL). These two phases were joined and poured
into a PVA aqueous phase (1%, w/v) containing
chitosan (0.2% in 1% acetic acid) and sonicated
(6 min, 80 Hz) to produce an oil-in-water (O/W)
emulsion. The emulsion was subjected to evaporation
under vacuum at 37 C for nanoparticles solidiﬁcation
and was puriﬁed by ultracentrifugation (19,9759g,
30 min, 25 C). The resultant supernatants were
collected for further analysis of drug content.
The amount of AmB encapsulated in nanoparticles
was determined by HPLC (Waters 2695 Alliance).
The mobile phase consisted of acetonitrile and 0.5%
acetic acid (80:20, v/v), at a ﬂow rate of 0.9 mL/min.
PDA detector was set at 408 nm.
Mean particle size and polydispersity index were
determined by dynamic light scattering (DLS, BIC 90
plus, Brookhaven Instruments Corp.) The measure-
ments were performed at a scattering angle of 90 at
25 C.
For zeta potential measurements, samples were
placed in the electrophoretic cell (Nano ZS90,
Malvern
Instruments,
UK)
and
a
potential
of
± 150 mV was established.
Morphology was performed by scanning electron
microscopy equipped with a ﬁeld emission gun (FE-
SEM) using acceleration voltage of 5.0 kVA. Aliquots
of the nanoparticles suspensions were deposited on the
sample port, and the obtained ﬁlms were coated with a
thin layer of gold. Micrographs were obtained with
increases of 25 Kx.
The in vitro release of AmB from chitosan/PLGA
nanoparticles was conducted by suspending the
nanoparticles (containing 5 mg of AmB) in 1 mL of
phosphate buffer sodium (PBS, 0.01 M, pH 7.4) and
sodium dodecyl sulfate (5%). The tubes were kept in a
shaker at 37 C at 150 rpm. At predetermined time
intervals,
the
suspension
was
centrifuged
at
19,975 9 g for 15 min to separate the released AmB
from the nanoparticles. The resulting precipitate was
dispersed in 1 mL of the release medium and
incubated until the next sampling. The released
AmB present in the supernatant was injected into the
HPLC to determine the amount of AmB released at
different time intervals. The experiment was per-
formed in triplicate and under sink conditions.

Discussion
Patients in the intensive care unit are predisposed to
acquire systemic fungal infections since they can be
immunocompromised
or
present
some
medical
intervention [24, 25]. One of the most important
routes to Candida spp access the bloodstream is
through bioﬁlms formed in catheters used in patients
[26]. AmB is the most utilized drug for the treatment
of systemic fungal infections since it offers a broad
spectrum of activity against various types of fungi.
Due to its low aqueous solubility, AmB is commer-
cially available as a micellar system with sodium
deoxycholate. However, it is available only in a
parenteral formulation and its clinical efﬁcacy is
limited by the developed toxicity after weeks of
treatment. The nephrotoxicity is the main side effect,
occurring in 50–90% of patients, and hemolytic
anemia is also a very common effect. Liposomal
AmB is a signiﬁcant effective alternative to treat
systemic fungal infections, although the AmB-related
toxicity was not fully eliminated, the cost is elevated
and parenteral route is also used [27]. The develop-
ment of an inexpensive, safe, and effective oral dosage
form could be ideal for the treatment of fungal
diseases. Nanotechnology has been applied to develop
AmB delivery systems, and the results show the
increase in AmB therapeutic index, reduction in its
toxicity, and application of new administration route
[19, 28–32].
In this study, polymeric nanoparticles were pre-
pared using PLGA, a polymer approved for human use
by FDA, due to its high biocompatibility and
biodegradability, which can be used to encapsulate
hydrophilic or hydrophobic drugs [33, 34]. The
obtained PLGA nanoparticles were coated with chi-
tosan, a natural, biodegradable, and biocompatible
polysaccharide derived from total or partial deacety-
lation of chitin extracted from crustaceans. The
inclusion of chitosan helps the stability of nanoparti-
cles and increase their biocompatibility, besides to
infer mucoadhesive properties to them [35]. Chitosan-
coated PLGA nanoparticles containing AmB were
successfully obtained by the emulsiﬁcation–evapora-
tion solvent technique. An oil-in-water emulsion was
obtained, and AmB and PLGA due to their hydropho-
bic characteristics comprised the internal phase of
emulsion surrounded by the surfactant and chitosan
aqueous phase. In the step of solvent evaporation,
organic solvents are eliminated and PLGA precipitates
in emulsion globule containing AmB, thus solidifying
the nanoparticles. Chitosan due to its aqueous solu-
bility remains in the aqueous phase and interacts with
nanoparticles surface forming a coat. The mean size of

The nanoparticles size, size distribution, and particle
charge have direct implication in biological proper-
ties. The size and surface charge of the nanoparticles
are crucial factors for their transport mechanisms,
inﬂuencing on drug bioavailability after oral admin-
istration [36]. Chitosan, due to its positive charge, is
preferentially used as a polymer for nanoparticles or as
a coating agent in nanoparticles intended for oral
administration. An electrostatic interaction occurs
between positively charged nanoparticles and mucin
(a component of mucus that is highly negative),
helping the internalization of nanoparticles by epithe-
lial cells [37]. The AmB release proﬁle presented an
initial fast phase of release, which may be due to AmB
desorption from the nanoparticles surface and due to
the hydrophilic characteristic of CS, allowing the entry
of dissolution medium in nanoparticles. After that, a
very sustained phase was stablished, probably due to
diffusion of AmB from nanoparticles matrix. The drug
can be released from nanoparticles by either erosion or
diffusion through the polymer or by a combination of
these two mechanisms, and the polymer characteris-
tics including molecular weight and proportion of the
molecules that constitute the polymer inﬂuence on the
release rate [38].
Considering the need to propose the oral route for
AmB delivery and reduce its toxicity, in this work we
developed chitosan-coated PLGA nanoparticles con-
taining AmB and evaluated the in vitro antifungal
efﬁcacy over a wide range of strains and also the
cytotoxicity over RBC. The choice of several clinical
strains of C. albicans for the assay, more than other
Candida’s species or other non-Candida species, is
justiﬁed due to several studies demonstrated the most
cases of candidiasis occur by C. albicans; however,
other species of Candida have been reported [39, 40].
An important analysis to quantitatively evaluate the
efﬁcacy of antifungal drugs is the MIC assay, which
consists in a minimum concentration of antifungal
drugs used, with the capacity of inhibiting the visible
growth of microorganisms in sensitivity tests by agar
or broth dilution [23]. According to our results, it was
observed
that
AmB-loaded
chitosan/PLGA
nanoparticles
presented
higher
MIC
value
than
EUCAST standard, although 50% of isolates showed
different B 2 twofold of dilution when compared to
MICs of plain AmB, suggesting a good agreement
between the methodologies tested for these samples.
The higher differences of the MICs with four twofold
were observed only in 20% of isolates, particularly in
C. glabrata species. Infections by C. glabrata are
becoming increasingly common, and the decreased
antifungal susceptibility in therapy has become an
important problem [41]. It is important to emphasize
that all tests were performed in triplicate and no isolate
showed MIC more than four twofold of dilution when
compared with the MICs of plain AmB and all were
sensible according to EUCAST document. The higher
MIC value of AmB-loaded nanoparticles, in the most
of tested samples compared to EUCAST, can be
explained considering the prolonged AmB release
from nanoparticles matrix. While plain AmB is fully
available to exert its antifungal mechanism, AmB
from chitosan–PLGA nanoparticles needs to be pro-
gressively released in order to exert the antifungal
mechanism, resulting in lower in vitro antifungal
effect. The results of susceptibility test of T. asahii
were important considering its high morbidity and
difﬁculty in treating it. This fungus is known to present
variable susceptibility to AmB, and studies have
demonstrated T. asahii resistance to AmB formula-
tions [42]. We can hypothesize that in this strain the
efﬁcacy of AmB-loaded nanoparticles was higher due
to the larger contact area provided by nanoparticles,
thus promoting greater contact of AmB with ergos-
terol [43]. Some studies have reported that the
electrostatic attraction between the negatively charged
cell membrane of fungi and the positively charged
nanoparticles is crucial for the antifungal activity of
drug-loaded nanoparticles [44, 45].
It is widely reported the interaction of AmB with
erythrocytes, causing hemolysis and resulting in
hemolytic anemia as a adverse effect. The AmB
aggregation state plays an important role in AmB
toxicity. The available commercial AmB formulations
are composed of a mixture of monomeric and
oligomeric species, and the last are toxic to mam-
malian and fungi cells, while monomeric forms are
selectively more toxic to fungi cells [28, 46, 47]. To
change the aggregation state of AmB by altering the
carrier may alter signiﬁcantly the selectivity of AmB
against cells. Nanoparticles can release lower amounts

C. tropicalis. In a recent work, we showed PLGA
nanoparticles containing AmB presented the same
efﬁcacy of free AmB in C. neoformans (same MIC
value), while AmB-deoxycholate did not present MIC
in the tested concentration range. The AmB hemolytic
toxicity was inhibited by nanoparticles. Moreover,
these nanoparticles were orally administered in rats
during
7 days,
and
the
histologic
examination
revealed AmB-deoxycholate treatment presented ini-
tial liver damage, while AmB-loaded nanoparticles
did not present any hepatic cellular alteration [49].
Pharmaceutical nanotechnology has shown impor-
tant results in the improvement of drugs used in
clinical practice, and its application in the treatment of
great impact diseases or emerging diseases is an
important step. Considering that the current antifungal
arsenal is limited and the AmB is still the most used
antifungal drug for the treatment of severe fungal
infections, even presenting high toxicity, our results
are very encouraging. Thus, it is concluded that
nanotechnology maintains the antifungal activity of
AmB while reducing its cytotoxicity over RBC,
opening a new avenue for the treatment of fungal
infections.
Conclusion
Chitosan-coated PLGA nanoparticles were success-
fully obtained by the emulsion solvent evaporation
method. Nanoparticles presented nanometric size, low
polydispersity, positive surface charge, and ability to
encapsulate AmB. Nanoparticles containing AmB
presented a negligible index of hemolysis while plain
AmB was very hemolytic. The in vitro antifungal
efﬁcacy of AmB nanoparticles was variable consider-
ing the strain tested. The results demonstrated the
potential of PLGA/chitosan nanoparticles as AmB
carriers; however, more in vitro and in vivo assays
must be carried out to state its oral use with reduced
toxicity.
