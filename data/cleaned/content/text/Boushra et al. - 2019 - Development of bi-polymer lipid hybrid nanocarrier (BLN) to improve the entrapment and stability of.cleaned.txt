## Materials Methods
2.1. Chemicals and reagents

Insulin (Recombinant Human, activity > 27.5 IU/mg), tetra methyl ammonium hydroxide, PEG 2000, PEG 4000 and PEG 6000 were
purchased from Sigma Aldrich (St Louis, USA), soy lecithin from Chem
Service (Westchester, USA), trimyristin from TCI- EP (Tokyo, Japan),
PLGA 50:50 from Purac (Gorinchem, The Netherlands) 1,2-dipalmitoylsn-glycero-3-phosphoethanolamineN-(lissamine rhodamine B sulfonyl)
(ammonium salt) from Avanti polar lipids (Alabama, USA), thiazolyl
blue tetrazolium bromide from Amresco (USA). Other chemicals such as
dichloromethane, Tween 80, acetonitrile, methanol and hydrochloride
acid (HCl) were all purchased from Fisher Scientific (Pittsburgh, USA).

2.2. Cell culture

Caco-2 cells, an established human colon epithelial cell line ,
were purchased from American Type Culture Collection (ATCC, Manassas, USA) for in vitro studies. The cells were cultured in RPMI-1640
medium supplemented with 1% penicillin-streptomycin (100U/ml) and
10% heat-inactivated fetal bovine serum (Hyclone, Logan, USA) and
maintained at 37 degC in humidified air containing 5% CO2. Cells were
passaged 1:5 approximately every 3 days at 85-90% confluence using
0.25% trypsin and 0.02% EDTA. Cells used for experiments were from
5th to 30th passages.

2.3. Preparation of nanoparticles

Conventional w/o/w SLN: SLN were prepared by emulsification solvent-evaporation technique based on double emulsion (w/o/w) .
An aqueous phase of 0.1 ml insulin in 0.01 M HCl (containing 500 mg
insulin) was added to 0.5 ml dichloromethane solution of 50 mg trimyristin and 25 mg soy lecithin. The mixture was treated with a probe
sonicator for 1 min in an ice bath (50 W, 20 kHz frequency, amplitude
50%) to form the primary w/o emulsion. This was followed by addition
of 1.5 ml of aqueous solution of 2% w/w Tween 80 to the primary
emulsion and 1 min sonication to form a w/o/w emulsion (F1). The
organic phase of the emulsion was evaporated by stirring at room
temperature until insulin loaded SLN were formed.
BLN: The first polymer PEG (MW grade: 2000, 4000 and 6000) was
dissolved in 0.01 M HCl in ratios listed in Table 1A to modify the internal aqueous phase of nanoparticles for insulin entrapment. To study
the impact of the second polymer (i.e. PLGA50:50), we further added
PLGA50:50 of different concentrations to the formulations with or
without 60% w/w of PEG 6000 within the internal aqueous core to
prepare formulations F11-13 and F14-F16, respectively (see Table 1B).
Insulin was then added to these aqueous phases and nanoparticles were
prepared similar to the conventional SLN F1. The process of BLN preparation is schematically described in Fig. 1B.

2.4. Particle size analysis and zeta potential determination

The size distribution, polydispersity index (PDI) and zeta potential
values of nanoparticles suspensions were determined by photon correlation spectroscopy (PCS) (Zetasizer(r) 3000HS, Malvern, UK). All samples were diluted with distilled water to get optimal kilo counts per
second of 50-200 for measurements. A minimum of three samples were
tested.

3.6. Cellular uptake of BLN and cytotoxicity

Fig. 5A presents the MTT assay result of Caco-2 intestinal endothelial cells treated with BLN and controls. Both BLN groups showed
low cytotoxicity on Caco-2 cells and could preserve up to 75% of its
viability at tested nanoparticles concentration of 10 mg/ml. The cytotoxicity of BLN was not significantly higher than the conventional SLN,
suggesting that the inclusion of polymers does not make the nanoparticles more toxic. Moderate toxicity of BLN was observed only at
high concentration range (20 and 40 mg/ml) where cell viability declined to around 60 and 55%, respectively. In clinical situation the
nanoparticles will be constantly diluted by the GI fluid instead of accumulating directly on the GI epithelium surface in static manner for
3 h so the in vivo toxicity is expected to be substantially lower.
Cellular uptake of BLN by Caco-2 cells was investigated using
fluorescence microscope. It was found that cells treated with fluorescently labeled BLN (Fig. 5B, right, BLN labeled in red, nuclei in blue)
showed noticeably stronger fluorescence than the untreated control
(Fig. 5B, left) under the same conditions. The images indicate substantial cellular interaction with and uptake of the newly developed
BLN.

3.7. In vivo glucose levels and relative pharmacological bioavailability

Fig. 6 presents the blood glucose profiles of animals treated with
free insulin (oral), free insulin (SC) and insulin loaded BLN (oral). As
expected, oral free insulin did not lower the blood glucose level.
Comparing oral BLN insulin with SC free insulin, the duration of hypoglycemic effect of the former was longer (5 h vs 3 h), the peak hypoglycemic effect was stronger (44.1% of baseline versus 61.5%). It
should be noted that rats dosed by oral gavage received 25 fold more
insulin than rats dosed by SC injection. With AAC calculated, this
translates into relative PA of 6.1%. All animals were visibly healthy
with no significant body weight loss after BLN treatment in the in vivo
study.
