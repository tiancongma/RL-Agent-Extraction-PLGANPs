A B S T R A C T
This study aims to develop chitosan-coated PLGA nanoparticles intended for nose-to-brain delivery of carmus­
tine. Formulations were prepared by the double emulsion solvent evaporation method and optimized by using
Box-Behnken Design. The optimized nanoparticles were obtained to satisfactory levels in terms of particle size,
PDI, entrapment efficiency, and drug loading. In vitro drug release and ex-vivo permeation showed sustained
release and enhanced permeability (approx. 2 fold) of carmustine compared to drug suspension. The AUC0-t of
brain obtained with carmustine-loaded nanoparticles via nasal administration in Albino Wistar rats was 2.8 and
14.7 times that of intranasal carmustine suspension and intravenous carmustine, respectively. The MTT assay on
U87 MG cell line showed a significant decrease (P < 0.05) in the IC50 value of the formulation (71.23 μg ml−1) as
compared to drug suspension (90.02 μg ml−1).These findings suggest chitosan coated nanoparticles could be used
to deliver carmustine via intranasal administration to treat Glioblastoma multiforme.
1. Introduction
Gliomas are among the most frequently occurring tumours of the
brain that account for around 80 % of all malignant primary tumours
[1,2]. Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) is one of the types of astrocytic
tumor (WHO classified as type IV), include >60 % of adults mainly of the
age group of 55 to 60 years [3–5] with a much higher ratio of incidence
in men as compared to women [6]. It is classified as a rare tumor but a
fatal disease with extremely poor diagnosis [7], having a survival rate of
about 14 to 15 months by the time it is diagnosed in a patient [8]. The
aggressive, invasive nature of GBM necessitates the application of a
multi-target approach for maximum therapeutic outcome in patients. At
present, the primary treatment of GBM includes resection of the tumor,
radiotherapy, and chemotherapy [9]. Temozolamide (TMZ) is the
standard chemotherapeutic agent approved for the treatment of GBM.
Frequent oral administration of TMZ is required to treat GBM due to its
short half-life (1.8 h only) [10], which would not be free from side
effects due to systemic distribution to other organs [11]. Another group
of antitumor drugs belongs to nitrosoureas, which works by DNA
alkylation and has been used for GBM treatment. It includes carmustine
(BCNU), lomustine (CCNU), and nimustine (ACNU). Systemic side ef­
fects, which mainly include severe toxicity to kidney, liver and bone
marrow, discourage their use. A few researchers reported the benefit of
using carmustine wafers in newly diagnosed GBM patients. Carmustine
wafers are placed in the resection cavity and this result in improved
survival with reduced systemic side effects in the patients [12]. How­
ever, the method is invasive and requires surgical implantation in the
brain which is not preferable.
A noninvasive approach such as an intranasal delivery system ap­
pears to be a boon that bypasses the BBB and aids in the treatment of
brain disorders [13]. Many researchers reported pre-clinical and clinical
studies claiming that the nose-to-brain delivery route possesses a great
capability to treat several brain diseases, including GBM treatment
modalities. Drug administered through intranasal delivery reach the

CNS via intra-and perineuronal pathways, the olfactory and trigeminal

tumor response after intranasal administration of various drugs, such as
methotrexate [15,16], 5-fluorouracil [17], 5-fluorouracil together with
acetazolamide [18]. They all concluded in their research that the size of
the tumor was considerably decreased among the treated rats. The
conventional treatment approaches, to target brain include intra-
ventricular drug diffusion, disruption of the BBB, as well as local rem­

brain tumor chemotherapy, a new strategy is desperately needed to
solve all the issues of targeting brain tumours.
Targeting brain via nasal route require drug delivery system which
could adhere in the nasal mucosa and penetrate through the olfactory
pathway. Reach to the brain by IN route majorly depends on the dosage
form and the delivery carrier or device. Delivery system such as
mucoadhesive nanoparticles/microparticles and nanogels reported
sustained and prolonged delivery of drug via nasal route [20]. In a
recent publication which reviewed the results of preclinical and clinical
data obtained for various formulations obtained using nasal adminis­
tration for the treatment GBM. The collected data are in support of nose
to brain delivery of anti tumor agent and indicated that this could be a
breakthrough in GBM treatment [21].
The natural polysaccharide chitosan (poly-cationic polymer) is
explored widely due to its mucoadhesive property as the amines in it
react with sialic residues existing on the layer of mucosa, which aids in
lowering the clearance rate from the nasal cavity [22]. Chitosan and it
derivatives based delivery carrier dendrimer, nanoparticles and hydro­
gel has been reported as safe and effective for IN administration for brain
targeting [23–25]. Intranasal administration of nanolipidic formulation
of tenozolamide chitosan gel reported enhanced penetration of the
nanocarrier and suggested the role of chitosan as penetration enhancer
[26].
Bruinsmann et al. [27] reported the role of molecular weight (MW)
of chitosan in coated lipidic nanoparticles for simvastatin brain delivery.
The release of the drug and mucoadherence properties is dependent on
the MW of the chitosan. Due to these favourable properties, chitosan was
chosen for the formulation, along with synthetic polymers such as PLGA
for development of suitable antitumor drug delivery carriers [28,29].
PLGA has many beneficial properties, including non-toxicity, biocom­
patibility, excellent biodegradability [30], preventing drug hydrolysis
and enzymatic degradation, lowering irritation in tissues due to the
polymeric shell and immune-stimulating effect, and improving treat­
ment effectiveness and efficiency [31].
The aim of this research was to develop chitosan-coated PLGA (poly-
lactide-co-glycolic acid) nanoparticles for carmustine by using a double
emulsion solvent evaporation technique. Formulation was optimized
with the help of Design Expert software and characterized for particle
size, shape, zeta potential, in vitro drug release, and ex-vivo permeation
through nasal mucosa. Further in-vitro toxicity assay and pharmacoki­
netic studies were conducted to investigate the intranasal delivery of
carmustine to the brain in Albino Wistar rats.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Drug and chemicals
Carmustine (MW 214.05) was acquired from Sigma-Aldrich (St.
Louis, MO). Polymer PLGA (PURASORB PDLG; DL-lactide/Glycolide
copolymer, ratio M/M%: 50/50) was procured as a generous gift from
PuracBiochem BV (Gorinchem, the Netherlands) and polymer chitosan
(MW: 120,000) was purchased from India Sea Foods (Kochi, Kerala,
India). Polyvinyl alcohol and all other reagents and chemicals were
procured from Merck Ltd. (Mumbai, India).
2.2. Animals
The healthy Albino Wistar rats weighing between 200 and 250 g of
either sex were used for the in vivo studies of 6–8 weeks, obtained from
the Central Animal House Facility in accordance with the guidelines by
the Institutional Animal Ethics Committee (IAEC) of Jamia Hamdard,
New Delhi, with the approved protocol number of 1555 from CPCSEA.
All the experiments were carried out in accordance with the guidelines
of the Committee for the Purpose and Supervision of Experimentation on
Animals (CPCSEA). All the animals were housed in a controlled envi­
ronment with free access in cages to standard chow pellets and water,
and to easily identify them, they were housed in different cages with
appropriate marking.
2.3. Preparation of chitosan coated PLGA nanoparticles
The process used for preparing chitosan coated PLGA NPs was double
emulsion solvent evaporation (w/o/w), in which first PLGA (1–3 mg/
ml) was dissolved in dichloromethane, and the drug, carmustine was
also added to it. This solution was then dropwise poured into PVA (1–2
% w/v) which was kept on vigorous stirring at 700 rpm. To evaporate
the last traces of dichloromethane, the dispersion was continuously
stirred for 4–5 h at 25 ◦C [32]. After complete evaporation of
dichloromethane, a 0.2 % w/v chitosan solution (prepared in 1 % glacial
acetic acid with pH 5.0) was added dropwise under continue stirring
condition and kept on stirred for 2–3 h continuously. Prepared chitosan-
coated PLGA NPs, were sonicated at 30 kHz usingUP50H® ultrasonic
processor (Hielscher-Ultrasound Technology, Germany) equipped with
a titanium horn of 1 mm in diameter at 20 % amplitude for about 11
min. To avoid degradation of the polymer and drug, the sonication was
performed by keeping the formulation in an ice bath to maintain the
temperature below 4 ◦C. NPs were collected by centrifugation at 10,000
rpm and 4 ◦C for 20 min [33].
2.3.1. Optimization of chitosan coated PLGA nanoparticles
Response surface methodology (RSM) especially Box–Behnken
design is widely preferred over other experimental designs such as three
level full Factorial design, Doehlert design and Central composite design
due to advantage of working with three level for each factors of which
two levels are covering the extreme low and high and a middle level. It
also require fewer run time and that is most preferable if the drug and
polymers involved in the experiment are costly [34]. Box-Behnken
Design-Expert Software (Design Expert 8.0.7.1, Stat-Ease Inc., Minne­
apolis, MN) was used to optimize this formulation and obtained 15 runs
with three factors and three levels. It was found to be the best tool for
exploring the quadratic response surface and constricting second-order
polynomial models, because it only needs a few runs with three inde­
pendent variables X1 = PLGA (mg), X2 = PVA (%), X3 = sonication time
(min), and two dependent variables Y1 = Particle Size (nm), and Y2 =
Polydispersity Index (PDI) (Table 1).According to screening results, the
variable ranges were chosen and designed, consisting of replicated low,

2.4. Characterization of carmustine loaded chitosan coated PLGA NPs
2.4.1. Particle size and zeta potential
Optimized chitosan coated PLGA Nanoparticles were diluted with
Milli-Q water to measure the size, polydispersity index (PDI) and surface
charge by using Zetasizer® (Nano ZS, Malvern Instrument, UK) equip­
ped with a red laser of wavelength λ0 = 633 nm (He-Ne, 4.0 MW) at
about 90◦angle and temperature of 25 ◦C. Particle and polydispersity
index (PDI) were analyzed by photon correlation spectroscopy (PCS)
and surface charge of nanoparticles were measured by Laser Doppler
Anemometer (LDA) [36,37].
2.4.2. Drug encapsulation efficiency (EE) and loading capacity (LC)
The optimized freeze-dried chitosan-coated PLGA nanoparticles
were dispersed in distilled water, stirred at 1000 rpm for 24 h and then
ultrasonicated for 10 min at a magnitude of 60 and at cycles of 0.8. This
colloidal dispersion was filtered by a 0.22μmmembrane filter and
centrifuged for 15 min at 15000 rpm and 4 ◦C by using a cooling
centrifuge (REMI, C-24 plus) and the aqueous phase (supernatant) was
examined for free carmustine obtained after centrifugation. The amount
of free carmustine was calculated as a difference between the total
amount taken to prepare the nanoparticles and the amount present in
the supernatant [38]. All the measurements were carried out in triplicate
and evaluated by a UV spectrophotometer at a wavelength of 232 nm.

2.4.3. Morphology study
The particle size of the selected formulation was observed using
Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM) (Tecnai G2 S-twin, FEI,
Netherland), which was operated at 120 kV and a scale of 500 nm,
making point-to-point resolution capable. For preparing TEM samples, a
small amount of NPs was dispersed in phosphate buffer (pH 6.4). Sam­
ples were dropped on to copper grids and dried at room temperature for
examination. Images were captured and analyzed using Keen View FM
and Olympus Soft Imaging Viewer, respectively, at 5000× magnification
[39].
2.4.4. In vitro release and kinetics study
The drug release from carmustine-loaded chitosan-coated PLGA NPs
was carried out using a dialysis membrane bag. Carmustine-loaded
chitosan-coated PLGA NPs (carmustine equivalent to 7.5 mg/ml) were
placed and tied in pre-treated cellulose membrane bags before being
immersed in 50 ml of dissolution media containing PBS (pH 6.4)
maintained at 37 ± 0.5 ◦C with continuous stirring at 100 rpm. At
various intervals of time (0.5, 1, 2, 4, 8, 12, 16 and 24 h), 3 ml samples
were withdrawn and sink conditions were maintained by replacing an
equal volume of fresh dissolution medium. Similarly, the above pro­
cedure was conducted for carmustine suspension. The amount of
carmustine was evaluated in triplicate by UV spectroscopy at λmax232
nm. Furthermore, drug release data obtained for chitosan coated PLGA
NPs was fitted to zero order, first order, and Higuchi and PeppasKors­
meyer equations to evaluate the release profile of the formulation
[40,41].

2.8. Pharmacokinetic study
Comparative bioavailability of carmustine via nose-to-brain delivery
of carmustine loaded chitosan coated PLGA NPs and carmustine sus­
pension was performed on healthy Albino Wistar rats of either sex, aged
6–8 weeks. The animals were distributed into 3 groups, each comprising
5 animals. Optimised carmustine-loaded chitosan-coated PLGA NPs
were given through the intranasal route in Group I and injected intra­
venously in Group II. Plain carmustine suspension was given through the
intranasal route in Group III. Each group received a calculated dose
equivalent to 1.712 mg/300 g rat estimated from carmustine dose of
5.707 mg/kg in rat [45]. Dosing was performed gently via intranasal
route and the rats were carefully handled from the posterior side in a
slanting position allowing them to inhale the formulation.
The rats were euthanized by cervical dislocation after an aesthesia at
different intervals of time for the collection of blood through cardiac
puncture as well as the brain. Approximately 1.5 ml of blood was
withdrawn from the retro-orbital plexus in an Eppendorf tube previously
containing 25 μl of EDTA solution (prepared as a 30 mg/ml EDTA so­
lution) as an anticoagulant. The brain and blood samples were collected
at 0.15, 0.5, 1, 2, and 4 h post-dose according to the following schedule
as shown in Table 4 with Dosing. The blood samples were collected in an
Eppendorf tube, mixed gently with anticoagulant and kept for 1 h at
room temperature. The homogenised brain was mixed in the mobile
phase (Acetonitrile: Water 70:30 v/v) and centrifuged at 10,000 rpm at
4 ◦C for 10 min before being placed in separate Eppendorf tubes, sealed,
and stored at −20 ◦C. HPLC method was used to analyse the concen­
tration of carmustine in blood and brain plasma under chromatographic
conditions with mobile phase Acetonitrile: water (70:30 v/v) at a flow
rate of 1.5 ml/min for 10 min at a wavelength of 232 nm [46].
The plasma concentration of carmustine was analyzed to calculate
various parameters such as the maximum plasma concentration (Cmax)
and the maximum time to reach concentration (Tmax). The area under
the curve from 0 to t (AUC0–4) and from 0 to infinity (AUC0-∞) was
estimated by the software WinNonlin (version 5.2) and PK1 (Pharsight,
Mountain View, CA, USA) whereas the elimination rate constant (Ke)
and t1/2 (half-life) were calculated with the help of PK 2 software. A
difference in probability level of <0.05 was considered statistically
significant. ANOVA was used to compare the PK data between different
formulations for statistical significance, followed by a Tukey-Kramer
multiple comparisons test using (GraphPad, Software Inc., CA, USA).

3. Results and discussion
Nanoparticles were prepared according to the double emulsion sol­
vent evaporation method and optimization of drug-loaded chitosan
coated PLGA nanoparticles was performed. Various concentrations of
drug (carmustine), PLGA and PVA were taken and were optimized for
particle size and polydispersity index (PDI) to obtain desired PLGA
nanoparticles. After obtaining optimized PLGA nanoparticles, different
concentrations of chitosan were taken and optimized for coating on
PLGA nanoparticles of the drug.

3.1.2. Effect of variables on particle size (Y1)
According to the results obtained from the experiments for particle
size was clearly influenced by amount of PLGA. Eq. (8) can explain the
effect of factor levels on particle size, the main effect of 3 independent
variables X1 = PLGA (mg), X2 = PVA (%), X3 = Sonication time (min)
represents the average result of changing 1 variable at a time from its
low level to its high level, as seen the size varied from 208 (Formulation
13) to 421 nm (Formulation 7). On analyzing the results of surfaces
predicted for particle size (Fig. 1a1, b1, c1), it was found that the in­
crease of PLGA and PVA results in reduction of the particle size of
polymeric nanoparticles whereas decreasing sonication time leads to
particle size reduction.
3.1.3. Effect of variables on polydispersity index (Y2)
The smaller PDI value is highly desirable in order to attain uniform
size distribution in the dispersion media. According to the results ob­
tained from the experiments, PDI varied from 0.221(Formulation 13) to
0.561 (formulation 11). Fig. 1a2, b2, c2 shows the influence of the
amount of PLGA, PVA and sonication time on PDI. On increasing the
amount of PLGA and sonication time the PDI increases whereas the
amount PVA content did not show any significant influence on
polydispersity index.
3.2. Characterization
3.2.1. Particle size, zeta potential and morphology
Chitosan coated PLGA nanoparticles of carmustine (optimized
formulation) showed a diameter of 232.5 ± 4.5 nm and PDI0.317 ±
0.17 (Fig. 2a), whereas the Zeta potential of nanoparticles was found to
be +30.8 mV (Fig. 2b) suggesting that the particles are not clustered
together. Owing to interaction between chitosan (positively charged),
PLGA (negatively charged) and carmustine (negatively charged) results
in highly positive zeta potential of the optimized formulation that en­
ables ease in binding with negatively charged tumor cells as well as
negligible cytotoxicity effect to the normal cell as reported in the liter­
ature available [47,48].
The shape and morphology depicted by TEM image of optimized
carmustine loaded chitosan coated PLGA NPs is shown in Fig. 2c. It
depicts roughly circular or sphere-shaped particles with a considerably

3.3. In-vitro drug release and kinetics study
At different time interval drug release kinetics of carmustine loaded
chitosan coated PLGA NPs(formulation) and Carmustine (drug suspen­
sion) was performed in phosphate buffer (pH 7.4). The release of Car­
mustine from nanoparticles showed biphasic release i.e., initially rapid
release might be due to drug absorbed on the NPs surface that are
weakly interacted followed by the slower sustained and continuous
release of Carmustine from the core of nanoparticles. The drug release
data obtained for optimized Carmustine loaded chitosan coated PLGA
NPs was fitted to all the release kinetic models. Drug release profile of
the carmustine formulation and carmustine suspension was shown in
Fig. 3a and it followed PeppasKorsemeyer as it shows the highest value
of related correlation coefficients (R2). Hence, the best fit model for the
nanoparticles was found by PeppasKorsemeyer and the release exponent
value (“n” value) observed to be n = 0.0201 that showed the release
mechanism of the drug characterized as the Fickian diffusion
mechanism.

3.6. In vitro cell viability assay (MTT assay)
The mechanism behind the MTT assay involves biochemical inter­
action of NAD(P)H-dependent cellular oxidoreductase enzyme which

diphenyltetrazolium bromide] to insoluble (E, Z)-5-(4,5-dimethyl thia­
zol-2-yl)-1,3-diphenyl formazan (formazan) which can be dissolved with
dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) giving out a purple color with characteristic
absorption at 540 nm and the purple color intensity is directly propor­
tional to the cell number (cell viability). Cytotoxicity of Chitosan coated
PLGA NPs formulation was expressed as IC50 value was studied on
U87mg cell lines (Fig. 5a). IC50 value for Carmustine standard drug
found to be higher (90.02 μg ml−1) as compared to Chitosan coated
PLGA NPs (71.23 μg ml−1). This difference in IC50 value concludes
increased anti-proliferative efficacy of Chitosan coated PLGA NP
(Formulation) over Carmustine drug suspension (Standard). The
viability plot and cell morphology of U87 cell lines treated with car­
mustine loaded chitosan coated PLGA NPs was shown in Fig. 5b and
compared with control. Results indicates that there was negligible
toxicity at low concentration till 12 h of incubation in both the cases
which may be possible due to the release of an absorbed drug from the
surface of chitosan coated PLGA NPs of carmustine. After 24 h incuba­
tion, a steep decrease in the cell mortality rate was observed which was
prolonged till 48 h might be due to slow and sustained release of the
drug in the interior parts of cells. In the blank formulation i.e., Chitosan
coated PLGA NPs without carmustine showed no cytotoxicity. The
outcome of study showed that carmustine loaded chitosan coated PLGA
NPs has been non-toxic on cell lines and improved anti-proliferative

better uptake to the brain. From previous published work, we can sug­

4. Conclusions
The current study presents a novel polymeric nanoparticulate
formulation of carmustine for glioblastoma multiforme via nose-to-brain
delivery that was successfully designed, optimized, and examined. The
effect of distinct variables on chitosan-coated PLGA nanoparticles of
carmustine was examined and demonstrated that optimized formulation
has appropriate particle size and PDI. The entrapment efficiency and
drug loading were also satisfactory. The formulation exhibited a
biphasic release, initially showing a burst release followed by a sus­
tained release over 24 h under in vitro release study. Moreover, the ex
vivo permeation study revealed a prominent permeation of chitosan-
coated PLGA nanoparticles of carmustine through goat nasal mucosa
in comparison to pure carmustine solution. The Confocal Laser Scanning
Microscopy (CLSM) Study showed that chitosan-coated PLGA nano­
particles of carmustine were able to penetrate across the mucosal layer,
be taken up by goat nasal epithelia, and transported into submucosal
layers, making this novel system a promising vehicle for nasal drug
delivery to target brain. The pharmacokinetic study in mice at different
time points revealed a significant increase in brain uptake of NPs as
assessed by the brain: blood ratio and DTE and DTP percentages. The
MTT assay on the U87MG cell line showed a mean IC50 value71.23 μg
ml−1, exhibiting a significant decrease in IC50value in comparison to
Carmustine. However, further exploration in pre-clinical as well as
clinical studies is needed to establish this nanoparticulate formulation of
carmustine to delivery through the nasal cavity to conclude it as much
potential clinically useful GBM pharmacotherapy.
