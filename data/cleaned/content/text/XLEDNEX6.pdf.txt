Development of bi-polymer lipid hybrid nanocarrier (BLN) to improve the
entrapment and stability of insulin for eﬃcient oral delivery

A B S T R A C T
Solid lipid nanoparticles (SLN) have demonstrated favorable properties for oral protein delivery. However, their
protein entrapment eﬃciency (EE%) values remain limited due to their hydrophobic nature. In this study, we
reported a new strategy in which two polymeric excipients were incorporated into the double-emulsion based
SLN to address this entrapment ineﬃciency issue. Using insulin as the model protein drug, the resulting hybrid
nanocarriers known as bi-polymer lipid nanocarriers (BLN) were evaluated for physicochemical properties, drug
and particle stability, in vitro biological behaviors and in vivo hypoglycemic eﬀect. It was found that BLN con-
taining PEG 6000 in internal aqueous phase and PLGA in lipid phase showed favorable size (around 240 nm) and
reasonably narrow particle size distribution. The bi-polymer strategy improved insulin EE% over standard w/o/
w SLN by 2.5-fold (∼50% versus 20%) while preserving the insulin chemical stability and biological activity.
BLN demonstrated good dispersion stability under gastrointestinal conditions, protected the entrapped insulin
against enzymatic degradation well, and were well internalized into Caco-2 cells with minimal cytotoxicity.
Pharmacological availability of oral insulin delivered by BLN (6.1%) was comparable to lectin-modiﬁed SLN. To
conclude, BLN is a promising hybrid nanocarrier design to achieve eﬃcient oral insulin delivery.
1. Introduction
Oral peptide/protein delivery is a highly appealing option due to
commercial, therapeutic and patient compliance reasons [1]. Up to
date, insulin remains the most commonly used protein drug. However,
for over 80 years, its delivery to diabetic patients still primarily relies
on subcutaneous (SC) injection [2]. Although this parenteral route is
satisfactory in terms of eﬃcacy, it is associated with several side eﬀects,
such as hyperinsulinemia, local hypertrophy, psychological discomfort,
pain and high probability of infections which all lead to poor patient
compliance especially considering that a typical diabetic patient re-
quires more than 60,000 injections in lifetime [3,4]. Hence, extensive
studies have been conducted in the past few decades to exploit mucosal
surfaces for the delivery of insulin, especially the nasal, pulmonary and
oral mucosae [5]. In particular, oral delivery is considered to be the
most convenient [2]. From the physiological perspective, oral insulin is
also superior because most of the insulin dose can be directly channeled
from the intestines to the liver (the primary site of action) (versus only
20% following sc injection) to avoid peripheral hyperinsulinemic side
eﬀects [6].
To achieve eﬃcient oral insulin delivery, several obstacles need to
be overcome. The foremost challenge is the harsh environment of the
gastrointestinal (GI) environment, including low regional pH, a pro-
tective mucous layer and digestive enzymes to break down ingested
proteins and peptides [7]. For insulin, it may be vulnerable to the GI
proteolytic enzymes such as pepsin, trypsin, chymotrypsin and car-
boxypeptidases [8]. Even if some insulin molecules can somehow re-
main stable, the majority of them still cannot successfully cross the GI
tract epithelium considering the large molecular weight and hydro-
philic nature of insulin itself. The overall result is a very low bioa-
vailability if free insulin is orally administered [9].
The use of sub micrometric colloidal carriers (diameter < 1 μm) has
been proposed as a promising approach for overcoming the aforemen-
tioned barriers. Among diﬀerent carriers, solid lipid nanoparticles
(SLN), ﬁrst described by Müller et al. [10], constitute a major class of
nanoparticles widely used in oral protein delivery. SLN are mainly

composed of a solid matrix based on very chemically diﬀerent lipid
molecules (e.g., waxes, mixtures of mono-, di-, and triacylglycerols,
fatty acids) [11]. They can overcome the membrane stability and drug-
leaching problems associated with emulsions and the toxicity problems
of many other nanoparticles [12]. They are commonly studied because
of several advantages such as high biocompatibility, avoidance of toxic
organic solvents and feasibility of production on large industrial scale
[13,14]. For oral insulin delivery, SLN may address two major pro-
blems. First, the solid, hydrophobic nature of SLN can protect the en-
trapped protein by making it diﬃcult for gastric acid and protease
enzymes to access [14]. Second, lipids also tend to interact well with
cell surface, and the lipid core of SLN has been reported to stimulate
chylomicron formation and facilitate lymphatic uptake through the gut
associated lymphoid tissue [15]. This could further enhance the oral
bioavailability of a protein drug.
Despite the good potential of using SLN for oral delivery of protein
drugs like insulin [16], two critical issues remain unresolved. First, the
hydrophobic nature of SLN makes them inherently more suitable for
lipophilic drug entrapment [14]. A protein drug is expected to ionize
and partition in the external aqueous phase during the emulsiﬁcation
process. This issue can be partly addressed using double emulsion
technique (i.e. w/o/w) but the protein entrapment eﬃciency (EE%)
remains too low to be practically useful (EE% typically near or below
20%) [17]. Second, the delicate molecular architecture of proteins can
be a signiﬁcant concern when preparing their delivery systems [18].
The aqueous/organic interface in an emulsion was shown to be a crucial
destabilizing factor leading to protein aggregation and adsorption at the
interface where they undergo unfolding by presenting their hydro-
phobic area to the organic solvent [18,19]. It was found that the for-
mation of the primary w/o emulsion, and to a lesser extent of the
secondary w/o/w emulsion, is mainly responsible for protein dena-
turation [19].
In order to achieve eﬃcient delivery of insulin, the strategy of in-
corporating additional materials, typically a biocompatible polymer
that interacts well with insulin, into lipids to form a “hybrid” nano-
carrier has become increasingly studied [20]. For instances, Wu et al.'s
combined polyethylene glycol with short chain fatty acids butyrate and
the resulting nanoparticles could achieve 2.87-fold higher oral bioa-
vailability [21]. Other examples include chitosan-incorporated lipo-
somes and fatty acid/quaternary ammonium/chitosan nanoparticles
[22,23] which both signiﬁcantly improved the pharmacological avail-
abilities over free insulin. In this study, a new bi-polymer lipid hybrid
nanocarrier (BLN) which incorporates two polymers within the w/o/w
based SLN is proposed. Fig. 1A illustrates its diﬀerence from the con-
ventional SLN. In BLN, the 1st polymer is a hydrophilic one intended to
modify the internal aqueous phase of the double emulsion (w/o/w)
during nanoparticle preparation. We hypothesize that the addition of
such a hydrophilic polymer increase the entrapment of the protein in
the internal aqueous phase and reduce its tendency to leak to the ex-
ternal aqueous phase, leading to improved EE%. Polyethylene glycols
(PEG) were selected because they are the most commonly used hy-
drophilic polymers in clinical practice [24], and their various char-
acteristics i.e. low polydispersity in molecular weight, high thermal
stability, good stability in both acidic and basic media, and low toxicity
(LD50 in animals > 2000 mg/kg body weight) are all well suited for
the proposed functions [25,26].
The 2nd polymer is intended to provide modest surface active
properties to help stabilize the aqueous/organic interface which stands
as the main cause of protein degradation. Poly (lactic-co-glycolic acid)
(PLGA) was chosen to perform this function. PLGA is biodegradable and
biocompatible polymer composed of lactic acid and glycolic acid. They
have a dual nature in structure with lactic acid being hydrophobic,
while the glycolic acid is hydrophilic [27]. With these amphiphilic
properties, we hypothesize that a more stabilized w/o/w system can be
formed which would indirectly contribute to higher EE% of insulin.
Speciﬁcally, the aims of the present work are to (i) investigate the
feasibility of BLN preparation, and (ii) characterize these BLN and
evaluate their potential advantage for oral insulin delivery with em-
phasis on both insulin EE% and stability. It is expected that this new
nanocarrier design can preserve the general advantages of SLN (e.g.
excellent biocompatibility, eﬀective drug protection) while achieving
signiﬁcantly more eﬃcient entrapment and delivery of insulin.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Chemicals and reagents
Insulin (Recombinant Human, activity > 27.5 IU/mg), tetra me-
thyl ammonium hydroxide, PEG 2000, PEG 4000 and PEG 6000 were
purchased from Sigma Aldrich (St Louis, USA), soy lecithin from Chem
Service (Westchester, USA), trimyristin from TCI- EP (Tokyo, Japan),
PLGA 50:50 from Purac (Gorinchem, The Netherlands) 1,2-dipalmitoyl-
sn-glycero-3-phosphoethanolamineN-(lissamine rhodamine B sulfonyl)
(ammonium salt) from Avanti polar lipids (Alabama, USA), thiazolyl
blue tetrazolium bromide from Amresco (USA). Other chemicals such as
dichloromethane, Tween 80, acetonitrile, methanol and hydrochloride
acid (HCl) were all purchased from Fisher Scientiﬁc (Pittsburgh, USA).
2.2. Cell culture
Caco-2 cells, an established human colon epithelial cell line [28],
were purchased from American Type Culture Collection (ATCC, Man-
assas, USA) for in vitro studies. The cells were cultured in RPMI-1640
medium supplemented with 1% penicillin-streptomycin (100U/ml) and
10% heat-inactivated fetal bovine serum (Hyclone, Logan, USA) and
maintained at 37 °C in humidiﬁed air containing 5% CO2. Cells were
passaged 1:5 approximately every 3 days at 85–90% conﬂuence using
0.25% trypsin and 0.02% EDTA. Cells used for experiments were from
5th to 30th passages.
2.3. Preparation of nanoparticles
Conventional w/o/w SLN: SLN were prepared by emulsiﬁcation sol-
vent-evaporation technique based on double emulsion (w/o/w) [29].
An aqueous phase of 0.1 ml insulin in 0.01 M HCl (containing 500 μg
insulin) was added to 0.5 ml dichloromethane solution of 50 mg tri-
myristin and 25 mg soy lecithin. The mixture was treated with a probe
sonicator for 1 min in an ice bath (50 W, 20 kHz frequency, amplitude
50%) to form the primary w/o emulsion. This was followed by addition
of 1.5 ml of aqueous solution of 2% w/w Tween 80 to the primary
emulsion and 1 min sonication to form a w/o/w emulsion (F1). The
organic phase of the emulsion was evaporated by stirring at room
temperature until insulin loaded SLN were formed.
BLN: The ﬁrst polymer PEG (MW grade: 2000, 4000 and 6000) was
dissolved in 0.01 M HCl in ratios listed in Table 1A to modify the in-
ternal aqueous phase of nanoparticles for insulin entrapment. To study
the impact of the second polymer (i.e. PLGA50:50), we further added
PLGA50:50 of diﬀerent concentrations to the formulations with or
without 60% w/w of PEG 6000 within the internal aqueous core to
prepare formulations F11-13 and F14-F16, respectively (see Table 1B).
Insulin was then added to these aqueous phases and nanoparticles were
prepared similar to the conventional SLN F1. The process of BLN pre-
paration is schematically described in Fig. 1B.
2.4. Particle size analysis and zeta potential determination
The size distribution, polydispersity index (PDI) and zeta potential
values of nanoparticles suspensions were determined by photon corre-
lation spectroscopy (PCS) (Zetasizer® 3000HS, Malvern, UK). All sam-
ples were diluted with distilled water to get optimal kilo counts per
second of 50–200 for measurements. A minimum of three samples were
tested.

2.5. Determination of insulin EE%
Nanoparticle suspensions were centrifuged at 50,000 rpm for 1 h at
4 °C. The supernatant was then removed and the nanoparticles washed
with 0.01 M HCl to remove any free insulin adsorbed onto nanoparticles
surface. The pellet was then dissolved in methanol and 0.01 M HCl was
added to dissolve insulin [30]. The suspension was centrifuged again at
15,000 rpm for 10 min and the supernatant was analyzed for insulin
content by HPLC. The results are expressed as EE%, i.e. the ratio be-
tween the actual and theoretical insulin loading percentage [14].

2.6. Chemical stability of insulin after BLN encapsulation
Simultaneous HPLC analysis of both human insulin and its main
decomposition product, A21-desamido insulin, was performed as pre-
viously reported for evaluation of the chemical stability of insulin
[31,32]. HPLC peaks were used to conﬁrm the stability of BLN -en-
trapped insulin by comparing them to peaks of standard insulin and
A21-desamido insulin. A21-desamido insulin was obtained by in-
cubating insulin in 0.01 M HCl at 40 °C for 48 h [27].
2.7. Biological stability of insulin after BLN encapsulation
The biological activity of the insulin after BLN entrapment was
evaluated using iLite ™Insulin Assay Ready Cells expressing insulin
receptors and the related reporter gene (Svar Life Science, Malmo,
Sweden) following the manufacturer's protocol. Brieﬂy, 100 mg insulin-
loaded BLN was incubated in 0.01 M HCl at 25 °C for 24 h to release a
portion of the entrapped insulin. The concentration of the released in-
sulin was quantiﬁed using HPLC as before and the BLN-released insulin
was diluted to 62.5, 125, 250, 500 ng/ml. Tested samples (BLN-released
insulin), positive control (fresh insulin) and negative control (insulin
free) were prepared by mixing with the diluent provided by the man-
ufacturer. The reporter cells were then treated with the diluted samples
or controls for 5 h at 37 °C and the cellular luminescence as a result of
insulin activity on reporter genes was determined using Moonlight
3010 luminometer (Pharmingen, San Jose, USA).
2.8. Dispersion stability in GI environment
Selected BLN suspensions were incubated at pH 2.5 and pH 6.8 at
37 °C and stirred at 100 rpm for 4 h to mimic the physiological condi-
tions following oral administration of the particles. Dispersion stability
of BLN was monitored by PCS to investigate any particle growth or
aggregation [33].

2.9. Enzymatic stability of insulin loaded BLN
In this study, enzymatic stability of insulin entrapped inside BLN
was evaluated against both pepsin and α-chymotrypsin. 1.5 ml of each
proteolytic enzyme (in pH 2.5 in case of pepsin and pH 7.4 in case of
chymotrypsin) was added into 1.5 ml of both insulin-loaded BLN and
free insulin solution. Diﬀerent insulin: enzyme ratios were ﬁrst screened
to optimize the experiment conditions. The mixture was immediately
incubated at 37 °C. Sample aliquots were withdrawn at selected time
intervals. Pepsin and α-chymotrypsin digestion reaction was stopped by
adding 0.1 N. NaOH and 0.1 N HCl, respectively [34]. The remaining
insulin concentrations in the samples were determined by HPLC as
described above.
2.10. Cell uptake study
Selected BLN were modiﬁed for ﬂuorescence detection by in-
corporation of 30 μl of 1 mg/ml ﬂuorescent lipid, 1, 2-dipalmitoyl-sn-
glycero-3-phosphoethanolamineN-(lissamine rhodamine B sulfonyl), to
the organic phase during double emulsion preparation. Caco-2 cells
were grown on sterile coverslips coated with poly-L-lysine for 24 h. The
coverslips were transferred to 35 mm × 10 mm cell culture dishes and
treated with ﬂuorescent nanoparticles for 4 h. At the end of incubation,
the cells were stained with DRAQ5 nuclear dye and rinsed with DPBS
three times. The coverslips were mounted on a clean glass slide and
examined using ﬂuorescence microscope Axiostar Plus epiﬂuorescence
microscope (Carl Zeiss, Oberkochen, Germany). Images were captured
with Insight camera model 8.0 and image analysis conducted using

2.11. MTT assay
Nanoparticle in vitro cytotoxicity was evaluated using MTT assay
with Caco-2 cells. Caco-2 cells were seeded on 96-well plates at a den-
sity of 10,000 cells per well [35]. When the cells were conﬂuent at 48 h,
they were treated with free insulin, blank BLN or insulin-loaded BLN
(1.25–40 mg/ml). After 3 h of treatment, the treatments were replaced
with fresh medium and incubated for 24 h at 37 °C. 20 μl 3-(4,5 di-
methyl-thiazol-2-yl)-2,5-diphenyl tetrazolium bromide (MTT) (5 mg/
ml) was then added to each well and the cells were incubated for 2 h.
The unreacted dye was removed and the formazan crystals were dis-
solved in DMSO (150 μl/well). Absorption at 560 nm was measured
using a microplate reader with 690 nm as reference. Untreated cells
containing only cell culture medium without nanoparticles served as
baseline control and the cell viability relative to blank control was
calculated.
2.12. Evaluation of hypoglycemic eﬀect in rats
Sprague–Dawley rats (250 ± 25 g, male) were housed under
normal conditions with free access to food and water. All animal ex-
periments comply with the ARRIVE guidelines and were carried out in
accordance with the National Institutes of Health guide for the care and
use of Laboratory animals (NIH Publications No. 8023, revised 1978).
The animal protocol was approved by The Institutional Animal Care
and Use Committee (ACUP: #4387). Rats were randomized into fol-
lowing groups: Negative control (no insulin at all), positive control (free
insulin administered subcutaneously, 2 IU/kg), oral insulin (free insulin
administered intragastrically with a gavage needle, 50 IU/kg), BLN
(BLN administered intragastrically with a gavage needle at an insulin
dose of 50 IU/kg). After the rats were anaesthetized by isoﬂurane in-
halation, blood samples were collected at diﬀerent time intervals from
the tip of the tail vein after needle puncture. Blood glucose levels were
determined with a veterinarian glucometer (AlphaTRAK 2™model,
Abbott, Illinois, USA).
Plasma glucose levels versus time were monitored as previously
reported. The initial blood glucose levels were regarded as 100% and all
other levels referred to them as % of initial level [36]. This eﬀect was
quantiﬁed by determining the area-above-the-curve (AAC) using the
trapezoidal method. Relative pharmacological availability (PA) of in-
sulin orally delivered by BLN was calculated using the following

either PEG or PLGA, which were both superior (P < 0.001) to the
conventional SLN F1.
3.3. Chemical stability and biological activity of insulin after entrapment in
BLN
Chemical stability of insulin in BLN (F15) was evaluated by HPLC.
The BLN trace (Fig. 3C) only showed the HPLC peak corresponding to
the insulin standard, with the same retention time RT (8.3 min) as
shown in Fig. 3A. The peak corresponding to A21-desamido insulin
(RT = 10.3 min in Fig. 3B, the main degradation product of insulin)
was not observed in the BLN trace [31]. The ﬁndings indicate that in-
sulin had retained its chemical stability and integrity after entrapment
within BLN.
Fig. 3D compares the biological activity of fresh insulin with the
insulin released from BLN as determined using cells transfected with
insulin-responsive reporter genes. While there were slight decreases in
the insulin in three of the four insulin concentrations tested, the dif-
ferences were not signiﬁcant (p > 0.05, n = 3) considering both in-
dividual data points and overall result. The biological activity of insulin
was preserved after entrapment in BLN.
3.4. Dispersion stability of BLN in simulated GI environment
Table 3 compares the mean particle diameter and PDI values of
freshly prepared F15 to that after 4 h incubation at gastric pH (2.5) or
intestinal pH (6.8) with stirring. No signiﬁcant changes were observed
at both pHs.
3.5. Enzymatic stability of insulin
The ﬁndings of the enzymatic stability of insulin in BLN against
pepsin and chymotrypsin degradation were shown in Fig. 4A and B,
respectively. In general, it was found that the insulin carried by BLN
was less vulnerable to enzymatic degradation. For instance, after
30 min of pepsin treatment at pH 2.5, only 10% of free insulin remained
intact, while around 60% of BLN-entrapped insulin remained intact.
Similar protection was obtained against chymotrypsin degradation
where BLN could protect around 25% of entrapped insulin compared to
only 2% in case of free insulin after 30 min of incubation at pH 7.4.
3.6. Cellular uptake of BLN and cytotoxicity
Fig. 5A presents the MTT assay result of Caco-2 intestinal en-
dothelial cells treated with BLN and controls. Both BLN groups showed
low cytotoxicity on Caco-2 cells and could preserve up to 75% of its
viability at tested nanoparticles concentration of 10 mg/ml. The cyto-
toxicity of BLN was not signiﬁcantly higher than the conventional SLN,
suggesting that the inclusion of polymers does not make the nano-
particles more toxic. Moderate toxicity of BLN was observed only at
high concentration range (20 and 40 mg/ml) where cell viability de-
clined to around 60 and 55%, respectively. In clinical situation the
nanoparticles will be constantly diluted by the GI ﬂuid instead of ac-
cumulating directly on the GI epithelium surface in static manner for
3 h so the in vivo toxicity is expected to be substantially lower.
Cellular uptake of BLN by Caco-2 cells was investigated using
ﬂuorescence microscope. It was found that cells treated with ﬂuores-
cently labeled BLN (Fig. 5B, right, BLN labeled in red, nuclei in blue)
showed noticeably stronger ﬂuorescence than the untreated control
(Fig. 5B, left) under the same conditions. The images indicate sub-
stantial cellular interaction with and uptake of the newly developed
BLN.
3.7. In vivo glucose levels and relative pharmacological bioavailability
Fig. 6 presents the blood glucose proﬁles of animals treated with
free insulin (oral), free insulin (SC) and insulin loaded BLN (oral). As
expected, oral free insulin did not lower the blood glucose level.
Comparing oral BLN insulin with SC free insulin, the duration of hy-
poglycemic eﬀect of the former was longer (5 h vs 3 h), the peak hy-
poglycemic eﬀect was stronger (44.1% of baseline versus 61.5%). It
should be noted that rats dosed by oral gavage received 25 fold more
insulin than rats dosed by SC injection. With AAC calculated, this
translates into relative PA of 6.1%. All animals were visibly healthy
with no signiﬁcant body weight loss after BLN treatment in the in vivo
study.
4. Discussion
Oral route is generally favored over other routes of drug delivery
due to ease of administration, less costly production methods, and high
patient compliance [1]. However, limited success has so far been
achieved in the ﬁeld of oral delivery of protein/peptide drugs owing to
several reasons. Among them enzymatic degradation, high tendency to
aggregate in GI tract, and poor permeation across the GI endothelium
are particularly critical [37]. The goal of the present study is to develop
a new polymer-lipid hybrid nanodelivery approach to solve these is-
sues. Our ﬁndings suggest that BTN, a novel modiﬁed form of the hy-
brid nanocarrier, can successfully achieve this goal.
Although there is no consensus about what physicochemical prop-
erties are optimal for eﬃcient oral drug delivery by nanocarriers, it is
expected that the nanocarriers should at least maintain the size without
substantial signs of aggregation at the GI pHs. BLNs in general de-
monstrated size good for particle-cell interactions (around 200–300 nm
diameter) and fairly uniform size distribution as reﬂected by its PDI
value. The studies with F15 (Table 3) showed that these values re-
mained nearly unchanged at gastric and intestinal pHs (Table 3). At a
minimum, BLNs should be able to stay well-dispersed to allow favorable
interactions with the GI tract endothelium in the harsh GI environment.
A lipid-based nanocarrier inherently does not entrap protein drugs
eﬃciently even using the w/o/w double emulsion approach. This
prompted us to include polymeric materials to overcome this key lim-
itation. We hypothesized that the addition of PEG can increase the
viscosity of the internal aqueous phase of the w/o/w system, thus
making it diﬃcult for the protein payload to leak to the external

HPLC has been demonstrated as a well-validated technique that allows
simultaneous analysis of both human insulin and its main decomposi-
tion product, A21-desamido insulin, therefore providing indication
about insulin stability during the entrapment process [31,32]. Our re-
sult (Fig. 3) shows that the insulin of BLN remained chemically and
biologically stable. This is in agreement with a previous study in which
addition of PEG400 helps to preserve the stability of nerve growth
factor in PLGA particles [42], as the polymeric ingredient could dis-
place the protein drug from the aqueous/organic interface during pre-
paration to minimize its degradation as a result of exposure to the or-
ganic phase. Fig. 4 further shows that BLN can signiﬁcantly protect the
entrapped insulin from enzymatic degradation by pepsin and chymo-
trypsin. Overall, our data demonstrate the ability of BLN to protect the
loaded protein from both chemical and biological degradation.
SLN are well known for their low cytotoxicity, even less than PLGA
nanoparticles, and have been extensively evaluated in vivo without any
signs of causing intolerable toxicity [43,44]. Fig. 5A shows that the
cytotoxicities of BLN and SLN were comparable, indicating the low
cytotoxicity of SLN can be preserved despite the inclusion of polymeric
materials. It may be argued that modest cytotoxicity was observed at
high nanoparticle concentrations. Considering that nanoparticles made
of FDA approved polyesters (e.g. PLGA) caused 100% cell death at 0.5%
concentration [15], the observed toxicity of BLN is actually remarkably
low. The low cytotoxicity of BLN may be attributed to rational selection
of the polymers used where PEG are practically non-toxic with animal
LD50 values generally greater than 2000 mg/kg body weight [26]. In
addition, the concentration of PLGA used was kept at minimal to pre-
serve the biocompatibility of the newly developed carrier. It should be
noted that the low cytotoxicity was achieved even when there was good
nanoparticle uptake by Caco-2 cells (Fig. 5B). In short, BLN preserves
the two key beneﬁts of SLN, i.e. good cell-nanoparticle interaction and
low cytotoxicity. This is important for the achievement of eﬃcient oral
uptake without excessive irritation to the GI tract.
The hypoglycemic eﬀect of oral BLN was studied in rats and the PA
value was calculated (Fig. 6). We used PA in this study instead of ab-
solute bioavailability because the hypoglycemic eﬀect as compared to
the standard SC route of insulin administration is often the most critical
concern when developing an oral insulin formulation. With AAC cal-
culated, the relative PA of BLN was estimated to be 6.1%. This is ac-
tually comparable to many recent formulations well-customized for oral
insulin delivery. For examples, the highest PA values achieved by
lectin-modiﬁed SLN, selenium nanoparticles and self-assembled le-
cithin/chitosan nanoparticles were 6%, 9.15% and 6.01%, respectively
[34,45,46]. In comparison, the BLN tested in this study is only the
prototype without any surface modiﬁcations. At a minimum, this study
showed that the hypoglycemic activity of the insulin entrapped in BLN
is preserved, indicating good biological stability, and BLN has excellent
potential to deliver oral insulin in an eﬃcient manner.
5. Conclusion
In this study of the BLN prototype, we demonstrated that a typical
w/o/w SLN can incorporate two commonly used biocompatible poly-
mers, PEG and PLGA, to achieve substantially more eﬃcient entrap-
ment of insulin, while preserving the key advantages of SLN such as low
cytotoxicity, good interaction with cells, and ability to protect the
payload from chemical and biological degradation in the harsh GI en-
vironment. Early in vivo study showed that BLN has signiﬁcant hy-
poglycemic eﬀect when administered by oral route. In future, we will
explore the option of mucoadhesive modiﬁcation of BLN to achieve
even higher oral bioavailability. More in depth in vivo quantiﬁcation of
insulin will also be performed to learn more about the pharmacoki-
netics/biodistribution of the insulin-BLN.
