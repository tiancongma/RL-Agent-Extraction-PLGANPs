2.13. Mouse model of S. Aureus-induced pneumonia

Single colonies of S aureus were selected and inoculated into 10 mL
fresh MH broth medium overnight, and then cultured by oscillating at
37degC and 150 r/min for 12 h (1 x 109 CFU/mL), centrifugally collected
the bacteria, washed 3 times with PBS, and finally re-suspended in 5 ml
PBS (2 x 109 CFU/mL). Each mouse was inoculated intranasally with 20
mL bacterial suspension after anesthesia. After post-infection, the mice
were intraperitoneal injected with CEP, CCPN (5 and 10 mg/kg) and
dexamethasone. After 7 days of observation, lung tissues were harvested
to detect the organ index, bacterial load, pathological change and wet
weight ratio. Blood samples were harvested to detect the red blood cell
count (RBC), white blood cell count (WBC), and neutrophil percentage
(Gran).

2.9. Hemolytic analysis

The S. aureus ATCC29213 strain was cultured to 1 x 106 CFU/mL,
and CCPN was added to the bacterial solution with final concentrations
of 1/4MIC, 1/2MIC and MIC, respectively. Then the bacterial solution
was placed in a shaking table at 37degC culturing for 4 h. 100 mL bacterial
supernatant was added to sterile PBS containing 25 mL defibrillated
rabbit blood in a total system of 1 mL, and the system was incubated at
37degC for 15 min. The mixed system was centrifuged at 10,000 x g for 2
min, and the hemolytic activity of each sample was determined by
detecting the absorbance value of the supernatant at 543 nm. DMSO
treated samples and 1 % Triton-X 100 treated samples were used as
negative controls (0 % hemolysis) and positive controls (100 %
hemolysis).

2.14. White blood cell count and protein content in BALF

BALF was centrifuged at 600 r/min for 5 min, and the precipitated
cells were suspended in 2 mL PBS, and the cells were counted using a cell
counting plate. In addition, the supernatant was collected, and the
protein concentration was detected using a kit.

2.10. Membrane function assay

To investigate effects of CCPN on membrane permeability, a PI-probe
was used. Overnight cultures of S. aureus were exposed to varying
concentrations of CCPN for 4 h. Following three washes with PBS via
centrifugation, the bacteria were incubated with 5 mL of 1 mg/mL PI
solution at 37 *C for 15 min. After washing and resuspending the stained
bacterial cells, they were fixed between slides and coverslips. Fluores
cence was measured using a spectrofluorometer with excitation and
emission wavelengths of 535 nm and 617 nm, respectively. The fluo
rescence intensity was analyzed using ImageJ software.

3.5. CCPN alleviated symptoms caused by S.aureus

The protective effect of CCPN was further evaluated in a mouse
model of S.aureus-induced SAP. This bacterial pneumonia model was
established by intranasally inoculating mice with S. aureus and allowing
the infection to progress for one week. In this model, the lungs undergo
recruitment of inflammatory cells, production of inflammatory cyto
kines, and an increase in protein permeability, ultimately manifesting as
pulmonary edema, hemorrhage, and significant damage to the alveolar
structure. After seven days, the model group mice showed a significant
decrease in body weight (Fig. 6A) compared to the control group and
developed symptoms including decreased appetite, reduced activity,
and coughing. However, these symptoms were alleviated to varying
degrees in mice treated with free CEP, CCPN or DEX, and the weight
gradually returned to the normal level. The wet weight ratio of lung
tissue (Fig. 6B) and calculation of organ index (Fig. 6C-G) also showed
that S. aureus could cause damages such as pulmonary edema in mice.
The histopathological examination of the lungs also revealed significant
destruction of the alveolar structure in the model group, accompanied
by extensive neutrophil infiltration. After treatment with CCPN, the
alveolar structure was preserved (Fig. 6H), with minimal inflammatory
cell infiltration, suggesting that CCPN exerts a significant therapeutic
effect on SAP.

## Discussion
In this study, we developed a drug delivery system by encapsulating
CEP within PLGA-chitosan nanoparticles, forming CCPN, and evaluated
its antibacterial effects against SAP. In vitro, CCPN inhibited bacterial
cell membrane formation and biofilm development. In vivo, CCPN
reduced the pathological changes of lung tissue in a mouse model of SAP
and down-regulated the levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines. These
findings indicate that CCPN has the potential to be a valuable thera
peutic option for treating SAP.

and anti-inflammation properties. CEP, as an effective agent against
pulmonary inflammation, has garnered substantial attention. However,
its low bioavailability and poor solubility have hindered its clinical
