Folate-Decorated PLGA Nanoparticles as A Rationally Designed Vehicle for The Oral Delivery of Insulin

Abstract
Aims: The present study reports a novel approach for enhancing the oral absorption and hypoglycemic activity of insulin via encapsulation in folate-(FA) coupled polyethylene glycol (PEG)ylated polylactide- co- glycolide (PLGA) nanoparticles (NPs; FA-PEG-PLGA NPs). Materials & methods: Insulin-loaded FA-PEG-PLGA NPs (size ∼260 nm; insulin loading ∼6.5% [w/w]; encapsulation efficiency: 87.0 ± 1.92%) were prepared by double-emulsion solvent evaporation method. The bioavailability and hypoglycemic activity of orally administered FA–insulin NPs were studied in diabetic rats. Results & conclusion: FA-PEG-PLGA NPs (50 U/kg) exhibited a twofold increase in the oral bioavailability (double hypoglycemia) without any hypoglycemic shock as compared to subcutaneously administered standard insulin solution. Insulin NPs maintained a continual blood glucose level for 24 h, which, however, was transient (<8 h) in the case of subcutaneous insulin and associated with severe hypoglycemic shock. Overall, we have developed a patient-compliant, oral nanoformulation of insulin, once-daily administration of which would be sufficient to control diabetes for at least 24 h.

Diabetes mellitus represents a group of syndromes, typified by hyperglycemia, resulting from impairment of insulin secretion, defects in insulin action or both. Insulin is a protein hormone, which plays a key role in regulating the carbohydrate and fat metabolism in our body. As insulin deficiency is a major cause of diabetes mellitus, exogenous insulin is used medically to treat patients with certain forms of this disease. The current regimen of insulin therapy involves multiple, daily subcutaneous (sc.) injections, which not only causes uncontrolled hypoglycemia but also a threatening punishment to diabetic patients with poor compliance. Moreover, the peripheral hyperinsulinemia caused by subcutaneously administered insulin, further aggravates patients’ conditions in the form of certain renal and cardiovascular complications Citation [1] . In order to circumvent the problems and side effects associated with sc. insulin, several alternative delivery routes such as pulmonary, nasal, transdermal, buccal and oral routes have been investigated. However, none of these delivery routes are free from limitations. For instance, the pulmonary route of delivery is challenged with problems related to poor bioavailability (BA), high dose and lower cost–effectiveness Citation [2,3] . Similarly, transdermally delivered insulin fails to transfer an adequate concentration of the drug across the skin barrier Citation [1] .
The oral route is considered to be the most convenient and patient-compliant route of drug administration Citation [4] . Oral delivery stands to be an attractive approach for the mitigation of pain caused by sc. administration of insulin and thereby, offers an effective means of circumventing many of the problems associated with conventional insulin therapy. Oral delivery of insulin can radically improve the glucose homeostasis by mimicking the physiological pattern of insulin release, which involves initial passage and utilization of 50% of insulin through liver (a major organ involved in glucose homeostasis), followed by peripheral action Citation [5–7] . However, the oral delivery of insulin seems to be beyond reach because of its high propensity to undergo chemoenzymatic degradation under the hostile environment of the GI tract and poor intestinal permeability Citation [1,2,7] . An array of nontargeted novel nanocarrier-based approaches have been investigated for the oral delivery of insulin so as to evade the enzymatic and permeability barriers of the GI tract. These include, and are not limited to, liposomes Citation [8,9] , polymeric nanoparticles (NPs) Citation [7,10–16] and solid lipid NPs Citation [17] . It is well documented that polylactide- co -glycolide (PLGA) is a biocompatible, biodegradable, US FDA approved and safely administered polymer employed for in vivo applications. The PLGA matrix protects the loaded bioactive from premature degradation in the GI tract and thereby facilitates its absorption via the specialized M cells of Peyer‘s patches. In some earlier reports from our group, we have already shown that PLGA-based nanosystems serve as effective delivery vehicles for a broad spectrum of bioactives including anticancer drugs and antioxidants Citation [18] . Attempts have also been made to deliver insulin orally using PLGA NPs Citation [19] . However, in the majority of cases the relative BA (BA R ) of the oral formulation is significantly lowered (3–6%) to the available marketed formulation of sc. insulin injection Citation [19,20] .
Ligand-mediated active targeting has emerged as a novel armamentarium for improving the site specificity of many therapeutic agents to their pathological site. As a targeting moiety, folic acid (FA) has shown immense promise in the tumor-specific delivery of a variety of anticancer agents Citation [21] . However, the strategy has seldom been explored for the oral delivery of bioactives. As for the oral delivery of insulin, it would be particularly useful if the protein could be encapsulated within a biocompatible nanosystem, surface modified with ligands, whose corresponding receptors are expressed on the GI tract. Such ligand-decorated nanocarriers were thought to improve the oral absorption of insulin through some specialized, receptor-mediated endocytic pathway. A similar attempt was made by Chalasani and coworkers, wherein vitamin B12-conjugated dextran NPs were used for enhancing the oral absorption of insulin via a cobalamin (vitamin B12)-mediated pathway in the rat intestine Citation [22,23] . Furthermore, some recent reports have embarked on the feasibility of using folate (FA) conjugates for improved uptake and transport of certain bioactives or vesicular systems across the GI tract Citation [24,25] . According to these reports, reckonable levels of FA receptors are also expressed by the GI tract so that intestinal absorption of FA is mediated through a specialized, acidic pH-dependent carrier-mediated process, which involves the reduced FA carrier as well as proton-coupled FA transporter/HCP1. Encouraged by these reports, we sought to explore the feasibility of using FA-decorated PEGylated PLGA NPs for enhancing the oral absorption of insulin, while avoiding premature degradation of the protein under the hostile GI tract environment Citation [26] . In line with that rationale, insulin-loaded FA-conjugated PEG-PLGA NPs (FA–insulin NPs) were prepared via double-emulsion, solvent evaporation method and freeze-dried. In addition to extensive physicochemical characterizations, the synthesized NPs were also analyzed for their chemical and conformational stability, as well as long-term storage stability. Finally, in the course of extensive pharmacodynamic (PD) and pharmacokinetic (PK) studies in diabetic rats, we demonstrated the successful oral delivery of insulin through folic acid conjugated nanoparticles. A meaningful PK–PD correlation was also established.
Materials & methods
Materials
PLGA 50:50 (inherent viscosity 0.4 dl/g in chloroform at 25°C) was procured from Boehringer (Ingelheim, Germany). FA was purchased from Sisco Research Lab. Pvt. Ltd., (Mumbai, India). Bovine pancreas insulin, N -hydroxysuccinimide, PEG– bis -amine (molecular weight: 3000 Da), polyvinyl alcohol (molecular weight: 30,000–70,000 Da), sodium azide, streptozotocine, trehalose and triethyl amine were purchased from Sigma Aldrich (MO, USA). Trifluoroacetic acid, pancreatin and pepsin were obtained from Loba Chemie (Mumbai, India). Ethyl acetate (lab research grade), acetonitrile (HPLC grade) and methanol (HPLC grade) were purchased from JT Baker, (NJ, USA). Ultrapure water (SG water purification system, Barsbuttel, Germany) was used for all the experiments. All other reagents used were of analytical grade.
Synthesis of polymers & their characterization
Amine-terminated PEGylated PLGA (NH 2 -PEG-PLGA) and FA-conjugated PEGylated PLGA were synthesized using carbodiimide chemistry as previously described in the literature with necessary modifications Citation [21,27–30] . Figure 1 is a schematic representation of the different steps involved in the synthesis of polymers.
Synthesis of the amine-terminated diblock copolymer
For the preparation of the amine-terminated diblock copolymer (NH 2 -PEG-PLGA), PLGA was activated using standard carbodiimide chemistry, as described in earlier reports Citation [21,27–30] . Thereafter, activated PLGA (1 g, 0.0294 mmol), dissolved in anhydrous dichloromethane (DCM; 8 ml) was added dropwise to five-times molar excess of PEG– bis -amine dissolved in 2 ml of anhydrous DCM. A catalytic amount (100 µl) of anhydrous triethylamine was added to the mixture with gentle stirring. The reaction was carried out at room temperature (RT) for 8 h under N 2 atmosphere. The reaction was quenched by adding ice cold diethyl ether to the reaction mixture. The resultant precipitated product, NH 2 -PEG-PLGA was repeatedly washed with methanol to remove unreacted PEG– bis -amine and finally dried under vacuum.
Synthesis of FA-coupled diblock copolymer
The FA-conjugated diblock copolymer (FA-PEG-PLGA) was synthesized by coupling activated FA with the free amine terminal of previously synthesized NH 2 -PEG-PLGA diblock copolymer. Briefly, 500 mg diblock copolymer dissolved in 5 ml anhydrous dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) was mixed with FA (13 mg, 0.029 mmol), N , N ´-dicyclohexylcarbodiimide, (8.96 mg, 0.0435 mmol) and 100 µl anhydrous triethylamine. The reaction was carried out at RT for 8 h under N 2 atmosphere. Finally, the reaction mixture was filtered and the filtrate was added dropwise into ice cold diethyl ether to obtain a precipitate of FA-PEG-PLGA, the precipitate was separated by filtration, repeatedly washed with methanol to remove DMSO and finally dried under vacuum. The unconjugated FA was removed by dissolving conjugated diblock copolymer in DCM; FA being insoluble in DCM was removed by filtration.
Characterization of synthesized polymers
The conjugation of FA on PLGA was preliminarily studied using UV and further authenticated via Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) and nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy.
UV analysis
The extent of FA conjugation (percentage molar conjugation) on NH 2 -PEG-PLGA was evaluated by UV spectroscopic analysis (BioTek Instruments, VT, USA). A standard calibration curve was prepared by recording the absorbance of serially diluted standard FA samples in DMSO at 358 nm (FA ε = 8643.5 M −1 cm −1 ). The amount of FA conjugated in a polymer was determined by recording the absorbance of PLGA-PEG-FA at 358 nm.
FTIR analysis
For FTIR analysis, PLGA, NH 2 -PEG-PLGA and FA-PEG-PLGA were dissolved in DCM. Subsequently, neat samples were analyzed upon evaporation of DCM by FTIR (Nicolet Impact 410, Perkin Elmer Instrument, MA, USA).
NMR analysis
For NMR analysis, PLGA, NH 2 -PEG-PLGA and FA-PEG-PLGA were dissolved in deuterated DMSO. 1 H-NMR analyses were carried out in a Bruker DRX 400 MHz NMR spectrometer (Billerica, MA, USA).
Preparation of insulin-loaded PLGA NPs
Insulin-loaded PLGA NPs were prepared by the previously reported double emulsion solvent evaporation method, with slight modification Citation [31] . Briefly, the stock solution (8 mg/ml) of insulin was prepared by dissolving in 0.01 M HCl containing 0.1% weight/volume (w/v) sodium azide. Insulin solution (0.5 ml; according to 5, 7.5 or 10% weight/weight [w/w] theoretical loading) was added dropwise to PLGA solution in ethyl acetate (5 ml) over a period of 2 min under magnetic stirring at 1200 rpm. The mixture was then homogenized (PT 4000, Polytron, Lucerne, Switzerland) at 10,000 rpm for 2 min to obtain the primary emulsion which was then added dropwise to 2% (w/w) polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) solution (10 ml) over a period of 10 min under continuous stirring followed by homogenization at 12,500 rpm for 5 min to obtain a double emulsion. . The entire process was carried out in an ice bath. The secondary emulsion was poured into 30 ml water and kept under magnetic stirring at RT until complete evaporation of ethyl acetate. Finally, NPs were separated and washed by centrifugation at 12,500 rpm for 10 min. The supernatant obtained was analyzed by a validated reverse phase (RP)-HPLC method Citation [32,33] to determine the amount of unentrapped insulin and the pellet was redispersed in water and centrifuged again. The same washing cycle was repeated twice.
Optimization of process variables
Effect of homogenization speed
Selection of an optimal homogenization speed was necessary to achieve optimum particle size, polydispersity index (PDI) and encapsulation efficiency. PLGA NPs were prepared at different homogenization speeds (10,000, 12,500 and 15,000 rpm), keeping the other experimental parameters, such as theoretical loading 5%, homogenization time 5 min and 2% PVA concentration, constant. Particle size and the PDI of PLGA NP dispersion was measured using ζ-sizer (Nano ZS, Malvern Instruments, Worcestershire, UK).
Effect of homogenization time
For optimization of homogenization time, PLGA NPs were prepared at previously optimized homogenization speed (i.e., 12,500 rpm for 4, 5 and 6 min) and its effect on particle size, PDI and encapsulation efficiency was studied.
Effect of theoretical drug loading
Finally, PLGA NPs were prepared at different insulin loading (i.e., 5, 7.5 or 10% w/w of polymer) and its effect on particle size and encapsulation efficiency was studied. The other experimental parameters such as homogenization speed (12,500 rpm), homogenization time (5 min) and stabilizer concentration (2% PVA) were kept constant.
Preparation of insulin-loaded NH 2 -PEG-PLGA & FA-PEG-PLGA NPs
Insulin-loaded NH 2 -PEG-PLGA and FA-PEG-PLGA NPs (insulin NPs) were prepared using the same protocol and with the same optimized parameters as described above for the preparation of insulin-loaded PLGA NPs.
Characterization of polymeric NPs
Particle size & ζ-potential measurement
All polymeric NP (insulin NPs) preparations were evaluated for their mean particle size and PDI using ζ-sizer by taking the average of six measurements. The ζ-potential of prepared NPs was determined on the basis of their electrophoretic mobility under an electric field using ζ-sizer by taking the average of 30 measurements.
Encapsulation efficiency & percentage of insulin loading
Encapsulation efficiency and percentage of practical insulin loading were determined by indirect method Citation [13] using the formulae described in Equation Equations 1 & 2 in Box 1 .
Briefly, NPs were centrifuged at 20,000 rpm for 10 min and the concentration of insulin in the supernatant was estimated by a validated HPLC method using a Shimadzu HPLC system (Kyoto, Japan) with SPD-M20A PDA detector and Princeton SPHER-300 C 18 (4.6 x 250 mm; NJ, USA), 5 μm column. Acetonitrile, water containing 0.1% trifluoro acetic acid (30:70 v/v) were used as the mobile phase with a flow rate of 1 ml/min. The injection volume was 30 μl and retention time of insulin was found to be 7.9 ± 0.05 min. The detection wavelength (λ max ) for insulin was 214 nm.
Freeze drying studies
All insulin NPs were freeze-dried using previously developed and patented stepwise freeze drying process Citation [101] . Briefly, 2 ml of the NP suspension was filled into glass vials. Trehalose was used as a cryoprotectant at different concentration (2.5, 5, 7.5 and 10% w/v) and its concentration was optimized in terms of size, PDI, reconstitution time and S f :S i ratio. The freeze-dried samples were resuspended in water and evaluated for size and PDI.
In vitro release studies
All nanoformulations were characterized for their in vitro release profile under different pH conditions that prevail in different body fluids (pH 1.2 in stomach, pH 6.8 in intestine and pH 7.4 in blood) Citation [34] . Briefly, 20 mg of freeze-dried NP formulations were dispersed in 10 ml of acidic media (pH 1.2; USP XXVII) and incubated at 37 ± 1°C for 2 h under in a shaker water bath operated at 100 rpm. At the predetermined time intervals, 0.5 ml of sample was withdrawn and NPs were pelletized by centrifugation at 12,500 rpm for 10 min. The pellet was dispersed in 0.5 ml fresh medium and added back in the release medium. The clear supernatant was employed for determination of insulin concentration. After 2 h the NPs were transferred to a 20 ml phosphate buffer of pH 6.8 and incubated at 37 ± 1°C for a further 4 h under continuous shaking at 100 rpm. Samples were collected at regular intervals and replaced by fresh media, as described above. Finally, after 4 h, NPs were transferred to phosphate buffer pH 7.4 and periodic sampling was carried for the next 18 h. All the collected samples were analyzed by the validated RP-HPLC method to determine the amount of insulin released.
Stability studies
In-process stability
Chemical stability
The chemical stability of insulin during the preparation of different NP formulations was determined by RP-HPLC analysis. Briefly, freeze-dried NPs (10 mg) were dispersed in 5 ml phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) and incubated for 24 h at RT. After 24 h, the NP suspension was centrifuged and different samples were subjected to the RP-HPLC analysis. The HPLC chromatograms were analyzed for the presence of any residual peak (monodesamido A21 or B3 insulin) other than the parent insulin peak Citation [33,35] . Subsequently, peak areas of the residual peaks were compared with that of parent peaks. Standard insulin solution was used as a control.
Native polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (PAGE) was also employed to evaluate the chemical stability of the entrapped insulin in the different polymeric NPs. Briefly, equal amounts of standard insulin (control) and insulin extracted/released from NPs were diluted with 2× native gel sample buffer and loaded into individual wells of 4–20% polyacrylamide gel. Samples were electrophoresed using a miniVE electrophoresis unit (GE Healthcare Bio-Sciences Ltd., Uppsala, Sweden) under conditions of constant voltage. The protein bands in the gel were visualized by staining with Coomasie blue dye. Protein standard in the molecular weight range of 4 kD (MultiMark ® Multi-Colored Standard, Invitrogen, CA, USA) was used to estimate molecular weight.
Conformational stability
The conformational integrity of insulin encapsulated in various nanoformulations was determined using far UV circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy (J-815, JASCO, Tokyo, Japan). Briefly, standard insulin solution and insulin extracted/released from different NPs were subjected to CD analysis in the far UV region of 260–190 nm. Data pitch and scanning speed were kept as 0.5 and 50 nm/min, respectively. The average of three accumulations for each sample was taken and baseline correction with blank phosphate buffer at pH 7.4 was carried out to obtain CD spectrum (observed ellipticity CD vs wavelength), which were subsequently converted to the molecular ellipticity using the software.
Storage stability of freeze-dried formulations
Freeze-dried NPs were assessed for their stability at 4 ° C and at RT over a period of 3 months according to the protocols reported in the literature Citation [36] . Briefly, insulin-loaded freeze-dried NPs were transferred to 5 ml glass vials, sealed with plastic caps and were kept in a stability chamber at 4 ° C and RT (25 ± 2°C, relative humidity 50 ± 5%). The different formulations were monitored for change in particle size, PDI and physical appearance.
Stability in simulated gastric fluid & simulated intestinal fluid
The stability of different insulin NPs was measured in simulated gastric fluid (SGF) and simulated intestinal fluid (SIF) was determined according to the protocols reported in the literature Citation [4] . Briefly, freeze-dried insulin-loaded NP formulations and standard insulin solution were incubated with SGF (pH 1.2) USP XXVIII (with enzymes) and SIF (pH 6.8) USP XXVIII (with enzymes). After 2 h, pepsin in SGF was inactivated by adding 0.5 M NaOH while pancreatin in SIF was inactivated by adding 0.5 M HCl. Nanoparticle dispersions were then centrifuged and the supernatant was analyzed for insulin content by RP-HPLC, as described earlier. The insulin was also extracted from the pellet by dissolving the pellet in 1 ml acetonitrile:0.5 M HCl (9:1 v/v) mixture followed by extracting insulin into the 2 ml 0.5 M HCl. Subsequently, the mixture was centrifuged to remove precipitated PLGA and insulin concentration in the supernatant was analyzed by the RP-HPLC method. The amount of insulin recovered (percentage) upon treatment with SGF and SIF was obtained by comparing these with the insulin recovered from the NPs untreated with SGF and SIF.
In vivo studies
Animals
Adult male Sprague–Dawley rats (250 ± 30 g) procured from the central animal facility of the National Institute of Pharmaceutical Education & Research (Punjab, India), were used for PD and PK evaluation of insulin NPs. Animals were housed in plastic cages under standard laboratory conditions with 12 h light/12 h dark cycle and free access to standard rodent dry diet and water ad libitum. All experimental protocols were duly approved by Institutional Animal Ethics Committee (IAEC) of the National Institute of Pharmaceutical Education & Research (Approval no. IAEC/09/63). All the experiments were performed in accordance with guidelines of the Committee for the Purpose of Control and Supervision of Experiments on Animals, India.
Induction of diabetes
Diabetes was induced in animals by intraperitoneal administration of streptozotocin (55 mg/kg) dissolved in ice cold 10 mmol citrate buffer at pH 4.4 (for preserving its conformational integrity) just before administration. Subsequently, animals were housed for 1 week under standard laboratory conditions. Glucose levels in the animals were estimated and only the animals with blood glucose levels above 250 mg/dl were considered as diabetic and used in this study Citation [37] .
Antidiabetic activity
Diabetic animals fasted for 12 h before and 24 h during the experiment; but were allowed water ad libitum and fed thereafter. Animals were divided inro eight different groups. Insulin-loaded plain PLGA, NH 2 -PEG-PLGA, OCH 3 -PEG-PLGA and FA-PEG-PLGA NPs were orally administered to the first four groups of animals in doses equivalent to 50 IU/kg of insulin The fifth group was orally administered with standard insulin solution (50 IU/kg). Untreated diabetic animals and animals with sc. administration (group 6 and 7) of standard insulin solution (5 IU/kg) were also used as controls. To further verify whether oral absorption of FA–insulin NPs followed a receptor-mediated pathway, FA-PEG-PLGA NPs were orally administered in animals, pretreated with excess of free FA. Results
Synthesis & characterization of polymers
The FA-conjugated and nonconjugated diblock copolymers (NH 2 -PEG-PLGA and FA-PEG-PLGA, respectively) were synthesized from activated PLGA according to the steps schematized in Figure 1 . The synthesized polymers were characterized by UV, FTIR and 1 H-NMR analysis.
Characterization of polymers by UV spectroscopy
Figure 2 presents the UV spectral pattern of free FA, NH 2 -PEG-PLGA and FA-PEG-PLGA. FA and FA-PEG-PLGA exhibited characteristic peaks of FA at λ max 363 and 357 nm, respectively. These peaks, however, were not present in the UV spectra of NH 2 -PEG-PLGA. UV spectral analysis revealed that FA constituted 38 ± 2 mol% of the total polymer.
Characterization of polymers by FTIR
The comparative FTIR spectra of PLGA, NH 2 -PEG-PLGA, FA-PEG-PLGA, NH 2 -PEG-NH 2 , physical mixture of PLGA and PEG– bis -amine have been presented in Figure 3 . The FTIR spectra of pristine PLGA revealed a prominent peak at 1756 cm -1 attributed to the carbonyl (-C=O) stretching frequency of the carboxylic acid group of PLGA (Figure 3A) . Following PEGylation, a significant reduction in the intensity of 1753 cm -1 band was observed. Concomitantly, new peaks appeared at 1664 and 1574 cm -1 , respectively, attributed to amide I (-CO stretching vibration) and amide II (-NH stretch) bands of NH 2 -PEG-PLGA (Figure 3B) . The final conjugate FA-PEG-PLGA showed a prominent peak at 1756 cm -1 (α-COOH group of FA) and somewhat broad and intensified peaks at 1644 cm-1 (-NH stretch), 1577 cm -1 (-NH bending). This could be assigned to the overlapping of amide linkages between FA (γ-carboxyl) and PEG-PLGA-NH 2 ; PLGA and PEG- bis -amine; and amide linkages native to the structure of FA (Figure 3C) .
Characterization of polymers by 1 H-NMR
The 1 H-NMR spectrum of PLGA shows characteristic proton peaks at δ 1.4, 5.2 and 4.8 ppm ( Supplementary Figure 1 ). Following PEGylation, an additional peak at δ 3.5 ppm (characteristic of -O-CH 2 -CH 2 - unit of PEG) appeared in the 1 H-NMR spectrum of NH 2 -PEG-PLGA (Figure 4A) . The 1 H-NMR spectrum of FA-PEG-PLGA not only presented characteristic peaks of PLGA and PEG at δ 1.47, 5.19, 4.90 and 3.17 ppm (Figure 4B) but characteristic signals of FA appeared at δ 8.6, 7.8 and 6.9 ppm, respectively. The peak at δ 8.6 ppm could be attributed to the pteridine ring proton of FA while the peaks at δ 7.8 and 6.9 ppm represented the aromatic protons of the p-amino benzoic acid unit of FA. These signals, however, were absent in the NMR spectrum of NH 2 -PEG-PLGA, confirming the presence of FA in PEGylated PLGA.
Preparation & optimization of insulin-loaded PLGA NPs
Insulin-loaded polymeric NPs were prepared by the double emulsion solvent evaporation method. The method was first optimized for the preparation of insulin-loaded plain PLGA NPs and subsequently, the same optimized parameters were applied to the optimized synthesis of other nanoformulations. The different process variables such as homogenization time, homogenization speed and percenatge of theoretical drug loading were optimized so as to produce PLGA NPs with desired particle size (<300 nm), size distribution (PDI below 0.2), encapsulation efficiency (>70%) and maximum possible practical loading.
Optimization of homogenization speed
Insulin NPs were prepared at different homogenization speed viz. 10,000, 12,500, 15,000 rpm at 5% (w/w) insulin loading and with 2% (w/v) PVA. The effect of homogenization speed on particle size, PDI and encapsulation efficiency is given in Table 1 . As the homogenization speed was increased from 10,000 to 12,500 rpm, a marked reduction in particle size was observed. However, changes in encapsulation efficiency were insignificant (p > 0.05). At 15,000 rpm, the reduction in PDI was insignificant (p > 0.05), albeit a slight increase in particle size was observed. Therefore, 12,500 rpm was the selected optimum homogenization speed for further optimization process.
Optimization of homogenization period
Table 2 shows the effect of the homogenization period on particle size, PDI and encapsulation efficiency of insulin-loaded PLGA NPs. As the homogenization period varied from 4 to 5 min, particle size was decreased with reduction in the encapsulation efficiency. An increase in particle size while a decrease in encapsulation efficiency was observed when homogenization was carried out for 6 min. Therefore, homogenization for 5 min at 12,500 rpm was selected for further optimization.
Optimization of theoretical drug loading
Insulin-loaded PLGA NPs were prepared at different insulin loading (i.e., 5, 7.5 and 10% w/w of the polymer) to determine the optimum percentage of insulin which can be successfully encapsulated in the polymer matrix. It is clear from Table 3 that insignificant changes (p > 0.05) in the particle size and encapsulation efficiency were observed when theoretical drug was changed from 5 to 7.5% (w/w). At higher theoretical loading (10% w/w), a significant reduction (p < 0.05) in the encapsulation efficiency of insulin was observed whereas the particle size of the NPs was decreased insignificantly (p > 0.05). Therefore, 7.5% (w/w) was selected as optimized theoretical loading.
Preparation of insulin-loaded NH 2 -PEG-PLGA & FA-PEG-PLGA NPs
Insulin-loaded NH 2 -PEG-PLGA and FA-PEG-PLGA NPs were also prepared using the 2% (w/v) PVA as a stabilizer with previously optimized process parameters such as homogenization speed 12,500 rpm, homogenization time 5 min and percentage of theoretical insulin loading 7.5% w/w.
Characterization of insulin-loaded NP formulations
Table 4 depicts the particle size, ζ-potential and encapsulation efficiency of the various insulin-based nanoformulations. All nanoformulations, irrespective of their specific surface chemistry showed a particle size of <300 nm, uniform PDI (<0.2) and variable ζ-potential ranging from -3 to -16 mV (Table 4) . In all cases, encapsulation efficiency was greater than 80% w/w with practical insulin loading of approximately 7% (w/w). Figure 5 shows the SEM images of insulin-loaded PLGA, NH 2 -PEG-PLGA and FA-PEG-PLGA NPs. As is evident from SEM images, the majority of particles in the field of view are spherical in shape, with size ranging from 230 to 270 nm. However, deformation in spherical shape was observed with a few particles, which may be a consequence of partial melting of the polymer due to the heat produced by the high energy electron beam. In this context, it may be noted that particle sizes observed from SEM correlated well with those measured by the ζ-sizer.
Freeze drying studies
All insulin NPs were freeze-dried using an optimized stepwise freeze drying cycle previously developed by our group Citation [101] . Trehalose was employed as the cryoprotectant. Variable concentrations (2.5, 5, 7.5 and 10.0% w/v) of trehalose were employed for the freeze drying of NPs. After freeze drying the obtained cake was redispersed in distilled water (2 ml) and particle size and PDI of the resultant dispersion was analyzed using Zetasizer. The different properties of freeze-dried NPs such as physical appearance, reconstitution behavior and size ratio (before and after freeze drying; S f :S i ) are given in Table 5 . An insignificant change (p > 0.05) in the particle size and PDI were observed, when the NPs were freeze-dried in the presence of 5% (w/v) trehalose. In all cases, the S f :S i ratio was close to unity (∼1.005). It is further clear from Figure 6 that all the nanoformulations, irrespective of their specific composition, produced intact ‘fluffy cake’ after freeze drying with 5% (w/v) trehalose. These cakes were easily redispersible in water on manual shaking (upside down for 20 s), suggesting that freeze drying in the presence of trehalose (5% w/w) has no detrimental effect on particle characteristics of the prepared nanoformulations. Of note, NPs freeze-dried in the absence of cryoprotectants showed a significant change in particle size and PD.
In vitro release study
As already stated, insulin is highly prone to degradation in the presence of gastric and intestinal enzymes. Hence, for a complete understanding of the in vitro release profile of insulin from NPs, avoiding enzymatic intervention was one of the essential prerequisites. To mimic the ideal physiological situation, insulin release from NPs over different time points was determined at various pH conditions (gastric pH 1.2 from 0–2 h, intestinal pH 6.8 from 2–6 h and plasma pH 7.4 from 6–24 h); however, keeping this in mind, the intrinsic proclivity of insulin towards chemoenzymatic degradation, enzyme-free release media was used. As evident from Figure 7 , all three nanoformulations (i.e., plain PLGA NPs, NH 2 -PEG-PLGA NPs and FA-PEG-PLGA NPs), presented a similar type of release profile, that is, an initial burst release, followed by a sustained release pattern. Quantification of drug release showed that plain PLGA, PEGylated PLGA and FA-PEG-PLGA NPs presented an initial burst release of 43 ± 2.65%, 37 ± 2.86% and 40 ± 2.46%, respectively, followed by a cumulative sustained release of 60 ± 2.85%, 52 ± 3.51% and 56 ± 3.17%, respectively at the end of 24 h. After 24 h, surface-modified NPs (NH 2 -PEG-PLGA and FA-PEG-PLGA NPs) showed less insulin release compared with the insulin-loaded PLGA NPs. The cumulative drug release was fitted into different release models namely zero order, first order, Higuchi‘s square root plot and Hixson Crowell cube root plot and the model giving a correlation coefficient close to unity was considered as order of release. As illustrated in Table 6 , after initial burst release, NPs followed Higuchi‘s square root pattern of release which involved diffusion alone during the initial phase, followed by combined diffusion and degradation in the later phases. To further check the accuracy of the drug release data, the release profile was cross-checked using the dialysis method. Not surprisingly, the results were comparable with those obtained via centrifugation method.
Stability studies
Chemical stability of insulin by RP-HPLC
Chemical stability of insulin was determined by the RP-HPLC method. The HPLC chromatograms of standard insulin (Figure 8A) and insulin extracted from various NP formulations have been presented in Figure 8B–D . The chromatogram of standard insulin solution demonstrates the appearance of a parent insulin peak at 7.9 ± 0.05 min and a residual peak at 9.1 ± 0.06 min. The percentage degradation of insulin was determined by comparing respective peak areas of residual and parent peak from the standard insulin and insulin extracted from different NPs formulations. Table 7 shows that all nanoformulations presented a similar degradation profile with insignificant difference (p > 0.05) as compared with the standard insulin solution (3.16 ± 0.33%).
Chemical stability of insulin by native PAGE
Chemical stability of insulin was also determined by native PAGE analysis. As evident from Figure 9 , the gel pattern clearly indicated the same level of bands in standard insulin and also insulin extracted from the NPs. Moreover, no additional band was envisaged in any of the insulin samples suggesting that no chemical degradation of insulin occurs during its encapsulation within NPs.
Conformational stability of insulin
Circular dichromism (CD) was employed to determine the conformational stability of insulin in various NPs formulations. Figure 10 shows the overlay of the CD spectrum of standard insulin and the insulin obtained from the different NP formulations. It is clear from Figure 10 that the CD spectra of the test samples superimposed with the CD spectrum of standard insulin solution and identical spectral pattern was observed in all cases. The ratio of the molecular ellipticity was calculated at 222 nm and 208 nm ([θ] 222 /[θ] 208 ). No significant difference was observed when standard insulin solution was compared with insulin extracted from NPs (p > 0.05).
Storage stability of insulin
The long-term storage stability of freeze-dried insulin NPs was studied as described previously. An insignificant change (p > 0.05) in particle size and PDI was observed when insulin-loaded NPs were stored at RT and 4°C for 3 months (Table 8) .
Stability in SGF & SIF
Table 9 presents the amount of insulin recovered when insulin NPs were incubated with SGF and SIF. As demonstrated in Table 9 , 89.47 ± 1.64% and 90.04 ± 0.97% of free standard insulin was degraded in SGF and SIF, respectively. By contrast, degradation of insulin formulated in NPs was considerably low. An aggregation of NH 2 -PEG-PLGA NPs in SGF and SIF was observed, whereas pristine PLGA and FA-PEG-PLGA NPs remained redispersed.
PD studies of NPs after oral administration
All insulin NPs were evaluated for their oral hypoglycemic activity as a function of time. As evident from Figure 11 , free insulin solution showed little or practically no activity upon oral administration. No hypoglycemia was observed in the case of the diabetic control as well as rats orally administered with standard insulin solution; rather, a rapid increase in basal glucose value was observed due to physical stress caused during blood collection. Of note, orally administered PLGA NPs revealed significant hypoglycemia wherein a 12 and 35% reduction in blood glucose level was observed after 6 and 18 h post-treatment, respectively. Compared with plain PLGA NPs, NH 2 -PEG-PLGA NPs exhibited a lesser hypoglycemic response. In the latter, only an 18% fall in glucose level was observed after 18 h (p < 0.05). In line with our hypothesis, FA-PEG-PLGA NPs exhibited the highest hypoglycemic response among all the treatment groups. The basal blood glucose level was decreased by 14% after 4 h (p < 0.05). Strikingly, a 57% decrease in the blood glucose level was observed, even after 18 h post-treatment (p < 0.001). Single oral administration of insulin-loaded FA-PEG-PLGA NPs maintained the blood glucose level for at least 24 h and the basal value was achieved after 36 h. Cumulative hypoglycemic effect was calculated as percentage reduction in the AUC of the diabetic control group using previously described formula. While little or practically no change in hypoglycemic activity with respect to diabetic control was observed upon oral administration of standard insulin, FA–insulin NPs exhibited 1.8-times (almost double) cumulative hypoglycemia (29.06 ± 1.69%) compared with the subcutaneously administered free insulin solution (cumulative hypoglycemia of 16.29 ± 2.16%) (Table 10) . Pharmacological availability of insulin upon oral administration of NPs was determined by comparing AAC after oral administration of NPs with AAC upon sc. administration of insulin solution with dose correction. Among all nanoformulations, FA–insulin NPs exhibited the highest BA R of 19.62 ± 1.68 (Table 11) .
PK studies of NPs after oral administration
Figure 12 shows the PK profile of sc. administered free insulin solution and orally administered insulin NPs. Subcutaneously administered insulin solution attained a maximum concentration of 46.5 µIU/ml in 1 h followed by a continuous decrease to basal value after 8 h. By contrast, orally administered FA-PEG-PLGA NPs maintained constancy in the blood insulin levels over a period of 24 h. BA R of insulin upon oral administration of insulin NPs was calculated against the 100% BA of sc. administered insulin. Different PK parameters upon oral administration of NPs are illustrated in Table 12 . We observed that orally administered FA–insulin NPs exhibited two-times higher AUC (150.97 ± 5.87 µIU.h/ml), as compared with the sc. administered insulin (296.32 ± 10.75 µIU.h/ml without dose correction).
Discussion
Diblock copolymer PEG-PLGA (NH 2 -PEG-PLGA) was synthesized through a carbodiimide-promoted coupling reaction between primary amine groups of PEG– bis -amine and carboxylic acid group of PLGA via amide linkage. During PEGylation, a five-times molar excess of PEG– bis -amine relative to activated PLGA was added in order to prevent amidation at both termini of PEG– bis -amine and thereby avoid the formation of the undesired triblock copolymer (PLGA-PEG-PLGA). The conjugation of PEG– bis -amine to PLGA was preliminarily studied by UV and finally confirmed via FTIR and proton ( 1 H) NMR spectroscopy (Figure 4) . The characteristic peaks of FA were present in the UV spectrum of FA-PEG-PLGA, testifying the successful conjugation of FA with PEGylated PLGA. From the results of UV spectroscopy, we observed that FA constituted 38 ± 2 molar percentage of the total polymer conjugate. As determined from PK studies, this amount was sufficient enough to enhance the absorption of insulin NPs in the GI tract and result in a twofold increase in its oral BA. The FTIR spectrum of PLGA presented characteristic bands of a free carboxyl group at 1756 cm -1 . The reduced intensity and blue shift of carboxylic acid peak to 1753 cm -1 and concomitant appearance of new peaks at 1664 and 1594 cm -1 were observed in the FTIR spectrum of NH 2 -PEG-PLGA. These changes may be attributed to the successful amidation of PLGA with PEG– bis -amine. The fact that this synthetic method yielded a covalent conjugate and not a physical mixture was further testified by analyzing the FTIR analysis of a 1:1 physical mixture of the two polymers which indicated the absence of an amide I peak at 1664 cm -1 (Figure 3E) . The FTIR spectra of FA-PEG-PLGA shows a prominent peak at 1756 cm -1 (Figure 3C) with higher intensity compared with the NH 2 -PEG-PLGA polymer. This could be possibly due to free α-carboxylic group of FA and also of unreacted PLGA. A slight shift in amide bond peak positions was also detected (1644 and 1577 cm -1 ) with concomitant increase in their intensity representing the formation of an amide bond between the γ-carboxylic group of FA and free amine group of NH 2 -PEG-PLGA.
The synthesis of NH 2 -PEG-PLGA was finally confirmed by 1 H-NMR analysis. While the peak at δ 3.5 ppm represented the -O-CH 2 -CH 2 group of the PEG unit, the peak at δ 1.4 represented the protons corresponding to the lactide unit of PLGA. The peaks at δ 5.2 and 4.8 ppm were representative of the -CH 3 and -CH 2 proton(s) of the glycolide unit, respectively. These results are also consistent with previously published reports Citation [28,30] . The extent of PEGylation (i.e., the molar ratio of the PLGA:PEG subunit in the synthesized NH 2 -PEG-PLGA) was further confirmed by calculating the ratio of the integral per proton (IPP) of the representative proton of the PLGA subunit (a, -CH 3 proton) and that of the PEG subunit (d, CH 2 -CH 2 proton). The ratio (IPP a :IPP d ) was almost close to unity (∼0.94), substantiating that the molar ratio of PLGA and PEG subunit in the synthesized diblock PEG-PLGA polymer was 1:1. From the NMR data, we can also estimate the molecular weight of the synthesized PEG-PLGA polymer. To recapitulate, the molecular weight of PLGA and PEG used in the present study were 34,000 D and 3000 D, respectively. As the ratio of PEG:PLGA in the polymer conjugate was 1:1, the molecular weight of the diblock copolymer was 37,000 D. When this diblock copolymer (NH 2 -PEG-PLGA) was further conjugated with the FA, characteristic peaks of FA appeared in the range of δ 6.9–8.6 ppm (Figure 4B) . These peaks substantiate successful derivatization of PLGA-PEG with FA. In this regard, it may be noted that the intensity of peaks in the range of 6.9–8.6 ppm are relatively weaker when compared with the proton signals of PLGA and PEG. With regards to NMR characterization of polymer-based bio-conjugates, it may be noted that when the molecular weight of a functional molecule attached to the polymer is considerably lower as compared with the pristine polymer, the signal intensity due to the functional moiety is often too weak to make any definitive structural assignment. As molecular weight of FA is 441 D, the theoretical molecular weight of FA-PEG-PLGA diblock copolymer will be close to 37,440 D. Stoichiometric calculations showed that FA constitutes only 1.17% (w/w) of the total molecular weight of the polymer. Hence, the proton signals, characteristic of FA are not that intense compared with the protons constituting PEG and PLGA units of the diblock copolymer. However, the results from FTIR and NMR experiments are strong enough to qualitatively evidence the conjugation of FA with PEG-PLGA.
Insulin NPs (e.g., PLGA, NH 2 -PEG-PLGA, FA-PEG-PLGA) were prepared by a double emulsion solvent methods which is considered to be one of the most suitable method for encapsulation of hydrophilic drugs, proteins and peptides within nanopolymeric matrices Citation [39,40] . During NP preparation, ethyl acetate, rather than DCM, was employed as a solvent for polymer. The lower hydrophobicity of ethyl acetate relative to DCM was thought to exert a lesser impact on the chemical and conformational stability of the protein during nanoencapsulation Citation [41] . The ratio of the internal aqueous phase to external organic phase was kept at 1:10 v/v so as to obtain very small size droplets of primary emulsion which finally resulted in NPs with smaller size and higher encapsulation efficiency. PVA, used as a surfactant in the external aqueous phase, was found to form a layer at the interface, between organic and external second aqueous phase thus preventing aggregation of NPs during evaporation of ethyl acetate Citation [34] . The nanoparticle preparation process was optimized in terms of different process variables such as homogenization speed, homogenization time, droplet size and percentage of theoretical drug loading. Homogenization was carried out at different speeds (10,000, 12,500 and 15,000 rpm) for 5 min. Reduction in the particles size was observed when homogenization speed was increased from 10,000 to 12,500 rpm due to the input of high shear, which led to the formation of smaller size droplets. On further increasing the homogenization speed to 15,000 rpm, the size of NPs decreased but the encapsulation efficiency declined due to the formation of smaller droplets, which may have resulted in increased interfacial surface area and subsequent outward diffusion of insulin from the inner aqueous phase to the outer aqueous phase. Considering these facts, a homogenization speed of 12,500 rpm was considered to be optimum and chosen for further studies. The homogenization period for the formation of NPs is also a critical parameter for controlling the particle size, encapsulation efficiency and insulin stability Citation [4] . In general, an increase in homogenization period is accompanied with a reduction in particle size; an increase in homogenization time exposed the nanosystem to turbulence for a prolonged duration, thereby enhancing the efficacy of droplet size reduction. Notably, the decrease in particle size was perceivable up to 5 min. Further increase in homogenization period was accompanied with increased particles size and decreased encapsulation efficiency, which is possibly an effect of overhomogenization. As the homogenization period increases, droplet sizes decrease up to a particular extent; however, over-reduction in droplet size increases their surface energy to such an extent that the nano-droplets reassemble to form aggregates, which is reflected on their increased particle size. Thus, taking into consideration our optimization results, a homogenization speed of 12,500 rpm for 5 min was selected for further optimization of reaction and process variables. The effect of theoretical drug loading on the particles size, PDI and encapsulation efficiency of insulin NPs was also studied. From Table 3 , it is clear that an insignificant difference in the particle size and PDI were observed at 5 and 7.5% theoretical insulin loading with optimal encapsulation efficiency. In addition, the least encapsulation efficiency was observed at 10% theoretical insulin loading which was possibly a consequence of saturation of emulsion droplets. Although particle size was supposed to increase as a function of theoretical drug loading, to our surprise, the smaller particle size was observed at 10% theoretical insulin loading. The apparent anomaly may be a consequence of unentrapped insulin, which being protein in nature acts as a surfactant and may assist in the formation of smaller sized emulsion droplets. Finally, a theoretical insulin loading of 7.5% w/w was selected for subsequent studies. All insulin NPs were prepared with the same optimized parameters as in the case of PLGA NPs.
Table 4 shows the particle size, ζ-potential and encapsulation efficiency of insulin NPs. It is worth mentioning that all NPs, regardless of their specific surface chemistry and composition, presented particle sizes and encapsulation efficiencies in the range of approximately 250–300 nm and approximately 80–87%, respectively. During optimization of PLGA NPs, acidic internals were used, which may have accelerated the acidic hydrolysis of ethyl acetate to acetic acid and ethanol. This may be a plausible reason behind premature precipitation of the polymer and formation of large-sized NPs with low drug encapsulation efficiency (∼75%). The neutralization of the acidic internal phase with NaOH enhanced the entrapment efficiency to approximately 89%. Thus, later, all the insulin-loaded NP formulations were prepared with the neutral internal phase using the previously optimized parameters. Insulin-loaded PLGA NPs presented negative ζ-potential (-13.6 ± 1.83 mV), which may be attributed to the free carboxylic acid group native to the structure of PLGA as the presence of negatively charged amino acid residues of unbound insulin is also associated with the surface of PLGA NPs. After PEGylation, the ζ-potential of the insulin-loaded NH 2 -PEG-PLGA NPs was increased to -3.37 ± 3.42 mV, due to interchange of negatively charged carboxylic acid groups with positively charged amine functions. Following FA conjugation, the ζ-potential again decreased to -16.6 ± 1.51 mV, which could be attributed to the unreacted α-carboxyl groups of FA and to some extent unbound insulin that adhered to the NP surface.
Long-term storage of NPs in colloidal form is a challenging task because of their physical (aggregation/particle fusion) as well as chemical instability (hydrolysis of polymer, drug leakage from NPs and chemical reactivity of drug). To circumvent this problem, NPs were freeze-dried with an aim to improve their storage stability Citation [42] by an optimized stepwise freeze drying cycle invented by our group Citation [101] . The addition of cryoprotectants, especially sugars, prevents the NPs from aggregating or fusing during the in-freezing step. These form a glassy matrix upon freezing and immobilize the NPs within the matrix. Trehalose is the most compatible cryoprotectant for biomolecules because its amorphous nature causes minimal damage to the peptides and proteins formulated within NPs. Trehalose also possesses other advantages such as high flexibility, low hygroscopicity and absence of internal hydrogen bonds, thereby preventing interparticle H-bonding, and higher glass transition temperature Citation [42] . From table 5 it is clearly evident that NPs freeze dried in the presence of 5% trehalose showed insignificant change in particle size and PDI whereas NPs freeze dried in the absence of trehalose were highly prone to agglomeration.
As NPs developed during the course of our study were meant for oral administration, it was necessary to evaluate the insulin release pattern under different pH conditions that ideally prevail in different parts of the GI tract (stomach and intestine among others). All NPs, irrespective of their surface chemistry and composition, exhibited Higuchi‘s pattern of drug release with the r 2 > 0.9. As discussed earlier, the observed initial burst release may be attributed to the desorption of surface-adsorbed drugs (Figure 7) . Because of their hydrophilic nature, insulin tends to migrate towards the outer aqueous phase. Hence, during NP preparation large amounts of hydrophilic drugs remain physically adhered to the outer surface of the polymer, resulting in an initial burst release. Notably, the polymeric matrix provides a protective sheath for the drug entrapped inside NPs so that at later stages of incubation, the drug releases from NPs in a controlled manner. The rate of release depends upon several factors such as the disintegration rate of the polymer, the diffusion of the drug from the polymeric matrix and the degradation of the polymer matrix. In comparison to pristine PLGA, both NH 2 -PEG-PLGA and FA-PEG-PLGA exhibited smaller burst release and lower cumulative release after 24 h, which may be attributed to some additional interactions between insulin and the hydrophilic PEG chains associated with the NPs. During NP preparation, insulin was subjected to various chemical and mechanical stresses. Chemical stresses included organic solvents, without interfaces, acidic conditions and pH shifts, while mechanical stresses included high shear homogenization, temperature fluctuation, centrifugation and vortexing. The exposure to organic interfaces and temperature fluctuations is fraught with the possibility of protein denaturation, whereas high speed homogenization or vortexing leads to aggregation. Moreover, as already discussed, insulin is also susceptible to degradation under acidic conditions. All these factors may negatively influence the bioactivity of insulin Citation [41,43,44] . It was therefore necessary to determine the chemical and conformational stability of insulin after the formation of different NPs. The chemical stability of insulin against degradation and aggregation was determined by RP-HPLC and native PAGE analysis. RP-HPLC chromatograms of test and control insulin samples were analyzed for the presence of residual peaks, which may have appeared due to the loss of the 21st amino acid aspargin of the A-chain (monodesamido A21 insulin) or third amino acid of the B-chain (monodesamido B3 insulin) Citation [35,43] . However, no statistically significant (p > 0.05) change was observed in the amount (percent) of degradation products (residual peak) associated with standard insulin solution and insulin extracted from various nanoformulations. Chemical stability of insulin was further confirmed by native PAGE analysis of standard insulin solution and insulin extracted from different polymeric NPs. As observed from the gel, all bands appeared at the same level unambiguously suggesting that no aggregation/degradation of insulin has occurred during the preparation, storage and release of NPs (Figure 9) .
Exposure to humidity and temperature often destabilizes freeze-dried NPs during storage. Crystallization of excipients and drugs may lead to the formation of an initial denser amorphous phase which finally transforms to a crystalline mass Citation [45] . The storage stability of the insulin NPs was performed at 4 ° C and RT with a relative humidity of 50% according to the International Conference on Harmonization guidelines. An insignificant change (p > 0.05) in the particle size, PDI, reconstitution time and S f :S i ratio was observed after the stability studies, which confirmed that particle characteristics of freeze-dried formulations were well preserved during stability studies.
Enzymatic degradation by gastric and intestinal enzymes is one of the major barriers for the oral delivery of insulin. It was hypothesized that encapsulation of insulin inside the NPs will protect the drug against chemoenzymatic degradation. To test this hypothesis, we checked the stability of all insulin-loaded nanoformulations in SGF and SIF. From Table 9 it can be inferred that the percentage of insulin recovered from insulin NPs was significantly higher (p < 0.05) than the percentage of insulin recovered from the standard insulin solution after incubation with SGF and SIF. Notably, all the three nanoformulations presented a comparable recovery profile (percent). As for size, no significant (p > 0.05) change was observed for PLGA or FA-PEG-PLGA NPs. However, amine-terminated NH 2 -PEG-PLGA NP revealed a significant increase (p < 0.05) in particle size under similar conditions. The observation, however, may be attributed to the near-to-neutral ζ-potential of NH 2 -PEG-PLGA NPs, which is too low to afford any electrostatic stabilization to the NPs.
To further comprehend how the surface modification of PLGA NPs influenced their oral absorption, all NPs were evaluated for their oral hypoglycemic activity. Oral administration of pure insulin solution revealed no hypoglycemic activity because of its intrinsic proclivity towards enzymatic degradation as well as poor permeability across the GI tract Citation [7] . An increase in the blood glucose level was observed in the animals belonging to the diabetic control group (Figure 11) . Interestingly, orally administered insulin NP formulations exhibited significant hypoglycemic response. Encapsulation of insulin within a nano-PLGA matrix not only prevented premature degradation of the protein/drug but concomitantly enhanced its absorption in the GI tract via specialized mechanisms. It is well reported by different research groups, including ours, that polymeric NPs are absorbed via a specialized region in the GI tract, namely M-cells in Peyer‘s patches Citation [4,39,46] . The increased hypoglycemic response of insulin NPs with respect to standard insulin was thus attributed to their specific absorption via the M-cells. In this connection, it is noteworthy to mention that hypoglycemic activity of NH 2 -PEG-PLGA NPs was significantly lower when compared with their pristine PLGA counterpart. The observed difference was seemingly a consequence of aggregation of NH 2 -PEG-PLGA NPs in the GI tract and/or nonspecific targeting of amine groups, associated with the PLGA core. As evident from particle characterization data, the ζ-potential of NH 2 -PEG-PLGA NPs (-3.37 ± 3.42 mV) was too low to afford any electrostatic stabilization to the synthesized NPs. As a result, NH 2 -PEG-PLGA NPs showed severe agglomeration and subsequent deterioration in particle size, when exposed to gastrointestinal fluids, namely SGF and SIF. In addition to the aggregation problem, it is also feasible that the highly reactive surface amine group binds nonspecifically at any site other than the GI tract and gets stuck there. To revalidate our concept, a control formulation based on PLGA-PEG-OCH 3 (molecular weight ∼39,000 D) NPs was synthesized and administered orally in diabetic rats. Similar to NH 2 -PEG-PLGA NPs, CCH 3 -PEG-insulin NPs also exhibited a ζ-potential near to neutral and aggregation in SGF and SIF. Nevertheless, hypoglycemic activity of rats treated with insulin-loaded PLGA-PEG-OCH 3 NPs was considerably higher than their amine-terminated counterparts suggesting that oral absorption of NP-encapsulated drugs critically depend on the nature of their surface functionality. Although NH 2 -PEG-PLGA NPs showed lower activity compared with its CCH 3 -terminated counterpart, amine functionalization of PLGA was crucial to realize the successful introduction of FA into the polymeric backbone. Among all the treatment groups, rats orally administered with FA–insulin NPs exhibited the highest hypoglycemic response. The results clearly indicate that integration of FA with PEG-PLGA played a key role in augmenting the oral absorption of nanocarriers in the GI tract through a FA receptor-mediated endocytic pathway. To prove the concept, the same FA–insulin NPs were orally administered into a separate group of animals, pretreated with excess of folate and antidiabetic activity was tested as described previously. As shown in Figure 11 , hypoglycemic response of FA–insulin NPs in animals pretreated with free FA was almost comparable to those treated with plain PLGA NPs (Figure 11) . These results clearly corroborate that FA–insulin NPs developed during the course of the study are absorbed in the GI tract via FA receptor-mediated endocytosis. FA-pretreatment blocked the endocytic pathway that was originally involved in the absorption of FA–insulin NPs by the GI tract and thus negatively influenced the NP-induced hypoglycemia.
As far as the current regimen of insulin therapy is concerned, sc. administration of this proteinaceous drug is associated with a severe hypoglycemic shock and patient inconvenience. The blood glucose level, as depicted in Figure 11 , often falls below the basal level, thereby leading to undesired nephrotoxicity as well as cardiotoxicity. Furthermore, hypoglycemia induced by sc. administered insulin is only transient and is maintained for no longer than 8 h. This necessitates frequent and multiple dosing, which, again, is a bitter punishment for patients with lower compliance. In this context, it will be even more favorable if the initial rate of hypoglycemic induction is retarded to some extent while prolonging the duration of hypoglycemia so that a daily single dose of the formulation will be sufficient to maintain the blood glucose level to normal. Encouragingly, insulin encapsulated within plain PLGA and FA-PEG-PLGA NPs exhibited more prolonged hypoglycemic activity without initial hypoglycemia, when compared with sc. administered insulin injection. For FA–insulin NPs, it was further interesting to note that a single dose of this nanoformulation maintained controlled hypoglycemia for a protracted duration of 24 h and the blood glucose level returned to basal level after 36 h of treatment. The results correlate nicely with the observed, slow and sustained release profile of insulin from our nanoformulations (Figure 7) . However, we acknowledge that the dose of oral insulin nanoformulations (50 IU/kg) used in the present case was ten-times higher than that of the sc. administered insulin standard (5 IU/kg) and also, the initial hypoglycemia induced by insulin injection is remarkably higher, when compared with the oral formulations. These facts may lead to an apparent impression that oral insulin NPs, developed over the course of the study, will not be able to compete with the marketed formulation. However, it is a well known fact that the BA of drugs administered via the oral route is always lower when compared with the parental route and, therefore, the dose of any orally administered therapeutic agent is at least ten-times higher than the parental formulation. Thus, although drug absorption via the oral route is less when compared with the parental route (∼100% BA), oral formulations are always preferred due to their higher patient compliance and also their cost–effectiveness. Currently, no oral formulation of insulin is available in the market. The freeze-dried FA-PEG-PLGA NPs, developed over the course of our study, can be filled in hard gelatin capsule shells. One capsule taken in the morning would be sufficient to control diabetes (blood glucose level) for at least 24 h. It may be further noted that although the dose of parental formulation is lower than that of the oral formulation, the former demands administration two- to three-times daily in order to maintain hypoglycemia. At the same time, administration once a day of the oral formulation is enough to maintain hypoglycemia for 24 h. Thus, overall dose of the oral formulation will be approximately three- to four-times higher than that of insulin injection. From an economic point of view, the cost of insulin is not very high; however, administration of insulin via sc. route requires sterile needles and syringes, which increases the overall cost of the technology. Furthermore, as already described, frequent dosing of sc. insulin is fraught with severe hypoglycemic shock, which was not conceived with the oral formulation. Hence, orally administered insulin NPs are expected to obviate the undesired cardio-/nephrotoxicity associated with marketed insulin formulation. Overall, the oral insulin delivery system, developed over the course of the study, is not only easy to administer but results in improved patient compliance, enhanced therapeutic profile and also better glucose homeostasis.
To further establish a correlation between the hypoglycemic activity of insulin NPs and actual plasma concentration of insulin, the plasma insulin levels of the various treated groups were determined. Due to their site-specific absorption in the GI tract, polymeric NPs showed a steady increase followed by a decrease in concentration of insulin over a period of 24 h (Figure 12) . Among all the nanoformulations, FA–insulin NPs demonstrated maximum C max (26.78 ± 3.66 µIU/ml) and subsequently, the highest oral BA (19.62 ± 1.68%). The results were encouraging, when compared with previously reported literature values. For example, hypromellose phthalate-coated PLGA NPs (20 IU/kg) exhibited a BA R of 6.27 ± 0.42% Citation [47] ; self-assembled NPs of polyglutamic acid and chitosan (30 IU/kg) demonstrated a BA R of 15.1 ± 0.9% Citation [13] . Similarly, a BA R of 13.21 ± 1.42% was demonstrated with a blend of NPs made of polycaprolactone and Eudragit ® (100 IU/kg; Evonik Industries, Essen, Germany) Citation [15] . Thus, orally administered FA-PEG-PLGA NPs demonstrates two-times higher AUC and cumulative hypoglycemia, when compared with the sc. administered free insulin solution. Subsequently, a meaningful correlation could be established between the PK and PD activity of FA–insulin NPs. As illustrated by PD as well as PK studies, FA-PEG-PLGA NPs revealed the highest efficacy among all the formulations. These results unambiguously corroborate that FA conjugation significantly impact the oral absorption of NPs. Considering all results, we propose a dual mechanism of absorption of NPs that involves absorption through M-cells as well as FA transporters in the intestinal wall. However, additional studies are needed to fully illustrate the mechanism of NP absorption.
Conclusion
In conclusion, we have successfully exploited FA-coupled PEGylated PLGA NPs for enhancing the oral BA of insulin via synergistic absorption through M-cells as well as FA transporters in the GI tract. Integration of FA with PEG-PLGA enhances the oral absorption of nanocarriers through FA receptor-mediated endocytosis. The formulation exhibited prolonged, controlled hypoglycemia for at least 24 h and could outwit many of the drawbacks (e.g., transient hypoglycemia with severe hypoglycemic shock) that are prevalent with the currently available therapy of sc. administered insulin injection. Furthermore, the freeze-dried FA-PEG-PLGA NPs can be formulated as capsules, a single dose of which would be sufficient to control the blood glucose level for at least 24 h. Currently, no oral formulation of insulin is available in the market. Only the sc. injections are available for the treatment of diabetes mellitus. Repetitive sc. injections, however, result in patient incompliance. Under such a scenario, encapsulation of insulin within FA-PEG-PLGA NPs pave the way towards an easy-to-administer, patient-friendly oral formulation that can combat diabetes with improved therapeutic profile as compared with the commercially available sc. insulin injection.
Future perspective
In the current scientific panorama, as oral delivery of insulin is beyond reach to the scientific community, the current findings may inaugurate a new direction for delivering insulin orally while enhancing its BA. Although the present work is preliminary, yet the same offers a platform technology for making feasible the oral delivery of insulin exploiting the carrier property of NPs (in terms of protecting insulin in harsh GI condition) and their FA-mediated transport of NP across the gastrointestinal membrane. The technology can also be extended to other delicate protein- and peptide-based biomolecules (e.g., vaccine antigens). The polymer used in the present formulation (PLGA) is very costly and can be replaced with some cheaper alternatives to further reduce the cost of formulation.
