The  interest  in  polymeric
 nanoparticles
 as imaging
 systems
 for  biomedical
 applications
 has
 increased
notably
 in  the  last
 decades.
 In this  work,
 PLGA
 nanoparticles,
 prepared  from
 nano-emulsion
 templating,
have
 been
 used  to  prepare
 novel
 ﬂuorescent
 imaging
 agents.
 Two  model
 ﬂuorescent
 dyes  were  chosen
and
 dissolved
 in  the  oil  phase
 of  the nano-emulsions
 together
 with  PLGA.  Nano-emulsions
 were  prepared
by
 the phase
 inversion
 composition
 (PIC)  low-energy
 method.
 Fluorescent
 dye-loaded
 nanoparticles
 were
obtained
 by solvent
 evaporation
 of  nano-emulsion
 templates.
 PLGA  nanoparticles
 loaded
 with
 the  ﬂu-
orescent
 dyes  showed
 hydrodynamic
 radii  lower  than  40  nm;  markedly
 lower  than
 those
 reported
 in
previous
 studies.  The  small  nanoparticle
 size  was  attributed
 to the nano-emulsiﬁcation
 strategy  used.
PLGA
 nanoparticles
 showed
 negative
 surface
 charge
 and
 enough
 stability
 to  be used for  biomedical
imaging
 purposes.
 Encapsulation
 efﬁciencies
 were higher
 than  99%,  which
 was  also  attributed
 to the
nano-emulsiﬁcation
 approach
 as well
 as  to the  low  solubility
 of the  dyes  in the  aqueous
 component.
Release
 kinetics
 of  both  ﬂuorescent
 dyes  from  the  nanoparticle
 dispersions
 was pH-independent
 and
sustained.
 These
 results  indicate
 that  the  dyes  could  remain
 encapsulated
 enough  time  to  reach
 any
organ
 and  that  the  decrease
 of  the  pH  produced
 during  cell  internalization
 by  the  endocytic
 route
 would
not
 affect  their  release.
 Therefore,
 it  can  be  assumed
 that  these  nanoparticles
 are
 appropriate
 as  sys-
temic
 imaging  agents.
 In addition,  in vitro  toxicity
 tests  showed
 that  nanoparticles
 are  non-cytotoxic.
Consequently,
 it  can  be concluded
 that  the  preparation
 of PLGA  nanoparticles
 from
 nano-emulsion
 tem-
plating
 represents
 a very  versatile
 technology
 that  enables
 obtaining
 biocompatible,
 biodegradable
 and
safe
 imaging
 agents  suitable
 for  biomedical
 purposes.

 The
 PIC

 consists

 the

tion
 of
 nano-emulsions
 by
 inversion
 of
 the
 spontaneous
 curvature
of
 the
 surfactant

 triggered

 a

 at
 constant

 The

 polymeric
 nanoparticles
 enables

 nanoparticles

 as
 previ-

 Thus,
 nanoparticle

 and
 150

 nature

 aqueous
 phase
 of

template

 [19,20];
 nanoparticle

 charge
 has
been
 tuned
 by
 using
 surfactant

 with
 different
 charges
 and
lipophilic
 compounds
 have
 been
 encapsulated
 with
 a
 high
 encap-
sulation
 efﬁciency
 [19].
The
 novelty
 of
 this
 work
 arises
 from
 the
 design
 of
 novel
PLGA
 nanoparticles,
 from
 nano-emulsion
 templating,
 using
 the
PIC

 as
 a
 versatile
 platform
 for
 the
 nanoencapsulation
 of
desired
 ﬂuorescent
 dyes.

 the

formation,

 in

 the
nano-emulsion.
 When
 the
 solvent
 is
 evaporated,
 a
 process
 leading
to
 nanoparticle

 the
 ﬂuorescent
 dyes
 remain
 entrapped
in
 the
 polymeric
 nanoparticle

 due
 to
 their

solubility
 (Table

 This
 could
 also
 explain
 the
 high

lation
 efﬁciencies
 obtained
 by
 Zhang
 et
 al.,
 [26]
 from
 60
 to

 wt%
using
 W/O/W
 template

 and
 also
 by
 Betancourt
 et
 al.,
[24]
 around

 wt%
 of
 Rho6G
 encapsulation
 from
 O/W
 template
emulsions,
 whose
 nanoparticles
 showed
 sizes
 of
 around
 150
 nm.
However,
 in
 another
 study
 by
 Trapani
 et
 al.,
 [25], where
 nanopar-
ticles
 with
 sizes
 around
 340
 nm
 were

 by
 ultrasounds,
 C6
encapsulation
 efﬁciencies
 reached
 values
 only

 to
 30
 wt%,
 which
could
 be
 attributed
 to
 the
 bigger
 sizes
 of
 these
 nanoparticles.
3.4.
 Release

 the
 ﬂuorescent
 dyes
Release
 of
 C6
 and
 Rho6G
 from
 nanoparticles
 was
 performed
 at
25 ◦C
 and
 37 ◦C
 (room
 and
 human
 body
 temperatures,
 respectively)
by
 means
 of
 the
 dialysis
 bag
 method
 and
 ﬂuorescence
 detection
(as
 described
 in
 Section
 2.2.9)
 using
 PBS
 as
 the
 receptor
 solution,
 at
neutral
 (pH
 =
 7.4)
 and
 acid
 (pH
 =
 4.0)
 pH
 values
 to
 simulate
 the
 pH
 of
different
 cell
 compartments
 [6]. Although
 the
 nanoparticle
 mech-
anism
 of
 cell
 penetration
 was
 not
 studied
 in
 this
 work,
 it
 seems
 to
be
 agreement
 that
 nanoparticles
 usually
 penetrate
 cells
 by
 different
kinds
 of
 endocytosis
 [6]. By
 the
 endocytic
 pathway,
 nanoparticles
would
 be
 engulfed
 by
 the
 endosomes,
 initially
 at
 a
 neutral
 pH.
 Then,
during
 the
 endocytic
 pathway,
 the
 pH
 gradually
 decreases,
 reaching
acidic
 pH
 values
 around
 4.5
 [6].
The
 percentage
 of
 C6
 and
 Rho6G
 released
 from
 NP(C6)
 and
NP(Rho6G),
 respectively,
 as
 a
 function
 of
 time
 at
 both
 pH
 values
and
 at
 both
 temperatures,
 are
 shown
 in
 Fig.
 5
 (and
 also
 in
 Fig.
 S4
from
 SI,
 with
 the
 same
 time-scale,
 for
 comparative
 purposes).
 The
release
 proﬁle
 for
 each
 dye
 was
 pH
 and
 temperature
 independent.
Around
 60
 wt%
 of
 C6
 had
 already
 been
 released
 during
 the
 ﬁrst
 hour.
However,
 after
 this
 period
 of
 time,
 the
 release
 was
 more
 sustained;
achieving
 80–100
 wt%
 of
 C6
 release
 after
 6
 h,
 at
 25
 and
 37 ◦C
 respec-
tively,
 where
 a
 plateau
 was
 reached
 and
 no
 more
 C6
 was
 released
up
 to
 the
 whole
 experimental
 time.
 In
 contrast,
 Rho6G
 release
 was
more
 sustained:
 only
 25
 wt%
 of
 Rho6G
 had
 been
 released
 within
the
 ﬁrst
 hour
 at
 25 ◦C
 and
 slightly
 lower
 at
 the
 37 ◦C,
 where
 the
burst
 release
 was
 maintained
 up
 to
 around
 6
 h.
 After
 this
 time,
 the
release
 was
 more
 sustained,
 achieving
 an
 almost
 complete
 release
after
 200
 h
 (8
 days).
 Although
 the
 Rho6G
 released
 between
 24
 h
 and
72
 h
 seemed
 to
 be
 slightly
 lower
 when
 the
 pH
 of
 the
 receptor
 solu-
tion
 is
 neutral,
 these
 differences
 cannot
 be
 attributed
 to
 an
 effect
of
 the
 pH,
 since
 neither
 the
 ﬂuorescent
 dye
 nor
 the
 PLGA

 pH
dependent
 properties
 at
 this
 pH
 range.
 The
 slower
 initial
 release
of
 Rho6G
 as
 compared
 to
 C6
 could
 be
 attributed
 to
 the
 smaller
size
 of
 NP(C6),
 as
 compared
 to
 NP(Rho6G).
 In
 addition,
 interactions
between
 Rho6G
 not
 only
 with

 PLGA

 with
polysorbate
 80

 hypothesized.
 Previ-

 that
 the
 smaller

 the
release
 of
 the
 encapsulated

 be
 explained
by

 higher

 [43]

to

 the
 present

 of

 present

 conﬁrmed
 that
 the
 pH
 neither
 the
 tempera-
ture
 of
 the
 environment
 (receptor

 did
 not
 inﬂuence
 C6
and

and

 in

 be

 conﬁrming
 the

 advanced

 the

 the

Rho6G

PLGA

 the
 sizes

 together

 in

 the

not

 of

 of

 the

 as

 the

 and

 In

Rho6G
