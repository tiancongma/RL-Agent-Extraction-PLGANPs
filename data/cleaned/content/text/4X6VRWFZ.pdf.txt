A B S T R A C T
Cisplatin is a potent and widely used chemotherapeutic agent to treat a variety of tumors. However, its clinical
use is associated with undesirable side eﬀects and acquired resistance to cisplatin. In this study, cisplatin loaded
hyaluronic acid (HA) functionalized poly (lactic-co-glycolic acid)–poly (ethylene glycol) nanoparticles (CP-HA-
PLGA-PEG-NPs) were fabricated using double emulsion solvent evaporation method to target CD44 receptor
expressed on cancerous cells. The developed nanoconstructs were characterized for various in vitro parameters,
including size distribution, zeta potential, morphology, drug loading and in vitro release. The HA content on the
HA-PLGA-PEG-NPs was quantiﬁed by a turbidimetric method. The in vitro anticancer study in human ovarian
cancer (SKOV-3) cells showed signiﬁcantly (p < 0.05) higher cytotoxicity of CP-HA-PLGA-PEG NPs as com-
pared to free cisplatin and non-targeted nanoparticles (CP-PLGA-PEG NPs). Further, laser scanning confocal
microscopy revealed that there was enhanced cellular uptake of HA-PLGA-PEG NPs in CD44-over expressing
ovarian cancer cell line (SKOV-3). The in vivo antitumor activity of CP-HA-PLGA-PEG-NPs was signiﬁcantly
(p < 0.05) higher than free cisplatin and CP-PLGA-PEG-NPs in Ehrlich tumor (solid) bearing mice. The results
demonstrated the potential of target speciﬁc nanoconstruct of cisplatin in the improved cancer chemotherapy.
1. Introduction
Chemotherapy is a key therapeutic approach for the treatment of
cancer. Most of the conventional chemotherapeutics are unable to dif-
ferentiate between cancerous and normal cells, and lead to adverse
eﬀects. The selective delivery of anticancer agents to cancer tissue
would be of great signiﬁcance in cancer chemotherapy. In order to
obtain better therapeutic eﬃcacy as well as reduce toxicity, numerous
drug delivery systems have been developed [1,2]. Nanoparticles are
receiving immense recognition as potential delivery vehicle in cancer
chemotherapy, in addition to their role as eﬀective gene delivery
modality and cancer stem cell targeting agent [3,4]. Further, nano-
particle mediated selective drug delivery, via passive or active tar-
geting, in the tumor tissue is of great signiﬁcance because it can reduce
the side eﬀect of chemotherapeutic agents [5]. The passive targeting of
the nanoparticles is based on the enhanced permeability and retention
(EPR) eﬀect of the tumor vessels. The major drawbacks associated with
this strategy are unsatisfactory tumor targetability and the possibility of
toxic eﬀects to normal cells as well. Active targeting is a promising
approach to resolve such limitations. In active targeting, speciﬁc ligand
can be conjugated directly to therapeutics or onto the surface of the
therapeutics containing nanovehicles that have a high binding aﬃnity
for receptors over expressed on cancer cells to improve tumor targeting
eﬃciency [5].
Hyaluronic acid (HA), or hyaluronan is a naturally occurring bio-
degradable, non-immunogenic, non-toxic, water-soluble and linear
anionic polysaccharide [6]. HA is made up of repeating disaccharide
units of β-1,4-D-glucuronic acid-β-1,3-D-N-acetylglucosamine. Recently,
hyaluronic acid has received wide recognition as an active targeting
ligand [7]. CD44 is a cell surface glycoprotein that is over expressed on
diﬀerent type of tumor tissue and acts as receptor for HA for tumor
targeting [8,9]. HA is responsible for several important cellular func-
tions, like cell adhesion and motility, cell proliferation and diﬀer-
entiation, and cell migration through speciﬁc interactions with its re-
ceptor. The recognition and interaction of HA with CD44 over
expressed in diﬀerent types of tumors increases the application of HA

Cisplatin (cis-diamminedichloroplatinum) is a commonly employed
anti-neoplastic agent for the treatment of diﬀerent type of solid tumors
such as small cell and non-small cell lung cancer, testicular cancer,
ovarian cancer, gastric cancer, and head and neck squamous sarcoma
[12]. However, its therapeutic application is associated with serious
side eﬀects, particularly nephrotoxicity, ototoxicity and neurotoxicity
[13]. The selective targeting of cisplatin to tumor tissue is required to
minimize adverse eﬀect and in turn, to improve its therapeutic poten-
tial. In order to achieve more selective cisplatin exposure, diﬀerent
kinds of carriers have been explored, including drug conjugate with

liposomes [20] and PLGA-PEG nanoparticles [21]. In addition, many
eﬀorts have been made for selective delivery of cisplatin by direct
conjugation of cisplatin with the HA molecule [22–25]. Additionally,
the surface of PLGA-PEG nanoparticles was modiﬁed with divers kinds
of ligands such as a prostate speciﬁc membrane antibody (PSMA) ap-
tamer [26] and CD44 antibody [27] for targeted delivery of cisplatin to
improve its eﬃcacy. In the present study, cisplatin loaded PLGA-PEG
nanoparticles were fabricated by double emulsion solvent evaporation
method and surface modiﬁed with HA by simple carbodiimide cou-
pling. The prepared nanoparticles were characterized for various in
vitro characteristics. Subsequently, the developed formulation was in-
vestigated for the in vitro cytotoxicity and cellular uptake potential in
SKOV-3 cell line. Further, in vivo anticancer activity was investigated in
Ehrlich tumor (solid) model.
2. Materials and method
2.1. Materials
PLGA (Resomer RG502H, viscosity 0.21 dl/g) was purchased from
Evonik Industries (Germany). Polyethylene glycol (PEG) diamine (PEG-
NH2, MW: 3.4 kDa), cisplatin (MW: 300), polyvinyl alcohol (PVA, MW:
31–50 kDa),
N-hydroxysuccinimide
(NHS),
N,N′-dicyclohex-
ylcarbodimide (DCC), N-(3-dimethylaminopropyl)-N-ethylcarbodimide
hydrochloride (EDC), coumarin-6, 4,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole dihy-
drochloride (DAPI), dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO), diethylether, anhy-
drous dichloromethane (DCM), streptomycin and penicillin were ob-
tained from Sigma–Aldrich (India). Hyaluronic acid sodium salt (HA,
MW: 13 kDa, Lot Number 024505, Speciﬁcation number LPD-9800042)
was purchased from Lifecore Biomedical, USA. Sodium diethyldithio-
carbamate (DDTC) was obtained from Himedia. All other chemicals
used were of analytical grade.
2.2. Synthesis of PLGA-PEG-NH2 copolymer
PLGA-PEG-NH2 copolymer was synthesized in two steps by using
carbodiimide chemistry [28]. Firstly, the carboxylic group of PLGA was
activated by the DCC/NHS, followed by conjugation of activated PLGA
with the amine group of PEG bis-amine. In the ﬁrst step, PLGA
(1 equiv.), DCC (3 equiv.) and NHS (3 equiv.) were added in 6 ml of
anhydrous dichloromethane, and the reaction was carried out for 24 h
with continuous stirring at room temperature under nitrogen environ-
ment. The resultant product was ﬁltered to remove by-product dicylo-
hexylurea using 0.45 μm PTFE syringe ﬁlter (Millipore). The activated
PLGA was puriﬁed and isolated by precipitation with excess cold an-
hydrous diethylether. The precipitated activated PLGA was centrifuged,
washed to remove unreacted components, dried under vacuum and
stored until use.
In the second step, activated PLGA (1 equiv.) and PEG-NH2
(2.5 equiv.) were dissolved in 6 ml anhydrous DCM and then triethy-
lamine was added to the solution. The reaction mixture was kept on
stirrer for 24 h at room temperature under nitrogen environment. The
product mixture was precipitated in ice cold diethyl ether to obtain
PLGA-PEG-NH2 di-block copolymer. The collected product was dried
under reduced pressure. The dried product was dissolved in anhydrous
DMSO and dialyzed in the Pur-A-lyzer (MWCO: 3.5 kDa) against water
at room temperature. The product was freeze dried and stored until use.
2.3. Characterization of PLGA-PEG-NH2 copolymer
The copolymer was characterized by FTIR spectroscopy (Spectrum
RXI, FTIR instrument, Perkin Elmer, USA) and 1H NMR spectroscopy
(UXNMR, Bruker Analytische Messtechnik GmbH, 400 MHz) using
deuterated chloroform. In order to know the degree of activation of
PLGA, the NHS content was quantiﬁed in activated PLGA. The hydro-
xamate method was used to determine the NHS content reacted with
PLGA [29]. Brieﬂy, 1 ml of sample solutions (containing 0.1–0.2 mmol
of the NHS moiety) or 1 ml of standard NHS (0.01–1 mmol of NHS)
were mixed with 0.2 ml of NaOH (2 N) and incubated at 60 °C for
10 min. The reaction mixtures were acidiﬁed with 1.5 ml of 0.85 N HCl
and then centrifuged to separate the precipitated PLGA. The super-
natant (2 ml) was mixed with 5% FeCl3 in 0.1 N HCI (0.5 ml). The
absorbance
was read
immediately
at 500 nm using UV-spectro-
photometer (Thermo Fischer Scientiﬁc, Finland). The NHS content in
the samples was calculated by comparing the absorbance of samples
with standard solution.
2.4. Preparation of cisplatin loaded PLGA-PEG-NH2 nanoparticles
CP-PLGA-PEG-NPs were prepared by using double emulsion solvent
evaporation method [21], with minor modiﬁcations. Brieﬂy, an aqu-
eous solution (0.5 ml) of cisplatin (2 mg/ml) was emulsiﬁed in di-
chloromethane containing polymer (1.75%, w/v) using ultrasonic
homogenizer (Labsonic M, Sartorious, Germany). The primary emulsion
was added drop wise to PVA solution (6 ml, 2%, w/v) containing cis-
platin (1 mg/ml) and the mixture was sonicated. The resultant emulsion
was subjected to magnetic stirring for removal of organic solvent. The
nanoparticles were collected by centrifugation at 19,000 rpm for
15 min (Sigma 3–30 K, Germany), and washed with water, and lyo-
philized (Advantage freeze dryer, VirTis, Gardiner, NY, USA) with
mannitol (6%, w/v) as cryoprotectant and stored in desiccators.
2.5. Preparation of cisplatin loaded hyaluronic acid conjugated PLGA
nanoparticles
HA sodium salt was dissolved in deionized water, desalted by dia-
lysis using Pur-A-lyzer (MWCO: 3.5 kDa) against hydrochloric acid so-
lution (0.1 N) and subsequently lyophilized. The CP-HA-PLGA-PEG-NPs
were prepared as described earlier [30]. Brieﬂy, desalted HA (1
equivalent) was dissolved in 2-(N-morpholino) ethane sulfonic acid
(MES) buﬀer (0.01 M, pH 5.5) and then EDC (2 equiv.) and NHS
(2 equiv.) were added to activate carboxylic groups of HA. Subse-
quently, the mixture was incubated with nanoparticles suspension
under continuous stirring for 2 h at room temperature. The CP-HA-
PLGA-PEG-NPs were puriﬁed by washing using centrifugation at
19,000 rpm for 15 min (Sigma 3–30 K), and lyophilized. The super-
natant was collected for the estimation of HA.
2.6. Characterization of cisplatin loaded nanoparticles
2.6.1. Evaluation of HA content onto nanoparticles
The estimation of HA conjugated onto nanoparticles was performed
by determination of HA in supernatant collected after centrifugation of
nanoparticles using hexadecyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB)
turbidimetric method [31], with slight modiﬁcation. Brieﬂy, 50 μl of
the HA standard solution or supernatant sample was mixed in triplicate
in a 96-well plate with 50 μl of sodium acetate buﬀer (0.2 M, pH 5.5)
and incubated at 37 °C for 10 min. Subsequently, CTAB (100 μl, 10 mM)

2.6.2. Particle size, polydispersity index and zeta potential measurement
The mean particle size, polydispersity and zeta potential of the
developed formulation were measured at 25 °C by dynamic light scat-
tering using Zetasizer (Zetasizer Nano ZS90, Malvern Instruments, UK)
operating at a scattering angle of 90°. To ensure the free diﬀusion and
unhindered Brownian motion of the particles, samples were suitably
diluted [32]. All measurements were performed in triplicate.
2.6.3. Morphology of nanoparticles
The morphological examination of CP-PLGA-PEG-NPs and CP-HA-
PLGA-PEG-NPs was performed using a transmission electron micro-
scope (TEM, Philips, Morgagni-268) operating at 70 kV. A drop of na-
noparticle suspension was kept on copper grids after sonication. The
samples were dried, treated with phosphotungstic acid (2%) and were
observed under the TEM.
2.6.4. Cisplatin loading in nanoparticles
The quantiﬁcation of cisplatin in the nanoparticles was carried out
by high performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) method [33,34].
The weighed amount of nanoparticles was suspended in sodium hy-
droxide solution (1 N) and incubated in a water bath shaker incubator
(New Brunswick Scientiﬁc, USA) at 37 °C overnight with constant
stirring. The suspension was centrifuged (19,000 rpm, 15 min) to col-
lect the supernatant. After addition of 50 μl of a solution of sodium
diethyl dithiocarbamate (DDTC, 10% (w/v) prepared with 0.1 N NaOH)
to an aliquot of the collected supernatant in microcentrifuge tube,
samples were incubated in a water bath shaker incubator (New
Brunswick Scientiﬁc, USA) at 37 °C for 1 h. The formed platinum–DDTC
complex was extracted with chloroform by vortexing and then cen-
trifuged to collect chloroform layer. An aliquot of samples was diluted
with acetonitrile and injected into a C18-column (250 mm × 5 μm). A
mixture of methanol:water (75:25) was used as mobile phase and the
eluate was monitored at 254 nm.
2.6.5. In vitro release
The weighed amount of CP-PLGA-PEG-NPs and CP-HA-PLGA-PEG-
NPs was separately suspended in phosphate buﬀer saline (PBS, pH 7.4
and pH 5.5). The suspensions were placed into microcentrifuge tubes
and incubated in water bath shaker (New Brunswick Scientiﬁc, USA) at
37 °C with constant stirring. At speciﬁed time interval, suspensions
were centrifuged (19,000 rpm, 10 min) to collect the supernatants and
replaced by equal volume of fresh PBS. Analysis of the samples was
performed by HPLC method using the procedure described above.
2.7. In vitro cell culture
2.7.1. Cell culture
Human ovarian cancer cell line (SKOV-3) was grown in complete
growth medium (RPMI-1640/DMEM medium) provided with fetal calf
serum (10%), streptomycin (100 μg/ml) and penicillin (100 units/ml)
in a CO2 incubator at 37 °C, 5% CO2 and 98% RH.
2.7.2. In vitro cytotoxicity study
The in vitro cytotoxicity of free cisplatin, CP-PLGA-PEG-NPs and CP-
HA-PLGA-PEG-NPs was performed by MTT assay. Cells were seeded
(1 × 104 cells per well) and incubated for 24 h prior to assay. The cells
were treated with diﬀerent concentrations of free drug or drug loaded
nanoparticles or blank nanoparticles for 48 h. The MTT (3-(4,5-di-
methylthiazol-2-yl)-2,5 diphenyltetrazolium bromide; 0.5 mg/ml) dye
was added to each well and the plates were incubated for 4 h at 37 °C.
Following this, excess media was removed and 150 μl of DMSO was
added to solubilize the purple formazan crystals. The plates were placed
on a shaker for 5 min for complete solubilization of dye and absorbance
was read at 570 nm on a microplate reader (BioTek Synergy HT) to
calculate IC50 by using Graph Pad prism (Software Version 5.0).
2.7.3. In vitro cellular uptake of nanoparticles
In order to investigate in vitro cellular uptake of PLGA-PEG-NPs and
HA-PLGA-PEG-NPs, coumarin-6 was used as a ﬂuorescence probe.
Coumarin-6-loaded PLGA-PEG-NPs (C6-PLGA-PEG-NPs) and C6-HA-
PLGA-PEG-NPs were prepared in the same way as described earlier
[35]. Brieﬂy, 20 μl of coumarin-6 (1 mg/ml) was added to the polymer
solution (1.75%, w/v) and then poured into 2% (w/v) PVA and emul-
siﬁed by sonication. The subsequent procedures for the preparation of
C6-PLGA-PEG-NPs and C6-HA-PLGA-PEG-NPs were the same as de-
scribed in Sections 2.4 and 2.5 respectively.
For in vitro cellular uptake study, SKOV-3 cells (1 × 105 cells/well)
were seeded in 6-well plate over glass cover slips for 24 h. Following
incubation, in order to maintain the equal concentration of coumarin-6
in the wells, the medium was changed with 2 ml of fresh medium
containing
C6-PLGA-PEG-NPs
(5 μg/ml)
or
C6-HA-PLGA-PEG-NPs
(6 μg/ml) and incubated for 1 h and 6 h. The cells were washed with
ice-cold PBS and ﬁxed with 4% (w/w) paraformaldehyde solution for
30 min at room temperature. Cells were further washed twice with PBS
and the nuclei were stained with DAPI (1 μg/ml in PBS). The cells were
imaged by using a laser scanning confocal microscope (Olympus
Fluoview FV1000) after mounting the cover slips over glass slides.
2.7.4. Receptor mediated cellular internalization
In order to examine the receptor mediated uptake of HA-functio-
nalized nanoparticles, SKOV-3 cells (1 × 105 cells/well) were seeded in
6-well plate over glass cover slips for 24 h. Prior to addition of nano-
particles, HA receptors were blocked with free HA (5 mg/ml) as de-
scribed previously [36]. Nanoparticles were added to the wells and
incubated for 6 h and the subsequent procedure was same as described
in Section 2.7.3.
2.7.5. Quantitative cellular uptake analysis
For the quantitative cellular uptake analysis, SKOV-3 cells were
cultivated and seeded into 96-well black plates at 1 × 105 cells/well.
The medium was changed with the C6-PLGA-PEG-NPs and C6-HA-
PLGA-PEG-NPs suspensions after the cells reached 80% conﬂuence. In
other set of experiment, prior to addition of nanoparticles, HA receptors
were blocked with free HA (5 mg/ml). After the designated time in-
terval (1 or 6 h), the nanoparticles suspension was removed and washed
the cell monolayer with 50 μl of ice-cold PBS (pH 7.4). After that, the
cells were lysed with lysis buﬀer (0.5% triton-X in 0.2 N NaOH). The
cellular uptake was quantiﬁed by measuring the ﬂuorescence intensity
in the desired wells using microplate reader (Inﬁnite M200 PRO,
TECAN, Switzerland) with the excitation wavelength at 450 nm and
emission wavelength 490 nm. The cellular uptake eﬃciency was re-
presented as the percentage of the ﬂuorescence coupled with testing
sample treated wells versus positive control.
2.8. In vivo antitumor activity
The antitumor activity of free cisplatin, CP-PLGA-PEG-NPs and CP-
HA-PLGA-PEG-NPs was evaluated in murine model according to the
protocols approved by the Institutional Animal Ethics Committee. The
Swiss albino mice (24–30 g) were used in this study and fed with
standard pellet diet and autoclaved water. Ehrlich ascites carcinoma
(EAC) cells were removed from the peritoneal cavity of mice harboring
8–10 days old ascitic tumor, and around 1 × 107 cells were inoculated
intramuscularly in the right thigh of mice for the growth of solid tumor
on day 0. The tumor bearing animals were randomly assigned to four

The results were presented as mean ± SD. The statistical analysis

3. Result and discussion
3.1. Characterization of PLGA-PEG-NH2 co-polymer
The synthesized co-polymer (PLGA-PEG-NH2) was characterized
using FTIR (Fig. 1A). The IR band at 1760 cm−1 in PLGA and PLGA-
PEG represents the C]O stretching in PLGA. A series of peaks between
2800 and 3000 cm−1 could be attributed to the CeH stretching
vibration correlated to methylene groups in the PEG chain. In the IR
spectrum of PLGA-PEG, the peak at 1664 cm−1 and 1535 cm−1 corre-
sponds to the amide I and amide II bond respectively [37]. The IR
spectra conﬁrm the successful formation of the amide bond between
PLGA and PEG.
In order to further characterize the co-polymer, the 1H NMR spectra
of pure PLGA, PEG and PLGA-PEG-NH2 were recorded (Fig. 1B). The 1H
NMR spectra of PLGA showed the typical signals approximately
1.21–1.75 ppm, assigned to the methyl groups of the D- and L-lactic acid
monomers of pure PLGA and the multiplets around 5.10–5.3 and
4.7–4.85 ppm, due to the CH groups of lactic acid and CH2 groups of
glycolic acid, respectively. Whereas PLGA-PEG copolymer revealed
characteristic peak of PLGA and peak at 3.65 ppm due to the methane
proton (CH2eCH2eO)m as the characteristic peak of PEG conﬁrm the
formation of PLGA-PEG conjugate. The NHS content in the reaction
products estimated by the hydroxamate method was in the order of
88%.
3.2. Preparation of PLGA-PEG-NPs and HA-PLGA-PEG-NPs
The actively targeted HA-PLGA-PEG-NPs were developed for the site
speciﬁc delivery of cisplatin through HA mediated selective binding to
CD44 receptor over expressed on tumor cells. The double emulsion
method was used to encapsulate cisplatin in PLGA-PEG-NH2 nano-
particles and subsequently HA was conjugated to the free surface amino
group of PLGA-PEG-NH2 nanoparticles using EDC/NHS carbodiimide
chemistry (Fig. 2).
3.3. Characterization of cisplatin loaded targeted nanoparticles
3.3.1. Estimation of HA conjugated to nanoparticles
The HA was successfully anchored to the surface of nanoparticles
and it was conﬁrmed by CTAB method which is reported to be more
accurate, sensitive and speciﬁc than the carbazole method [38]. CTAB
is a cationic surfactant that binds to negatively charged polysaccharides
such as HA and form complex [39]. The turbidity due to the formation
of these insoluble complexes is proportional to the concentration of HA.
It was found that 95.4 ± 5 μg of HA was conjugated to per milligram
of nanoparticles. The conjugation of HA was also conﬁrmed by a re-
duction in zeta potential of HA functionalized nanoparticles compared
to nanoparticles without HA as described in Section 3.3.2.
3.3.2. Particle size, polydispersity and zeta potential
CP-PLGA-PEG-NPs and CP-HA-PLGA-PEG-NPs were prepared using
an emulsion solvent evaporation technique. CP-PLGA-PEG-NPs showed
mean
particle
size,
polydispersity
index
and
zeta
potential
of
169.43 ± 5.71 nm, 0.111 ± 0.033 and −4.01 ± 0.34 mV respec-

216.26 ± 4.64 nm, 0.084 ± 0.017 and −10.38 ± 0.84 mV respec-
tively (Fig. 3B and D). A slight increase in the mean diameter of HA
conjugated nanoparticles with respect to plain counterpart could be due
to the presence of HA moieties. Nanoparticles exhibited a negative zeta
potential value that was lower for hyaluronated nanoparticles com-
pared to plain counterpart, due to the negative carboxylic residues of
HA. This decrease in zeta potential value of HA-PLGA-PEG-NPs con-
ﬁrmed the localization of anionic HA polymer component on the sur-
face of nanoparticles [40].

3.3.4. Drug loading
The low drug loading has been observed in cisplatin nanoparticles
due to physico-chemical characteristics of cisplatin [21,41]. It is not
easy to encapsulate cisplatin into the hydrophobic carrier due to its
insoluble nature in organic solvent and slightly soluble nature in aqu-
eous systems. In an attempt to increase the drug loading proﬁle, cis-
platin was added in external aqueous media as reported earlier [18,21].
In order to quantify cisplatin using HPLC, cisplatin has been derivatized
with DDTC that forms hydrophobic complex, which has a maximum
absorption at 254 nm. The drug loading in case of CP-PLGA-PEG-NPs
was quantiﬁed as 4.08 ± 0.611 μg/mg nanoparticles. However, in
case of CP-HA-PLGA-PEG-NPs, the drug loading was estimated as
3.61 ± 0.707 μg/mg
nanoparticles.

3.3.5. In vitro release study
The in vitro release of cisplatin from CP-PLGA-PEG-NPs and CP-HA-
PLGA-PEG-NPs in PBS (pH 7.4 and pH 5.5) at 37 °C is shown in Fig. 4C.
The release proﬁle of cisplatin from nanoparticles revealed a biphasic
characteristic involving an initial and rapid release phase followed by a
sustained release at both pH values. The initial burst release on day one
is contributed by cisplatin fraction which was adsorbed to the surface of
the nanoparticles. The initial rapid release is also aﬀected by PEG
component of the nanoparticles, a higher PEG amount may contribute
to greater drug release. The burst release might be due to higher drug
content in the vicinity of the surface or faster drug diﬀusion or both
[21]. The sustained release is corresponding to release of the fraction of
the cisplatin content present inside the polymeric matrix. For CP-PLGA-
PEG-NPs, in the ﬁrst 48 h the drug release in order of 50% and 45% was
observed at pH 5.5 and pH 7.4, respectively. A signiﬁcant diﬀerence
(p < 0.05) in release was observed on day seven and nine at pH 5.5
(78% and 90%, respectively) as compared to pH 7.4 (66% and 82%,
respectively). In the case of CP-HA-PLGA-PEG-NPs, in the ﬁrst 48 h the
drug release in order of 56% and 47% was observed at pH 5.5 and pH
7.4, respectively. A signiﬁcant diﬀerence (p < 0.05) in release was
observed on day seven at pH 5.5 (77%) as compared to pH 7.4 (64%). It
has been observed that as compared to pH 7.4, the drug release was
slightly high at pH 5.5 (mimics the pH of tumor microenvironment)
which could be correlated to the comparatively higher polymer de-
gradation at lower pH [42]. The observed pattern of cisplatin release
from nanoparticles could be helpful for avoiding toxicity by reducing
the release in systemic circulation at pH 7.4 and favoring mass of the
release at tumors site.

that the nanoparticles would deliver many drug molecules during their
internalization by the cells, causing intracellular concentration of cis-

a previous report [43]. Although, nanoparticles oﬀer facilitated uptake
of drug, the cellular uptake of CP-PLGA-PEG-NPs could be reduced by

cisplatin-loaded nanoparticles.
3.5. Cellular uptake study
To examine the eﬀect of CD44 receptor on cellular uptake, the ex-
tent of cellular internalization of C6-PLGA-PEG-NPs and C6-HA-PLGA-
PEG-NPs by SKOV-3 cells was evaluated by CLSM. The time dependent
cellular internalization was observed (Fig. 5A). Cells treated with C6-
HA-PLGA-PEG-NPs showed very strong ﬂuorescence intensity as com-
pared to cells incubated with C6-PLGA-PEG-NPs especially after 6 h. It
indicates the cellular internalization of the coumarin-6 loaded HA-
PLGA-PEG-NPs is mainly based on a HA receptor mediated endocytosis
mechanism. It is further supported by the quantitative measurement of
cellular uptake conducted by ﬂuorescence spectroscopy (Fig. 5B). Cel-
lular uptake studies showed that there was signiﬁcantly (p < 0.05)
higher internalization of HA conjugated PLGA-PEG nanoparticles in
comparison to plain counterpart.
In order to investigate the competitive eﬀect of HA on HA-PLGA-
PEG-NPs internalization, cells were pretreated with HA before adding
nanoparticles. HA-PLGA-PEG-NPs treated group exhibited reduced
ﬂuorescence intensity. These results suggested that the enhanced cel-
lular uptake of C6-HA-PLGA-PEG-NPs was dependent on the HA-CD44
speciﬁc interaction which is in agreement with a previous report [44].
On the other hand, free HA had no interference on the ﬂuorescence
intensity of PLGA-PEG-NPs. In conclusion, CLSM study demonstrated
that C6-HA-PLGA-PEG-NPs bound to and internalized in CD44 over
expressing SKOV-3 cells in a CD44 dependent way. Therefore, it can be
assumed that HA-PLGA-PEG nanoparticles carry chemotherapeutic
agent across the cell membrane through endocytosis and target eﬃ-
ciently into the cells.

4. Conclusion
Cisplatin is one of the most potent and widely used chemother-
apeutic agents, however, its clinical applicability is compromised due to
its oﬀ-target eﬀects. Therefore, targeted delivery of cisplatin to tumor
tissue could be of great signiﬁcance. In this investigation, cisplatin
loaded targeted nanoparticles were successfully developed and char-
acterized. The targeted nanoparticles exhibited improved cytotoxicity
in CD44 receptor over-expressing cell line (SKOV-3) as compared to
non-targeted cisplatin nanoparticles. This could be attributed to the
enhanced cellular uptake of the HA-coupled nanoparticles, as con-
ﬁrmed by cellular uptake study. The advantage of site-speciﬁc accu-
mulation of the drug in the desired area was translated in the improved
in vivo eﬃcacy of the targeted nanoparticles of cisplatin. Therefore,
looking into the chemotherapeutic importance and adverse eﬀect of
cisplatin, this study, demonstrating the potential of the HA conjugated
nanoparticles in the targeted delivery of the cisplatin, could be seminal.
