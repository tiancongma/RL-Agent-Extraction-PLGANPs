## Abstract
Purpose The purpose of this study was to develop inhalable pirfenidone (PFD) nanoparticles for the delivery of the medicine
to specific alveolar epithelial cells (AECs) in the idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF) treatment. PFD has major problems
like low oral route absorption due to the presence of food, faster elimination half-life, and potent phototoxicity. PFD-loaded
poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) nanoparticles provide enhanced release of drug and reduce its toxicity, dose, and dosing frequency by targeting the AECs.
Methods The PFD-loaded PLGA NPs were prepared by a single emulsion solvent evaporation method. The preliminary
studies were carried out with process parameters like the selection of organic phase, effect of organic phase volume, effect of
stabilizer (PVA) concentration, drug:polymer ratio, and sonication time. The effects of two formulation variables including
the drug:polymer ratio and surfactant concentration were examined by using 32 full factorial design applied to get optimized
formulation. The final product was lyophilized and made free-flowing powder formulation for pulmonary administration.
Results Optimized PFD-loaded PLGA NPs showed initial burst release followed by sustained up to 71.19 +- 5.39% at
24 h. The particle size, polydispersity index, zeta potential, and % entrapment efficiency were found to be 523 +- 6.42 nm,
0.183 +- 0.011, 1.62 +- 0.22 mV, and 44.14 +- 3.74% respectively.
Conclusions There was no significant aggregation of PFD-loaded PLGA NPs during 6 months of stability study and spherical shape nanoparticle with smooth surface as per SEM study. Hence, PFD-loaded PLGA NPs can be a suitable alternative
to the currently available therapy.

Drug-Excipient Compatibility Studies

Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) and differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) were carried out by using
the drug-excipient compatibility studies.

In Vitro Drug Release Study

The release of PFD was evaluated by previously calculated
and weighed amount of dried PFD-loaded PLGA NPs (20 mg)
in 100 mL of phosphate buffer saline (PBS) solution pH 7.4.
Dialysis membrane of molecular weight cut-off of 12 kDa was
used for drug release study. The nanoparticles were poured
into a dialysis bag and kept in a basket of USP type I dissolution apparatus (shown in Fig. S2). The system was stirred at
100 rpm and was kept at 37 +- 0.5 degC . Five milliliters of
sample was collected from the medium at time intervals of
0, 0.5, 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, 12, and 24 h, and that was replenished by
fresh PBS solution. Using a UV-visible spectrophotometer
set to 311 nm, the collected samples were passed through
a 0.22-um filter membrane and evaluated for PFD content.

Sample Preparation and Analysis by FTIR The FTIR spectra
(Thermo Scientific, USA) of pure PFD, PFD with PLGA,
and the optimized formulation were recorded with KBr and
compressing the mixed to generate self-supporting discs. The
KBr press compression strength was set at 5 tons for 5 min
between 4000 and 400 cm-1; the scanning range was retained.

Sample Preparation and Analysis by DSC DSC thermograms
of pure PFD and PFD:PLGA were generated by DSC 60
(Shimadzu, Japan). The samples (PFD and PFD:PLGA)
were heated in an airtight aluminum pan at a scanning rate
of 10 degC from room temperature to 300 degC with a 1-min
hold period. To keep up in an inactive environment, a rate
of 20-mL/min nitrogen gas was purged.

Morphology by Scanning Electron Microscopy

Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) analysis of the optimized formulation was carried out for the outer surface
morphology of nanoparticles detected with SEM. The NPs
were lyophilized and fixed on metal stubs using doublefaced adhesive tape, then coated with gold to a thickness of
16 mm (Emitech K550 equipment), and viewed with a Zeiss
DSM 940. A microscope with a digital imaging captures
system (DISS of Point Electronic GmbH, Halle, Germany).
In a SEM examination, electrons are transferred from sample
particle surfaces .

Mean Particle Size and Polydispersity Index Measurement

The nanoparticle size analyzer Zetatrac (Microtrac Inc.,
USA) was utilized to examine the particle size and polydispersity index of PFD-loaded PLGA NPs. The charged particles are oscillated using a high-frequency AC electric field.
The modulated power spectrum (MPS) technique is used to
examine the Brownian motion power spectrum, which is a
component of the power spectrum originating from oscillating particles. The freshly prepared sample of NP dispersion
(containing 1% w/v NPs) was diluted with 1.0-mL water.
The PFD-loaded PLGA NPs dispersed sufficiently, and that
sample was used after being sonicated for 2 min . The
evaluations showed no sample disintegration or aggregation,
and each sample measurement was performed in triplicate.

Mathematical Modeling of Drug Release Kinetics

To understand better the mechanism of drug release from
formed nanoparticles, data from in vitro release studies were
fitted to multiple kinetic equations. The kinetic models used
were zero order, first order, Higuchi, and Korsmeyer-Peppas
model. Statistical comparisons were conducted using Microsoft Excel and one-way ANOVA. The criteria for significance were set at p < 0.05 .

Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FTIR) Study

The FTIR spectra of pure drug PFD and physical mixture of
PFD and PLGA 50:50 are shown in Fig. S3. The major peaks
shown to the drug PFD confirm the presence of different
groups. O = C stretch (a, b unsaturated ketone) at 1612 cm-1

physical mixture of PFD and PLGA. C-N aromatic stretch
vibration (3deg amine) in pure PFD, and physical mixture

stretching vibration, C-H aliphatic stretching vibration, and
C = C phenyl stretching vibration are obtained 1530 cm-1,
3054 cm-1, and 825.6 cm-1, respectively, in the narrow
range with similar intensity [4, 19]. Between the spectra
of PFD and PLGA, there was no significant shifting or loss
of functional peaks. Hence, FTIR confirmed no chemical
interaction between PFD and polymer.

Formulation of Dry Powder Inhaler

To ensure pulmonary delivery acceptability, the optimized
formulation of PFD-loaded PLGA NPs was transformed into
a dry powder inhaler (DPI). To convert the nanoparticles in
the form of a dry powder inhaler, freeze-dried nanoparticles and anhydrous inhalable grade lactose were manually
blended with a geometrical dilution technique . The bulk
density and tapped density were carried out after anhydrous
lactose mixed with optimized PFD NPs in 1:1 ratio. The
good flow feature of the dry powder inhaler was used to
optimize it. To check the modifications of nanoparticles
in the form of DPI, the optimized formulation was further
evaluated for MPS and ZP.

Aerodynamic Properties by Andersen Cascade Impactor (ACI)

Differential Scanning Calorimetric (DSC) Studies

A nonviable eight-stage Anderson cascade impactor (ACI,
Thermo Fisher Scientific Inc.) was used to measure the mass
median aerodynamic diameter (MMAD) of the optimized
PFD nanoparticles as a DPI. The nanoparticles were aerosolized as a DPI using Rotahaler device with a flow rate of
23.7 L/min through the cascade impactor. After deposition
of DPI in each chamber, the content of PFD was determined
by dissolving it in methanol and examined with a UV-visible
spectrophotometer set at lmax 311 nm. The MMAD, geometric standard diameter (GSD), and % fine particle fraction (FPF) were calculated using "MMAD Calculator." Fine
particle fraction (particles < 5 mm) represents particles with
a high possibility of entering deep into the lungs. Particles
that are extra fine (< 1.0 mm) enhance peripheral lung deposition, and inhalers with a fine particle fraction that is flow rate
independent deliver a more uniform dosage to the lungs .

Effect of Formulation Factors on In Vitro Release Studies

The drug release study of all the batches was carried out
including optimized batch as it showed the maximum EE.
The dissolution profile showed biphasic behavior consisting of initial burst release followed by a slow-release phase
(Fig. 7). The presence of surface-free drugs can be attributed
to the initial burst release in the external phase and drug
adsorbed on the surface of particles while the slow release
was due to drug encapsulated within the PLGA matrix .
According to the release pattern, a loading dosage of around
40% of the drug might be released after 2-3 h of administration as a burst release, while the rest (up to 80%) would
sustain the therapeutic dose for a long time (24 h), perhaps
preventing significant variations in the drug plasma level.
The release kinetics followed the Higuchi model
(r2 = 0.997). The drug release behavior from the polymer
matrix followed a biphasic pattern, with an initial burst followed by a slower sustained release. The release of PFD
from PLGA nanoparticles may be majorly due to the diffusion of PFD from the pores on the particle surface.

Aerodynamic Characterization

The PFD-loaded nanoparticles (optimized batch) were taken
for the in vitro aerodynamic study. The deposition of PFD
nanoparticle-loaded DPI in the different chambers based on
particle size of the cascade impactor was evaluated. The
deposition of particles in various sections of the trachea
and lungs is determined by factors such as particle size and
shape. Particle size in the range of < 1 um can reach deeper
to alveoli. The PFD-loaded PLGA nanoparticles were loaded
to cascade impactor. Fine particle fraction and extra-fine
particle fraction were found to be 71.39% and 28.83%. The
emitted dose from DPI was found to be 91.09%. FPF is
directly proportional to the in vivo drug deposition into the
lung. With a geometric standard deviation (GSD) of 1.92,
the optimized DPI revealed MMAD of the nanoparticles
particles with a size of 3.52 mm. An MMAD of less than
5 um indicates targeted delivery to the lungs, whereas a GSD
of less than 3 indicates a narrow aerodynamic diameter distribution. Hence, prepared DPI has good potential to reach
deep into the lungs.
