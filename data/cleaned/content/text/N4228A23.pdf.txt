Biodegradable
 polymeric
 particles
 have  been
 widely
 used  in  the  biomedical
 ﬁelds
 such as  drug
 carriers,
tissue engineering,
 and  medical
 imaging.
 It  has  been  recognized
 that  the  physical
 properties
 of
 particles,
such
 as  size  and  shape,
 have
 a profound
 inﬂuence
 on  their  functions.
 However,
 it  is  not  easy  to pre-
pare
 non-spherical
 particles
 by  the  conventional
 emulsion-based
 method
 because
 the  minimization
 of
interfacial
 energy
 leads
 to the  spontaneous
 formation
 of  spherical
 emulsions
 and  it is  difﬁcult
 to control
the
 uniformity
 and size.  In order  to  solve  this
 problem,
 we  developed
 a direct
 method
 to fabricate
 non-
spherical
 polylactic-co-glycolic
 acid  (PLGA)
 particles
 with  uniform  size.  This  method
 employed
 a common
agent,
 phosphate
 buffer
 saline
 (PBS),  as  the  deformation
 initiator
 and  combined
 the  premix
 membrane
emulsiﬁcation
 technique
 with
 the  solvent
 evaporation
 method
 to  prepare
 uniform
 non-spherical
 particles
of
 various
 shapes
 with aspect  ratios  from
 2 to 40  and  various
 sizes
 ranging
 from  800  nm  to  60 m  (long
axis).
 We  found
 that
 the  external
 driving
 force  of  stirring  and  the  interfacial
 tension
 of  the  droplet  dic-
tated
 by the  constitution
 of  PBS  and  polyvinyl
 alcohol
 (PVA)  have  synergistic
 effects
 on the deformation.
Through
 confocal
 laser  microscopy
 (CLSM),  the  deforming
 process
 was  observed
 visually
 in  the solvent
and
 gel  state  during  the  solidiﬁcation.
 Moreover,
 octreotide
 acetate
 as  a  model
 peptide
 was
 successfully
encapsulated
 into  the  non-spherical
 particles.

1.
 Introduction
Biodegradable
 polymeric
 particles
 have
 been
 widely
 investi-
gated
 in
 the
 biomedical
 ﬁeld
 as
 drug
 delivery
 systems
 [1,2], tissue
engineering
 scaffolds
 [3], imaging
 agents
 [4,5], etc.
 It
 has
 been
acknowledged
 that
 the
 properties
 of
 polymeric
 particles
 have
 a
profound
 inﬂuence
 on
 their
 biological
 effects
 such
 as
 degradation
proﬁle,
 bio-distribution,
 as
 well
 as
 interaction
 with
 cells
 [6–8].
 For
example,
 nanoparticles
 smaller
 than
 100
 nm
 could
 preferentially
accumulate
 and
 diffuse
 into
 tumor
 tissues
 through
 leaky
 vessels
because
 of
 the
 “enhanced
 permeability
 and
 retention”
 (EPR)
 effect
of
 tumors
 [9]. It
 was
 reported
 in
 another
 work
 that
 compared
to
 nanoparticles
 (265
 nm),
 intraperitoneal
 injected
 polylactic-co-
glycolic
 acid
 (PLGA)
 micro-particles
 (5
 m)
 could
 cause
 a
 higher
incidence
 of
 residue
 in
 the
 peritoneum
 of
 mice
 because
 of
 reduced
clearance
 [10].
In
 recent
 years,
 apart
 from
 investigating
 size
 effects,
 many
researchers
 have
 paid
 more
 attentions
 to
 the
 design
 of
 particle
shape
 and
 morphology
 and
 have
 started
 to
 explore
 the
 inﬂuence
of
 these
 new
 attributes
 on
 the
 biological
 effects.
 For
 example,
Muro
 et
 al.
 [11]
 reported
 that
 micro-sized
 elliptical
 disks
 possessed
longer
 half-lives
 in
 blood
 circulation
 compared
 to
 spherical
 parti-
cles.
 They
 speculated
 that
 this
 phenomenon
 might
 result
 from
 the
ﬂow
 alignment
 property
 of
 elliptical
 disks
 similar
 to
 long
 circulat-
ing
 discoid
 blood
 erythrocytes,
 which
 could
 reduce
 the
 chance
 of
collisions
 with
 vascular
 walls.
 Champion
 et
 al.
 [12]
 observed
 that
particle
 shape,
 rather
 than
 the
 particle
 size,
 played
 the
 dominate
role
 in
 phagocytosis,
 and
 they
 [13]
 further
 found
 that
 the
 elongated
particles
 exhibited
 reduced
 phagocytosis
 as
 a
 result
 of
 decreasing
the
 high
 curvature
 regions
 of
 spheres
 to
 two
 single
 points
 of
 the
spheroidal
 particle
 ends.
The
 shape
 and
 morphology
 have
 proven
 to
 be
 critical
 factors
that
 can
 signiﬁcantly
 inﬂuence
 particle
 function.
 However,
 they
have
 not
 been
 fully
 explored
 for
 the
 possible
 reasons
 stated
 below.
The
 fabrication
 of
 non-spherical
 biodegradable
 particles
 is
 difﬁcult
because
 the
 minimization
 of
 interfacial
 energy
 leads
 to
 the
 forma-
tion
 of
 a
 spherical
 shape
 during
 most
 of
 preparation
 processes
 [14].
Then,
 it
 is
 difﬁcult
 to
 simultaneously
 adjust
 the
 shape
 and
 morphol-
ogy.
 Moreover,
 precise
 and
 independent
 control
 over
 size,
 shape,
and
 morphology
 is
 still
 a
 challenge.
In
 recent
 years,
 several
 methods
 have
 been
 proposed
 to
produce
 non-spherical
 particles
 including
 soft
 lithography
 [15],
self-assembly
 [16], microﬂuidics
 [17], and
 ﬁlm
 stretching
 [18,19].
For
 example,
 DeSimone
 et
 al.
 [20]
 fabricated
 monodispersed
non-spherical
 polymeric
 particles
 using
 a
 method
 called
 particle
replication
 in
 nonwetting
 templates
 (PRINT).
 Photocurable
 perﬂu-
oropolyether
 (PFPE)
 molds
 with
 low
 surface
 energy
 were
 used
 to
emboss
 liquid
 precursor
 compounds
 on
 highly
 ﬂuorinated
 surfaces
to
 fabricate
 isolated
 particles
 with
 superior
 shape
 and
 compo-
sition
 control.
 However,
 because
 the
 PFPE
 molds
 were
 derived
from
 patterned
 silicon-based
 master
 templates,
 this
 method
 had
limitations
 as
 it
 required
 specialized
 equipment
 and
 a
 complex
preparation
 process.
 Other
 methods
 mentioned
 above
 also
 suffered
from
 drawbacks
 including
 complicated
 processes,
 special
 equip-
ment
 requirements,
 size
 limitations,
 and
 harmful
 conditions
 for
bioactive
 drugs.
 In
 addition,
 nearly
 none
 of
 these
 methods
 investi-
gated
 the
 possibility
 of
 simultaneous
 control
 over
 morphology
 and
shape.
 Hence,
 there
 is
 an
 urgent
 need
 to
 develop
 a
 simple,
 versatile,
scalable
 method
 to
 fabricate
 uniform
 non-spherical
 particles
 with
various
 morphologies.
In
 recent
 years,
 Heslinga
 et
 al.
 [21]
 developed
 a
 one-step
 fab-
rication
 method
 to
 produce
 prolate
 spheroidal
 PLGA
 particles
 via
the
 simple
 emulsion
 solvent
 evaporation
 method
 in
 the
 presence
of
 Tris
 base
 (Trizma)
 and
 high
 surfactant
 concentration.
 They
 found
that
 a
 high
 aqueous
 phase
 viscosity,
 a
 basic
 aqueous
 phase
 pH,
 and
hydrophilic
 end
 groups
 on
 the
 polymer
 chain
 could
 promote
 the
formation
 of
 elliptical
 particles.
 This
 method
 was
 advantageous
 in
its
 simplicity,
 high
 particle
 yield,
 and
 easy
 control.
 However,
 it
 still
had
 some
 shortcomings.
 First,
 because
 of
 the
 use
 of
 mechanical
stirring
 to
 prepare
 the
 emulsion,
 this
 method
 could
 not
 prepare
particles
 with
 nanometer
 size
 and
 guarantee
 uniformity,
 which
would
 restrict
 applications
 in
 areas
 requiring
 nanoparticles
 and
could
 cause
 poor
 repeatability
 in
 the
 preparation
 and
 investigation
of
 related
 functions.
 Moreover,
 although
 both
 rough
 and
 smooth
surfaces
 occurred
 in
 the
 fabricating
 process,
 this
 work
 did
 not
 focus
on
 the
 regulation
 of
 particle
 morphology.
Herein,
 we
 chose
 a
 more
 commonly
 used
 inorganic
 salt,
 phos-
phate
 buffer
 saline
 (PBS),
 to
 successfully
 initiate
 the
 deformation
process
 of
 emulsion
 droplet.
 By
 combining
 the
 emulsion
 solvent
evaporation
 method
 with
 the
 premix
 membrane
 emulsiﬁcation
technique,
 uniformity
 in
 size
 and
 shape
 of
 non-spherical
 particles
could
 be
 easily
 achieved.
 In
 addition,
 we  intensively
 investi-
gated
 the
 inﬂuence
 of
 process
 parameters
 on
 the
 deformation
and
 morphology
 transition,
 and
 more
 importantly,
 elucidated
 the
deformation
 mechanism
 of
 this
 approach
 based
 on
 both
 theoret-
ical
 derivation
 and
 practical
 observation.
 In
 the
 end,
 we
 chose
octreotide
 acetate
 (OCT)
 as
 a
 model
 peptide
 drug
 to
 demonstrate
the
 possibility
 of
 producing
 uniform
 peptide
 loaded
 non-spherical
particles.

increasing

 Possible
 reason
 was
 the
 competition
 between
decreased
 
 and
 increased
 electrolytes
 of
 PBS.
 The
 presence
 of
 elec-
trolytes
 could
 form
 a
 double
 electric
 layer
 around
 the
 PLGA
 droplet
by
 adsorption
 with
 the
 carboxylic
 ions
 (–COO–),
 the
 terminal
 group
of
 the
 PLGA
 polymer
 chain.
 The
 double
 electric
 layer
 was
 beneﬁ-
cial
 to
 stabilize
 emulsions
 and
 inhibit
 deformation.
 Thus,
 when
 PBS
concentration
 increased
 beyond
 5
 mM,
 the
 degree
 of
 decrease
 in

 (Fig.
 1a)
 became
 smaller,
 thus
 the
 increased
 electrolytes
 effect
played
 the
 dominate
 role.
 Similar
 results
 were
 also
 found
 in
 the
studies
 by
 Heslinga
 [21]
 and
 Li
 [25]. They
 found
 that
 when
 the
Trizma
 and
 STP
 initiator
 concentrations
 were
 increased
 beyond
a
 critical
 value,
 the
 percentage
 of
 deformed
 particles
 decreased,
probably
 as
 a
 result
 of
 the
 increased
 amount
 of
 electrolytes
 present
in
 the
 aqueous
 phase
 at
 higher
 Trizma
 and
 STP
 concentrations.
In
 our
 system,
 PBS
 is
 an
 important
 molecule
 for
 initiation
 of
deformation
 in
 the
 emulsion
 solvent
 evaporation
 method.
 It
 is
cheap
 and
 easy
 to
 obtain,
 and
 without
 PBS,
 only
 the
 spherical
shape
 has
 been
 fabricated.
 To
 our
 knowledge,
 it
 is
 the
 ﬁrst
 time
an
 inorganic
 molecule
 has
 been
 used
 to
 initiate
 emulsion
 deforma-
tion.
 Existing
 literature
 has
 focused
 on
 the
 effects
 of
 organic
 small
molecules
 such
 as
 Tris,
 STP,
 glycerol,
 and
 epirubicin
 as
 deformation
initiators
 in
 the
 emulsion
 solvent
 evaporation
 method
 [21,27,28].
These
 organic
 molecules
 were
 proven
 to
 facilitate
 the
 formation
of
 hydrogen
 bonds
 with
 the
 carboxyl
 groups
 of
 polymers
 such
 as
PLA,
 and
 could
 thus,
 initiate
 droplet
 deformation.
 Here,
 we
 spec-
ulated
 that
 the
 PBS
 might
 change
 the
 interfacial
 environment
 by
forming
 hydrogen
 bonds
 with
 the
 –COOH
 group
 of
 the
 PLGA
 chain.
Therefore,
 PBS
 could
 reduce
 the
 interfacial
 tension
 between
 the
hydrophobic
 PLGA
 droplets
 and
 the
 outer
 water
 phase.
 On
 the
other
 hand,
 the
 PLGA
 emulsions
 could
 be
 stabilized
 by
 the
 effect
 of
hydration
 layer
 provided
 by
 the
 PVA,
 which
 could
 resist
 the
 defor-
mation
 by
 the
 mechanical

 force.
 However,
 the
 ionization
and
 hydrolysis

 NaH2PO4 and

 deprive
 the
 water
molecule,
 resulting
 in
 the
 decreased
 thickness
 of
 hydration
 layer.
Moreover,
 another
 possible
 reason
 might
 be
 the
 inﬂuence
 of
 PBS
on
 the
 solubility
 of
 methylene
 chloride
 in
 the
 water
 phase,
 which
could
 affect
 the
 stretching
 and
 solidiﬁcation
 time.
 However,
 the
detailed
 effects
 of
 PBS
 still
 need
 to
 be
 investigated
 in
 the
 future.
In
 addition,
 PBS
 is
 a
 mixture
 of
 salts
 consisting
 of
 Na2HPO4 and
NaH2PO4. Adjusting
 the
 amount
 of
 the
 two
 kinds
 of
 salts
 could
change
 the
 pH
 of
 the
 water
 phase
 and
 lead
 to
 various
 surface
 mor-
phologies
 (Fig.
 3a–d),
 which
 was
 another

 of

The

 for
 this
 transformation
 in

 could
be
 attributed
 to
 the
 hydrophilicity
 of
 PLGA
 polymer

 and
 the
mobility
 of
 the
 polymer

 Despite
 the
 general
 hydrophobic
nature
 of
 PLGA,
 its
 surface

 could
 be
 tuned
 by

ing
 environmental

 [29]. Speciﬁcally,

 ∼5,

 of

 (pKa =

 were

 charged
 (–COO–),
 leading
to
 low

 and
 high
 hydrophilicity.
 Then,
 the
 thorns
were
 formed
 along
 with
 the
 diffusion
 of
 methylene
 chloride
 into
the
 water
 at
 the
 low
 pH.
 However,
 with
 the
 pH
 increasing,
 the
interfacial
 tension
 was
 decreased.
 At
 the
 highest

 although
 the
thorns
 were
 formed,
 the
 mobility
 of
 matrix
 diminished
 the
 large
thorns
 formation.
 Therefore,
 the
 particles
 showed
 smooth
 surface
as
 eggs.
 However,
 more
 works
 will
 be
 done
 in
 the
 future
 to
 deeply
investigate
 the
 effect
 of
 pH
 on
 the
 particle
 shape
 and
 morphology.
Besides
 the
 inﬂuence
 of

 was
another

 for

 As

the

 with

rate.

 easy
 to
 understand
 either

the

 “driving

 or

 the
 positive
 relationship
between
 Ca
 value

 shear
 rate

 in

independently
 and

 by

 As

 the

 the
 
 value
and

smaller

 when

using

 the

 d, therefore

 the

 In
