Abstract
Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF) is a progressive lung disease of unknown cause, with few effective therapies available
and high mortality rates. Our preceding research indicated that formononetin (FMN) could improve the symptoms of the
bleomycin-induced pulmonary fibrosis and be a promising drug against IPF. In this study, an inhalable formononetin-loaded
poly(lactic-co-glycolic) acid (PLGA) large porous microspheres (FMN-PLGA-MSs) was prepared by the method of emul-
sion solvent evaporation. SEM showed that FMN-PLGA-MSs were loose particles existing many pores on the surfaces,
and the measured mean geometric diameter was more than 10 µm. The encapsulation efficiency (EE) and drug loading
efficiency (DL) were 87.72 ± 6.34% and 4.18 ± 0.30%. FMN in FMN-PLGA-MSs could be rapidly released within 2 h and
sustainably released for 21 d. Cell tests and q-RT-PCR tests showed that FMN could inhibit the activation of fibroblasts
and the deposition of extracellular matrix (ECM) by acting on the TGF-β1/Smad3 signaling pathway. FMN-PLGA-MSs
showed higher antifibrotic effects than free FMN oral administration in the pulmonary fibrosis models of mice, remarkably
improving pulmonary function, decreasing hydroxyproline levels, and attenuating lung injuries. By formulating formonon-
etin into microsphere preparations, its solubility can be significantly enhanced, enabling effective pulmonary drug delivery.
This approach not only improves lung targeting but also reduces systemic toxicity. Additionally, it facilitates superior lung
deposition and extends the retention time of the formononetin within the lungs. Taken together, FMN-PLGA-MSs may be
a promising inhaled medication for the treatment of IPF.
Keywords  Formononetin · Large porous microspheres · Poly(lactic-co-glycolic) acid · Pulmonary fibrosis
Introduction
Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF) is often considered
the most severe and lethal progressive interstitial lung dis-
ease [1, 2]. It is distinguished by excessive proliferation of
fibroblasts and deposition of extracellular matrix (ECM),
which accelerates patient morbidity and mortality [3, 4]. The
median survival of patients with IPF is 2.8 years [5]. Due to
our limited understanding of the underlying pathobiologi-
cal mechanisms of IPF, the treatment presents a series of
clinical challenges [6]. The Food and Drug Administration
(FDA) has only approved two drugs, pirfenidone and nin-
tedanib, for the treatment of mild and moderate IPF [7, 8].
However, these two drugs are only capable of mitigating the
decline in pulmonary function among patients, yet they fail
to enhance survival rates and are associated with significant

In order to better combat IPF, many new drugs have been
researched and developed. For instance, vardenafil [11, 12],
ifetroban [13], and yinfenidone hydrochloride [14] have all
shown potential as therapeutic agents. Furthermore, extracts
obtained from natural plants, such as curcumin [15] and
gypenosides [16], have shown good anti-pulmonary fibrosis
therapeutic effects both in vitro and in vivo.
Formononetin (FMN), an isoflavone compound with low
polarity, is isolated from natural plants, such as red clover
[17]. FMN is typically abundant in a variety of edible plants,
such as Mongolian Milkvetch Root, Liquorice, Reticulate
Millettia, and others. The active compound has demonstrated
extensive practical value in the fields of food, cosmetics and
medicine [18–20]. According to Juliana et al., formononetin
has shown good qualities in anticancer, anti-inflammatory,
antimicrobial and antiviral, antioxidant, neuroprotective, and
so on [21]. It has been reported that formononetin can sig-
nificantly improve renal fibrosis, liver fibrosis and cardiac
fibrosis, and so on [22–24]. Our previous research found that
FMN could improve the symptoms of IPF and might be a
promising drug for IPF treatment. However, the poor water
solubility and the low bioavailability of FMN limit its clini-
cal application. Improving the water solubility and targeting
of FMN through formulation methods is a feasible approach
to enhance its IPF treatment effect.
Pulmonary administration is an effective drug delivery
system for the therapy of lung diseases, such as asthma,
emphysema, pneumonia, pulmonary fibrosis, and chronic
obstructive pulmonary disease [25]. Compared with tra-
ditional oral and injection, pulmonary drug delivery is an
efficient delivery method for lung diseases. It can produce
a high local concentration at the lung pathological site with
smaller drug dose and lower systemic side effects [26].
With the aim of reducing drug phagocytosis by mac-
rophages and improving the therapeutic effect when applied
via pulmonary administration, various formulations have
been designed [27]. Compared with other novel drug deliv-
ery systems, microspheres can achieve higher drug loading
and better sustained-release effect [28]. So far, more than
10 types of microsphere preparations have been introduced
to the market. Porous microspheres are a dosage form for
pulmonary drug delivery. In the process of microsphere
preparation, an appropriate amount of pore-forming agent
is added to produce drug-loaded porous microspheres with
low density (less than 0.4 g/mL) and large geometric par-
ticle size (greater than 10 μm) [29]. These characteristics
can avoid phagocytosis by pulmonary macrophages, slow
down particle aggregation, deposit the drug in the deep
lung, provide sustained release, and improve the thera-
peutic effect of the drug [30–32]. The carrier for prepar-
ing porous microspheres includes biodegradable polymers
such as polylactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA), albumin, algi-
nate, chitosan, and lipids such as dipalmitoylphosphatidyl-
choline and triglycerides, among which PLGA is the most
commonly used carrier [33–35]. PLGA is a functional poly-
mer compound synthesized from polylactic acid (PLA) and
polyglycolic acid (PGA) in a certain proportion. It has good
biocompatibility, degradability, non-toxicity, and has been
approved by the FDA for drug delivery [36, 37]. Hu et al.
prepared PLGA porous microspheres containing curcumin
for pulmonary delivery in the treatment of IPF. In vivo
studies showed that curcumin-loaded porous microspheres
significantly reduced the hydroxyproline content induced
by bleomycin, decreased the synthesis of collagen I, and
exhibited better anti-fibrotic effects compared to curcumin
powder [15]. Porous microspheres loading RLX (Recombi-
nant Human Relaxin-2) was prepared by Liu, which not only
reduced excessive collagen deposition, but also reversed
fibrosis to some extent [38].
Here, we prepared FMN-loaded PLGA large porous
microspheres (FMN-PLGA-MSs) for the treatment of ble-
omycin-induced pulmonary fibrosis via intratracheal instil-
lation. We characterized the FMN-PLGA-MSs, including
particle size, drug loading efficiency and encapsulation effi-
ciency. Furthermore, we evaluated the in vitro release pro-
files and uptake by fibroblasts. We revealed the anti-fibrotic
theory of FMN through measuring a train of in vitro mark-
ers in the cells, including Collagen I, α-SMA, fibronectin.
Finally, the therapeutic efficacy and in vivo biodistribution of
FMN-PLGA-MSs were demonstrated in a mouse model. It
is expected that FMN-PLGA-MSs can provide a new thera-
peutic strategy for IPF treatment.

Preparation of FMN‑PLGA‑MSs
FMN-PLGA-MSs were prepared by water-in-oil-in-water
(w/o/w) emulsion solvent evaporation method [39]. In short,
PLGA (120 mg) and FMN (6 mg) were dissolved in dichlo-
romethane (2 mL), then an AB solution (1% w/v, 1 mL) was
added and was mixed uniformly. The resulting solution was
sonicated in the ice bath at an amplitude of 60% for 2 min
(1 s, 1 s) to form the W/O emulsion. Then, the PVA solution
(1% w/v, 18 mL) was mixed into the emulsion. The above
system was homogenized using high shear homogenizer for
1 min at 3500 rpm to attain W/O/W emulsions. Organic
solvent was evaporated at room temperature under mechani-
cal stirring at a speed of 250 rpm overnight. At last, the
final emulsions were centrifuged at 6000 rpm for 10 min
and were lyophilized by freeze dryer (LGJ-12A, Beijing
Sihuan Qihang Technology). The cold well temperature was
−65℃ and the vacuum degree was 0–20 Pa in the freeze-
drying process. Blank PLGA MSs without FMN, as well as
Coumarin-6-loaded and Cy7-loaded PLGA MSs, were also
elaborated using the same method as mentioned above.
Characterization of FMN‑PLGA‑MSs
The volume diameters of FMN-PLGA-MSs were measured
by dynamic light scattering (Mastersizer 2000, Malvern,
U.K.).
The crystal states of samples (including FMN-PLGA-
MSs, blank PLGA-MSs, PLGA, free FMN, the physical
mixture of FMN/PLGA, and the physical mixture of FMN/
blank PLGA-MSs) were measured by X-ray diffractometer
(XRD, Ultima IV, Rigaku, Japan). For XRD analysis, a cop-
per tube was powered at 40 kV and 40 mA, the scanning θ
range was from 10° to 40° at a trend of 0.02°, and the scan-
ning step size was 10°/min.
The preliminary surface morphology of FMN-PLGA-
MSs were observed using optical microscope (Ni-E, Nikon,
Japan) at 40 × magnification. The detailed surface morpholo-
gies of FMN-PLGA-MSs were observed by field-emission
scanning electron microscope (SEM, JSM-7800F, JEOL,
Japan). The FMN-PLGA-MSs were fixed on a metal plate
using a double-sided conductive adhesive and then sputter-
coated with gold under in a vacuum. Images were captured
under a voltage of 15.0 kV and a beam curret of 200 nA.
The FT-IR spectrometer (TENSOR 37, BRUKER, Ger-
many) was used to evaluate the interaction between polymer
and drug. Besides, FT-IR spectra of PLGA, FMN-PLGA-
MSs, blank PLGA-MSs, FMN powders, the physical mix-
ture of FMN/PLGA and the physical mixture of FMN/blank
PLGA-MSs were recorded in the range of 4000 to 400 ­cm−1.

In Vitro Release Study
The drug release profiles of FMN from FMN-PLGA-
MSs were studied using the dialysis method. Free FMN
and FMN-PLGA-MSs were individually placed in 2 mL
of simulated lung fluid (SLF) [40]. These solutions were
transferred to dialysis bags (molecular weight cut-off of
12,000–14,000 Da), which were suspended in 40 mL of SLF.
The suspensions were placed on a thermostatic oscillator at
37 ± 0.5℃ and 150 rpm. 1 mL SLF was sampled to detect the
drug concentration by UV–vis spectrophotometry at 301 nm
and 1 mL of fresh SLF was added back at pre-specified time
points.

Cellular Uptake
We used qualitative and quantitative methods to evalu-
ate the uptake of FMN-PLGA-MSs in MLg and NIH-3T3
cells. For the qualitative method, we seeded MLg and NIH-
3T3 cells onto slides, and placed in 24-well plates, then
cultured at 37°C and 5% ­CO2 for 24 h. Further, cells were
incubated with Coumarin-6-loaded PLGA microspheres
(C6-PLGA-MSs) for different time periods (0, 5, 15, 30,
60, and 120 min). After incubation, the plates were rinsed
three times with PBS, fixed with 4% (w/v) paraformaldehyde
for 10 min at room temperature, and then stained with DAPI
for another 3 min. Finally, the cells were rinsed three times
with PBS. At last, the coverslips were mounted and observed
using confocal laser scanning microscopy (TCS SP8, Leica,
German).
For the quantitiative method, we measured the cellular
uptake of C6-PLGA-MSs using the flow cytometric. MLg
and NIH-3T3 cells were incubated with C6-PLGA-MSs for
30, 60, and 120 min. After incubation, the cells were rinsed
with PBS, trypsinized for 2 min, harvested, rinsed again with
PBS and analyzed by LSR Fortessa flow cytometer (Becton,
Dickinson and Company, USA).
Animal Study
We used the method of endotracheal injection of bleomycin,
as described previously, to establish a mouse model of pul-
monary fibrosis. Healthy mice were given saline as a control.
The BLM (2 U) solutions, which were dissolved in normal
saline, were endotracheally injected into the other mice for
7 consecutive days to induce pulmonary fibrosis.
Forty-two mice were equally divided into seven groups:
control group, model group, 100 mg/kg NDNB group,
50 mg/kg FMN group, 100 mg/kg FMN group, 25 mg/kg
FMN-PLGA-MSs group and 50 mg/kg FMN-PLGA-MSs
group. On the 7th day after bleomycin injection, the mice
in the NDNB group and the free FMN group were orally
administered NDNB and FMN, respectively. The mice in
the FMN-PLGA-MSs group were instilled intratrache-
ally with FMN-PLGA-MSs, using a small animal aerosol
drug delivery device (HY-LWH03, YSKD Bio-technology,
Beijing, China). No treatment was administered for the
control group. On the 14th day, pulmonary function was
tested in the mice. The high-resolution lung scans in mice
were acquired using computed tomography (CT, NEMO II,
PINGSHENG Healthcare Inc., China), and analyzed with
three-dimensional (3D) finite element algorithms (AVA-
TAR3, PINGSENG Healthcare Inc., China). Then the mice
were sacrificed after the completion of the bleomycin treat-
ment. The lung tissues were fixed by immersion in the 4%
(w/v) paraformaldehyde solution and further investigated
using H&E and Masson staining.

In Vivo Biodistribution Study
We used Cy7 as a fluorescence probe to observe the bio-

Statistical Analysis
We used GraphPad Prism 8.0.1 software for statistical
analysis. The two-tailed Student’s t-test was utilized to
compare differences between the two groups, and one-way
analysis of variance (ANOVA) was used for comparisons
among three or more groups. Results were expressed as
mean ± standard deviation. In all analysis, p < 0.05 was
ranked implying important.
Results and Discussion
Preparation and Characterization
FMN-PLGA-MSs were prepared by the water-in-oil-in-
water (W/O/W) emulsion solvent evaporation method [43].
Figure 1a-b showed that the FMN-PLGA-MSs suspension
was uniform, and the lyophilized FMN-PLGA-MSs were
white loose particles. The final FMN-PLGA-MSs con-
tained 4.18 ± 0.30% FMN with a high encapsulation effi-
ciency of 87.72 ± 6.34%.
The FMN-PLGA-MSs had a mean geometric size of
3.335 µm (D10), 13.041 μm (D50), 25.339 µm (D90) and
a span of 1.687 (Fig. 1c).
The microscopic image showed the homogeneous size
distribution of FMN-PLGA-MSs (Fig. 1d). The scanning
electron microscopy (SEM) images showed that FMN-
PLGA-MSs were uniform in size and smooth spheres with
a lot of pores and channels on the surfaces (Fig. 1e).
X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis showed that the crys-
tal structure of the FMN-PLGA-MSs was distinct from
those of the free FMN powder, the physical mixture of
FMN and blank PLGA microspheres, and the physical
mixture of FMN and PLGA (Fig. 1f). Free FMN existed
in a crystalline form, and the specific peaks of FMN were
still present in the physical mixtures but absent in the
FMN-PLGA-MSs. This indicated that FMN adopted an
amorphous form with high dispersion within the PLGA
microspheres due to the high solubility of FMN in PLGA
polymers [44].
Figure 1g revealed the FTIR spectra of free FMN, free
PLGA, and FMN-PLGA-MSs in the wavenumber range
of 400–4000 ­cm−1. In the spectra of FMN-PLGA-MSs,
the characteristic peaks of FMN at 1247 ­cm−1 (vAr-O) and
1637 ­cm−1 (vC=O) were not clearly visible, the character-
istic peak of PLGA at 2950 ­cm−1 (vC–H) and 1760 ­cm−1
(vC=O) were clearly observed and significantly increased,
the above results indicated that FMN was encapsulated into
PLGA MSs successfully. The increased peak intensity might
be attributed to hydrophobic interaction between the ben-
zene ring of FMN and the long carbon chain of PLGA. The
hydrophobic interaction was beneficial for the compatibility
between the carrier material and drug, and stabilized the
FMN-PLGA-MSs.
Release Test
The drug release of FMN from FMN-PLGA-MSs in the SLF
was investigated (Fig. 1h). The results showed that FMN
released from FMN-PLGA-MSs for more than 21 days.
FMN-PLGA-MSs showed a faster FMN release than free
FMN in the early stage. Briefly, about 11.40% of FMN
was rapidly released from FMN-PLGA-MSs in the first
2 h, whereas the initial burst release of free FMN was only
3.40%. Furthermore, FMN in FMN-PLGA-MSs was sustain-
ably released until the 21st day, and nearly 67.23% FMN was
released from FMN-PLGA-MSs.
The rapid release of FMN from FMN-PLGA-MSs may
be attributed to the highly exposed FMN adsorbed on the
surface of FMN-PLGA-MSs, where numerous channels pro-
vide release pathways for the embedded FMN [36, 45]. The
rapid release allows FMN to enter lung tissues and achieve
sufficient accumulation before the FMN-PLGA-MSs are
phagocytized by lung macrophages. In addition, the release
behavior of MN-PLGA-MSs has a certain relationship with
the particle size. When the microspheres have a wide parti-
cle size distribution, the smaller microspheres aggravate the
burst release of the group, while the larger microspheres lead
to a slow release rate in the later stage, which makes the slow
release effect. Slow-release properties of FMN-PLGA-MSs
could be beneficial for long-term treatment of IPF.

Inhibition of the TGF‑β1/Smad3 Signaling Pathway
TGF-β1 is known as one of the most important pro-fibrotic
factors and plays an important central regulatory role in the
pathogenesis of pulmonary fibrosis. The luciferase reporter
gene assay was used to detect the effect of FMN and FMN-
PLGA-MSs in inhibiting TGF-β1/Smad3 signaling pathway.
In Fig. 2b, TGF-β1 group had the highest fluorescence inten-
sity, indicating CAGA-NIH-3T3 cells expressed the highest
amount of luciferase protein. After treatment with FMN and
FMN-PLGA-MSs for 18 h, the fluorescence intensity signifi-
cantly decreased, indicating that FMN could inhibit TGF-β1/
Smad3 signaling pathway to downregulate luciferase pro-
tein and improve cell fibrosis. Moreover, the fluorescence
reduction intensity of high-dose 5 μM FMN group and 5 μM
FMN-PLGA-MS group was higher than that of low-dose
10 μM FMN group and 10 μM FMN-PLGA-MS group,
respectively, indicating that FMN improved CAGA-NIH3T3
cells fibrosis in a dose-dependent manner. The fluorescence
reduction intensities of FMN-PLGA-MSs groups were not
as high as those of FMN groups, which might be due to
the sustained-release effect of microspheres. The release of
FMN from microspheres took time, while FMN-PLGA-MSs
treatment of cells only lasted for 18 h, and FMN was not
released in time, resulting in less therapeutic effect than free
FMN groups.
Inhibition on Migration of Lung Fibroblasts
Wound healing assay is often used to verify the effects of
free FMN and FMN-PLGA-MSs on TGF-β1-induced fibro-
blast migration. In Fig. 2c-d, compared with TGF-β1 group,
both FMN and FMN-PLGA-MSs inhibited fibroblast migra-
tion, with the FMN-PLGA-MSs group exhibiting a stronger
inhibitory effect than the FMN group. These results indicate
that FMN and FMN-PLGA-MSs can inhibit the migration
of fibrotic MLg cells and NIH-3T3 cells, consistent with
the above results of the TGF-β1/Smad3 signaling pathway
inhibition.
Attenuation on TGF‑β1‑activated Lung Fibroblasts
TGF-β1 is the most critical fibroblast-activating factor
involved in the development of IPF. To investigate whether
FMN could inhibit TGF-β1-induced fibroblast activation,
we cultured MLg cells with TGF-β1 (5 ng/mL), free FMN
(10 μM, 20 μM) and FMN-PLGA-MSs (10 μM, 20 μM)
to evaluate the gene expression levels of α-SMA, colla-
gen I and fibronectin. As shown in Fig. 3, both free FMN
and FMN-PLGA-MSs could decrease TGF-β1-stimulated
mRNA expression levels of a-SMA, collagen I and fibronec-
tin. These results suggest that FMN act was an inhibitor
of TGF-β1/Smad3 signaling pathway, which could inhibit
TGF-β1-induced myofibroblast activation and downregulate
extracellular matrix deposition. After encapsulating FMN
into FMN-PLGA-MSs, it had the same inhibitory effect
on TGF-β1-induced lung fibroblast activation and ECM
accumulation.

and NIH-3T3 cells at different time points. MLg and NIH-

slight green fluorescence appeared in MLg and NIH-3T3
cells, indicating that C6-PLGA-MSs could be taken up by
fibroblasts in a short time. With the extension of incubation

and strong fluorescence appeared at 120 min. Meanwhile,
flow cytometry results showed the similar trend (Fig. 4b).
The above results indicate that PLGA microspheres can be
rapidly and efficiently internalized by fibroblasts [48].

Pulmonary Fibrosis Inhibition
The therapeutic effect of FMN-PLGA-MSs was evaluated
in a mouse model of BLM-induced pulmonary fibrosis [50].
Seven days after injection of bleomycin, mice were adminis-
tered different doses of FMN (50, 100 mg/kg), FMN-PLGA-
MSs (25, 50 mg/kg), or NDNB (100 mg/kg) once daily.
Based on their safety profile in mice, FMN-PLGA-MSs
were instilled intratracheally into the lungs using a small
animal aerosol drug delivery device at lower doses. NDNB,
a drug used to treat IPF, was used as a positive control.
Fourteen days after BLM injection, changes in pulmonary
function were measured (Fig. 5a). BLM-induced pulmonary
fibrosis led to an increase in inspiratory resistance (Ri) and
expiratory resistance (Re), along with an obvious reduc-
tion in forced vital capacity (FVC) and dynamic compli-
ance (Cdyn). The pulmonary function of FMN-PLGA-MSs
(50 mg/kg) treated mice was significantly improved, with
effects superior to those of free FMN (50, 100 mg/kg) and
comparable to those of NDNB (100 mg/kg). In addition,
Fig. 5b showed that the FMN-PLGA-MSs could alleviate
BLM-induced weight loss compared with the model group,
the alleviative effects of FMN-PLGA-MSs (25, 50 mg/kg)
were similar to those of NDNB (100 mg/kg).
The lung tissue structure of healthy mice was continuous
and intact, and the alveoli had distinct alveolar cavities. The
lung tissue of the BLM-induced mice was damaged and the
alveolar walls were thickened. As shown in Fig. 5c, FMN-
PLGA-MSs (50 mg/kg) were significantly decreased the
percentage of fibrotic areas and hydroxyproline (HYP), the
reduction effect was similar to NDNB [51]. The same results
were observed in CT images (Fig. 5d). The lungs in saline
group appeared dark on CT images and the 3D image simu-
lated by the software was a complete lung tissue, indicating
the pulmonary function was good. The gray area of the lungs
in BLM group increased significantly and the 3D image sim-
ulated by the software was a defective lung tissue, indicating
the lung had developed fibrosis seriously. Compared with the
BLM group, the gray area of the lungs in FMN groups and
FMN-PLGA-MSs groups showed significant improvements,
and the 3D images were more complete. It was worth noting
that FMN-PLGA-MSs (50 mg/kg) with intratracheal instil-
lation had the best therapeutic effect, which were similar to
NDNB (100 mg/kg).
Furthermore, HE staining and Masson staining were
used to detect the histological status of lung tissue samples
[52]. HE staining (Fig. 6a) revealed that FMN-PLGA-MSs
(50 mg/kg) had a stronger effect in attenuating the varia-
tion in alveoli and the infiltration of pulmonary interstitial
mesenchyme. Besides, the mice lungs in the FMN-PLGA-
MSs (50 mg/kg) group had similar to the that of healthy
mice. Masson staining (Fig. 6b) showed similar results. Both
FMN-PLGA-MSs and FMN reduced collagen production
in lung tissues in IPF, with the former exhibiting a stronger
effect.
The above results suggest that FMN-PLGA-MSs even at
a lower dose, show a stronger anti-pulmonary fibrosis effect
than free FMN.

In Vivo Biodistribution Study
In vivo imaging system was used to detect the tissue distribution
of microspheres in vivo, mice were administered with Cy7-
PLGA-MSs by intratracheal instillation, the results were shown
in Fig. 7. The fluorescence intensity indicates that Cy7-PLGA-

other major organs such as the heart, liver and kidneys. Almost
all of the fluorescence was observed in the lungs, while other
major organs showed low or almost no fluorescence intensity.
The retention time of Cy7-PLGA-MSs in the lungs was up to
48 h, which was conducive to targeted treatment of pulmonary
fibrosis, reducing side effects. However, the retention time of
Cy7-PLGA-MSs in the lungs was not long enough. Next, we
will focus on improving the pulmonary release of FMN and
prolonging its pulmonary retention time, so as to reduce the
frequency of administration and improve patient adaptability.

avoiding the systemic side effects associated with tradi-
