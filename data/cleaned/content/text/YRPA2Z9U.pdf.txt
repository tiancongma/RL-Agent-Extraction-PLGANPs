Auranofin‑loaded PLGA nanoparticles alleviate cognitive deficit
induced by streptozotocin in rats model: modulation of oxidative
stress, neuroinflammatory markers, and neurotransmitters
Shiv Kumar Kushawaha1 · Mahendra Singh Ashawat2 · Ashish Baldi1 
Received: 10 April 2024 / Accepted: 20 June 2024 / Published online: 5 July 2024
© The Author(s), under exclusive licence to Springer-Verlag GmbH Germany, part of Springer Nature 2024
Abstract
Alzheimer’s disease remains an unsolved neurological puzzle with no cure. Current therapies offer only symptomatic relief,
hindered by limited uptake through the blood–brain barrier. Auranofin, an FDA-approved compound, exhibits potent anti-
oxidative and anti-inflammatory properties targeting brain disorders. Yet, its oral bioavailability challenge prompts the
exploration of nanoformulation-based solutions enhancing blood–brain barrier penetrability. The study aimed to investigate
the neuroprotective potential of auranofin nanoparticles in streptozotocin-induced AD rats. Auranofin-containing polylactic-
co-glycolic acid nanoparticles were formulated by the multiple emulsion solvent evaporation method. Characterization was
done by determining entrapment efficiency, particle size distribution, surface charge, and morphology. An in vivo study was
performed by administering streptozotocin (3 mg/kg/i.c.v., days 1 and 3), auranofin (5 and 10 mg/kg), auranofin nanoparticles
(2.5 and 5 mg/kg), and donepezil (2 mg/kg) for 14 days orally. Behavioral deficits were evaluated using the open field test,
Morris water maze, objective recognition test, change in oxidative stress levels, and AD markers in the brain. Following the
decapitation of the rats, the brains were excised to isolate the hippocampus. Subsequent analyses included the quantification
of biochemical and neuroinflammatory markers, as well as the assessment of neurotransmitter levels. The characterization of
auranofin nanoparticles showed an entrapment efficiency of 98%, an average particle size of 101.5 ± 10.3 nm, a surface charge
of 27.5 ± 5.10 mV, and a polydispersity index of 0.438 ± 0.12. In vivo, administration of auranofin and auranofin nanopar-
ticles significantly reversed streptozotocin-induced cognitive deficits, biochemical alteration, neuroinflammatory markers,
and neurotransmitter levels. The present finding suggests that auranofin nanoparticles have more significant neuroprotective
potential than auranofin alone. The therapeutic efficacy may be attributed to its antioxidant and anti-inflammatory proper-
ties, as well as its positive neuromodulatory effects. Therefore, our findings suggest that it could be a promising candidate
for Alzheimer’s disease therapy.

Introduction
Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is an age-linked morbidity char-
acterized by changes in amyloid peptide leads to cognitive
impairment and dementia (Beltagy et al. 2024). It is the
most common cause of dependency, disability, and mortal-
ity among older people, which imposes a substantial social
and economic burden on the community. Approximately 50
million individuals worldwide are affected by dementia, with
nearly two-thirds of cases attributed to AD, positioning AD
as the seventh leading cause of global mortality (Kaplan
et al. 2021). The primary manifestations noted in AD across
various stages encompass lapses in current event recall, a

gradual decline in memory function, behavioral degrada-

loss of communication skills over time (Lane et al. 2018;
Nichols and Szoeke 2019; Sagbakken et al. 2018). Ulti-

tion of activities within the context of daily routines. The
pathological progression of the disease is characterized by
the extracellular accumulation of toxic proteins, specifically
amyloid-β, the presence of intracellular phosphorylated tau
proteins forming neurofibrillary tangles, significant synap-

and Meyer-Luehmann 2020; Knopman et al. 2021; Schiel

and dysregulation of neuroinflammatory cytokines within
the cerebral milieu (Alhowail 2024; Gurram et al. 2023).
Moreover, disease progression entails the degeneration of
cholinergic neurons, leading to a reduction in acetylcholine
(ACh) concentration, particularly within the hippocampus
and neocortex (Abolhasani et al. 2023).
The intracerebroventricular (i.c.v.) administration of
sub-diabetogenic streptozotocin (STZ) in the animal model
serves as a reliable method for inducing AD-like dementia
in rats. This induction leads to prolonged deficits in brain
glucose, decreased concentrations of hippocampal choline
acetyltransferase (ChAT), enhanced acetylcholinesterase
(AChE) function, and progressive deficits in learning, mem-
ory, and cognitive behavior (Yuliani et al. 2021). Addition-
ally, this model results in significant deposition of amyloid β
(Aβ), hyperphosphorylation of tau, formation of neurofibril-
lary tangles, and a decline in synaptic connectivity (Gong
et al. 2020; Naderi et al. 2018; Xu et al. 2018). Furthermore,
STZ administration triggers oxidative stress, neuroinflamma-
tion such as tumor necrosis factor and interleukin (TNF-α
and IL-6), and neurochemical alterations in dopamine (DA),
norepinephrine (NE), and serotonin (5-HT3), thereby expe-
diting the onset of AD (Hira et al. 2019). The characteristics
observed in the STZ experimental model closely resemble
those encountered in AD.
Unfortunately, FDA-approved therapies such as cholinest-
erase inhibitors, and N-methyl-D-aspartate receptor antago-
nists have proved their efficacy in reducing the symptoms of
AD but none of them can able to halt the disease progression
and stop the brain cell death associated with AD (Cummings
et al. 2019). In essence, there exists a compelling necessity
for a novel therapeutic approach aimed at constraining the
progression of AD.
Auranofin (AF) is a sulfur-containing compound
chemically named as 2,3,4,6-tetra-O-acetyl-l-thio-β-D-
glucopyrano-sato-S-(triethyl-phosphine). Corroborating
this, a study has explained that gold compounds, specifi-
cally auranofin, previously approved for the treatment of
rheumatoid arthritis, demonstrate significant potential as
agents with prospective anticancer, antiprotozoal, antiviral,
antifungal, and antibacterial properties (Bolan et al. 2012;
Elkashif and Seleem 2020; Fifer et al. 2016; Schäberle and
Hack 2014; Thangamani et al. 2017).
Furthermore, a study has reported that AF elicits positive
effects by activating antioxidant enzymes and anti-inflam-
matory mechanisms. These actions contribute to preventing
changes in neurotransmitter levels, enhancing mitochondrial
activity, and reducing protein accumulation in neurons (Wall
et al. 2021). Notably, in previous studies, AF has undergone
in vitro and in vivo investigations to elucidate its neuro-
protective properties. These investigations specifically tar-
geted the attenuation of glia-mediated neuroinflammation
(Madeira et al. 2015), the inhibition of cytotoxic secretion
by human astrocytes, along with the consequent neuropro-
tective effects (Madeira et al. 2013), and the mitigation
of amyloid-β plaque pathology in a transgenic AD mouse
model at a younger age (Upīte et al. 2020).
But, a limitation associated with AF is that only a small
amount reaches the brain due to its poor absorption or lim-
ited passage across the blood–brain barrier (BBB) (Glen-
nås et al. 1997; Kim et al. 2010; Madeira et al. 2013; Walz
et al. 1983). AF accumulates progressively in various tissues
throughout the body, with the highest gold (Au) concentra-
tions detected in the kidneys, spleen, and lungs. Previous
research conducted by Walz et al. (1983) demonstrated that
AF exhibits limited permeability across the blood–brain bar-
rier (BBB). Moreover, accumulating evidence indicates that
auranofin penetrates the central nervous system (CNS) at
low micromolar concentrations (0.2–5 μmol/L). This sug-
gests that sufficient quantities of auranofin can cross the
blood–brain barrier, thereby exerting in vivo protective
effects analogous to those observed in vitro (Madeira et al.
2014).
Thus, nanoparticles (NPs) present a promising strategy
due to their facile formulation, predictable bio-behavior,
and brain-targeting potential at smaller sizes. They neces-
sitate a reduced payload for achieving therapeutic drug levels
(Dadwal et al. 2018; Sharma, and Baldi 2018; Kaur et al.
2018), exhibit sustained/controlled release profiles (Bajwa
et al. 2021a, b), lower dose-dependent toxicity, and enhance
drug absorption (Kushawaha et al. 2024a), and bioavailabil-
ity (Dewangan et al. 2023; Bajwa et al. 2022) owing to their
diminished particle size (Kumar et al. 2018). In the preced-
ing decade, there has been a focused exploration into nano-
particles, characterized by dimensions of about 100 nm and
possessing distinctive physio-chemical attributes (Dewangan
et al. 2023). This investigation has primarily centered on
their emergence as novel therapeutic modalities aimed at
addressing AD (Anand et al. 2022).
Polylactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA) NPs have emerged
as the most successful strategies for overcoming not only
the adverse side effects but also improving their lipophilic-
ity and bioavailability (Kushawaha et al. 2024a). Notably,

PLGA has emerged as promising carriers for delivering vari-
ous drug molecules, showcasing their versatile potential in
multiple therapeutic applications including cancer, cardio-
vascular disease, vaccine delivery, and neurodegenerative
disorders (Cheng et al. 2007; Cunha, et al. 2021; Meissner
and Lamprecht 2008; Mishra et al. 2014; Bajwa et al. 2023;

This study aimed to address the need for more efficient
AF delivery into the brain and identify the possible neu-

have explored the neuroprotective capabilities of AF NPs
in mitigating cognitive deficits induced by ICV-STZ and
the associated biochemical, neuroinflammatory makers, and
neurochemical alterations in the hippocampus of rats to effi-
ciently deliver AF into the brain.
Materials and methods
Animals
The animals were housed in a standard facility (25 ± 2 °C
and RH 60 ± 5%), under a 12-h light and dark cycle includ-
ing water and diet ad libitum. Wistar rats (male, 250–300 g)
were obtained from CSIR, Palampur, Kangra, H.P. All the
experiments were performed as per the protocol approved
by Institutional Animal Ethics Committee of Laureate
Institute of Pharmacy, Kathog, (HP), India (CPCSEA/
LIPH/2019/10).
Chemicals
Auranofin (Item No. 15316; Cayman Chemical, > 95% High-
Performance Liquid Chromatography) was obtained from
Pro Lab, New Delhi, India, and dissolved in 5% DMSO
solution. PLGA was purchased from Nomisma Healthcare,
Gujarat, India. STZ, 5,5′-dithiobis (2-nitro benzoic acid)
(DTNB), acetylthiocholine iodide (AChI), and malondialde-
hyde were procured from Sigma-Aldrich, USA. TNF-α and
IL-1β were procured from Krishgen Biosystem, India. All
other chemicals reduced glutathione (GSH), thiobarbituric
acid (TBA), and dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) were obtained
from Hi Media, Mumbai. The other reagents used were of
the analytical grade.
Formulation of AF NPs
Drug‑excipient compatibility study
Physical properties of the active pharmaceutical ingredient
AF and PLGA were initially validated through compara-
tive analysis with established standards. Fourier Transform
Infrared Spectrophotometry, using the KBr pellet technique,
recorded IR spectra of the drug, polymer, and AF-loaded
PLGA NPs in the wavelength range of 3500 to 1000 ­cm−1
using a Bruker IFS66 FTIR instrument (Baran et al. 1999).
Preparation of nanoparticles
The water-in-oil-in-water (W/O/W) double emulsion solvent
evaporation technique was used to develop AF-loaded PLGA
NPs (Kushawaha et al. 2024a). AF (40 mg) was diluted with
2 mL of PBS, whereas PLGA (200 mg) was solubilized in
5 mL of dichloromethane. The organic polymer phase was
combined with an aqueous suspension of AF using ultrasoni-
cation at 20 amplitudes for 20 s at cold temperature to pro-
duce the primary emulsion (W/O). To 30 mL of polyvinyl
alcohol (0.5% w/v), the primary emulsion was added drop-
by-drop. The mixture was then agitated at 300 rpm for a fur-
ther 6 h at a given temp. The NPs produced were separately
obtained by ultracentrifugation at 20,000 rpm for 20 min at
4 °C. The obtained NPs were rinsed using double-distilled
water twice and passed from a filter (0.22 µm) to remove
extra PVA. Then, the particles were freeze-dried and stored
at –20 °C till further use. The formulation was optimized
using central composite design methodology, using design
expert software (Design Expert, USA), and tested further for
neuroprotective (Data not shown).
Characterization of NPs
Morphology, particle size, and drug loading
and entrapment efficiency
Zeta potential and surface charge of AF-loaded PLGA NPs
were examined by using NanoSizer Zeta Series, Malvern
Instruments, UK. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM,
JSM-IT300, JEOL 6400 MA USA) and atomic force micros-
copy (AFM, Multimode 8 Nanoscope V, Bruker, USA) were
used to observe morphology. Drug loading and entrapment
efficiency were assessed by ICP-MS.
In vivo study
Intracerebroventricular (ICV) infusion of STZ
The normal moving animals were chosen for the surgery and
individual rats received anesthesia intraperitoneal ketamine
hydrochloride (100 mg/kg/i.p.) and xylazine (5 mg/kg/i.p.)
respectively. As per Kushawaha et al. (2024a), surgery was
carried out by using coordinates (ML, 1.5 mm; AP, 0.8 mm;
DV, 3.6 mm). Two heparinized polypropylene tubes (23
gauges, approximately 3 cm length) were cannulated 3.6 mm
deep into the brain through two burr holes in the skull. The
open end of the cannula was sealed, and dental cement
applied, dried to affix the cannula to the skull, followed by

suturing of the incised skin. Post-surgery, a single intraperi-
toneal dose of gentamicin (40 mg/kg) was administered, and
daily topical application of antiseptic powder (Neosporin)
was applied at the incision site to prevent sepsis. Sham sur-
gery, involving similar procedures minus skull drilling and
cannula placement, was conducted. STZ (3 mg/kg) was dis-
solved in citrate buffer (pH 4.4) and each rat received STZ
(1 µL/min) slowly infused, bilaterally on alternate days (1
and 3) using a Hamilton micro syringe pump in a volume of
10 µL (Kushawaha et al. 2024a).
Experimental protocol
A total of seven groups were created from all the animals
(n = 6). The whole protocol consists of 30 days. The first
group was treated with 5% DMSO solution which serves as
a sham and the remaining six groups were given ICV-STZ
(3 mg/kg, on days 1 and 3) followed by a different dose
of AF, AF NPs, and donepezil from the 15th day of STZ
administration to 29th days (Fig. 1, Table 1). Then, behav-
ioral parameters were performed at the end of the protocol
by using a photoactometer, Morris Water Maze (MWM),
and Object Recognition Test (ORT). On the 30th day, rats
were sacrificed with an overdose of ketamine hydrochlo-
ride and xylazine respectively. Brains were isolated, then
the hippocampus was collected and homogenized for bio-
chemical, inflammatory, neurochemical, and histopathologi-
cal analysis.
Behavioral parameters
Actophotometer  For the measurement of locomotor activ-
ity, photoactometer (IMCORP, India) was used. Animals
were habituated for 3 min over an activity cage, firstly. Then,
the animal was placed individually and locomotor activity
was recorded for 5 min. The values were expressed in terms
of counts per 5 min (Kushawaha et al. 2024b).
Morris Water Maze  Spatial memory and learning capabilities
were assessed using the MWM test, as per the previously
outlined methodology, and responses were recorded through
Any Maze software. The acquisition trials spanned four con-
secutive days (25th to 28th), with a probe test conducted on
the 29th day. The MWM apparatus utilized was a circular
tank measuring (150 cm in diameter and 60 cm in height)
filled with water (25 ± 1 °C) to a depth of 30 cm. Rats under-
went a 5-day training period, each session comprising five
trials with a maximum length of 120 s and an approximate
trial interval of 30 s. The primary measure assessed was
escape latency (in seconds), indicating the time taken by
rats to locate the concealed platform. In instances where
an animal failed to find the hidden platform within 120 s,
manual placement on the platform for 30 s ensued. This
standardized 1-day training pattern was consistent for all
rats, ensuring uniform evaluation of their learning abilities.
On the 29th day, corresponding to 24 h post the acquisition
phase, a probe test was executed by removing the platform
from the pool. Rats were permitted to navigate freely in the
water for a duration of 60 s, and the duration spent in the
target quadrant, where the concealed platform was formerly
located, was documented. The time allocated to the target
quadrant served as an indicator of the extent of memory
consolidation subsequent to the acquisition trial (Kushawaha
et al. 2024a).

Object Recognition Test  The novel object recognition test
was implemented following the previously outlined method-
ology (Jaiswal and Kumar 2022) with specific adaptations.
To conduct the ORT, a wooden open box apparatus meas-
uring 60 × 40 × 60 cm was utilized. The objects employed
in this study were made up of painted wood blocks with
two distinct shapes (triangle and square) and measured
10 cm × 5 cm × 10 cm. The day before testing (day 22),
rats were accustomed to the test box for 15 min per day for
two consecutive days in the absence of any objects. Fol-
lowing this, the rats were returned to their home cage. On
day 23, 24 h subsequent, the initial 3-min sample trial (T1)
was initiated. Two objects, denoted as sample objects FO1
and FO2, characterized by akin color and shape, were posi-
tioned at divergent corners within the enclosure. Following
the culmination of T1, the rats were reintroduced to their
respective home cages, and a time gap of 60 min was intro-
duced as an intertrial interval prior to the commencement
of T2. In the course of the subsequent 3-min choice trial
(T2), one of the objects that had been introduced during
the initial T1 trial was replaced with a novel object (NO).
In order to mitigate potential biases arising from location
or object preferences, a systematic approach was employed
where all feasible permutations and spatial arrangements
of the objects were equitably utilized. Rigorous cleaning
procedures were implemented for both the apparatus and
objects after every trial to preclude the influence of olfac-
tory cues. Exploratory behavior was characterized by the
act of orienting the nose toward the objects at a proximity
of 2 cm or less, and/or making physical contact between the
nose and the objects. During both T1 and T2, the durations
that the rats spent exploring the two objects were measured
and recorded individually. A series of variables were subse-
quently computed, encompassing the cumulative duration
of exploration exhibited toward the two indistinguishable
objects during the initial trial (T1). The rats’ capacity to
differentiate between the familiar and novel object during
the subsequent trial (T2) was ascertained by contrasting the
duration of exploration directed at the familiar object with
that directed at the novel object.
Tissue homogenate preparation
On day 30, to separate the hippocampus, all the rats were
slaughtered, and their brains were taken for homogenization.
The brains were centrifuged at 10,000 g for 15 min after
being homogenized in phosphate buffer (pH 7.4). Biochemi-
cal estimates were made using the resultant supernatant.
Biochemical parameters
Estimation of malondialdehyde (MDA)  The method outlined
by Wills (1966) was used to assess the amount of MDA. The
amount of MDA was measured by reaction with thiobarbi-
turic acid at 532 nm by using an ELISA microplate reader.
Reduced glutathione (GSH) estimation  GSH was estimated
by the method of Ellman (1959) at 412 nm with the help of
an ELISA microplate reader.
Superoxide dismutase (SOD) activity  The method earlier
given by Kono (1978) was used to measure SOD activity.
The change in absorbance was measured for 2 min at 30-s
intervals using a spectrophotometer at 560 nm.
Catalase activity  Catalase activity was quantified according
to the method outlined by Aebi (1984). The assay mixture
consisted of 0.05 M phosphate buffer (pH 7.0), 0.019 M
­H2O2, and 0.05 mL of phenazine methosulfate in a total
volume of 3 mL. The evaluation encompassed monitoring
alterations in absorbance at 420 nm to determine catalase
activity.
Acetylcholinesterase (AChE) assay  The quantification of
AChE activity in the hippocampus of the brain followed
the procedure outlined by Ellman et al. The assay mixture
comprised 0.05 mL of supernatant, 3 mL of 0.01 M sodium
phosphate buffer (pH 8), 0.10 mL of acetylcholine iodide
(AChI), and 0.10 mL of 5,5′-dithiobis (2-nitrobenzoic acid)
(DTNB), commonly known as the Ellman reagent. The
alteration in absorbance was promptly measured at 412 nm
utilizing an ELISA (Model: ER-181 s). AChE activity in the
supernatant was quantified as nanomoles per milligram of
protein (Ellman et al. 1961).
Estimation of TNF‑α and IL‑6
TNF-α and IL-6 quantifications were performed using a rat
TNF-α immunoassay kit (Krishgen Biosystems, India). The
Quantikine rat TNF-α and IL-6 immunoassay, employing a
4.5-h solid-phase sandwich enzyme-linked immunosorbent
assay (ELISA) design, was utilized for measuring the levels
of TNF-α and IL-6. The quantification involved employing
a microtiter plate reader to measure absorbance at 450 nm
in a solid-phase ELISA setup.
Neurochemical estimation
Neurotransmitter levels in the rat brain, including NE, DA,
and 5-HT3, were assessed using the methodology outlined
in earlier investigations. The mobile phase employed in this
study consisted of a sodium citrate buffer (pH 4.5) and ace-
tonitrile in a ratio of 87:13 (v/v). The sodium citrate buffer
was prepared by combining 10 mM citric acid, 25 mM
NaH2PO4, 25 mM ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA),
and 2  mM 1-heptane sulfonic acid. The experimental

procedure was conducted under electrochemical conditions,
with a sensitivity range of 5 to 50 nA and a fixed voltage
of + 0.75 V. The separation was carried out at a flow rate of
0.8 mL/min. During the experimental day, the hippocampus
was isolated, weighed, and homogenized using 0.2 M per-

Histopathological analysis by hematoxylin and eosin
staining
Immediately following the last behavioral test, rats were
decapitated and slaughtered for histopathological analy-
sis. The extracted brains were placed in a (10% v/v) for-
malin solution. Tissues were fixed in paraffin blocks and
using a microtome, divided into 3 mm slices. By the use of
hematoxylin and eosin, (5–10 µm) slices of the brain were
stained and photographed using a binocular microscope. To
ensure meaningful comparisons of cognition and memory
using histology images of the hippocampus, it is crucial that
the images are taken from a similar region of interest. This
consistency allows for accurate assessment and comparison
of morphological or pathological changes. By maintain-
ing a standardized approach in selecting and imaging these
regions, researchers can reliably compare the histological
features that correlate with cognitive and memory functions.
Statistical analysis
Mean ± S.E.M is used to express the values. Two-way
ANOVA was used to assess the data of the spontaneous pho-
toactometer, ORT, MWM, proinflammatory markers, and
catecholamines followed by Tukey’s multiple comparisons
tests. The probe trial outcome (MWM model) and param-
eters of biochemical were studied with one-way ANOVA
testing, which is followed by a multiple comparisons test,
with the statistics program GraphPad Prism, and statistical
significance was preferred at P < 0.05 (version 5.0, La Jolla,
CA, USA).
Results
FTIR spectra
On a Bruker IFS66 FTIR instrument, PLGA FTIR spec-
tra were captured using the KBr pellet method. A stretch-
ing broad peak located at 2996.85 ­cm−1 is indicated by
OH, C = O stretching aliphatic acid at 1761.21 ­cm−1, and
C-O stretching at 1092.01 ­cm−1. FTIR spectra of AF were
recorded and CH stretching aliphatic at 2962.87 ­cm−1, C = O
ester at 1752.25 ­cm−1, C-O stretching at 1233.36 ­cm−1,
and Ac-O-CH2 and C-O stretching were observed at
1037.46 ­cm−1. AF-loaded PLGA NPs peaks were observed,
AF C = O stretching at 1754.71 ­cm−1 and C-O stretching at
1225.69 ­cm−1, while PLGA C-O stretching at 1088 ­cm−1,
C = O acid was observed at 1761.23 ­cm−1. The findings
demonstrated compatibility between AF and PLGA (Fig. 2).
Characterization of optimized AF NPs
The particle size, zeta potential, morphology, SEM, and
AFM of the AF-loaded PLGA NPs were evaluated in the
optimized formulation. The percentage yield of the AF
NPs was found to be 59.85 ± 11.62% and the entrapment
efficiency was found to be 98 ± 1.2%. The hydrodynamic
diameter of AF NPs was observed to be 101.5 ± 10.3 nm
and the low polydispersity index (PDI) indicated a mono-
modal size distribution. Additionally, the zeta potential of
the NPs was observed to be 27.5 ± 5.10 mV with a low PDI
of 0.438 ± 0.12, indicating uniform particle size distribution
of optimized NP (Fig. 3a, b). The scanning electron micro-
graph of AF PLGA NPs confirmed the particle size range
and non-fused spherical shape with its smooth surface. The
nanoparticles displayed a spherical shape, sub-micrometric
size, and no signs of aggregation. An approximately identi-
cal size range of NPs was also observed by analysis using
AFM (Fig. 4a, b), even though the addition of gold com-
pound did not affect the shape of the NPs.
Effect on beam cross time using actophotometer
in ICV‑STZ‑infused rats
Rats administered with ICV-STZ exhibited a marked reduc-
tion in spontaneous locomotor activity. Compared to the
sham group, STZ-treated rat's behavior showed a reduction
in the beam cross time significant (P < 0.001) on day 30th
(Fig. 5). When compared to STZ-treated rats, AF (10 mg/
kg) and AF NPs (5 mg/kg) exhibited significantly (P < 0.05,
P < 0.01) enhanced locomotor performance. However, AF
NPs (5 mg/kg, p.o.) were more significant (p < 0.05) than AF

Discussion
The study presents a novel therapeutic strategy for Alz-
heimer’s disease involving AF NPs tailored for targeted
brain delivery. This approach aims to overcome the chal-
lenge of efficiently transporting therapeutic drugs across the
blood–brain barrier, which is crucial for effective Alzhei-
mer’s therapy. The study’s findings suggest that these AF-
loaded NPs demonstrated neuroprotective properties against
AD induced by ICV-STZ infusion.
ICV injection of STZ recapitulates similar pathological
changes as AD; it gradually decreases cognitive abilities,
increased neuroinflammatory mediators, elevated oxida-
tive stress, and causes neurochemical alterations (Ahn et al.
2020; Ravelli et al. 2017). STZ-induced Alzheimer’s in rats
was confirmed by using behavioral instruments like an acto-
photometer, MWM, and ORT, along with these biochemi-
cal, neurochemical, and inflammatory markers were also
assessed.
The growing research suggests that AF has neuroprotec-
tive and anti-neuroinflammatory properties at low micro-
molar doses AF prevented neurotoxicity caused by human
microglia and astrocytes (Madeira et al. 2013; Madeira et al.
2014). In contrast, AF has been demonstrated to inhibit the
microglial secretion of nitric oxide and reduce the expres-
sion of the Cox-2 inflammatory enzyme and the proinflam-
matory cytokines TNF-α and IL-6 (Madeira et al. 2015).
Similarly, the literature shows that the density of Aβ in the
CA1 hippocampal area in 14-month-old male APPNL-G-F/
NL-G-F AD mice was considerably reduced by AF (Upīte
et al. 2020). These properties of AF indicated its ability to
modulate the cognitive deficit effect of STZ-induced AD.
Notably, PLGA NPs have been reported to improve drug
penetration across the BBB both in vitro and in vivo for
donepezil, galantamine, and memantine (Zhi et al. 2021).
Moreover, the administration of PLGA has demonstrated
potential in attenuating Alzheimer’s disease pathology in
animal models and safeguarding cultured mouse cortical
neurons against Aβ induced lethality (Sánchez-López et al.
2018). Indeed, AF NPs could show a better effect against
STZ-induced AD due to its change in the physicochemical
properties of a drug such as lipophilicity and bioavailability,
and showed no signs of toxicity.
Recent reports by authors demonstrated that aurothioglu-
cose nanoparticles have neuroprotective properties against
AD induced by ICV-STZ due to their appropriate targeting
and efficient transport of therapeutic drugs across the BBB
(Kushawaha et al. 2024a).
In the current study, the formulation of AF NPs was pre-
pared and various characterization parameters like entrap-
ment efficiency, polydispersity index, particle size, and zeta
potential were found to be appropriate for accessing the
pharmacological evaluation. Notably, the favorable small
particle size (101.5 nm) facilitated the effective traversal of
the BBB, as supported by previous studies (Chenthamara
et al. 2019; Garg et al. 2023). Additionally, the suitable zeta
potential indicated sustained stability of the nanoformula-
tion, and the circular morphology of AF NPs was observed
to enhance a surface area, potentially optimizing drug
transport across the blood–brain barrier (Kushawaha et al.
2024a). This innovative approach shows promise in improv-
ing therapeutic efficacy and demonstrating neuroprotective
properties of AF in AD.
Drug-loaded NPs, when administered orally, undergo
complex biodistribution, forming a drug depot that sustains
slow release, as evidenced by preclinical and clinical stud-
ies. This mechanism aims for targeted delivery to the brain,
preventing burst release and mitigating dose-dependent tox-
icity in target organs. The positive charge on NPs is expected
to interact with caveolae-mediated processes in endothelial
cells at BBB, influencing targeted drug entry and elicit-
ing the desired pharmacological action (Kushawaha et al.
2024a).
In line with the preliminary study, the STZ-induced rats
model exhibited a significant decrease in beam cross time,
an increase in escape latency, a decreased time spent in the
target quadrant, and changes in the ORT (Gong et al. 2020;
Nakaya et al. 2011). Similarly, administration of AF and AF
NPs significantly reversed the locomotor activity, memory,
and learning test, and change in object location task against
STZ-induced AD models. The impact of AF NPs surpassed
that of AF alone was also found significant.
Several scientists have mentioned in studies that oxida-
tive stress, as well as neuroinflammatory cytokines, acts as a
pathological marker for several neurodegenerative diseases
(Picca et al. 2020; Yahaya et al. 2020). According to previ-
ous reports, reactive oxygen species (ROS) are associated
with mitochondrial dysfunction responsible for STZ-induced
cognitive alteration demonstrated in AD (Wei et al. 2019).
Similar to this, ICV-STZ-infused rats in the current study
had a considerably higher MDA burden and extremely
decreased biological protective antioxidant enzyme GSH
along with SOD and catalase in the hippocampus region.
In contrast, treatment with AF and AF NPs showed a sub-
stantial increase in GSH, SOD, and catalase levels and a
decline in MDA levels. Significantly, the impact of AF NPs
surpassed that of AF alone. As a result, its potent antioxidant
activities and ability to reduce neuronal damage by restoring
GSH, SOD, and catalase while lowering MDA levels point
to a neuroprotective role of gold compound.

Previous studies demonstrated that ICV injection STZ
causes neuronal injury by increasing proinflammatory
cytokines including IL-6,and TNF-α in rat brains (Abutaleb
and Seleem 2020; Ganz et al. 2018; Kinney et al. 2018). In the
current investigation, the ICV-STZ-administered rats had con-
siderably higher TNF-α and IL-6 levels. However, treatment
with AF and AF NPs showed a significant decrease in the level
of TNF-α and IL-6 in the rat’s hippocampus. Significantly, the
impact of AF NPs surpassed that of AF alone. Consequently,
it suggests a possible anti-inflammatory property, which may
act by decreasing cytokine levels in the rat brain.
The modulation of hippocampal activity is acknowledged
to be influenced by various neuromodulators and neurotrans-
mitters. Previous studies have illustrated the high enrichment
of cholinergic and monoaminergic axon terminals in the hip-
pocampus (Rimpi & Rahul 2020; Wang et al. 2019). These
neurotransmitters are recognized for their pivotal roles in the
encoding, storage, and expression of memory (Cui et al. 2018;
Wang et al. 2018). The current study demonstrated that ICV-
STZ-infused rats had significant elevation in AChE activity
and significantly reduced monoamine levels including NE,
DA, and 5-HT, while treatment with AF and AF NPs dem-
onstrated a considerable recovery of AChE and NE, DA, and
5-HT3 levels in hippocampus rats, indicating a positive neu-
romodulator effect. Significantly, the impact of AF-loaded
nanoparticles surpassed that of AF alone.
In a general scientific context, microglial cells, serving as
the principal defenders against brain injuries, have the poten-
tial to transition into a detrimental state, compromising neu-
ronal survival. In this investigation, histological examination
of the hippocampal brain region in rats subjected to ICV-STZ
infusion unveiled pronounced neurotoxicity attributable to the
overactivation of microglial cells. In contrast, treatment with
AF and AF NPs significantly restored normal neuronal histol-
ogy in hippocampus rats. Significantly, the impact of AF NPs
surpassed that of AF alone. As a result, the findings indicated
that AF and AF NPs are crucial in the treatment of cognitive
impairment.
Within this investigation, the neuroprotective potential of
AF NPs was confirmed by improvement in cognitive function
and restoration of biological antioxidant enzymes as well as
decreased proinflammatory markers. However, AF and AF NPs
dose-dependently reduced STZ-induced changes in hippocam-
pal neurotransmitters and detected histological abnormalities
in the hippocampus areas of the brain. The study also proved
that AF NPs showed a more significant effect than AF alone.
Conclusion
This study elucidates the improved brain accessibility of AF
facilitated by nanoformulations utilizing PLGA. Notably,
the encapsulation of AF within NPs exceeds the efficacy
observed with stand-alone AF administration. Recent stud-
ies have shown that AF NPs have a neuroprotective impact
against STZ-induced AD rats. AF and AF NPs both reverse
STZ-induced behavioral and biochemical alterations and
decrease inflammatory cytokines as well as restore neu-
rotransmitter imbalance. Along with that, it also prevents
STZ-induced histological alterations. Furthermore, its anti-
inflammatory, free radical scavengers and neuromodulator
properties led to the neuroprotective effect. As a result, our
findings imply that it is a strong contender to combat the
striking pathology markers and symptoms that character-
ize neurodegenerative diseases like AD. Thus, it could be
a promising therapeutic approach for the therapy of AD.
Moreover, a more in-depth inquiry is necessary to clarify
the influence of these compounds on central neurotransmis-
sion, recognizing the study’s limitations related to the inabil-
ity to evaluate brain drug concentrations due to laboratory
constraints. The author’s laboratory is currently addressing
formulation challenges to enhance translation potential,
employing Quality-by-Design and Six Sigma methodolo-
gies for regulatory-compliant manufacturing with scalability.
Furthermore, preclinical studies are required to explore the
molecular mechanism of AF to establish therapeutically effi-
cient and commercially acceptable formulations.
