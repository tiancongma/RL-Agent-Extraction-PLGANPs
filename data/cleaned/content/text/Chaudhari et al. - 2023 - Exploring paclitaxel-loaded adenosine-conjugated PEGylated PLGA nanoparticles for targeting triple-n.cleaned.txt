## Abstract
In present investigation, we developed paclitaxel (PTX)-loaded adenosine (ADN)-conjugated PLGA nanoparticles for
combating triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC), where ADN acts as a substrate for adenosine receptors (AR) that are
overexpressed in TNBC. Using synthesized PLGA-PEG-ADN, PTX-loaded nanoparticles (PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs)
were prepared via emulsion diffusion evaporation process that rendered particles of size 135 +- 12 nm, PDI of 0.119 +- 0.03,
and entrapment-efficiency of 79.26 +- 2.52%. The NPs showed higher %cumulative release at pH 5.5 over 7.4 with Higuchi
release kinetics. The PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs showed ~ 4.87- and 5.22-fold decrease in %hemolysis in comparison to
free PTX and Intaxel(r), indicating their hemocompatible nature. The ADN modification assisted cytoplasmic internalization of particles via AR-mediated endocytosis that resulted in ~ 3.77- and 3.51-fold reduction in IC50 and showed apoptosis
index of 0.93 and 1.18 in MDA-MB-231 and 4T1 cells respectively. The pharmacokinetic profile of ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs
revealed higher AUC and t1/2 than Intaxel(r) and Nanoxel(r) pharmacodynamic activity showed ~ 18.90-fold lower %tumor
burden than control. The kidney and liver function biomarkers showed insignificant change in the levels, when treated with
PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs and exhibited no histological alterations in the liver, spleen, and kidney. Overall, the optimized
particles were found to be biocompatible with improved anti-TNBC activity.

polyvinyl alcohol (PVA), Tween(r) 80, and PluronicTM
F-127) were screened. The process parameters like sonication cycle (at different amplitudes of sonication, i.e., 25, 30,
35, and 40%) and theoretical drug loading (2.5, 5, 7.5, and
10% w/w) were thoroughly optimized. For details, kindly
follow the supplementary file Sect. 2.

In vitro hemolytic toxicity

The prepared formulations are intended for intravenous
administration hence, evaluating their hemolytic potential
is crucial. The protocol followed was similar to our previously reported method [27, 28]. Blood samples were collected from Swiss albino (25-30 g) mice in heparinized centrifuge tubes and were centrifuged at 4 degC and 2500 RPM
for 5 min to separate the RBCs. The buffy layer as well as
the supernatant formed was removed, and the RBC pellet
which settled at the bottom was washed with isotonic solution at least 3 times.
Finally, the formed RBC pellet was resuspended in PBS
(pH 7.4) and was considered as 100% RBC suspension. For
hemolytic study, RBC suspension with 1% v/v concentration
was prepared using PBS (pH 7.4). Free PTX, PTX-loaded
PLGA NPs, and PTX containing marketed formulations, i.e.,
Intaxel(r) and Nanoxel(r) (equivalent to 1 mg of free PTX),
were added to 1 mL of RBC suspension. Further, 0.1% v/v
Triton-X(tm) 100 and PBS (pH 7.4) was used as positive control and negative control, respectively. The samples were
incubated for 1 h at 37 degC in shaker bath, followed by centrifugation at 2500 RPM while maintaining temperature at
4 degC for 5 min. The supernatant was separated and pellet was
stored. The supernatant was left at RT for 30 min to allow
hemoglobin undergo oxidation. The spectrophotometric

## Conclusion
Ethics approval and consent to participate All the procedures performed in this study involving animals were in accordance with the
ethical standards of NIPER and protocols were approved by the Institutional Animals Ethics Committee (Approval number: IAEC/19/72).

The developed PTX-loaded PLGA NPs, having ADN as the
targeting ligand and PEG as the linker, showed improved
anticancer activity in comparison to free PTX and marketed
counterpart Intaxel(r). The ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs achieved
loading of 7.5% w/w, which is sufficient for in vivo dosing and for achieving the desired therapeutic concentration.
The particles exhibited pH-dependent release behavior,
i.e., slow release at physiological pH and higher release at
acidic (tumor microenvironment) pH. Such slow-release
behavior at physiological pH prevented direct exposure of
PTX to RBCs and exhibited hemocompatibility. The ADN
modification over PLGA NPs render higher internalization
of particles that resulted in > 3.5-fold reduction in IC50 values in TNBC cells. Further, the ADN modification allowed
particles to exhibit higher apoptosis index in TNBC cells
when compared to non-modified PLGA NPs and free PTX
group. The PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs exhibited improved
pharmacokinetic parameters (AUC, MRT, and t1/2) in comparison to Intaxel(r), other non-targeted and non-PEGylated
PLGA particles.
ADN modification assisted PLGA NPs in achieving
tumor specific distribution and retention which resulted
in lower organ toxicity and insignificant alteration in the
kidney and liver biomarker levels when compared to control group. Further, PTX ADN-PEG-PLGA NPs showed
superior anti-cancer efficacy (in terms of tumor volume
reduction and % tumor burden) when compared to all other
tested groups including Intaxel(r) and Nanoxel(r). Overall, the
developed strategy, i.e., to involve ADN as targeting ligand
for TNBC, has been successfully explored with promising
