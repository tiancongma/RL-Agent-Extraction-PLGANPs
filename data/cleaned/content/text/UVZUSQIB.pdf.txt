Assessment of the Antigenotoxic Activity of Poly(D,L‑lactic-co-glycolic
acid) Nanoparticles Loaded with Caﬀeic Acid Phenethyl Ester Using
the Ames Salmonella/Microsome Assay
Tülin Arasoğlu*,†
and Serap Derman‡
†Department of Molecular Biology and Genetics, Faculty of Science and Letters and ‡Department of Bioengineering, Faculty of
Chemical and Metallurgical Engineering, Yildiz Technical University, 34220 Esenler, Istanbul, Turkey
ABSTRACT: In the present study, the antigenotoxic activity of poly(D,L-lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) nanoparticles (NPs)
loaded with caﬀeic acid phenethyl ester (CAPE) was investigated in comparison to free CAPE using the Ames Salmonella/
microsome assay. Additionally, to elucidate the impacts of the type of solvent eﬀect on antigenotoxic activity, the following
systems were tested: CAPE in water (poor solvent), ethyl alcohol (good solvent), and PLGA NPs (unknown). The eﬀect of the
NP system on solubility was investigated for the ﬁrst time by assessing the antigenotoxic potential. In this study, the CAPE/
PLGA NPs were synthesized using an oil-in-water (o/w) single-emulsion solvent evaporation method with an average size of
206.2 ± 1.2 nm, ζ potential of −19.8 ± 2.5 mV, encapsulation eﬃciency of 87.2 ± 2.5%, and drug loading of 53.3 ± 1.8%.
According to the results of the antigenotoxic activity, the highest antimutagenic activity in both applied strains was found for
CAPE in ethanol, and the lowest activity was detected for CAPE in water. Our study has shown that NP systems exhibit high
antigenotoxic activity, which is similar to the results of CAPE dissolved in ethanol. These results have shown that NP systems
increase biological activity of hydrophobic substances by increasing their solubility and that the use of PLGA instead of organic
solvents in drug production may provide an increase in their medical utility.
KEYWORDS: caﬀeic acid phenethyl ester, poly(D,L-lactic-co-glycolic acid), PLGA, nanoparticle, antigenotoxicity activity, Ames
■INTRODUCTION
In recent years, particles sized between 10 and 1000 nm are
often used for pharmaceutical applications as a drug carrier.
The drug or active substance is dissolved, encapsulated,
adsorbed, or covalently attached to the nanoparticle (NP)
system.1 The increase in bioavailability and solubility of drugs,
their controlled release, and their protection from enzymatic
degradation are provided using nanoparticular systems.2,3 As a
result of their small size, increased surface/volume ratio,
cationic surface charge, or attachment properties, which target
moiety and mobility; the cell uptake rates of NPs are higher
than systems without NPs.4,5 Because of these advantages, NP
systems, especially polymeric NP systems, are often preferred
for delivering a wide range of drug molecules. Because
polymeric NPs can be designed with many diﬀerent polymers,
either natural or synthetic, they provide ﬂexibility for designing
drug delivery systems in a desired manner. The drug release
from NP systems can be controlled by changing the polymer
properties, such as composition, molecular weight, hydro-
phobicity, and crystallinity.6 The most commonly used polymer
for preparation of NP systems that was approved by the United
States Food and Drug Administration (U.S. FDA) is poly(D,L-
lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA). The biodegradability, bio-
compatibility, and metabolization properties that can be
achieved via normal pathways without any side eﬀects are
signiﬁcant advantages of these polymers.1,7,8
Many studies have reported that the half-life of active
molecules is increased, the duration of their action is extended,
and the solubility and bioavailability of drugs is improved by
encapsulation to PLGA NP systems.6,7,9−11 These systems
increase the bioavalibility and biocompatibility of hydrophobic
anticancer agents in biological systems.7,9,10,12
Caﬀeic acid phenethyl ester (CAPE) is one of the most
important components of propolis and a ﬂavonoid-like
molecule, C17H16O4 (Figure 1).13 In addition to exhibiting
anticancer and antioxidant properties, it has other biological
activities; for example, it is anti-inﬂammatory,14 antiallergic,15
antitumoral,16 antiulcerative,17 antimutagenic,18 cardioprotec-
tive,19 and an immunomodulator.20
Besides these numerous advantages, the CAPE molecule is
unstable and easily decomposes in biological systems as a result
of its ester bond, (α−β unsaturated carbonyl) and the catechol
groups in its structure (Figure 1A).21 Several studies have
shown that the half-life of the CAPE molecule in biological
systems is 20−28 min and is independent of the dose.22−24
Therefore, the use of high and repeated doses of CAPE is also
required for in vitro and in vivo studies.2,5,7 In addition, the
application of CAPE molecules in biological systems is also very

literature, diﬀerent strategies increase the solubility of
molecules with solvent systems, conjugation to polymers, or
encapsulation to particles.25 Even if using the organic solvent
increases the biological eﬀect of molecules, the organic solvents
may create toxicity in biological systems.1
To our knowledge, there is no study in the literature in which
compounds with antigenotoxic potential may become more
useful with the use of PLGA nanoparticular systems. Therefore,
this is for the ﬁrst study to show that increasing the low-
solubility properties of plant-derived active substances or
chemically synthesized compounds by encapsulation to NPs
positively inﬂuences its antigenotoxic activity. In the present
study, an antigenotoxicity test was used to investigate the eﬀect
of diﬀerent solvents or encapsulation system on the enhance-
ment of the biological activity of CAPE. The Ames technique
was used as an antigenotoxicity test in our study. In the Ames
technique, it used Salmonella typhimurium TA98 and TA100
strains with or without a S9 metabolic activation system against
quinoline compounds, aromatic amines, and polycyclic
aromatic hydrocarbon mutagens. The Salmonella/microsome
assay Ames test is the gold standard for mutagenic and
antimutagenic determination of chemical compounds and has
been shown to be the most predictive in vitro assay for human
carcinogenesis in the literature.
We investigate the solubility of antigenotoxic agents on
antigenotoxicity using diﬀerent strategies, such as the use of
organic solvents and encapsulation to PLGA. For the ﬁrst time
in the literature, the eﬀect of increasing solubility on the
biological activity of CAPE was evaluated using an Ames
antigenotoxicity assay against direct/indirect mutagens in the
presence/absence of a S9 metabolic activation system. For this
purpose, ﬁrst, CAPE was encapsulated to a PLGA NP system
(CAPE/PLGA NPs). After encapsulation of CAPE into the
PLGA system, the enhancement of its biological activity by
increasing the solubility of CAPE was evaluated using the Ames
antigenotoxicity assay. To elucidate the eﬀect of the
antigenotoxic agent−solvent interaction on antigenotoxic
activity, the chosen systems were CAPE in water (poor
solvent), ethyl alcohol (good solvent) and PLGA NPs
(unknown). Thus, this is the ﬁrst time that encapsulation to
a PLGA NP system was compared to a solvent system using the
Ames assay. Also, the antigenotoxic activity of the CAPE-loaded
PLGA NPs were investigated for the ﬁrst time.
■MATERIALS AND METHODS
Chemicals. CAPE, acid-terminated D,L-lactide-co-glycolide [50:50,
inherent viscosity of 0.45−0.60 dL/g, and molecular weight (MW) of
38 000−54 000], and polivinylalchol (PVA) were purchased from
Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, MO, U.S.A.). Ethanol and dichloromethane
(DCM) were obtained from Riedel de Haen. Ames microplate format
mutagenicity assay 98/100 kit was provided from Anaria-Xenometrix.
All of the chemicals and solvents were of analytical grade and used
without further puriﬁcation. Ultrapure water was obtained from
Millipore Milli-Q gradient system.
Preparation of PLGA NPs Loaded with CAPE (CAPE/PLGA
NPs). In the present study, CAPE/PLGA NPs were fabricated by a oil-
in-water (o/w) single-emulsion solvent evaporation technique.26 A
total of 40 mg of CAPE and 100 mg of PLGA were dissolved in
ethanol and DCM, respectively. These organic solutions were mixed
together and poured dropwise by injector to the water phase including
3% PVA. This mixture sonicated simultaneously by a microtip probe
sonicator for 2 min with energy output of 70 W. Obtained o/w
emulsion was added dropwise to 35 mL of 0.1% PVA solution and
stirred by a magnetic stirrer overnight at 25 °C to allow for the
evaporation of organic solvent. The synthesized NPs was separated
and collected by centrifugation (9000 rpm at 4 °C for 30 min). The
supernatant was decanted, and NPs were washed 3 times by ultrapure
water. After that, suspended NPs were lyophilized for 48 h and stored
at −20 °C until used. Empty PLGA NPs were synthesized by the
identical method without using CAPE.
Characterization of CAPE/PLGA NPs. Dynamic and electro-
phoretic light scattering methods were used to determination of the
particle size distribution and ζ potential of the NPs, respectively.27
After evaporation of the organic solvent, a certain amount of NP
suspension was used for size, ζ potential, and polydispersity index
(PDI) analysis. The encapsulation eﬃciency (EE) of the NPs was
analyzed by the indirect quantiﬁcation method using ultraviolet−
visible (UV−vis) spectroscopy at 323 nm. A previously constructed
standard calibration curve was used for the determination of the CAPE
amount in the supernatant. EE and drug loading capacity (LC) of the
NPs were calculated as follows:

The shape and surface morphological of the NP were investigate by
scanning electron microscopy (SEM, JSM-7001FA, JEOL, Japan)28
and transmission electron microscopy (TEM, JEM-1400, JEOL,
Japan).29 A colorimetric method that is based on the determination

and the iodine molecule30 was used to determine the residual PVA
amount on the surface of NPs.
In Vitro Release of CAPE/PLGA NPs. In vitro release of NPs was
studied by dissolution/centrifugation methods, with minor modiﬁca-
tion.3 As a result of the low water solubility of CAPE, the release study
was carried out the phosphate-buﬀered saline (PBS)/ethanol (4:1
volume ratio) mixture, and the pH of the mixture was adjusted to 7.2.
Lyophilized NPs were suspended in PBS/ethanol at a concentration of
1 mg/mL and incubated at 37 °C with shaking at 150 rpm. At
predetermined time intervals (2, 4, and 6 h and 1, 2, 3, 4, 7, 8, and 10
days), the solutions were centrifuged at 9000 rpm for 30 min. The
resultant supernatants were used to quantify the released CAPE
amount. Obtained precipitates were suspended in the same volume of
PBS buﬀer. The supernatant was analyzed using UV−vis spectroscopy
at 323 nm, and calculation of the CAPE concentration in the
supernatant was carried out using a previously constructed CAPE
calibration curve in the PBS/ethanol (4:1) mixture.
Ames/Bacterial Reverse Mutation Assay. Antimutagenicity of
free CAPE in water, free CAPE in ethanol, and CAPE/PLGA NPs in
water were determined using the Ames microplate format
mutagenicity assay 98/100 kit (Anaria-Xenometrix, Mason, OH,
U.S.A.). The study was carried out in accordance with the kit
protocol31 but modiﬁed for detecting antimutagenic activity.32 In the
study, the positive controls (mutagens) were 2-nitroﬂuorene (2-NF, 2
μg/mL), 4-nitroquinalone-N-oxide (4-NQO, 0.1 μg/mL), and 2-
aminoanthracene (2-AA, 2.5 μg/mL for TA100 and 1.0 μg/mL for
TA98) for S. typhimurium TA98/TA100 strains. The negative controls
were water and ethanol used as a solvent for all studies. The S9
metabolic activation system used as a model of the intact mammalian
system is a cofactor supplemented post-mitochondrial fraction
prepared from the livers of rats exposed to enzyme-inducing agents,
such as Aroclor 1254. The purpose of the use of the hepatic
microsomal S9 fraction in the Ames technique is to biotransform the
natural or synthetic compounds and their metabolites and to
determine the mutagenic potential of the metabolites.33,34 In other
words, the S9 fraction is used in the assessment of the potential of
converting agents identiﬁed as “indirect mutagens” into mutagenic
agents by metabolic activation.34,35 In the presented study, the S9
fraction was purchased commercially.34,35 The 30% mix (S9 fraction
and cofactors) used in the study was prepared according to the Ames
microplate format (MPF) test protocol, and the Booster solution

In this study, the free CAPE and CAPE/PLGA NP stock
concentrations were prepared as 4 and 7.42 mg/mL, respectively,
applied at a ﬁnal concentration in the range from 0.438 to 14 μg/mL

In the antimutagenicity test performed with the S. typhimurium
TA98/TA100 strains, frozen stock cultures were thawed and prepared
by incubating at 37 °C and 200 rpm for 16−18 h in tubes containing
10 mL of growth medium and 10 μL of ampicillin (50 mg/mL). After
incubation, the overnight cultures that reached OD600 of ≥2.0 were
exposed together with a mutagen agent and (i) free CAPE in water,
(ii) free CAPE in ethanol (10%), (iii) CAPE/PLGA NPs in water, and
(iv) empty PLGA NPs in water in 24-well plates, separately. Then, the
culture was incubated for 90 min at 37 °C by adding the exposure
medium to each well. Afterward, the culture in the histidine-free
medium was run in triplicate in a 384-well plate, in which each
concentration was transferred to 48 wells by dispersing 50 μL into the
384-well plate, followed by adding the indicator medium to each well
of 24-well plates. After 48 h of incubation at 37 °C, the prototroph
cells succeeded to reproduce in the absence of histidine as a result of
the mutagenic eﬀect, caused by the pH change in the medium. The
color change in the medium as a result of pH (from purple to yellow)
is determined as a mutagenic eﬀect, but the opposite is the
antimutagenic eﬀect. In our Ames MPF study, each well is scored
for either a yellow (positive control = mutagenic eﬀect) or a purple
(negative control = antimutagenic eﬀect) well. The number of purple
wells that do not contained revertant colonies (applied together with
mutagen and test samples) are compared to the number of positive
control (only containing the mutagen agent) and negative control
(only containing the solvent) wells.

Prescreen Determination of the Dose Range. The cytotoxicity
and solubility of the test samples toward S. typhimurium TA98 and
TA100 strains were determined as described in the detailed kit
protocol.31 As a result of the very low water solubility of CAPE, in
solubility and toxicity studies, only the antigenotoxic concentration
range of CAPE was used for 4 mg/mL. The overnight culture prepared
for the Ames MPF test was also used. The concentration range of test
samples is between 4 μg/mL and 4 mg/mL. After incubation of the
bacterial culture and the test items in exposure medium at 37 °C for 90
min, spectrophotometric measurements are made at OD600. The well
containing the non-cytotoxic dose of CAPE should be more blurred
because the pure image is an indication of bacterial lysis. In addition,
there should be no sediment at the bottom in the well containing the
appropriate dose.
Statistical Analysis. In the evaluation of antigenotoxicity results,
the decreased number of positive wells and dose dependency for test
samples were used as criteria for statistical analysis.31,37,38 Anti-
genotoxicity experiment data were expressed as the mean ± standard
deviation (SD) of the positive well (yellow) number of three
independent experiments for each concentration of test samples.
The data were further analyzed for statistical signiﬁcance using analysis
of variance (ANOVA), and the diﬀerence among means was compared
by the high-range statistical domain using Tukey’s test. A level of
probability was accepted signiﬁcantly when the p value was below
0.05.36,39,40 Additionally, the NP characterization results are also
presented as the mean ± SD of the triplicate measurement.
■RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
Characterization of CAPE/PLGA NPs. In the present
study, PLGA NPs loaded with CAPE were obtained using a
single-emulsion solvent evaporation method. The obtained NPs
were characterized by their particle size (Z average), PDI, ζ
potential, EE, and drug LC. The results are summarized in
Table 1.
The particle size measurement of the CAPE/PLGA NPs was
carried out using a dynamic light scattering method, and the
results showed a unimodal population of NPs (Figure 2A) with
a mean diameter of 206.2 ± 1.2 nm. The NPs had low values of
polydispersity (0.064 ± 0.02), which indicates that the particle
size was highly uniform. The ζ potential, which was measured
by the electrophoretic light scattering technique, is a key
indicator for the stability of the NP and was −19.8 ± 2.5 mV
(Figure 2B). This high negative charge prevents agglomeration
and provides stability when suspended in water.
In the present study, the EE and LC of the CAPE/PLGA
NPs were determined by measuring the unencapsulated CAPE
concentration in the supernatant. The EE and drug LC of the
NP were calculated as 87.2 ± 2.5% and 53.3 ± 1.8, respectively,
using the results from an indirect method.
A colorimetric method was used to determine the residual
PVA amount on the surface of NPs. According to the methods,
the remaining PVA amount on the surface of the NP was
calculated as 4.24% (w/w).
SEM and TEM were used to investigate the morphologic
properties of the CAPE/PLGA NPs, and the obtained
micrographs are shown in panels C and D of Figure 2,
respectively. The NPs have a smooth surface and are spherically
shaped, and their size distribution is uniform without any
agglomeration. Both SEM and TEM images are consistent with
the dynamic light scattering results, indicating that the

Ames Bacterial Reverse Mutation Assay. In this study,
the Ames MPF assay was used to determine antimutagenicity in
a liquid format, and reversion was assessed by growth in a
histidine-free medium with a colorimetric assay. Two diﬀerent
S. typhimurium strains that have lost the ability to produce
histidine as a result of the diﬀerent point mutations generated
in the histidine (His) operon, were used with the Ames MPF
assay. These strains cannot grow unless histidine is added to the
medium. A second mutation that may occur in the strains with
this property reverts the His−auxotroph into a prototroph.
The reverted strains will then grow in histidine-free media.
The mutagenic and antimutagenic properties of the test
samples are determined by exposing the auxotroph Salmonella
strains to various concentrations of these samples and
observing the revertion phenomenon. In the present study,
the S. typhimurium TA98 strain for the detection of frameshift
mutations and the S. typhimurium TA100 strain for the
detection of base substitution mutations were used.
First, the appropriate dose of the test samples was
determined for the Ames MPF study. For this purpose, the
dissolved dose without any precipitation in the bottom of the
plate was selected. The CAPE concentration in the colloidal
structure where precipitation did not occur was detected as 4
mg/mL, and this dose used the highest concentration in the
Ames test.

at concentrations of 7 and 14 μg/mL (Figure 4C). In the study
conducted against the 2-AA mutagen in the presence of S9
metabolic activation, the antigenotoxic activity was detected
with only a concentration of 14 μg/mL (Figure 4D). Free
CAPE in the ethanol solvent completely inhibited the
mutagenesis induced by 2-NF + 4-NQO on the S. typhimurium
TA100 strain in three doses (3.5, 7, and 14 μg/mL; Figure 4E).
For the study containing S9 metabolic activation system in
medium, the free CAPE’s antimutagenic eﬀect was detected in
only the 14 μg/mL dose against 2-AA (Figure 4F). In the
absence of the S9 metabolic activation system on the TA100
strain, the inhibition rates of CAPE/PLGA NPs and free CAPE
dissolved in ethanol were found to be very close to each other.
Inhibition rates for both samples were observed to be 100.00%
at 7−14 μg/mL, but the inhibition rate in CAPE/PLGA NPs
with 1-fold dilution was reduced to 86.36% (Table 2). The
study conducted on the TA100 strain in the presence of the S9
metabolic activation system showed that the CAPE/PLGA NPs
and free CAPE dissolved in ethanol have 81.75 and 100.00%
inhibition rates at the highest concentration, respectively
(Table 2). The eﬃciency of free CAPE dissolved in ethanol
has continued at 7 μg/mL concentration, with an inhibition of
61.80%.

The presence of the ortho-dihydroxyl group in the catechol ring
of CAPE may be modiﬁed in the CYP450 system that provides
biotransformation to carcinogenic metabolites of polycyclic
aromatic hydrocarbons and, thus, inhibited the biotransforma-

In the present study, the toxicity and antigenotoxicity of the
empty PLGA NPs were investigated in parallel with CAPE in
water/ethanol and CAPE in NPs. As previously reported in
various literature,3,45−47 it had been found that empty PLGA
NPs do not have any biological activity and toxicity. In
accordance with these ﬁndings in our study, empty PLGA NPs
showed no antigenotoxic activity (data not shown). Addition-
ally, it is thought that the response under solvent control shown
in panels C and E of Figure 3 and panels C, E, and F of Figure
4 is due to the combined use of CAPE formulations and
mutagens. Therefore, in studies on antigenotoxicity, not only
the test agent but also the test agent and mutagen interactions
should be evaluated together for cytotoxicity.
According to our results, it was shown that CAPE in ethanol
has the highest antimutagenic activity. For its application in
water, the antigenotoxic activity was only observed at the
highest dose (14 μg/mL) in the TA100 strain. It has been
found that the nanoparticular system that was produced by
encapsulation of CAPE into PLGA exhibits antigenotoxic
activity close to that of CAPE dissolved in ethanol (with/
without S9). This can be explained because nanoparticular
systems increase the eﬀect on the biological system, especially
by increasing the solubility of the active substance in the
hydrophobic structure. Many studies have shown that
encapsulation of the active substances in the nanoparticular
system enhances their biological activity. For example,
Mukerjee and Vishwanatha indicated that the solubility of
curcumin is increased by encapsulation in the PLGA system
and that the anticancer activity provided by the sustained
release was evaluated as a potential candidate for cancer
therapy.9 In another study, Wang et al. synthesized a quercetin-
loaded nanoformulation and showed that the water solubility of
quercetin increased by about 1000-fold.48 Singh et al. found
that, tea polyphenol-loaded PLGA NPs provided more than 30
times DNA damage prevention compared to free tea
polyphenol.49 In an eﬀort to improve oral bioavailability,
estradiol (E2), which has a high oral absorption but low
bioavailability, has been loaded onto PLGA NPs. The produced
NPs were shown to be more intracellularized than free active
molecules in the male Sprague Dawley (SD) rat intestinal ﬂora
and did not exhibit any inﬂammatory response. These data
suggest that PLGA NPs have great potential as carriers for oral
delivery of estradiol.50 Our results are consistent with the
literature, including biological activity studies of hydrophobic
molecules encapsulated in PLGA NPs. To the best of our
knowledge, no study has used the Ames method to determine
the antimugenic and antigenotoxic properties of biologically
active agent-loaded PLGA nanosystems. Only one paper has
studied the cytotoxicity and mutagenic eﬀect of PLGA-based
NPs using the same method. In that study, both empty PLGA
NPs and those loaded with retinyl palmitate were evaluated
using the Ames method and both were found to be as non-
genotoxic.51 In our study, we demonstrated that the
antigenotoxic/antimutagenic activity of the active substances
was increased by PLGA encapsulation.
In conclusion, as determined in several studies, CAPE has an
inhibitory eﬀect on the genotoxic action of several known
mutagens because of the its antioxidant property18,52 and
inhibits activation of the indirect mutagen by aﬀecting
microsomal enzymes. In our study, the antimutagenic activity
of CAPE was clearly observed, and our results are consistent
with the literature. Because solvent additions are required to be
readily absorbed as a result of the low solubility of hydrophobic
molecules, CAPE is typically dissolved in diﬀerent organic
solvents. Unfortunately, these solvents not only diminish the
eﬀect of molecules but also produce toxicity.1 Besides the low
water solubility, instability in the biological system and rapid
degradation signiﬁcantly reduce the biological eﬀect of CAPE.
Because negligible antigenotoxic activity for CAPE in water and
higher antigenotoxic activity for CAPE in ethanol were
detected, our results conﬁrmed this phenomenon. Because
the antigenotoxic activity obtained in CAPE/PLGA NP systems
is similar to CAPE in ethanol, biocompatible and biodegradable
PLGA NP systems could be an alternative to organic solvents,
such as ethanol, methanol, or dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO).
Moreover, the use of PLGA NPs that are nontoxic and readily
degradable in biological systems, instead of organic solvents in
drug production, provides an increase in the medical use of
hydrophobic active compounds. Also, the presence of the
sustained release in PLGA nanosystems will increase the
eﬀectiveness of the treatment.
