Aim: Poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) nanoparticles containing both paclitaxel (PTX)
and gemcitabine hydrochloride (GEM) were prepared and the effect of lyophilization
and spray drying on physicochemical characteristics of these nanoparticles were
evaluated. Methods: Nanoemulsions were prepared by oil-in-water emulsion
solvent diffusion technique using sonication and high-pressure homogenization.
Nanoemulsion was dried using lyophilization or spray drying and drug content
analyzed by high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC). Results: The particle
size of the nanoemulsion was 183.6 ± 10.8 nm and the entrapment efficiency for PTX
and GEM was 72.01 ± 1.35% and 6.95 ± 3.97%. Many properties including particle size,
stability, surface morphology, moisture content, release characteristic and cellular
uptake in MDCK and MDA-MB-231 cells were affected by the method of drying.
Conclusion: The lyophilized nanoparticles were smaller in size with higher stability
and cellular uptake than spray-dried nanoparticles.

Chemotherapy with a combination of anti-
cancer agents has been adopted to reduce
drug resistance and to improve therapeutic
effectiveness in cancer treatment [1]. It is desir-
able for the drugs intended for use in com-
bination to have non overlapping toxicities
and different molecular targets so as to avoid
cross-tolerance [2]. Paclitaxel (PTX) and gem-
citabine hydrochloride (GEM) is an exten-
sively studied combination for use in variety
of cancers including advanced non small-cell
lung cancer (NSCLC) and breast cancer [3,4].
PTX is a widely used hydrophobic micro-
tubule stabilizing agent which is US FDA
approved for palliative therapy of patients
with ovarian and breast cancer [5]. Because
of its aqueous insolubility, PTX is adminis-
tered intravenously using Cremophor EL and
ethanol (Taxol®). However, Cremophor EL is
known to exert a range of harmful biological
effects including severe anaphylactic reac-
tions, hence much effort has been invested
in developing a better drug delivery system.
Nanoparticle formulations containing PTX
is one such approach [6]. GEM, on the other
hand, is a hydrophilic antineoplastic antime-
tabolite used for a broad spectrum of cancers.
In a study by Kosmidis et al., it was predicted
that PTX possibly enhances the antitumor
activity of GEM and the combination was
shown to have an additive effect [7]. Nanopar-
ticle-assisted combination therapies achieve
the benefits of both of a combination therapy
as well as a nanodelivery system. The entrap-
ment of multiple drugs in a single carrier
also offers the features of vehicle uniformity,
ratiometric drug loading and temporal drug
release in contrast to simultaneous delivery of
single drug-containing particles [1].
The entrapment of a hydrophobic and a
hydrophilic drug into a single matrix requires
a polymer with tunable physicochemical prop-
erties. Polymers allow the flexibility to be tai-
lored for specific needs along with advantages
of biodegradability, higher stability, sustained
release and higher loading capacity for drugs

that are poorly soluble in water. The entrapped drug
can be either dispersed in the polymer matrix or con-
jugated/attached to the polymer surface [8]. Poly(lactic-
co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) is one of the most successfully
developed biodegradable polymers which is also FDA
approved for use in parenteral administration. PLGA
nanoparticles have been widely studied for encapsula-
tion of hydrophobic drug; however, hydrophilic drug
entrapment is more challenging. Several studies have
also reported hydrophilic drug encapsulation by poly-
mer–drug conjugation methods, but very few stud-
ies describe direct encapsulation or attachment of the
hydrophilic drug on the hydrophobic polymer [9,10].
One of the frequently encountered problems in pre-
paring nanosystems is the physical and chemical insta-
bility of the nanodispersions on storage, which is being
addressed by drying the nanoparticles to solid powders
[11]. The dried powders can be used after reconstitution
or as powders for inhalation. Lyophilization and spray
drying are the two most commonly used drying tech-
niques. Since freeze drying is more suitable for labora-
tory scale production there is a need to explore other
drying techniques like spray drying which is more
scalable at lower cost. The primary challenges of spray
drying are maintaining the particle size and increasing
the entrapment efficiency of the drug [8]. The different
process conditions employed in lyophilization and spray
drying, however, may yield dried particles with different
physicochemical properties. Lyophilization and spray
drying are frequently employed drying techniques for
nanoparticles, yet very few studies describe its effect on
end product performance so as to obtain a product with
higher stability and better physicochemical properties.
The aim of this work was to investigate the feasibil-
ity of preparation of a PTX and GEM combination
loaded PLGA nanoparticle system with high entrap-
ment and sustained release characteristics and to study
the effect of two different drying techniques on the
physicochemical characteristics of this nanodelivery
system.
Materials & methods
Materials
Purasorb® PLGA (L:G 75:25, copolymer with 75%
lactic acid and 25% glycolic acid, intrinsic viscosity:
0.2  dl/g) (Lot #1110000392) was a gift from Purac
biomaterials (The Netherlands). Commercial grade
PTX was obtained from Tecoland Corporation (USA)
and GEM hydrochloride was obtained from APAC
Pharmaceutical LLC (USA). Poly(vinyl alcohol) (MW:
85000–124000, 87–89% hydrolyzed, Lot #363081),
was purchased from Aldrich Chemical Co. (WI, USA).
Propylene glycol was purchased from professional
compounding centers of America Inc, (TX, USA).
High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC)
grade acetone and dichloromethane were purchased
from Sigma Aldrich (WI, USA). Dulbecco’s modi-
fied Eagle medium, Roswell Park Memorial Institute
medium (RPMI-1640), penicillin/streptomycin, tryp-
sin-EDTA, L-glutamine, sodium pyruvate, nonessen-
tial amino acids, ammonium acetate, Optima LC/MS
grade acetonitrile, methanol and water were purchased
from Fischer Scientific (NJ, USA). MDA-MB-231 and
MDCK cells were purchased from American Type
Culture Collection (VA, USA). Fetal bovine serum was
purchased from Atlanta biological (GA, USA).
Methods
Formulation of PLGA nanoparticles
Poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) (300  mg)
and PTX (46.8  mg) were dissolved in an
acetone:dichloromethane
(1:2)
solution.
GEM
(19.9 mg) was dissolved separately in 1 ml propylene
glycol. The GEM solution was added drop wise to
the organic solution of PLGA and PTX. The organic
phase was added to 2% PVA solution in an aqueous
to organic phase ratio of 3:1 and sonicated at 30W
for 3  min (Sonicator 3000, Misonix sonicator, CT,
USA). This primary emulsion was then subjected to
three cycles of high-pressure homogenization (HPH)
(Microfluidics M110P, MA, USA) at 15,000 psi to yield
the nanoemulsion. The nanoemulsion was then either
spray dried (Buchi Mini Spray Dryer B-290, Switzer-
land) or lyophilized (Millrock Technology LD85, NY,
USA) following the evaporation of organic solvents in
an ice-bath under nitrogen flush to generate a colloidal
dispersion (n = 3).
Analysis of particle size, surface charge & physical
stability of the nanoparticles
Particle size and surface charge were determined for
the nanoemulsion following HPH using ZetaPlus,
Brookhaven Instruments Corporation, Holtsville, NY.
The particle size and zeta potential of the blank and
drug loaded, lyophilized and spray-dried nanoparticles
were determined by preparing 0.5 mg/ml nanosuspen-
sion of these nanoparticles in filtered deionized water.
Ten readings were recorded for each measurement and
each measurement was taken in triplicate. The values
are reported as mean  ±  SD. The stability of lyophi-
lized and spray-dried nanoparticles was inspected at
room temperature (25°C) over a period of 60 days by
recording particle size and zeta potential in triplicates
at specific time points.

cles, specially modified mica surface (APS mica) was

Differential scanning calorimetry
A differential scanning calorimeter (DSC, Shimadzu
DSC-60, Kyoto, Japan) was connected to a thermal
analysis operating system (Shimadzu TA-60WS).
Approximately 3–5 mg of the sample was nonhermeti-
cally crimped in an aluminum pan and heated from
room temperature to 300°C at a rate of 10°/min in an
atmosphere of nitrogen (flow rate = 20 ml/min). PTX
and GEM (drugs), lyophilized nanoparticles (blank
and PTX-GEM loaded) and spray-dried nanoparticles
(blank and PTX-GEM loaded) were analyzed.
x-ray diffraction analysis
The x-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis of the PTX and
GEM (pure drug), lyophilized nanoparticles (blank
and PTX-GEM loaded) and spray-dried nanoparticles
(blank and PTX-GEM loaded) was performed at room
temperature. The sample was filled in a copper holder
and exposed to Cu Kα radiation (40 kV × 40 mA) in a
wide angle x-ray diffractometer (Bruker D8 Advance,
WI, USA). The instrument was operated in a step-scan
mode, in 0.05° 2θ increments, and counts were accu-
mulated for 1.0 s at each step over the angular range
of 5–40° 2θ.
Karl Fisher titrimetry
The moisture content in the spray-dried and lyophi-
lized, blank and drug-loaded nanoparticles was deter-
mined by Karl Fisher titration (Mettler DL18 Karl
Fischer titrator, NJ, USA). Approximately 10–20 mg
of sample was used and moisture content was deter-
mined by biamperometric titration. Percent moisture
content was determined for the known weight of the
sample (n = 3).
HPLC analysis
An HPLC method was developed for determin-
ing PTX and GEM content in the nanoparticles.
The chromatographic separation of both PTX and
GEM was achieved by isocratic elution on Water’s
Nova-Pak C8 4  μm (2.1  ×  150  mm) column. The
mobile phase composition was acetonitrile: metha-
nol: 0.1  M ammonium acetate solution in water
(48.5:16.5:35  v/v/v). The flow rate of 0.5  ml/min
was maintained for a total run time of 6 min and the
column effluents were monitored at 234nm using a
photo diode array (Shimadzu SPD 10ADvp, Kyoto,
Japan). The retention time of GEM and PTX was 1.2
and 2.3 min, respectively.

weighed and extracted by biphasic extraction using
dichloromethane and water as solvents for extraction
of PTX and GEM respectively. The amount of PTX

In vitro release studies were carried out in side-by-
side 3 ml glass diffusion chambers (PermeGear Inc.,
Hellertown, PA) with donor and receiver chambers
maintained at 37°C. The donor and receiver chambers
were separated by 0.1  μm polycarbonate membrane
(Spectra/Por®Dialysis Membrane 2000Da). Phosphate

buffered saline (pH = 7.4) with 0.5% (w/v) Tween 80
was used as the release medium. Three milliliter of the
release medium was added to both the chambers and
stirred using magnetic stirrers. At time 0, about 26 mg
of lyophilized drug loaded particles or 29 mg of spray-

Determination of cellular uptake
Cellular uptake of PTX-GEM lyophilized and spray-
dried nanoparticles was studied with respect to drug
solution at equivalent concentrations for both the
drugs in MDCK and MDA-MB-231 cell lines. Cells
were cultured in Thermo Scientific Nunc™ 6-well
tissue culture plates. MDA-MB-231 and MDCK
cells were seeded at a density of approximately 4 × 105
and 1 × 106 cells per well, respectively and incubated
until confluent. A stock solution of 18.7 mg PTX and
7.6 mg GEM was prepared in 1 ml DMSO. 100 μl
of stock solution was added to 30  ml of Dulbecco’s
modified Eagle medium (for MDCK cells) or RPMI-
1640 media (for MDA-MB-231 cells). Lyophilized
and spray-dried nanoparticle suspensions were pre-
pared in the respective growth media at a drug con-
tent equivalent to the free drug treatment. The con-
fluent cell monolayers were treated with 2 ml of the
prepared drug solution or nanoparticle suspension
equivalent and allowed to incubate for 0.5, 1, 2, 3 and
6 h at 37°C. Following incubation, cells were washed
with ice-cold phosphate buffered saline three-times to
remove free drug and nanoparticles that had not been
uptaken. Cells were then lysed mechanically by scrap-
ing and probe ultrasonication (Sonicator 3000, Miso-
nix sonicator, CT, USA), and cell lysates were collected
in microcentrifuge tubes. Homogenized lysate (20 μl)
was analyzed for the total cellular protein content using
the BCA protein assay (Pierce, Rockford, IL). The
lysate was then centrifuged 17,000 × g for 10 min at
20°C (accuSpin Micro R, Fisher Scientific, Fairlawn,
NJ). The supernatant was analyzed by HPLC analysis
for PTX and GEM content. The cellular uptake was
reported as the mean ± SD of μg of PTX and GEM
content per mg of total cellular protein (n = 3).
Subcellular localization studies
The subcellular localization of the nanoparticles loaded
with a fluorescent marker Rhodamine 6G was evalu-
ated in MDA-MB-231 and MDCK cells. In brief,
MDA-MB-231 and MDCK cells were cultured on BD
Falcon 8-chamber slides at a seeding density of 2 × 105
cells per chamber and incubated overnight. Cells were
treated with the same nanoparticle concentration used
in the uptake study, dispersed in Dulbecco’s Phosphate-
Buffered Saline (DPBS) with lysotracker green (50 nM)
for 2 and 5 min. The treatment was washed three-times
with ice cold Dulbecco’s Phosphate-Buffered Saline and
fixed with 1% (v/v) paraformaldehyde for 10 min. The
wells were removed and the cells were mounted using
Fluorosheild mounting media with DAPI (4’,6-diamid-
ino-2-phenylindole)and sealed with coverslips. The
slides were viewed using a multiphoton confocal micro-
scope (Carl Ziess, Germany) at the Nebraska Center for
Cell Biology, Creighton University.
Statistical data analysis
Experimental data was statistically analyzed using a
single factor ANOVA. The differences were termed
statistically significant at p < 0.05.

Differential scanning calorimetry
In order to examine the physical state of PTX and
GEM in the PTX-GEM nanoparticle formulations
and elucidate the mechanism of entrapment of these
two drugs, DSC analysis was performed. An overlay
of DSC curves of PTX and GEM with blank and drug
loaded lyophilized nanoparticles is shown in Figure 3A
and spray-dried nanoparticles is shown in Figure 3B.
PTX and GEM show sharp endothermic melting peaks
at 222.95°C and 270.77°C, respectively, correspond-
ing to the reported melting points. The DSC curves of
PTX-GEM loaded nanoparticles had no endothermic
peak for PTX or GEM, indicating that the drugs are
present in a noncrystalline state in the polymer matrix.
x-ray diffraction analysis
An overlay of powder XRD patterns of GEM and
PTX [12] (pure drug) with lyophilized blank and PTX-
GEM nanoparticles is depicted in Figure 4A and with
spray-dried blank and PTX-GEM nanoparticles is
shown in Figure 4B. The XRD of the two drugs gave
unique fingerprint patterns owing to their crystalline
structure. However, both lyophilized and spray-dried
blank and drug-loaded nanoparticles did not show
the characteristic fingerprints for either drug in their
XRD pattern. This confirms that the drugs are present
in a noncrystalline state in the nanoparticles.

Entrapment efficiencies & drug loading
The paclitaxel and gemcitabine entrapment in nano-
emulsion after three washings was 72.01  ±  1.35%
for PTX and 6.95  ±  3.97% for GEM. The drug
loading for the lyophilized nanoparticles without
washing was 40.00  ±  4.67  μg/mg for PTX and
12.07 ± 2.83 μg/mg for GEM. In case of spray-dried
nanoparticles it was 39.97  ±  4.05  μg/mg for PTX
and 16.89 ± 1.03 μg/mg for GEM. Since the nano-
emulsion was directly subjected to drying without
washing, the drug loading efficiency for both the
drugs was close to 100%. There was no significant
difference in the drug content of lyophilized and
spray dried nanoparticles.

The in vitro release profile for GEM in lyophilized and

Discussion
Formulation of the delivery system
Spontaneous emulsification solvent diffusion method,
first described by Niwa et al., was used with modifi-
cations for preparation of primary emulsion [13]. This
method was modified to facilitate entrapment of a
hydrophilic drug by using water soluble organic sol-
vent for emulsification. Song et al. studied the factors
like effects of concentration and type of PLGA and sta-
bilizer on size of nanoparticles in spontaneous emul-
sification solvent diffusion. The study indicated that
increased hydrophilic and hydrophobic drug entrap-
ment and decreased particle size can be obtained with
75:25 L:G PLGA and 2% PVA [14]. HPH has been
reported to be a more efficient method of preparation
of submicron size monodispersed particles as compared
with ultrasonication [15]. In this study, probe sonication
was used to form a primary emulsion. However, further
reduction in particle size and particle size distribution
was obtained by using three cycles of HPH.
The entrapment of paclitaxel and gemcitabine
was calculated from the nanosuspension after filtra-
tion and washing. The most challenging part was to
ensure optimum entrapment of gemcitabine. Being
highly hydrophilic, gemcitabine escapes into the exter-
nal aqueous phase leaving only smaller quantity to be
entrapped. The maximum entrapment for gemcitabine
was around 10%. However, during centrifugation
the drug which is encapsulated near the surface of
the particle may diffuse out of the formulation and
will lead to underestimation of the drug entrapment
efficiency.
Particle size, zeta potential & morphology of
lyophilized & spray-dried nanoparticles
The particle size of lyophilized nanoparticles obtained
in this study was retained after drying from the nano-
suspension. The final product was sponge-like and eas-
ily dispersible in water. Vandervoort et al. showed that
PVA helps maintain the particle size of the nanopar-
ticles after lyophilization [16]. PVA has also been
reported to improve the stability and freezing resis-
tance of the nanoparticles and precludes the use of any
cryoprotectant during lyophilization [17].
The particle size and size distribution of particles
obtained after spray drying was higher than lyophi-
lized nanoparticles. Similar results were obtained in a
study by Ambrus et al. for particle size of freeze dried
and spray dried nanosuspension of poorly water soluble
drug meloxicam [18]. The particle size of a spray-dried
formulation depends on the droplet size distribution

The slightly negative charge of the particles is
imparted by the PVA in the preparation. Because of
the presence of uncapped carboxylic acid end groups,
nanospheres made from PLGA without addition of a

on PVA concentration and independent of the method
of drying.
The physical stability of the lyophilized and spray-
dried nanoparticles was tested by the particle size
and zeta potential analysis over a period of time and
indicated that the lyophilized nanoparticles were
stable at least over a period of 60 days. Many reports
study the stability of the colloidal nanosuspension
over time, however physical stability of the nanopar-
ticles after drying needs careful consideration in order
to assess increases in particle sizes, if any, on standing.
The results of this study suggest that submicron sized
spray-dried nanoparticles show lower stability than
lyophilized nanoparticles. The higher particle size and
free-flowing nature of the spray-dried particles may
result in higher particle contact and increase in particle
size on storage as opposed to sponge like structure of
lyophilized particles which may reduce particle contact.
The AFM micrographs showed obvious size-
difference in lyophilized and spray-dried nanoparticles.
The spray-dried nanoparticles showed aggregation in
some cases, however both spray-dried and lyophilized
nanoparticles were spherical in shape.

drugs followed by degradation. The endothermic melt-

lyophilized and spray-dried drug loaded nanoparticles

In vitro release studies
There was no significant difference between the drug
loading of lyophilized and spray-dried nanoparticles.
These nanoparticles were prepared without washing,
so the theoretical drug loading efficiency was 100%.
This takes into account any unentrapped or free drug
in the system. The release studies were carried out in
phosphate buffer with 0.5% Tween 80 to increase PTX
solubility to provide sink conditions during the in vitro
release studies [22]. The release profiles of GEM and
PTX were very different because of their differences in
physicochemical properties as well as drug loading.
Gemcitabine showed a biphasic release profile simi-
lar to the release profile described by Faisant et al. for
a hydrophilic drug 5-fluorouracil in PLGA matrix [23].
The release of GEM from both the formulations con-
stituted of a burst release (i.e. release of an initial large
bolus of drug) over the first 10 h, followed by a sus-
tained release which was approximately 0.44%/h for
lyophilized and 0.29%/h for spray-dried nanoparticles.
There was a significant difference in the release char-
acteristics of GEM from lyophilized and spray-dried
nanoparticles. Lyophilized nanoparticles gave a larger
cumulative release than spray-dried nanoparticles. In
spite of the low entrapment of GEM, sustained release
was observed for this hydrophilic drug over a period of
24 h. A possible explanation for this could be due to
adsorption of GEM on PLGA surface. Soppimath et al.
suggested that the release rate from the nanoparticles
may be dependent upon desorption of the adsorbed
drug from the polymer surface along with drug dif-
fusion through the matrix and erosion of the matrix
[24]. The higher total release in lyophilized nanopar-
ticles may, therefore, be attributed to more weakly
adsorbed GEM.
The PTX release profile for lyophilized and spray-
dried nanoparticles was close to a zero-order. PTX
release from lyophilized nanoparticles showed an initial
lag time of around 24 h followed by a sustained linear
release. The spray-dried nanoparticles also released drug
in a sustained and linear manner but with no initial lag
time. The initial lag phase in lyophilized nanoparticles
may be due to the presence of PTX in the core of the
nanoparticles that contribute to the slow diffusion of
the drug from the core across the polymeric matrix [24].
Liu et al. have reported delivery of a combination doxo-
rubicin and PTX in mesoporus silica nanoparticles [25].
In this study, the release amount of PTX was found to
be minimal and undetectable over first 24 h, which is
consistent with our findings. In case of both lyophilized
and spray-dried nanoparticles, the rate of linear release
was constant at approximately 0.035%/h.
Cellular uptake & subcellular localization
The cellular uptake of PTX and GEM from free drug
solution versus nanoparticles was investigated over the
course of 6 h in MDA-MB-231 (cancer) and MDCK
(normal) cells. The constant increase in the uptake
of PTX by MDA-MB-231 cells over a period of 6 h
(time dependent) was also seen by Trickler et al. [26].
This increase in PTX uptake over time was faster from
nanoparticles than PTX solution. The total amount of
PTX per mg of protein content taken up by both the cell
lines was higher in the case of lyophilized nanoparticles
than spray-dried nanoparticles. This may be attributed
to the smaller size of lyophilized nanoparticles. The
overall uptake of PTX and GEM from nanoparticles
was more in MDA MB 231 cells than MDCK cells.
This may be due to enhanced uptake of nanoparticles
by cancer cells than normal cells. Confocal microscopy
of MDA-MB-231 and MDCK cells further confirmed
the uptake of nanoparticles into the cells rather than the
adsorption of the particles to the cell surface. The pres-
ent study demonstrated that Rhodamine 6G contain-
ing nanoparticles had accumulated in the cytoplasm in
the majority of tumor cells as verified by colocalization
of lysotracker green and rhodamine.
Conclusion
The polymeric nanoparticles containing hydrophobic
(PTX) and hydrophilic (GEM) drugs entrapped in
a single PLGA matrix were developed and character-
ized. Lyophilization and spray drying were evaluated
as methods of drying a nanoemulsion with respect to
physicochemical and cellular properties. The method
of emulsification solvent diffusion combined with high-
pressure homogenization gave nano-sized emulsion
with high entrapment for hydrophobic drug. High-
pressure homogenization reduced the particle size of
the sonicated nanoemulsion by around 40%. There was
no significant increase in the particle size after lyophi-
lization but there was around four-times the increase in

particle size after spray drying, which was confirmed
by atomic force microscopy. The lyophilized and spray-
dried nanoparticles contained PTX and GEM in a non-
crystalline state and gave sustained release for both the
drugs. It can be concluded that using different drying
techniques can yield PTX-GEM nanoparticles with
different physicochemical and other properties that
include particle size, drug release and cellular uptake.
Future perspective
The present study evaluated the entrapment of a hydro-
philic and a hydrophobic drug into PLGA nanoparti-
cles. Future studies should include effects of a different
polymeric solid lipid system on hydrophilic drug entrap-
ment. Since this study involves use of high concentration
of PVA, an alternative to this stabilizer (e.g. poloxamer)
or a combination of stabilizers can be tested. The chemi-
cal stability of PTX and GEM in these nanoparticles
should be evaluated over time at different pH conditions
and compared with free drug solutions. Because of their
size advantage, spray-dried nanoparticles can be tested
for inhalation therapy for potential use in lung cancer.
Finally, these nanoparticles should be tested in vivo for
determining bioavailability, efficacy and toxicity and
correlating it with the results of these in vitro studies.

Executive summary
Formulation
• Paclitaxel and gemcitabine were studied as a potential drug combination by simultaneous entrapment in
PLGA nanoparticles prepared by modified emulsification solvent diffusion technique with high-pressure
homogenization for size reduction and Lyophilization and spray drying as drying techniques.
Drug entrapment
• The entrapment efficiency of hydrophobic drug paclitaxel was higher than the hydrophilic drug gemcitabine.
Particle size and morphology
• The particle size was retained after lyophilization while it increased in case of spray dried by 1.7-times in case
of blank nanoparticles and 3.6-times in case of drug loaded spray-dried nanoparticles and particles were
found to be spherical in morphology.
Physical state of the drugs
• Both the drugs were found to be in a noncrystalline form in lyophilized and spray-dried formulations as
confirmed by DSC and x-ray diffraction analysis.
Cellular uptake
• The cellular uptake of paclitaxel in lyophilized NP was 1.6-fold higher while gemcitabine was 2.5-fold higher as
compared with spray-dried NP in the MDA-MB-231 cells.
Comparison of lyophilized and spray-dried formulation
• Particle size, morphology, moisture content, release characteristics and cellular uptake were found to change
with method of drying. However, zeta potential and physical state of the drug were found to be unaffected
by drying technique used.
