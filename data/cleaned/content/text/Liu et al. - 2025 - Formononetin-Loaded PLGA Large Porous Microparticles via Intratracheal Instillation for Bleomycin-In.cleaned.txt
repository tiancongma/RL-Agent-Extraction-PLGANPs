## Abstract
Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF) is a progressive lung disease of unknown cause, with few effective therapies available
and high mortality rates. Our preceding research indicated that formononetin (FMN) could improve the symptoms of the
bleomycin-induced pulmonary fibrosis and be a promising drug against IPF. In this study, an inhalable formononetin-loaded
poly(lactic-co-glycolic) acid (PLGA) large porous microspheres (FMN-PLGA-MSs) was prepared by the method of emulsion solvent evaporation. SEM showed that FMN-PLGA-MSs were loose particles existing many pores on the surfaces,
and the measured mean geometric diameter was more than 10 um. The encapsulation efficiency (EE) and drug loading
efficiency (DL) were 87.72 +- 6.34% and 4.18 +- 0.30%. FMN in FMN-PLGA-MSs could be rapidly released within 2 h and
sustainably released for 21 d. Cell tests and q-RT-PCR tests showed that FMN could inhibit the activation of fibroblasts
and the deposition of extracellular matrix (ECM) by acting on the TGF-b1/Smad3 signaling pathway. FMN-PLGA-MSs
showed higher antifibrotic effects than free FMN oral administration in the pulmonary fibrosis models of mice, remarkably
improving pulmonary function, decreasing hydroxyproline levels, and attenuating lung injuries. By formulating formononetin into microsphere preparations, its solubility can be significantly enhanced, enabling effective pulmonary drug delivery.
This approach not only improves lung targeting but also reduces systemic toxicity. Additionally, it facilitates superior lung
deposition and extends the retention time of the formononetin within the lungs. Taken together, FMN-PLGA-MSs may be
a promising inhaled medication for the treatment of IPF.

Pulmonary administration is an effective drug delivery
system for the therapy of lung diseases, such as asthma,
emphysema, pneumonia, pulmonary fibrosis, and chronic
obstructive pulmonary disease . Compared with traditional oral and injection, pulmonary drug delivery is an

a high local concentration at the lung pathological site with
smaller drug dose and lower systemic side effects .
With the aim of reducing drug phagocytosis by macrophages and improving the therapeutic effect when applied
via pulmonary administration, various formulations have
been designed . Compared with other novel drug delivery systems, microspheres can achieve higher drug loading
and better sustained-release effect . So far, more than
10 types of microsphere preparations have been introduced
to the market. Porous microspheres are a dosage form for
pulmonary drug delivery. In the process of microsphere
preparation, an appropriate amount of pore-forming agent
is added to produce drug-loaded porous microspheres with
low density (less than 0.4 g/mL) and large geometric particle size (greater than 10 mm) . These characteristics
can avoid phagocytosis by pulmonary macrophages, slow
down particle aggregation, deposit the drug in the deep
lung, provide sustained release, and improve the therapeutic effect of the drug . The carrier for preparing porous microspheres includes biodegradable polymers

such as polylactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA), albumin, alginate, chitosan, and lipids such as dipalmitoylphosphatidylcholine and triglycerides, among which PLGA is the most
commonly used carrier . PLGA is a functional polymer compound synthesized from polylactic acid (PLA) and
polyglycolic acid (PGA) in a certain proportion. It has good
biocompatibility, degradability, non-toxicity, and has been
approved by the FDA for drug delivery [36, 37]. Hu et al.
prepared PLGA porous microspheres containing curcumin
for pulmonary delivery in the treatment of IPF. In vivo
studies showed that curcumin-loaded porous microspheres
significantly reduced the hydroxyproline content induced
by bleomycin, decreased the synthesis of collagen I, and
exhibited better anti-fibrotic effects compared to curcumin
powder . Porous microspheres loading RLX (Recombinant Human Relaxin-2) was prepared by Liu, which not only
reduced excessive collagen deposition, but also reversed
fibrosis to some extent .
Here, we prepared FMN-loaded PLGA large porous
microspheres (FMN-PLGA-MSs) for the treatment of bleomycin-induced pulmonary fibrosis via intratracheal instillation. We characterized the FMN-PLGA-MSs, including
particle size, drug loading efficiency and encapsulation efficiency. Furthermore, we evaluated the in vitro release profiles and uptake by fibroblasts. We revealed the anti-fibrotic
theory of FMN through measuring a train of in vitro markers in the cells, including Collagen I, a-SMA, fibronectin.
Finally, the therapeutic efficacy and in vivo biodistribution of
FMN-PLGA-MSs were demonstrated in a mouse model. It
is expected that FMN-PLGA-MSs can provide a new therapeutic strategy for IPF treatment.

The drug release profiles of FMN from FMN-PLGAMSs were studied using the dialysis method. Free FMN
and FMN-PLGA-MSs were individually placed in 2 mL
of simulated lung fluid (SLF) . These solutions were
transferred to dialysis bags (molecular weight cut-off of

could be beneficial for long-term treatment of IPF.

Attenuation on TGF-b1-activated Lung Fibroblasts

TGF-b1 is the most critical fibroblast-activating factor
involved in the development of IPF. To investigate whether
FMN could inhibit TGF-b1-induced fibroblast activation,
we cultured MLg cells with TGF-b1 (5 ng/mL), free FMN
(10 mM, 20 mM) and FMN-PLGA-MSs (10 mM, 20 mM)
to evaluate the gene expression levels of a-SMA, collagen I and fibronectin. As shown in Fig. 3, both free FMN
and FMN-PLGA-MSs could decrease TGF-b1-stimulated
mRNA expression levels of a-SMA, collagen I and fibronectin. These results suggest that FMN act was an inhibitor
of TGF-b1/Smad3 signaling pathway, which could inhibit
TGF-b1-induced myofibroblast activation and downregulate
extracellular matrix deposition. After encapsulating FMN
into FMN-PLGA-MSs, it had the same inhibitory effect
on TGF-b1-induced lung fibroblast activation and ECM
accumulation.

Pulmonary Fibrosis Inhibition

The therapeutic effect of FMN-PLGA-MSs was evaluated
in a mouse model of BLM-induced pulmonary fibrosis .
Seven days after injection of bleomycin, mice were administered different doses of FMN (50, 100 mg/kg), FMN-PLGAMSs (25, 50 mg/kg), or NDNB (100 mg/kg) once daily.
Based on their safety profile in mice, FMN-PLGA-MSs
were instilled intratracheally into the lungs using a small
animal aerosol drug delivery device at lower doses. NDNB,
a drug used to treat IPF, was used as a positive control.
Fourteen days after BLM injection, changes in pulmonary
function were measured (Fig. 5a). BLM-induced pulmonary
fibrosis led to an increase in inspiratory resistance (Ri) and
expiratory resistance (Re), along with an obvious reduction in forced vital capacity (FVC) and dynamic compliance (Cdyn). The pulmonary function of FMN-PLGA-MSs
(50 mg/kg) treated mice was significantly improved, with
effects superior to those of free FMN (50, 100 mg/kg) and
comparable to those of NDNB (100 mg/kg). In addition,
Fig. 5b showed that the FMN-PLGA-MSs could alleviate
BLM-induced weight loss compared with the model group,
the alleviative effects of FMN-PLGA-MSs (25, 50 mg/kg)
were similar to those of NDNB (100 mg/kg).
