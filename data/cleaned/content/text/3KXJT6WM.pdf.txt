Abstract
The aim of the present study was to evaluate the antimicrobial activity of nanoparticle and free
formulations of the CAPE compound using different methods and comparing the results in the
literature for the Ô¨Ārst time. In parallel with this purpose, encapsulation of CAPE with the PLGA
nanoparticle system (CAPE-PLGA-NPs) and characterization of nanoparticles were carried out.
Afterwards, antimicrobial activity of free CAPE and CAPE-PLGA-NPs was determined using
agar well diffusion, disk diffusion, broth microdilution and reduction percentage methods. P.
aeroginosa, E. coli, S. aureus and methicillin-resistant S. aureus (MRSA) were chosen as model
bacteria since they have different cell wall structures. CAPE-PLGA-NPs within the range of
214.0Ôā†¬ĪÔā†8.80 nm particle size and with an encapsulation efÔ¨Āciency of 91.59Ôā†¬ĪÔā†4.97% were
prepared using the oil-in-water (o‚Äďw) single-emulsion solvent evaporation method. The
microbiological results indicated that free CAPE did not have any antimicrobial activity in any of
the applied methods whereas CAPE-PLGA-NPs had signiÔ¨Ācant antimicrobial activity in both
broth dilution and reduction percentage methods. CAPE-PLGA-NPs showed moderate
antimicrobial activity against S. aureus and MRSA strains particularly in hourly measurements at
30.63 and 61.25 őľg ml‚ąí1 concentrations (both pÔā†<Ôā†0.05), whereas they failed to show
antimicrobial activity against Gram-negative bacteria (P. aeroginosa and E. coli, pÔā†>Ôā†0.05). In
the reduction percentage method, in which the highest results of antimicrobial activity were
obtained, it was observed that the antimicrobial effect on S. aureus was more long-standing
(3 days) and higher in reduction percentage (over 90%). The appearance of antibacterial activity
of CAPE-PLGA-NPs may be related to higher penetration into cells due to low solubility of free
CAPE in the aqueous medium. Additionally, the biocompatible and biodegradable PLGA
nanoparticles could be an alternative to solvents such as ethanol, methanol or DMSO.
Consequently, obtained results show that the method of selection is extremely important and will
inÔ¨āuence the results. Thus, broth microdilution and reduction percentage methods can be
recommended as reliable and useful screening methods for determination of antimicrobial
activity of PLGA nanoparticle formulations used particularly in drug delivery systems compared
to both agar well and disk diffusion methods.

1. Introduction
Caffeic acid phenethyl ester (CAPE) is a natural bioactive
compound obtained from the honeybee [39]. CAPE has been
reported to have various biological and pharmacological
effects, e.g. antiviral [16], antioxidant [47, 54], antiallergic
[44], anti-carcinogenic [7], anti-inÔ¨āammatory [36], immuno-
modulatory [43] and antibacterial [16]. There are several
studies in the literature on antimicrobial activity of CAPE on
Enterococcus faecalis, Listeria monocytogenes, Staphylo-
coccus aureus, Bacillus subtilis, Pseudomonas aeruginosa
and Haemophilus inÔ¨āuenzae [26, 27, 51, 58]. These studies
show that RNA, DNA and cellular proteins are possible tar-
gets of CAPE [26, 27, 39, 51, 58]. Additionally, Takaisi-
Kikuni and Schilcher suggest that the antimicrobial action of
CAPE is probably based on the inhibition of bacterial RNA
polymerase [56]. Moreover, Lee et al reported that the anti-
microbial effect of CAPE is related to outer membrane
damage and reactive oxygen species synthesis in bacteria
[29]. A recent study carried out by Firat et al found that
CAPE prevents bacterial translocation in an experimental
intestinal obstruction model in rats [17]. Although CAPE has
a crucial role in many biological activities as a therapeutic
compound, its biological effectiveness and bioavailability are
limited by low water solubility due to its hydrophobic char-
acter [30]. In recent years, research studies have been focused
on improving the bioavailability of poorly bioavailable drugs
and
phytocompounds
by
nanoencapsulation
techniques.
These nanoparticles, exhibiting larger surface area to volume
ratio, have signiÔ¨Ācant effects on pharmacokinetics and phar-
macodynamics of bioactive compounds. Also, nanoparticles
that have very small particle sizes are regarded as ideal
vehicles for site-speciÔ¨Āc drug delivery [31]. PLGA (poly-D,L-
lactide-co-glycolide) has been one of the most important and
preferred nanoparticle systems in the pharmaceutical industry
as a drug carrier in recent years. Additionally, PLGA is a
suitable system for encapsulation of antimicrobial com-
pounds, particularly with hydrophobic structure, due to its
high biodegradability, biocompatibility and low toxicity
[1, 3]. For instance, Esmaili et al showed that rifampicin
loaded PLGA nanoparticles importantly increased the in vitro
antibacterial effect compared to free rifampicin against both
Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria, on the grounds
that the nanoparticle formulation has better penetration of
bacteria cell walls [15] Hill et al reported that the MIC value
of cinnamon bark extract loaded PLGA was lower (PÔā†<Ôā†0.05)
than the MIC of free CBE for both S. typhimurium and L.
monocytogenes [18]. In another study, it was stated that
PLGA encapsulated thymoquinone with sustained release
property preserved the antimicrobial activity in addition to
antibacterial property exhibited against E. coli, S. aureus and
S. typhi strains [40]. In the previous studies concerning the
antimicrobial activity of the PLGA encapsulated compounds,
it is observed that antimicrobial methods such as agar dilu-
tion, broth microdilution and disk or agar well diffusion are
preferred [15, 18, 29, 40]. However, these methods and/or
others that take place in the literature differ signiÔ¨Ācantly in
their properties, so the results obtained are affected due to
many factors such as the method selected, micro-organisms
used and the degree of solubility or the diffusability of the test
compounds
[60].
Also,
the
antimicrobial
susceptibility
methods used for the evaluation of antimicrobial activity of
PLGA delivery systems have not been validated and, there-
fore, these methods are not standardized. Comparing these
methods is important for ensuring standardization. Moreover,
no study regarding the comparison and evaluation of the
different methods to determine the antimicrobial activity of
PLGA nanoparticle formulations in the literature, particularly
in drug delivery systems, has been found.
Hence, the primary objective of this study was to syn-
thesize and characterize a CAPE loaded PLGA. Subse-
quently, the main purpose of our study was to evaluate the
antibacterial activity of the CAPE-PLGA-NPs and free CAPE
using four different methods and comparing the results from
these four different methods with each other. Evaluated dif-
ferences in the results between distinct methods are particu-
larly based on the factors such as the method selected,
bacteria used, nanoparticle‚Äďbacterium interactions in different
medium environments, time-dependent release in nano-
particles and the amount of released substance. P. aeroginosa,
E. coli, S. aureus and MRSA were chosen as model bacteria
to evaluate the effect of PLGA encapsulated CAPE on
microbial organisms since they have different cell wall
structures (Gram positive or negative).

3.2. Evaluation of antibacterial activity of CAPE loaded PLGA
nanoparticles
Encapsulation of bioactive agents into nanoparticle delivery
systems is being increasingly tested in several areas with the
purpose of improving the biocompatibility and bioavailability
of hydrophobic compounds. It has been observed in the lit-
erature that PLGA is commonly used in drug delivery sys-
tems. In the last decade, there have been various studies
published on the use of PLGA polymer as an ideal carrier
system for the encapsulation of substances such as cucurbi-
tacin B [23], curcumin [2], quercetin [46], antibiotics [15],
plant extracts [5] etc.
The main purpose of this work was to determine the
antibacterial activity of CAPE-PLGA-NPs in comparison
with the free drug formulation. We used four methods to
attain accurate results both quantitatively and qualitatively:
the agar well diffusion assay, disk diffusion assay, broth
microdilution method and reduction percentage method. The
results of qualitative analysis were obtained based on the
presence or absence of inhibition zones/zone diameters. On
the other hand, quantitative analysis results consist of the
data obtained from the spectrophotometrical measurements
and the numerically counted colonies. In this study, Gram-
negative (P. aeroginosa and E. coli) and Gram-positive
bacteria (S. aureus and MRSA) were used for the anti-
microbial tests.

the principle that antimicrobial agents diffuse through the
agar medium. Thus these tests can give false-negative results,
as nanoparticles can agglomerate and may not diffuse inside
the agar medium [52]. Additionally, it may be that the

3.2.4. General discussion.
In the present study, using the
four different methods, the nanoparticle formulation of CAPE
was examined in terms of antimicrobial activity. However,
the obtained results showed that the method of selection is
extremely important and will inÔ¨āuence the results. Qualitative
methods (agar well and disk diffusion methods) allow
simultaneous testing of a large number of agents considered
to be antimicrobial, in a relatively easy and inexpensive
manner. However, these methods are unable to generate a
MIC value, because the results of these tests are considered
qualitative, giving results of susceptible, intermediate or
resistant. In many studies, diffusion methods were used to
determine the antimicrobial activity of different types of
nanoparticle [3, 19, 40, 42]. When these survey results and
our results were compared, it is observed that there are many
factors that may affect the results of this method in
nanoparticle systems, such as the size, structure, diffusion
ratio, solubility, types of nanoparticle system and bacterial
strains tested. Also, Wiegand et al have stated that diffusion
tests with different samples can only be compared under
stable conditions and containing a diffusible agent (e.g.
antibiotics) [60]. Thus, comparison and evaluation of the
results from diffusion methods with a different method
(especially the quantitative methods) is considered to be a
correct strategy.

methods (broth dilution and reduction percentage methods)

4. Conclusion
In this study, CAPE was successfully encapsulated into the
PLGA nanoparticles by a o‚Äďw single-emulsion solvent eva-
poration technique, resulting in a particular delivery system
able to improve the antimicrobial properties of CAPE. After
detailed characterization, the antimicrobial activity of CAPE-
PLGA-NPs was determined by using disk diffusion, agar well
diffusion, broth microdilution and reduction percentage
methods in comparison with free CAPE.
In conclusion, it could be shown that the method selected
for determination of antimicrobial effects of nanoparticle
systems will inÔ¨āuence the results obtained, and therefore the
evaluation of the antimicrobial efÔ¨Ācacy. For the Ô¨Ārst time in
this study, disk diffusion, agar well diffusion, broth micro-
dilution and reduction percentage methods were used com-
paratively for determining the antimicrobial activity of CAPE
loaded PLGA. The variation occurring between different
susceptibility testing methods in our study reveals the need to
apply multiple methods when conducting an in vitro anti-
microbial activity assay for an encapsulated active ingredient.
Evaluating the previous studies, it was observed that several
methods for the determination of the antimicrobial activity of
nanoparticle systems (PLGA, silver, gold, silver‚Äďtitania, Ag-
NPs, ZnO-NPs) are available [32‚Äď34, 38, 62] but not stan-
dardized. In terms of PLGA, there are no data related to
comparison and evaluation of the different methods to
determine the antimicrobial activity of PLGA nanoparticle
formulations. Our study suggested that broth microdilution
and reduction percentage methods can be recommended as
reliable and meaningful screening methods for determination
of antimicrobial activity of PLGA nanoparticle formulations
used particularly in drug delivery systems.
Ensuring standardization and validation of the anti-
microbial susceptibility methods used in the evaluation of
nanoparticle systems will be beneÔ¨Ācial for routine practice in
clinical laboratories. Also, it is necessary to continue the
research on the evaluation of the methodologies used for
measurement of the antimicrobial activity of PLGA nano-
particle systems.
