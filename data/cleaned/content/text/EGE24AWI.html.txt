Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) is the most common and malignant form of astrocytoma with a median survival time of 1–2 years after diagnosis. The major treatment hurdle is the limited delivery of drugs to the brain due to the insurmountable blood brain barrier (BBB). Literature review reveals that nanoparticulate delivery system, in particular, magnetic polymeric nanoparticles in the presence of an external magnetic field may have enhanced permeation across the BBB. Therefore, the objective of this study was to fabricate the core of the polymeric nanoparticles with magnetic property. This core was fabricated by a modified seed synthesis method using tris(acetylacetonate) iron (III) [Fe(acac)3] as a precursor. To prevent the oxidation of Fe(acac)3, an overcoating with oleic acid was performed. The resulting oleic acid magnetic nanoparticle (OAMNP) was successfully prepared with a yield of 187.5 ± 4.2 mg. The particle size of the core was 9 ± 0.3 nm with a polydispersity index (PDI) of 0.189 ± 0.001. OAMNP was dispersed within the polymeric matrix comprising of poly(lactic acid)–methoxy poly(ethylene oxide) (PLA–PEG) and poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) (PEG PLGA) by single emulsion solvent evaporation technique. The resulting nanoparticle had a particle size of 201 ± 2.1 nm with a PDI of 0.197 ± 0.003. The presence of co-polymer block linkage on the surface of OAMNP was qualitatively confirmed by proton NMR and FT-IR analysis. The mean entrapment efficiency of OAMNP within the PEG PLGA matrix was 43%. The iron concentration was 60 ± 3.1 µg/ml which was below is cytotoxic concentration, and in line with published literature. Furthermore, in the presence of an external magnetic field superior efficacy of OAMNP PEG PLGA NPS in U-138 cells was observed.

The conventional treatment approaches for GBM such as radiotherapy, surgery and chemotherapy have not shown promising results in terms of increasing survival time in patients suffering from GBM [1]. Also, the use of novel drug delivery system, for example, drug–loaded microchips have not yet reached pre-clinical stage, and therefore may require time for its translation into the clinical. Among others, the lack of biodegradability, higher chance of electrical malfunctioning, energy requirements and issues with communication to the implant are some of the drawbacks of this technology [1]. On the other hand, nanoparticles, owing to their small (i.e., <300 nm) size can cross cell barriers, target tumor cells through surface functionalization, have increased plasma circulation half-life, and possesses ability to protect the loaded drug from potential biological degradation [2]. However, presence of tight junctions and selective nature of the BBB imparts great hindrance for such delivery systems to passively cross and deliver significant amount of drug across the highly guarded BBB [3]. Hence, application of external magnetic field application may help overcome this challenge. Magnetic field application enhances the permeation of magnetic nanoparticles across the blood brain barrier (BBB) in a non-invasive manner. Also, from past few years magnetic nanoparticles or magnetic drug delivery therapy have been explored as better alternative to typical chemotherapeutics agents against several types of cancer [4]. Study have shown that with the application of a magnetic field, a dual-targeting (i.e., brain drug delivery and anti-glioma treatment) has been achieved [5]. In this method of developing nano particulate system, the authors encapsulated two drugs instead of one, which increased the payload and in turn increases the overall complexity of the system. In comparison, the purpose of this work was to design the nano particulate system encapsulating a single drug and having magnetic property. To this end, the synthesis of superparamagnetic nanoparticles has been studied extensively in the past two decades in terms of both scientific interest as well as their technological application [6]. The term superparamagnetic suggests that nanoparticles comprising of metallic core within its core loses its magnetism property upon removal of the externally applied magnetic field [7]. These nanoparticles owing to their magnetic property also possess an additional advantage over conventional nanoparticles (i.e., polymeric nanoparticles) due to sensitivity to an external magnet, apart from its smaller size and surface charge which aids its permeation across the BBB. The advantage of this approach is that it does not require surface functionalization with antibody or additional surfactant in the nano-formulation which further complicates the fabrication and characterization of the nanoparticles. One of the applications of the magnetic nanoparticles for drug delivery is that a higher targeting efficacy can be achieved in presence of the external magnetic field. We hypothesize that a well-characterized oleic acid loaded nanoparticles (OAMNP) when loaded within a polymeric matrix will be an ideal delivery system for loading of chemotherapeutic agent required for the treatment of glioblastoma (GBM). Therefore, the current work will support the next and subsequent step of developing drug or chemotherapeutic agent loaded nano particulate system. OAMNP is often synthesized by thermal decomposition of iron acetyl acetonate (Fe(acac)3) or similar species [8]. We used (Fe(acac)3) and iron pentacarbonyl [Fe(CO)5] individually to determine the effect of iron precursor on the yield and overall characterization of OAMNP. Hence, the aim of the present work, was to synthesize magnetic nanoparticle using single iron precursor, with an overcoat of oleic acid dispersed in PEG PLGA matrix and thus characterized for in vitro GBM cell uptake, eventually for brain targeting of potential chemotherapeutic agent in the brain to treat GBM.

2. Material and reagents

Olyl amine, oleic acid and hexane decanediol was purchased from TCI, (Portland, OR).Fe(acac)3 was purchased from Alfa Aesar, (Ward Hill, MA). Iron pentacarbonyl [Fe(CO)5], Poly(D,L-lactide-co-glycolide) (PLGA) 50:50 with a molecular weight of 40000–75000 Da, poly(vinyl alcohol) (PVA) 87%–89% hydrolyzed with an average molecular weight of 13000–23000 Da, dichloromethane (DCM), dioctyl ether 99%, oleic acid 93% (GC), 1,10 phenanthroline, Mohr’s salt solution, 98% ethanol spectrophotometric grade, and dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, MO). Human glioblastoma cells (U-138), Eagle's Minimum Essential Medium (EMEM) (ATCC® 30-2003™), and fetal bovine serum were purchased from ATCC. All reagents were of analytical grade and were used as received.

3. Methods

3.1. Synthesis and characterization of OAMNP

3.1.1. Synthesis of OAMNP

3.1.1.1. Size controlled synthesis

A modified size-controlled synthesis of OAMNP was used to synthesize OAMNP [9]. Briefly, in a round bottom flask Fe(acac)3 (2 mmol) was mixed in phenyl ether (20 ml) with 1,2-hexadecanediol (10 mmol), oleic acid (6 mmol), and oleylamine (6 mmol) and was first heated to 100 °C for 20 min, followed by 200 °C and 260 °C for 10 min, respectively. After this, the reaction mixture was allowed to cool down to room temperature. The brown-red colored dispersion was washed with ethanol thrice. After each washing step, the mixture was centrifuged at 10,000g for 15 min. The supernatant was discarded and pelletized OAMNP was isolated. After this, hexane was added to pelletized OAMNP to give dark-brown dispersion. The final product (OAMNP) dispersed in hexane was stored until further characterization was conducted. For ease of handling, the dispersion was dried under vacuum and stored as dry powder at room temperature

3.1.1.2. Thermal decomposition

In this method, iron pentacarbonyl [Fe(CO)5] was thermally decomposed to prepare OAMNP [1]. Oleic acid (1.43 ml) was heated with dioctyl ether (10 ml) at 80 °C under vacuum for 30 min. Subsequently, nitrogen flux was then started to maintain an inert atmosphere. The temperature of mixture was gradually raised to 100 °C and allowed to stand at that temperature for 30 min. After this, iron pentacarbonyl (0.28 ml) was added to the round bottom flask assembly dropwise, which was followed by heating the mixture to 300 °C for an additional 30 min. The heat was then turned off and the obtained mixture was allowed to cool to room temperature. Ethanol was added to precipitate the formed OAMNP, and the mixture was centrifuged at 10,000 g for 15 min. The pelletized OAMNP was dissolved in hexane and re-precipitated with ethanol. The final OAMNP was dissolved in hexane and stored at room temperature until further characterization.

3.1.2. Characterization of OAMNP

3.1.2.1. Particle size analysis

The particle size was carried out by Malvern zeta sizer (Nano-ZS, Malvern, Westborough, MA). For particle size analysis, 1 ml of OAMNP dispersed in hexane was transferred to a glass cuvette (PCS1115) and introduced into the equipment. Samples were allowed to briefly equilibrate to 25 °C and subsequently particle size was analyzed.

3.1.2.2. Particle size analysis by transmission electron microscopy (TEM)

The morphology and size of synthesized OAMNP were further characterized by TEM. A drop of a diluted suspension of OAMNP nanoparticles in hexane was deposited on a carbon coated copper grid and allowed to air dry for 3 h. Particles were imaged using a JEOL JEM-1200EX (JOEL, Peabody, MA) 80 kV. The NIH Image J software was used to calculate the mean particle diameter from the TEM photomicrograph.

3.1.2.3. Attenuated total reflectance Fourier transform infra-red (ATR FT-IR)

Dried sample of OAMNP was placed on the ATR cell and the spectra were acquired from the range of 700–4000 cm−1 in the transmittance mode using an IR spectrophotometer (Perkin Elmer, Waltham, MA).

3.1.2.4. Determination of iron content in OAMNP

Iron content analysis was carried out by 1,10 phenanthroline assay [10]. In respective tubes, 25, 50, 75, and 100 mg of dried OAMNP was weighed, to which 200 µL of concentrated hydrochloric acid was added. The mix was maintained at room temperature for 1 h. After this, the sample was diluted to 1 ml with purified water. Then, to 50 µL of the aliquot was added 2 ml of 0.5 M ammonium acetate-acetic acid buffer, (pH 3.0) followed by 100 µL of 10% aqueous solution of hydroxylamine hydrochloride. This step facilitates the reduction of iron from ferric [Fe(III)] to ferrous [Fe(II)] state. To this solution was added 500 µL of 0.3% aqueous solution of 1,10 phenanthroline and the solution was incubated at room temperature for 1 h. At end of 1 h, the color change to blue color was observed. The absorbance was measured at 511 nm using a visible spectrophotometer (Beckman Coulter, Indianopolis, IN). The concentration of iron in the samples was calculated from a standard curve, which was prepared using Mohr’s salt solution in 0.01 N HCl in the concentration range of 0.1–100 µg of Fe/ml.

3.2. Synthesis and characterization of OAMNP loaded PEG PLGA nanoparticles

3.2.1. Synthesis of OAMNP loaded PEG PLGA nanoparticles

To prepare OAMNP loaded PEG PLGA nanoparticles, the modified single emulsion solvent evaporation method was used [1]. Different amounts of oleic acid magnetic nanoparticles (OAMNP) (i.e., 25 mg, 50 mg, 75 mg, and 100 mg, respectively) were added to 1 ml of Dichloromethane (DCM) along with 20 mg of methoxy PEG-PLA and 80 mg of PLGA. The two DCM solutions were mixed and emulsified in aqueous poly (vinyl alcohol) (PVA) (5% w/v, 4 ml, pH 12) for 1 min using a Branson Sonifier. The resultant emulsion was dispersed in aqueous PVA (0.3% w/v, 50 ml, pH 12). The DCM was removed from this dispersion under reduced pressure for 30 min. The OAMNP loaded PEG PLGA was dispersed in water and centrifuged at 15,000 g for 15 min. The pellet was washed by repeating the centrifugation step thrice. The washed pellet was lyophilized using a freeze dryer (Labconco, Kansas City, MO) to yield OAMNP loaded PEG PLGA nanoparticles.

3.2.2. Characterization of OAMNP loaded PEG PLGA nanoparticles

3.2.2.1. OAMNP loading and OAMNP encapsulation efficiency

The OAMNP loading in each formulation was determined by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA). Each formulation as shown in Table 1 was weighed (5 mg) and placed in a TGA pan and heated from 25 to 600 °C, at the rate of 20 °C min−1 under a continuous nitrogen flux of 20 ml min−1. The amount of residuum is the amount of OAMNP in each sample (experimental loading). The amount of maghemite taken in mg to prepare the sample is the theoretical loading.(1)

3.2.2.2. FTIR

For FTIR analysis, particles were taken on the sample cell and FTIR spectra were recorded on a Perkin Elmer (spectrum 2 FTIR spectrophotometer). The samples were scanned, and spectra were acquired between the range of 4000–700 cm−1.

3.2.2.3. Determination of PEG PLGA linkage by NMR

The freeze-dried sample was reconstituted in deuterated chloroform (d6-CDCl3). Samples were transferred into glass NMR tubes and analyzed by H1 NMR with magnetic strength of 400 MHz (Bruker, Billerica, MA) using an auto shimming mode.

3.2.2.4. Prussian blue staining and intracellular iron concentration

3.2.2.4.1. Cell line and cell culture

U-138 cells (Homo sapiens brain glioblatoma cell lines) (ATCC) ( Manassas, VA) were used. These cell lines were maintained at 37° ± 0.5 °C, 5% CO2, with Eagle's Minimum Essential Medium Fig. 1 (EMEM) (with sodium pyruvate and L-glutamine) containing 10% fetal bovine serum and 1% penicillin/streptomycin.

For subsequent analysis and/or sub-culturing, the following general procedure was followed. Once the cells were confluent in the prepared T-25/75 flask, growth mediums (depending upon the cell-line) were aspirated leaving the attached cell monolayer undisturbed. Then, the cell monolayer was washed twice with sterile DPBS (pH 7.4). After aspirating the DPBS used for washing the cells, 2–3 ml of 0.25% Trypsin-EDTA solution for cell detachment was added to the flask which was then placed in the incubator (Fisher Scientific, Marietta, OH) at 37° ± 0.5 °C, 5% CO2 for not more than 5 min. Cell detachment was confirmed under the microscope at 10× magnification. And then, a fresh growth medium (10 ml) was added to the flask and aspirated several times for repeatedly flushing to ensure that all the cells are in suspension. And then, the cell suspension was transferred to a 50 ml sterile polypropylene tube. The cell suspension was then centrifuged at 1500 rpm at 4 °C for 5 to 7 min to pelletize the cells. The supernatant was discarded, and the cells were re-suspended in 10 ml of the growth medium by aspirating slowly several times to break the lumps of the cell pellet. Once a homogenous cell suspension was obtained, a cell count was performed using a hemocytometer (0.1 mm deep, Bright-Line® improved Neubauer, Hausser Scientific, Horsham, PA). All cell culture studies were performed under biosafety hood, aseptically. And all cells were grown as adherent monolayers in drug-free culture medium for more than 2 weeks before the assay.

3.2.2.4.2. Cell uptake for iron in presence and absence of magnet

U-138 cells were seeded in 6-well plates (Corning Incorporated, Corning, NY) at the density of 2.0 × 105cells per well and were allowed to attach for 24 h at 37. The following day, the medium was changed and continued to be incubated for 3 h with OAMNP PEG PLGA NPS with 60 μg/ml of iron concentration in the presence and absence of a magnet. Wells without the influence of a magnet, and growth medium served as controls. After 3 h formulation was removed, and cells were washed with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) three times and were stained by Prussian blue staining. The above experiment was carried out in triplicate.

3.2.2.4.3. Determination of cell uptake of iron by Prussian blue staining

The intracellular ferric iron was measured by the Prussian blue staining experiment. U-138 cells were seeded in 6-well plates and treated with the formulation as described previously. Subsequently, cells were washed with PBS three times. After fixation by 4% paraformaldehyde solution for 20 min, cells were incubated at 37 °C with Prussian blue solution comprising an equal volume of 2% hydrochloric acid aqueous solution and 2% potassium ferrocyanide (II) trihydrate for 30 min. Cells were washed thrice and finally observed using a microscope at the magnification of 40×. Pictures were taken for control wells, wells treated with the formulation in presence of a magnet, absence of magnet.

3.2.2.4.4. Determination of intracellular iron concentration

To all treated and control wells after washing with PBS, 400 μL of 0.5% Triton X-100 in 0.2 N NaOH was added to the sample wells to lyse the cells. The mixture was then centrifuged, and the supernatant was separated into different tubes. In the tube, 200 µL of concentrated hydrochloric acid was added and incubated for 1 h at room temperature. The sample was then diluted to 1 ml with water. To 50 µL of aliquot was added 2 ml of 0.5 M ammonium acetate-acetic acid buffer, (pH 3.0), followed by 100 µL of 10% aqueous solution of hydroxylamine hydrochloride to reduce Fe(III) to Fe(II). To this solution was added 500 µL of 0.3% aqueous solution of 1,10 phenanthroline and the solution was incubated for 1 h at room temperature. Iron concentration was determined as described previously.

4. Statistical analysis

All data are presented as mean ± SD and the difference between groups was analyzed by using SigmaStat version 3.5 (Palo Alto, CA). Differences between the two groups were evaluated by the Paired t-tests. In each case, a p-value<0.05 was considered as a representation of significant difference.

5. Results and discussion

5.1. Synthesis of OAMNP

5.1.1. Size-controlled seed synthesis

In the modified size-controlled seed synthesis method, the effect of temperature and reaction time on the synthesis of OAMNP was studied. At a temperature of 100 °C for 30 min followed by 260 °C for 30 min, the product turned black because of unconjugated oleic acid. However, at a temperature of 100 °C for 20 min, followed by 200 °C for 10 min and 260 °C for 10 min, the product was found to be a uniform red brick dispersion because of the conjugation of ferrous or Fe(acac)3 and oleic acid. Therefore, these temperature conditions were used for the fabrication of OAMNP. Furthermore, this method was found to be sufficient to yield uniform red brick dispersion of OAMNP. The obtained dried powder was weighed to get the mean yield of the OAMNPs which was about 187.5 mg, which was comparatively higher than the reported method (i.e., 62 mg) followed earlier before optimization of reaction time and temperature.

5.1.2. Thermal decomposition

The obtained dried form of OAMNP synthesized by thermal decomposition was weighed to get the yield. The mean yield was 71.12 mg and the particle size was 74.7 ± 1.5 nm. The PDI was 0.527 ± 0.102, which was observed to be higher than 0.2, which indicated high polydispersity in particle size distribution, and hence the dispersed system doesn’t represent mono-dispersed particles [11].

5.1.3. Comparison of size-controlled seed synthesis and thermal decomposition method

Particle size and yield of the nanoparticles were chosen as two parameters for comparison of the above two methods (Fig. 1). The data were compared and shown in Table 2. As shown in the table, variability in particle size and yield of OAMNP can be observed. Size controlled seed synthesis exhibited a lower particle size and better yield of OAMNP. The possible reason could be a coating of oleic acid on iron (Fe) that provides surfactant action leading to particles of smaller size and narrow size distribution [12]. If the conjugation occurred, lower oleic acid is in free form which can increase the particle size of the system (i.e., OAMNP). Based on this, size-controlled seed synthesis method was selected for further development.

5.2. Characterization of OAMNP

5.2.1. Particle size analysis

Upon analysis by the particle sizer, an average particle size of OAMNP was observed to be 9.3 ± 0.3 nm with a mean PDI of 0.189. The results indicated narrow size distribution of the fabricated OAMNP.

The TEM image (Fig. 2) exhibited the uniform spherical shape of the particles, which were in agreement with those reported in the literature for the nanoparticles exhibiting superparamagnetism properties [3].

5.2.2. Effect of magnetic field on OAMNP

OAMNP of hydrodynamic diameter 9 ± 0.3 nm synthesized were tested for responsiveness against magnet by applying a field of 1.5 T for 1 h as shown in Fig. 3. Another property following superparamagnetism is the responsiveness of OAMNP to the external magnetic field. The OAMNP after being exposed to an external magnetic field moved towards the source of magnetism (magnet) and lost all magnetism once the applied magnetic field was removed. Hence, the OAMNP were super-paramagnetic. The formulation was then used for further experiments.

5.2.3. FTIR analysis

The presence of the magnetic core and conjugation between ferrous - oleic acid in OAMNP was confirmed by (FTIR) spectroscopy. As shown in Fig. 4, OAMNPs had a strong FTIR peak in the region 722 cm−1 from the iron oxide skeleton (–O–Fe). Intense bands appeared in the OAMNP at 2923/2853 and 1465 cm−1 due to –CH2 asymmetric/symmetric stretching and bending vibrations of the hydrophobic oleic acid chain. This confirms the binding of oleic acid to the iron in OAMNP [13]. The hydrophilic carboxyl group on oleic acid caused asymmetric vibration of the CO at 1743 cm−1.

5.2.4. Determination of iron content in OAMNP

The iron content of OAMNP nanoparticles of various formulations is shown in Table 3. As shown in the table, the iron concentration increases with an increase in the amount of OAMNP. With 75 mg OAMNP, the iron concentration being 60 ± 3.1 mg was found to be below the cytotoxic concentration as reported in the literature [14].

5.3.2. FTIR analysis

As seen in Fig. 6, the hydrophilic carboxyl group of oleic acid stretch is shown at 1738 cm−1. This shift was not present in the IR spectrum of composite nanoparticles synthesized without loading OAMNP (Fig. 7). This stretch had shifted from 1743 cm−1 in unloaded OAMNP FTIR spectra thus confirming the presence of OAMNP core in composite nanoparticles. The stretch of the C–O bond is shown at 1090 cm−1 and the peak at 1365 cm−1 indicated asymmetric and symmetric stretching of C–O–C [15]. The peaks at 3328 and 2190 cm−1 show –CH2– stretching.

5.3.3. Confirmation of PEG PLGA matrix by NMR

In 1H NMR of OAMNP NPS (Fig. 8), the large peak at 3.32 ppm was attributed to the methylene protons of PEG, while the integrated signals around 1.3 ppm were assigned to the methyl protons of the D- and L-lactic acid repeat units of PLGA. Thus, the presence of PEG PLGA was confirmed by NMR.

5.3.4. OAMNP loading and OAMNP encapsulation efficiency

The dry OAMNP and was used to characterize the encapsulation efficiency of OAMNP in PEG PLGA NPS. As shown in Fig. 9, the OAMNP loading increased along with OAMNP input until that in formulation D, where OAMNP input was 100 mg. In this case, the OAMNP loading was only 2–3% higher than the loading observed when 75 mg OAMNP was input. These findings were in-line with previous results from our lab, when dry OAMNP was used [1]. The maghemite contents and encapsulation efficiencies of the various formulations are shown in Fig. 10. The encapsulation of OAMNP in the polymeric system was highest with 75 mg OAMNP as compared to other amounts of OAMNP inputs.

Hence, taking into consideration the iron content in dry OAMNP characterized and highest encapsulation efficiency when the maghemite input was 75 mg, the synthesized OAMNP had an iron concentration of 60 μg/ml which is below the toxic concentration as established in vitro study conducted by Silva et al. [10]. The author investigated the in vitro biocompatibility of SPIONS using three cell lines; and reported that up to 100 μg/ml SPION-PEG350 showed non-toxicity, while SPION-PEG2000 showed no signal of toxicity even up to 200 μg/ml. Hence, OAMNP loaded composite nanoparticles synthesized here had iron concentration within the safe concentration range <100 μg/ml.

5.3.5. Intracellular iron and Prussian blue staining

Results from the cell uptake study of OAMNP is shown in Fig. 11. It can be observed that, in the presence of an external magnetic field, all the OAMNP PEG PLGA NP were accumulated to the side of the magnet (Fig. 11A). The presence of OAMNP in OAMNP loaded PEG PLGA NPS allowed direct visualization of their uptake by Prussian blue staining in U-138 cells. As shown in Fig. 11C, there was no blue color that appears in the cells incubated with OMNP PEG PLGA NPS. Fig. 11B shows U-138 cells that were incubated with the OAMNP PEG PLGA NPS, and were stained in intensive blue color at the center of the well beneath which the magnet was attached. However, the intracellular uptake of iron was quantified by 1,10 phenanthroline assay in a different set of wells since the wells for Prussian blue visualization were fixed. The result indicated the high intracellular iron concentration as compared to those wells treated without a magnet. The intracellular iron concentration was 22.9 ± 3.9 μg/ml. In comparison, the U-138 cells incubated with OAMNP PEG PLGA NPS in absence of magnet (data not shown) showed a little weak blue color appearance in only a few cells. The intracellular iron concentration was only 8.2 ± 2.4 μg/ml. Hence, the presence of a magnet enhanced the uptake of OAMNP PEG PLGA NPs in U-138 cells as compared to the cells incubated with the same formulation without a magnet. The control wells incubated with blank NPS showed that the cells were similar density and that no toxicity was observed due to PEG PLGA polymeric system.

6. Conclusions

Size controlled seed synthesis method is suitable for the fabrication of OAMNP. The presence of iron oxide skeleton (–O–Fe) and oleic acid chain was confirmed by the FTIR. TEM analysis showed OAMNP had narrow size distribution with an average particle size of 10 ± 3.5 nm. With 75 mg OAMNP input, the synthesized composite nanoparticles had an iron concentration of 60 µg/ml (i.e., safe iron concentration). OAMNP encapsulation efficiency in PEG PLGA NPS was 43% w/w when 75 mg OAMNP were loaded in the polymeric matrix comprising of PEG PLGA. Prussian blue assay showed that the OAMNPs was taken up by the U-138 GBM cells. Iron content analysis showed that the iron inside the cells incubated with magnet was higher as compared to cells incubated without a magnet. The above method was optimized to give sufficient yield to the narrow particle size distribution and smaller size and responded as magnetized against the external magnetic field. They were loaded in polymeric NPS and evaluated for the iron content once taken up by U-138 cells owing to particle size and a positive charge on its surface. The in vitro results prove that the above OAMNP loaded polymeric system will potentially be efficacious in the future in vivo GBM rodent model.
