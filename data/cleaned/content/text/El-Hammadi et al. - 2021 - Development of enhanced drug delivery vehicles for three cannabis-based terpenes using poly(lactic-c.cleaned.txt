3.2. Factors affecting terpene loss during NP preparation

To optimize the encapsulation capacity of the NPs, the possible loss
of terpene during each of the different steps of NP production was
investigated by examining the solvent evaporation step, the NP collec
tion process, and the addition of cryoprotectant before freeze-drying.
MC was selected as the model terpene because it possesses the highest
volatility and vapor pressure among the involved terpenes, making it the
most challenging.

The morphological characterization of the NPs was performed by
image analysis obtained by scanning electron microscopy in a FEI
TENEO microscope. NP suspension (free of trehalose) was diluted down
to around 0.8 mg/mL. Particles were covered with an 8-9 nm Pd/Pt
shell under vacuum (Leica EM SCD500).

Although no external heating was applied during production, MC

the starting MC was retained. This finding indicates that MC entrapped
physically in the polymer (or loaded in the NPs) may be effectively
retained and protected from loss during the evaporation step.

CB13-loaded PEG-PLGA significantly improved the drug analgesic effi

significantly increased the polydispersity of the NPs (p < 0.05), all

in aqueous suspension, preventing undesirable changes upon storage,
and to avert the instability of encapsulated drug. However, freeze-drying
may also induce changes to NP properties, which may influence its size
and robustness. To minimize NP instability upon freeze-drying, some
sugars such as trehalose may be employed as cryoprotectants, which
prevent aggregation of NPs and protect them from the mechanical stress
of ice crystals . Thus, MC loss in trehalose-free NPs
provides extra evidence that terpene leakage from damaged NPs can
occur by its evaporation/sublimation during freeze-drying.
MC-, CPh-, and NL-loaded PEG-PLGA NPs were also prepared and
extensively characterized. PEG is a non-ionic hydrophilic polyether that
is highly biocompatible. Conjugation of PEG to PLGA-based NPs (the so-
called PEGylation of NPs) brings about several interesting advantages
including enhanced aqueous solubility, minimized opsonization,
reduced aggregation and improved stability, minimized immunoge
nicity, and prolonged biological half-life . Additionally,
surface coating with PEG can minimize hydrophobic or electrostatic
interactions between coated particles and human mucus resulting in
reduced mucoadhesion. While high MW PEG can increase mucoadhe
sion, dense coverage (e.g. 65-70 %) with low Mw PEG (e.g. 2 kDa), in
particular, has been found to produce rapid mucus penetration of coated
NPs. This is of particular interest for oral delivery, as the reduced in
teractions can facilitate the NPs to pass through the intestinal mucus
