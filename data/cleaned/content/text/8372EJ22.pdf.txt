RESEARCH ARTICLE
Urate crystal degradation for treatment of gout: a nanoparticulate
combination therapy approach
Sanjay Tiwari & Harinath Dwivedi & Koshy M. Kymonil &
Shubhini A. Saraf
Published online: 27 February 2015
# Controlled Release Society 2015
Abstract The objective of the present work was to de-
velop polymeric nanoparticles of uricase and aceclofenac
(NSAID) and to incorporate them into gel, for delivering
drugs to synovial joints, for effective treatment of Gout.
Nanoparticles containing uricase and aceclofenac were
prepared by double emulsion solvent evaporation method
and emulsion solvent evaporation, using PLGA (50:50)
as carrier, respectively. Process parameters were opti-
mized using Taguchi L4 orthogonal array and L9 array,
respectively. The formulations were characterized for
particle size, entrapment efficiency, surface charge,
in vitro drug release, ex vivo drug permeation, and urate
crystal degradation activity. The particle size and entrap-
ment efficiency for optimized batch was found to be
228.8 nm and 81.26 % for uricase nanoparticles and
288.5 nm and 85.36 % for aceclofenac nanoparticles,
respectively. The developed nanoparticles formulations
displayed zero order and Higuchi release kinetics with
non-Fickian diffusion, respectively. The in vivo studies
were performed in rabbit model. Topical application of
gel containing polymeric uricase nanoparticles alone and
a combination of both, uricase nanoparticles and
aceclofenac nanoparticles in rabbit model test groups,
provided complete removal of urate crystals and inflam-
mation within 40 and 25 days of treatment, respectively.
The combination treatment therapy resulted in effective
treatment of gout due to degradation of crystals and anti-
inflammatory response.

Introduction
Gout is a common systemic rheumatic disease. Most frequent-
ly, it causes recurrent attacks of acute arthritis and can some-
times lead to chronic arthropathy, tophi depositions, and renal
diseases. This metabolic disorder develops due to excessive
production of uric acid in the body due to metabolism of
purines which get deposited into the joints in the form of
monosodium urate crystals. Initial symptoms of gout usually
consist of intense episodes of painful swelling in a single joint,
most often in the feet especially the big toe. It can affect the
insteps, ankles, heels, knee, wrists, fingers, elbows, etc. caus-
ing pain, swelling, redness, heat, and stiffness in joints [1].
Gout can be treated by xanthine oxidase inhibitors, drugs
that increase the renal elimination of urate. Drugs that de-
crease inflammation which is caused by deposition of urate
crystals are NSAIDs, colchicines, corticosteroids, etc. Poly-
ethylene glycol (PEG)–uricase is another potentially powerful
agent for treating refractory gout in those cases where other
treatments are not possible. PEG–uricase is effective in dis-
solving tophi and could have a role in Bdebulking^ tophi in
advanced gout before switching to another agent for mainte-
nance treatment [2].
Uricase is normally found in animals but has disappeared
in the course of evolution in human beings [1]. It catalyzes the
oxidation of less water-soluble uric acid (7 mg/dl at 37 °C)
into allantoin, which is nearly ten times more water soluble,

Human skin (mainly stratum corneum) acts as a barrier for
transdermal drug delivery and also provides a unique drug
delivery pathway by by-passing the first-pass metabolism.
Therapeutic agents from topical formulations penetrate via
intercellular, intracellular, and trans-appendageal routes,
resulting in transdermal delivery to deep tissues and into the
systemic circulation [3]. An in vitro study performed on
uricase-containing lipid vesicles explicitly indicated that the
uricase entrapped in alkaline enzymosomes possesses higher
uricolytic activity than that of the native uricase solution [4].
Nanoparticles possess considerable potential in topical deliv-
ery of therapeutic agents due to their distinctive features. Be-
cause of their smaller size and increased surface area, nano-
particles facilitate enhanced permeation of drugs through the
skin when compared with other drug delivery systems [5]. The
permeation of nanoparticles across skin increases in the pres-
ence of permeation enhancers. The objective of the present
work was to develop two polymeric nanoparticle gel systems
for the delivery of uricase and aceclofenac, respectively, into
the synovial joint for the effective treatment of Gout [1].
Materials and methods
Materials
Uricase and uricase assay kit (Amplex red uric acid/uricase
assay kit-A22181, Invitrogen detection technologies) was pro-
cured from Invitrogen Bio Services India Pvt. Ltd. Bangalore,
India. Poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA) (50:50) was ob-
tained as a gift sample from Evonik Degussa India Pvt. Ltd.,
Mumbai, India. All the other chemicals, reagents, and solvents
used were of analytical grade.
Methods
Preparation of crystals
Crystals of monosodium urate (MSU) were prepared by dis-
solving 4 g of uric acid in 800 ml of boiling water containing
24.5 ml of 1 N sodium hydroxide. After adjusting pH of the
solution to 7.2, by the addition of concentrated hydrochloric
acid, the solution was cooled and stored overnight at 4 °C [6].
The solvent was evaporated by heating the mixture at 60 °C to
obtain the crystals. The crystals were sterilized by heating at
180 °C for 2 h using a tray dryer and stored in a sterile envi-
ronment until use. The percentage yield of MSU crystals was
found to be 99.26 %. The crystals (50 mg) were suspended in
5 ml phosphate-buffer saline (PBS) of pH 7.2. This 10 mg/ml
suspension was sonicated for 3 min using a probe sonicator
(0.25 amplitude and 0.5 Hz) to obtain rod-shaped crystals of
uniform size range of 5–25 μm in length, determined by op-
tical microscope [6].
Substrate selectivity of uricase
The activity of uricase was determined by treating 1 g of
accurately weighed MSU crystals with 10 ml of PBS
(pH 7.2), containing 2 μU uricase in a petri-plate. The petri-
plate was incubated at 37±0.5 °C for 48 h. Uricase activity
was identified by visualization and determination of the loss in
weight of urate crystals at different time intervals, obtained
after centrifugation at 5,000 rpm for 15 min at 20 °C. The
visual observations were validated by determining weight loss
after centrifugation and also visually through microscopy. The
study was performed in triplicate (n=3).
Quantitative estimation of uric acid by assay kit
The standard curve of uric acid was prepared by adding 50 μL
of the Amplex Red reagent/HRP/uricase working solution to
each microplate well containing 50 μL uric acid (5, 10, 15, 20,
and 25 μM) and control (50 μL 1× reaction buffer). The re-
action mixture was incubated for 30 min at 37 °C, and
protected from light (all experiments were performed in
amber-colored glassware and in dark condition). The absor-
bance was measured using a micro-plate reader (Star Fax -
2600, Medical, Awareness technology, UK).
Determination of uricase activity by Lineweaver-Burk plot
The catalytic activity of uricase was characterized by
Lineweaver-Burk plot with uric acid as the substrate;
Michaelis-Menten constant and maximum velocity were de-
termined and found to be 0.0353±0.000252 mM and 5.8×
10−5±4.13×10−7 mM/min, respectively.

Nanoparticles were prepared by double emulsion solvent
evaporation method.

The optimized formulation was prepared by mixing an
aqueous solution of uricase (200 mU in 1 ml), non-aqueous
solution of PLGA in acetone (4 g in 10 ml), and emulsifying
agent (0.1 ml of span 80) at 2,000 rpm on an ice bath. The

frequency (Sartorius LABSONIC®-P, Germany) in a pulsatile
cycle of 8 s of sonication and then 24 s of rest for a period of
48 min. The formulated nanoemulsion was added dropwise
into 88 ml of polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) solution (0.3 %w/v)
using a syringe (22 no. needle), under stirring at 8,000 rpm on
an ice bath. The nanoemulsion was stirred for 4 h to remove
the solvent. The nanoparticle suspension was centrifuged at 8,
000 rpm (3–18K, Sartorius) for 1 h at 10 °C to remove micron-
sized particles, if any. The supernatant was further centrifuged
at 10,000 rpm for 1 h at 10 °C to obtain the required range
(<400 nm) of nanoparticles in the supernatant [7]. The opti-
mized batch was prepared in triplicate for estimation of stan-
dard deviation of characterization parameters.
Formulation of nanoparticles containing aceclofenac
and process optimization
Emulsion solvent evaporation method was selected for the
formulation of nanoparticles containing aceclofenac. The pa-
rameters for formulation optimization of nanoparticles con-
taining aceclofenac were selected on the basis of Taguchi or-
thogonal design (L-9), which are summarized in Table 3.
The predicted values of optimized variables
(aceclofenac:PLGA ratio, aqueous:non-aqueous ratio, and
sonication time) at 95 % confidence by two-sided test were
determined for batch (ANop) using software (Design Expert
version 8.0 freeware) with desirable limits of high entrapment
efficiency and particle size in the range of 200–300 nm. The
optimized batch variables as determined through the software
along with their standard deviation are recorded in Table 4.
The optimized batch (ANop) was formulated by dissolving
an accurately weighed 0.5 g of aceclofenac and 1.5 g of PLGA
(50:50) in 10 ml acetone with stirring for 5 min. The above
solution was poured dropwise in 160 ml of a PVA solution
(0.3 %w/v) using a syringe (22 no. needle) under stirring, at 8,
000 rpm to form the nanoemulsion. The stirring was contin-
ued for 4 h. The nanoparticulate suspension was separated by
centrifugation at 8,000 rpm for 1 h at 10 °C to remove micro-
particles and the supernatant was further centrifuged at 10,
000 rpm for 1 h at 10 °C to obtain nanoparticles in the desired
size range of less than 400 nm in supernatant [7]. The resultant
nanosuspension was concentrated in a vacuum evaporator
(Buchi type evaporator and chiller, IKA HB10, IKA RV10)
to obtain the desired concentration of 10 mg/ml of entrapped
drug. The optimized batch was prepared in triplicate for esti-
mation of standard deviation of characterization parameters.
In vitro release study of uricase and aceclofenac
Accurately measured 1 ml of uricase nanosuspension was
mixed with 9 ml of PBS (pH 7.2) and shaken on a rotary
shaker at 50 cycles/min for 30 h. Sample of 0.1 ml each was
withdrawn at intervals of 5 h each and analyzed for enzyme
content at 560 nm, with the help of an enzyme assay kit, using
ELISA micro-plate reader. Similar in vitro release studies per-
formed with nanosuspension equivalent to 10 mg of
aceclofenac (1 ml), after suitable dilution, was analyzed for
aceclofenac content at 273.6 nm on a UV spectrophotometer
(UV-1700, Shimadzu Corporation, Kyoto, Japan). The study
was performed in triplicate.

Formulation of uricase nanoparticulate gel and aceclofenac
nanoparticulate gel
The formula optimization of the gel was done by factorial
design (23) in which the three variables (concentration of
carbopol 940, concentration of permeation enhancer, stability
time) were optimized on the basis of evaluation parameters
(viscosity, mucoadhesive strength, and percentage release of
nanoparticles).
Topical gels are prepared using various polymers like
carbopol, gelatin, etc. Most of carbopol grades have favorable
rheological properties for topical applications and can form
gels at concentrations as low as 0.5 % w/w. The optimization
of formulation parameters of carbopol 940 gel was done using
factorial design (23). The various batches designed for process
optimization, along with variables and their responses are re-
corded in Table 5.
The predicted values of optimized variables at 95 % confi-
dence by two-sided test were determined for gel using soft-
ware (Design Expert version 8.0 freeware) and are recorded in
Table 6.
The optimized batch was formulated by mixing an accu-
rately weighed quantity (0.23 g) of carbopol 940 with 10 ml of
water and kept for swelling for 4 h. To the above mixture,
6.0 % w/w eucalyptus oil (a permeation enhancer), and
nanosuspension of uricase equivalent to 100 mU were added
and homogenized. The pH was adjusted to 7.0 with
triethanolamine. The so-formed gel (uricase nanoparticulate
gel (UNG)) was kept for stabilization for 24 h at 25 °C. Sim-
ilarly, aceclofenac nanoparticulate gel (ANG) was prepared
using nanosuspension of aceclofenac equivalent to 1 g of
drug. The prepared UNG was stored in dark at 0±2 °C, so
that no loss in enzyme content takes place during storage, until
use.

Ex vivo permeation studies of UNG and ANG across goat

Similar permeation studies were performed on both the gels

Stability studies of nanoparticulate gels (UNG and ANG)
Stability testing was performed to assess the drug and formu-
lation stability as per ICH guidelines. The stability studies
were carried out for the optimized formulations (UNG and
ANG). The optimized formulations were sealed in a glass vial
and kept at 0±2, 8±2, 30±2, and 45±2 °C at 75±5 % RH for
2 months. The samples were collected at definite time inter-
vals of 15 days (0, 15, 30, 45, and 60) and analyzed for the
drug content, viscosity, and mucoadhesive strength. The en-
zyme content, after 45 days storage at different conditions,
was calculated using ELISA microplate reader and Amplex
red uric acid/uricase assay kit-A22181, Invitrogen detection
technologies, while aceclofenac content was calculated
spectrophotometrically.
In vivo studies
The in vivo study was performed according to the protocol
approved by the Institutional Animal Ethics Committee as per
approval registration number 645/02/C/CPCSEA and refer-
ence number BBDGEI/ IAEC/30/2011.
Animals
For the study, 21 albino rabbits weighing 2.0–3.0 kg were
used. Standard laboratory conditions at ambient temperature
of 25±2 °C and relative humidity of 50±5 % with a 12 h light/
12 h dark cycle were maintained. Rabbits were fed with a
commercial pellet diet and water ad libitum. Experimental
rabbits were divided into four groups.
(1)
Group I control (three animals): Animals in which gout
was induced but no treatment was provided.
(2)
Group II test (six animals): Animals in which gout was
induced and treated with UNG applied once in a week as
well as ANG applied daily.
(3)
Group III test (six animals): Animals in which gout was
induced but treated with UNG applied once in a week.
(4)
Group IV test (six animals): Animals in which gout was
induced and treated with marketed aceclofenac gel.
These groups were prepared to estimate the effect of
nanoparticulate gels separately, in combination (UNG and
ANG) and to compare with marketed aceclofenac gel.

Skin irritation test
The skin irritation test was performed on three New Zealand
white rabbits (2.0–3.0 kg). The animals were kept under lab-
oratory conditions at ambient temperature of 25±2 °C and
relative humidity of 50±5 %. The hair of the abdominal skin
was removed 24 h prior to the study. The skin irritation test
was performed by applying 0.5 g of the ANG equivalent to
5 mg of the drug, over a 9-cm2 area on hairless abdominal skin
of the rabbits. The animals were observed for 14 days for signs
of edema and erythema.
Induction of gout by monosodium urate crystal in rabbits
Gout was induced by intrasynovial injection (using needle no.
18) of sterilized monosodium urate crystal suspension
(30 mg/ml) in water for injection [8]. Induction of gout was
confirmed by measuring inflammatory responses (swelling
and redness) and X-ray radiograph of inflamed joints and
comparison with the X-ray of a normal joint.
After 1 week of induction of gout, topical nanoparticulate
gel was applied over the affected gouty joints of the test rab-
bits, and responses were measured. Test group II animals were
treated with UNG (1 g gel-UNG containing 1 mU uricase
once in a week) and ANG (0.5 g gel-ANG containing 5 mg
aceclofenac once daily). The delivery of nanoparticles to sy-
novial joints was confirmed by collecting synovial fluid after
24 h of application of the gel. The treatment was carried out till
the joints regained their normal condition.
Test Group III animals were treated with UNG (1 g gel-
UNG containing 1 mU uricase once in a week). The delivery
of nanoparticles to the synovial joint was confirmed by
collecting synovial fluid after 24 h of application of gel.
Test Group IVanimals were treated with 1 % w/w marketed
aceclofenac gel (0.5 g marketed gel containing 5 mg
aceclofenac applied once daily) and the efficacy of treatment
determined at various time intervals.
Estimation of uricase and aceclofenac in synovial fluid
Synovial fluid (20 μl) was collected after 24 h of gel applica-
tion with the help of an 18-gauge needle. Estimation of uricase
was done by an enzyme assay kit (n=3), while estimation of
aceclofenac was done spectrophotometrically by recording
the absorbance at 273.6 nm (n=3).
Assessment of inflammation and treatment
Inflammation was assessed by measuring the diameter of knee
joints using vernier calipers. The inflammatory response of
gout in joints were observed as redness and swelling.
X-ray radiographs of knee joints of animals (control group
I, Test groups II, III, and IV) were taken prior to induction,
after induction of gout and after treatment by an X- ray ma-
chine (AJ4106 at 54 KV, 6 mA and exposure time 0.01 s) to
predict the level of deposition and degradation of
monosodium urate crystals within the gouty joints. The results
of inflammatory responses were analyzed by applying one-
way ANOVA test among all the groups (I, II, III, and IV)
and t test between I and II, I and III, I and IV, and II and III.
Results
Characterization of uricase
Uricase activity and substrate selectivity were determined by
treating MSU crystals with the enzyme solution at 37°±
0.5 °C. Urate oxidase activity on urate crystals was deter-
mined by visual examination and by estimating the loss in
weight of urate crystals. Loss in weight of urate crystals after
48 h was found to be 16.8±0.208167 %. This study indicates
that uricase degrades monosodium urate crystals at body
temperature.
Quantitative estimation of uricase was done using Amplex
Red uric acid/Uricase assay kit (A22181). Km and Vmax values
of enzyme were determined by Lineweaver-Burk plot and
were found to be 0.0353±0.000252 mM and 5.8×10−5±
4.13×10−7 mM/min, respectively (n=3).
Characterization parameters of UNop, ANop, UNG, and ANG
The nanoparticles of uricase, aceclofenac, and nanoparticulate
gels of uricase and aceclofenac were characterized for various
parameters which are recorded in Figs. 1 and 2 and Tables 7
and 8.
The shelf life of UNG was found to be 645.12 days at 0±
2 °C and 45.22 days at 8±2 °C. The estimated shelf life of
ANG was found to be 2619.64 days at 0±2 °C, while at 30±
2 °C the same was 1947.56 days. The shorter shelf life of
UNG may be due to decreasing stability of uricase with in-
crease in temperature, while the shelf life of ANG was not
affected much due to stability of aceclofenac at room
temperature.

The presence of uricase or aceclofenac in synovial fluid was
confirmed by estimating uricase or aceclofenac inside synovi-

concentration of uricase and aceclofenac in synovial fluid of
different test rabbits was found in the range of 0.095 to
0.145 mU/ml and 0.278 to 0.393 mg/ml, respectively.

toms of gout throughout the course of therapy. Animals of test
group III were found to be free from urate crystals and inflam-
matory responses after 40 days of treatment with gel UNG,
once a week, indicating its effectiveness. The animals of test
group II were found to be free from crystal deposition and
inflammatory responses after treatment of 25 days (Fig. 3)
while in test group IV animals, the inflammatory responses
were reduced but crystals remained in the joint even after
40 days of treatment. The one-way ANOVA statistical analy-
sis of inflammatory responses (Table 9) for control group I,
test group II, test group III, and test group IV animals
displayed significant difference. The F test value was found
to be 13.33, which was greater than tabulated p value (p=
3.20). The group pairs were analyzed on basis of calculated
critical difference (CD=0.08) between groups. The mean in-
flammatory responses of test group II, III displayed greater
difference than critical difference (0.18>0.08) from control
group I, while test group IV displayed a lesser value (0.05<
0.08). These results indicate that group II (UNG and ANG
combination treatment) and III (UNG treatment alone)
showed greater effectiveness than marketed formulation
(group IV) and control group. The test group II and III did
not shown any significant difference for 40 day-treatment pe-
riod due to restoration of normal joint conditions. These
groups were further analyzed by t test for their 25-day treat-
ment responses. The t test value was found to be 5.41, which
was greater than tabulated p value (p=1.81). The statistical
result of t test indicated that group treatment differed signifi-
cantly. The group II animals were completely normalized dur-
ing 25-day treatment while group III animals retained the in-
flammatory responses.

the use of different polymers and solvents. Optimization was
done by Taguchi orthogonal (L-4) design in which, most of
the time, responses are affected by a small number of main
effects and lower order interactions while higher order inter-
actions are relatively unimportant. Nanoparticles possess a
characteristic particle size and size distribution which are con-
sidered as most important characteristics for drug release. A
smaller size (228.8 nm) of nanoparticles was obtained due to
progressive particle size reduction by sonication. Sonication
causes increase in temperature (nearly 35 °C in 5 min), which
could lead to enzyme inactivation. To prevent this, sonication
was performed in a pulsatile manner, on an ice bath. The
resting period (24 s) after sonication (8 s), provided suitable
time for dispersion of heat generated during sonication. The
uricase nanoparticles were prepared on an ice bath so that
none to minimum loss of enzymatic activity takes place. This
may be concluded on the basis of entrapment of uricase in
UNop. The entrapment efficiency of optimized batch of nano-
particles was found to be 81.26±1.42 %, which suggests that
loss of enzyme did not take place during nanoparticles
formulation.
The nanoparticles which cross through skin are in a smaller
size range, but nanoparticles in the size range of 200–300 nm
permeate through hair follicles and other mechanisms. This
enabled localized delivery at inflamed site. The low
polydispersity index confirmed efficiency of the formulation
over particle size control. The amount of drug (enzyme)
entrapped inside the formulation determines the quality of
formulation and its utilization for dosing. The entrapment ef-
ficiency of nanoparticles depends on various parameters like
polymer ratio, phase ratio, method of preparation, etc. As in
case of UNop and ANop, the drug (uricase or aceclofenac) to
polymer ratio was very high which resulted into encapsulation
of maximum drug into polymer matrix. In case of UNop, the
organic phase could also have prevented the leakage of en-
zyme into the aqueous dispersion medium due to low solubil-
ity of enzyme in non-aqueous solvents, during formulation.
The large volume of dispersion medium may have prevented
agglomeration of nanoparticles thereby preventing the loss of
drug (uricase or aceclofenac) which could have been
entrapped and lost through larger microparticles, if formed.
Polymer degradation can also be affected by the par-
ticle size of formulation. For instance, the degradation
rate of PLGA was found to increase with increasing
particle size. It has been reported that in smaller nano-
particles, the degradation products of PLGA diffuse out
while in larger ones, this accelerates further degradation
of PLGA [10]. The particle size can be controlled by
altering uricase to PLGA ratio and sonication time,
which results in stability as well as desired permeability
of the nanoparticles through skin barrier. Nanoparticles

On analyzing the release profile of PLGA nanoparticles
containing aceclofenac, an initial burst release, most likely
due to the rapid release of surface associated drug molecules;
a second phase of release which was slower than the first and
was probably due to material degradation via chain scission,
and a third phase which was the slowest and could mainly be
due to polymer erosion, which led to the loss of bulk material,
was observed. PLGA nanoparticles showed release of drug or
enzyme for duration of more than 30 h which supports the
slow degradation of crystals into the joints as well as removal
of inflammatory responses. Uricase retained bioactivity after
incorporating into nanoparticles. This was concluded on the
basis of in vitro release, studied by enzyme assay which is
specific for estimation of uricase. As uricase has a short cir-
culating half life and only catalyzes the breakdown of crystals
while not getting metabolized, a prolonged release of both
uricase as well as aceclofenac was required for effective
treatment.
Terpenes are major constituent of eucalyptus oil. Terpenes
causes disruption of intercellular lipid/protein organization
and/or extraction of skin micro-constituents; these are respon-
sible for maintenance of barrier status. The eucalyptus oil
interacts with liquid crystals of skin causing dispersion and
swelling in lamellar when applied in small amount. This in-
teraction results into increase in permeation across stratum
corneum. Hence, they promote the transdermal permeation
of nanoparticles [11]. The permeation enhancer might not af-
fect the physical stability of uricase, as enzyme was incorpo-
rated in polymeric nanoparticles and its content was quite low
(1 mU) while polymer content was high (20 mg). The perme-
ation enhancer present in external phase (gel) might preferen-
tially permeate into skin layers and change permeability of
various membranes. The drug and enzyme may remain encap-
sulated in nanoparticles when incorporated in gel. This was
hypothesized on the basis of in vitro permeation study of
UNG and ANG, when a polymer dissolving solvent
(acetone) was added to the withdrawn samples, enzyme, or
aceclofenac concentration increased. This indicates that large
portion of nanoparticle containing enzyme or drug remains
encapsulated in nanoparticles and presence of permeation en-
hancer could enhance permeability through barrier.
The UNG and ANG were applied topically and were
transdermally absorbed. Permeation of nanoparticles
across different membranes depends on structural organi-
zation of the barrier. Membranes from animal sources
(goat knee skin) tend to have higher resistance to nano-
particle permeation compared with synthetic membranes
and egg membrane, probably because of the more com-
plex biochemical composition of the former. The results
indicated that most of the uricase nanoparticles were able
to cross the skin and synovial membrane barriers to
reach synovial fluid. The synovial fluid collected from
the test group animal showed the presence of free en-
zyme or drug and encapsulated also. During estimation
of drug or enzyme concentration in synovial fluid, poly-
mer dissolving solvent again increases the concentration
of drug or enzyme. But the actual synovial fluid volume
cannot be determined for animals and thus exact extent
of free drug or enzyme reaching the synovial joint was
not determined. In mammals (Rabbit), uricase is not
found endogenously [1]. The absence of endogenous ac-
tivity on urate crystals may be concluded on basis of
control group animal where crystals and inflammatory
symptoms remained, even after 45 days. Ex vivo perme-
ation of uricase nanoparticles from gel (UNG) confirmed
the extent of nanoparticles reaching the target tissue by
crossing the skin barrier. This was corroborated by the
findings of Abdel-Mottaleb et al. who reported that poly-
meric nanoparticles (NP) of diameter ranging from 50 to
1,000 nm had around 3-fold stronger and deeper pene-
tration tendency when administered to a dermatitis in-
flammation model [12]. A detailed study of transport
mechanism was not undertaken in the present study.
However, evidence obtained from results of in vivo study
substantiated the fact that transport had taken place,
which was promoted by nano size, lipophilicity of poly-
mer and presence of permeation enhancer.
Test group II and III animals showed a better response than
groups I and IV, as mean difference of ANOVA showed great-
er difference than critical difference (0.18 and 0.23, respec-
tively). The UNG treatment alone was also found to be effec-
tive in group III. Test group II animals required lesser time
duration for alleviation of symptoms of gout, exhibiting better
management of gout as compared to the test group III, as t test
value greater than p value (5.41>p=1.81). The probable
mechanism in treatment of test group II animals would be to
enhance the crystal degradation by uricase and removal of
inflammatory response by aceclofenac. Therefore, the combi-
nation therapy of these two gels would provide a shorter du-
ration of treatment.

It is reasonable to expect that any condition leading to a
decrease in hyperuricemia in synovial fluid will promote dis-
solution of deposited crystals and also inhibit formation of
other crystals. This is achieved primarily by using urate-
lowering therapy (uricase). MSU crystals disappear from the
synovial fluid after free urate reduction to normal levels. The
reduction in concentration of free urate in synovial fluid leads
to shift in the crystallization equilibrium to backward direc-
tion. Uricase has low activation energy, high substrate enzyme
stabilization energy, and also requires lower Gibb’s free ener-
gy to form transition state due to which it releases high amount
of transition state binding energy. These parameters signify its
high catalytic efficiency, due to transition state stabilization,
and efficiency in the degradation of uric acid and urate crystals
[13, 14].
Developed nanoparticulate uricase and aceclofenac
topical gel delivery system provided promising results
for degradation of deposited urate crystals and reduction
in inflammation in rabbit knee joints. In the chronic con-
dition of gout when large crystals are deposited in syno-
vial joint, they result into stiff, inflamed, and painful
joints. This treatment provides complete degradation of
urate crystal by topical application of UNG and ANG
gels in a short duration of time. This therapy results into
an effective treatment of chronic gout. The developed
system may prove to be a simple and effective system
for the treatment of painful gouty arthritis in humans.
