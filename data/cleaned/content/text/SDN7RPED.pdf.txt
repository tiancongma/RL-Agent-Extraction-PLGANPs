Garlic oil–loaded PLGA nanoparticles with controllable size
and shape and enhanced antibacterial activities
Sherif Ashraf Fahmy, Wael Mamdouh
Department of Chemistry, School of Sciences and Engineering, The American University in Cairo, AUC Avenue,
P.O. Box 74, New Cairo 11835, Egypt
Correspondence to: W. Mamdouh (E-mail: wael_mamdouh@aucegypt.edu)
ABSTRACT: The present work focuses on producing polymer composite nanoparticles (NPs) composed of garlic oil (GO) and poly
(lactic-co-glycolic) acid (PLGA) by the single emulsion/solvent evaporation (SE/SE) method and high-speed homogenizing. Different
preparation parameters were found to greatly affect the stability and size uniformity of the prepared PLGA/GO NP formulations,
which were carefully controlled. Scanning electron microscopy, Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy, and UV–vis spectroscopy and
dynamic light scattering and zeta potential were used to characterize the NPs. Antibacterial assessment of the prepared PLGA/GO
NPs against E. coli and S. aureus was carried out. Interestingly, the NP size ranged between 201 and 319 nm, which is 10 times less
than the size of the regular GO particles in bulk solution. The antibacterial activities show enhancement by 70–78% of bacterial inhi-
bition compared with a GO bulk solution. This work sheds light on the potential use of GO NP formulations as nanobiotics and the
critical NP preparation parameters that need to be considered. V

INTRODUCTION
Bacterial infections are considered to be the second main cause
of death worldwide and the third main cause of death in devel-
oping countries, where food is considered one of the major
causes of bacterial infections. Foodborne illness is a concerning
problem that is caused by ingesting food products or beverages
that are contaminated by pathogenic microorganisms.1
There is a great tendency at present to replace synthetic antimi-
crobials with natural ones and to develop new natural antibacte-
rial formulations that would be able to prevent and treat illness
related to different types of foodborne microorganisms and relate
these natural products to the modern drug discovery and modern
targeting systems. The use of natural antimicrobials is promising
and is preferred over using synthetic ones because they do not
cause bacterial strains to develop resistance, they do not affect the
normal flora, and they are less likely to cause allergic reactions.2,3
Natural products such as herbs, plant extracts, and oils are
widely used in folk medicine to treat and prevent a wide variety
of diseases such as microbial infections and cardiovascular dis-
eases. In addition, natural products have also been used as die-
tary supplements and have become one of the foundations
beneath the discovery and design of synthetic pharmaceutical
drugs that are used currently. This is mainly because their natu-
ral active constituents possess many pharmacological activities.4
In this regard, numerous studies on using natural products to
fight some of the most common diseases have been reported.
Egypt has a unique climate and geographical conditions that
make it the only source for over 342 species of medicinal plants,
such as black seed anise, coriander, garlic, fennel, and aloe vera.5,6
Garlic (Allium sativum L.), in addition to its use as a spice in
food, was used in traditional Chinese medicine 3000 years ago
in the treatment of headaches, intestinal worms, and tumors
and as an insecticide. Garlic was demonstrated to inhibit the
growth of a wide range of bacteria, fungi, and viruses. In this
regard, garlic has been reported to be a promising natural anti-
biotic. In addition, currently garlic is used as anticholesterol,
antiplatelet,
anti-thrombolytic,
antihypertensive,
antioxidant,
anti-inflammatory, and anticancer natural medicine.7–9
Garlic exists in many forms, such as natural extracts, powder, or
oil. Garlic oil (GO) contains two major thiosulfinate products:
diallyl disulfide and diallyl trisulfide.10
GO suffers from some drawbacks, such as strong odor, water
insolubility, and volatility, which hinder its use in pharmaceutical

Nanotechnology has shown great potential recently in the phar-
maceutical and biomedical fields and is considered a revolution-
ary approach to the prevention and treatment of diseases.11
Nanotechnology plays an important role in targeted drug delivery
systems (TDDSs), and many types of nanocarriers are involved in
the TDDSs, such as carbon nanotubes, fullerenes, liposomes, and
polymer nanocomposites (PNCs). PNCs are composed of a poly-
meric matrix reinforced with a filler material whose size is on the
nanoscale. They have been widely used in TDDSs particularly
because of the capability of PNCs either to capture the drugs
within their structures (encapsulation) or to bear the drugs on
the polymeric surface structures (surface decoration). This, in
turn, increases the stability of the drugs, as well as extends their
half-lives. More importantly, they will be well targeted to the
organs of interest and increase the stability of volatile drugs,
which will help in the delivery of antibiotics.12,13
The polymers used in PNCs should satisfy some requirements,
such as biocompatibility, biodegradability, and FDA approval
(especially for applications that are directly related to human
contact).
Among
these
polymers
are poly(ethylene
glycol)
(PEG) and poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA).12–15
Many methods are involved in the preparation of polymeric
NPs, particularly those prepared from synthetic polymers. Single
emulsion/solvent evaporation (SE/SE) is one of the most com-
monly used preparation methods, mainly due to the wide size
ranges, suitability for both hydrophilic and hydrophobic drugs,
and compatibility with a wide variety of solvents.15–17 Many
examples have been reported on the utilization of different
nanoparticles (NPs) in the preparation of natural nanobiotics,
such as the preparation of eugenol and cinnamaldehyde PLGA
NPs, with a pronounced antibacterial activity and controlled
release against Salmonella spp. and Listeria spp. Moreover, carva-
crol/PLGA NPs were found to reduce the elasticity and stability
of S. epidermidis biofilms.18,19 It is worth mentioning that many
factors play a crucial role in controlling the particle size and
shape, which consequently affect the antibacterial activities of
PLGA NPs, which is the main objective of this work. Several
PLGA/GO NP preparation parameters were examined, such as
(1) homogenization time (5, 10, and 15 min) and homogeniza-
tion speed (less than 13,000 rpm); (2) separation techniques
such as solution settling during preparation, Buchner filtration
of the solutions, and the use of biological filters for solution fil-
tration; and finally (3) mechanical shaking of the solutions. The
best methods were selected, and the optimum sizes of PLGA/
GO NPs were chosen for antibacterial studies later on.
EXPERIMENTAL
Materials
All chemicals were of analytical grade and used as received from
the source without any further purification. The 50/50 D-lac-
tide/glycolide copolymer with 0.2 dL g21 (Purasorb PDLG) was
purchased from Purac Biochem (Gorinchem, The Netherlands),
and poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG 6000) was purchased from Bio-
tech, Ottawa, Canada. Dichloromethane (DCM), Polaxmer 407
purified non-ionic surfactant, sodium hydroxide (NaOH), lac-
tose, and garlic oil blend were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich
(Darmstadt, Germany).
Preparation of Monodispersed PLGA/GO NPs
The single emulsion/solvent evaporation technique was used in the
preparation of PLGA/GO NPs as described below. The method
used to prepare the PLGA/GO NPs was adapted from a number of
different published protocols with some modifications.20–26
The detailed steps to prepare different PLGA/GO NP samples
that have stable, unchanged nanoscale sizes and monodispersity
are illustrated in Figure 1 (and in more detail in the Supporting
Information). In this regard, three preparation parameters were
investigated: homogenization speed, homogenization time inter-
val, and mechanical shaking of the solutions.
PLGA/GO NPs were prepared by the oil/water solvent evapora-
tion technique using a high-speed homogenizer (IKA T18
basic ULTRA-TURRAX, 79219 Staufen, Germany) at different
homogenization time intervals (5 min, 10 min, and 15 min).
The colloidal nanoemulsion obtained was divided into two
parts: nanosuspension (NS, path I) and nanofiltrate (NF, path
II), as illustrated in Figure 1. In path I, NS was prepared at a
homogenization time interval of 5 min and was left to settle for
less than 24 h. The supernatant was separated and then filtered

Characterization of PLGA/GO NSs and NFs
The morphology, particle size, zeta potential, structural compo-
sition, and optical properties of each of the colloidal PLGA/GO
composite NF and NS samples were determined using different
techniques,
including
scanning
electron
microscopy
(SEM;

centrifuged at the same conditions mentioned earlier. Part of
the supernatant was taken out and diluted to 25 mL with abso-
lute ethanol (dilution factor) and then inserted in the UV–vis
spectrophotometer to determine the GO% in the GO NPs using
the calibration curve (data are not shown).29 The same steps
mentioned above were followed for determining GO% in
PLGA-GO-NF-01 and NF-06.28,29

sites NS and NF were tested against E. coli (ATCC 8739) as a
gram-negative bacterium and S. aureus (ATCC 6538) as a gram-
positive bacterium. The colony counting method (CCM) was
employed (Supporting Information).30
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
The physicochemical properties of monodisperse PLGA/GO are
compared below.
It has been reported that the size of many polyester NPs was
affected by many factors, such as the amount of the loaded
drug, polymer/surfactant ratio, molecular weight of the poly-
mer, and the method of colloidal NP preparation.31,32
In this regard, all of the preparation parameters of the polymer/
GO NPs (such as the concentration of PLGA, concentration of
GO, amount of stabilizing agent, and homogenization speed)
were kept constant to identify the effect of the homogenization
time on the physicochemical properties of the monodisperse
PLGA/GO NPs. In addition, the effects of other factors, such as
(1) homogenization speed and time, (2) solution settling, (3)
the use of a Buchner funnel in solution filtration, (4) the use of
biological filters in solution filtration, and (5) mechanical shak-
ing of the solutions by vortex stirring, on the PLGA/GO NP
preparation were carefully examined.

Furthermore, the average zeta potential for PLGA-GO-NS-01
was found to be 233.69 6 3.35, indicating that the surface
charges of the PLGA/GO composite NPs limit their aggregation.
Therefore, PLGA-GO-NS-01 achieves the highest stability and
the minimum particle aggregation. The zeta potential changes
either in the negative or positive direction in turn indicate that
the stability of the particle suspension increases and the particle
aggregation
decreases
as
the
charged
particles
repel
one
another.35,36 Moreover, as has previously been reported,37,38 the
zeta potential of polyester NPs, such as PLGA (without a surfac-
tant such as Polaxmer 407), is high due to the presence of
uncapped carboxylic end groups of the polyester at the NP sur-
face, which provide high negative charges. Upon the addition of
a surfactant to the polyester, a layer formed on the surface of
the polyester NPs that shielded the negative surface charge of
the polyesters in an amount-dependent manner (as the amount
of the surfactant increases, the shielding effect increases).38 As a
result, in the current work, all PLGA NPs were prepared using
Polaxmer 407 as a surfactant at a constant concentration of
1.25%, while the PLGA concentration was kept high (400 mg)
to reduce the shielding effect of Polaxmer for the surface nega-
tive charges of the PLGA carboxylic end groups. This allows
PLGA-GO-NS-01 to achieve the highest negative charge of the
zeta potential. In the same context, the excess surfactant has
been removed by centrifugation at high speeds where the par-
ticles with average size of 200–300 nm were left to sediment.
This left the excess surfactant in the supernatant, which was
then discarded. This was found to be the most effective method
for removing any excess surfactant in order not to alter the neg-
ative charges on the particle surface.39
On the other hand, the average zeta potential for PLGA-GO-
NF-01 and PLGA-GO-NF-06 was found to be 235.37 6 4.62,
which has a slightly greater negative charge than that of PLGA-
GO-NS-01. This might be because the filtration through a
Buchner funnel decreased the agglomeration of the particles,
which caused the negative charge of the uncapped carboxylic
end groups of PLGA to be more exposed on the surface of the
particles. Therefore, PLGA-GO-NFs achieved the highest stabil-
ity and the minimum particle aggregation.
Structural Evaluation of PLGA/GO NPs by FTIR
Characterization
The infrared (KBr) spectra of GO, PLGA microparticles, PLGA-
GO-NS-01, and PLGA-GO-NF-01 and NF-06 are presented in
Figure 4(a–d). The FTIR spectrum of PLGA-GO-NF-01 was
found to be the same as that of PLGA-GO-NF-06, as they were
obtained from the same solution. In addition, the FTIR spec-
trum of PLGA-GO-NS-01 was found to be the same as that of
PLGA-GO-NF-01 and NF-06. This observation proved that the
Buchner filtration decreased the size of the particles, but at the
same time it has no significant effect on the chemical structure
of the compounds.
As illustrated in Figure 4, the FTIR spectrum of PLGA showed
spectral bands at m 5 3500 cm21, which could be attributed to
OH stretching vibrations. Moreover, the peak observed at
m 5 1750 cm21 could correspond to a C@O group. In addition,
the peaks observed at m 5 1450 cm21 and m 5 1360 cm21 could
correspond to CAH bending. Furthermore, the peaks appearing
at m 5 1130 cm21 and m 5 1090 cm21 could be attributed to CAO
stretching. Finally, the peak observed at m 5 750 cm21 could cor-
respond to CAH bending. These spectral bands were found to be
in good agreement with previously published reports.40
On the other hand, the FTIR spectrum of GO showed spectral
bands at m 5 3080 cm21, corresponding to the asymmetric
stretching vibration of @CH2. Moreover, the peak observed at
m 5 3008 cm21 is attributed to CAH stretching, while the peaks
observed at m 5 2977 cm21 correspond to the symmetric stretch-
ing vibration of @CH2, and at m 5 2904 cm21 to ACH2 stretch-
ing. In addition, a very intense peak observed at m 5 1634 cm21

As seen in Table II, the GO% slightly increased with increasing
homogenization time from 5 to 10 to 15 min. This might be
attributed to the longer homogenization time allowing the GO
to be effectively incorporated within the PLGA microparticles
and PLGA NPs. Moreover, this might be due to the longer
homogenization time that increased the particle sizes, and thus
the large NPs were able to carry a higher percentage of GO
than small ones.
By comparing the GO% in PLGA-GO-NS-01, NF-01, and NF-
06, it was shown that the GO% is higher in NSs than in NFs.
This could be because the high pressure applied by vacuum suc-
tion during solution filtration using a Buchner funnel caused
the leakage of GO outside the PLGA NPs. Nevertheless, the
GO% in the PLGA/GO NFs is reasonable and sufficient to carry
out the antibacterial tests.
The SE/SE technique is effective in loading more than 50% of
the GO within the PLGA matrix. This is considered to be a
noticeably high GO%, noting that GO is volatile. Moreover, this
preparation method was found to be beneficial in overcoming
the volatility of GO during use or storage, as GO will be kept
and protected within the PLGA NPs. Finally, this formulation is
expected to improve the stability, solubility, and bioavailability
of GO.7
Moreover, it has been reported that PLGA has a high affinity
for carrying different types of guest molecules in its structure.
PLGA is an aliphatic polyester, and it has been reported to have
the appropriate mechanical strength to carry a variety of guest
molecules, such as drugs, micromolecules, vaccines, peptides,
and proteins. In addition, PLGA is characterized as acting as a
reservoir in which drug molecules can be loaded through physi-
cal, chemical, or electrostatic interactions according to their
physicochemical properties.40
Solubility and Odor of PLGA/GO NPs
GO suffers from some drawbacks, such as strong odor, water
insolubility, and volatility, which hinder its use in pharmaceuti-
cal formulations. We succeeded in overcoming these drawbacks
by increasing the water solubility and masking the strong odor
in our prepared formulations. To our knowledge, there is no
previously published work that is concerned with these draw-
backs. The water solubility of PLGA/GO NPs is shown in Figure
S3. In addition, a preliminary sensory evaluation test was
conducted to confirm the ability of our GO formulations to
mask the strong odor of garlic (described in the Supporting
Information).
Antibacterial Evaluation of PLGA/GO NPs
The percent of bacterial inhibition of the PLGA-GO-NS-01, NF-
01, and NF-06 formulations were tested against E. coli and S.
aureus, as represented in Figure 5(a,b). PLGA microparticles
and GO were used as controls. The results are presented in
Table III, where the percent of bacterial inhibition for each for-
mulation is presented.

From Table III, the antibacterial activities have significantly inc-
reased upon reducing the particle size of GO (3508–3700 nm)
by more than 10-fold in PLGA/GO composites. More impor-
tantly, the antibacterial activities have increased by 71–77%
from the GO% of bacterial inhibition in the case of PLGA-GO-
NS-01. This is a significant observation that illustrates the cru-
cial role of controlling the particle size, which directly affects
the antibacterial activity. Similarly, PLGA-GO-NF-01 and NF-06
showed similar antibacterial activities and revealed an increase
in antibacterial activity of 75% from the GO% of bacterial inhi-
bition. This could be due to the large particle sizes of GO in
bulk form, which decreased the surface area, leading to mini-
mizing the ability of GO to penetrate the bacterial cell wall. In
contrast, the PLGA/GO NPs have much smaller particle sizes
that increased the surface area, leading to an increase in the
contact of GO with the bacterial cell wall. The efficiency of GO
NPs in inhibiting bacterial growth could possibly be due to a
few reasons: (1) the NPs were able to fuse to the bacterial cell
wall, releasing the conjugated GO across the cell wall; (2) NPs
were able to penetrate into the bacterial cells due to their large
surface areas; (3) improving the hydrophilicity of GO leads to
better delivery of GO to its site of action;46–48 and (4) the
degree of diffusion of the GO NPs through the bacteria was
higher than for the GO bulk form.49 Furthermore, it is obvious
that there is no remarkable difference in the antibacterial activi-
ties of the NPs prepared at homogenization times of 10 and
15 min.
In addition, all of the composite formulations have high nega-
tively charged zeta potentials. Accordingly, the GO composite
NPs would be able to be endocytosed by the phagocytic cells,
leading to the release of GO into cells.49 It has previously been
reported that increasing the zeta potentials could increase the
extent of phagocytosis.49 Moreover, as seen in Table III and Fig-
ure 5(a,b), E. coli was more resistant than S. aureus for most of
the formulations, except for PLGA-GO-NF-01 and NF-06. This
is most probably because the gram-positive bacteria have a loose
polyglycan outer layer that allows the penetration of polymeric
NPs conjugated with GO into the cytoplasmic membrane,
allowing the GO to exert its antibacterial action. On the other
hand, E. coli has an additional outer membrane with a bilayer
phospholipid structure that acts as a molecular sieve that pre-
vents large molecules from penetrating the bacterial cytoplasmic
membrane.25,45,50
In addition, as seen in Table III, the antibacterial activities of
the NPs with a particle size range of 200 to 219 nm are slightly
lower than that of the rest of the NPs. Additionally, the maxi-
mum antibacterial activities were observed for the NPs with a
particle size range of 215 to 294 nm. Hence, as the particle sizes
decreased, the ability of the NPs to interact with the bacterial
cell wall and penetrate it decreased. A similar observation has
been reported before.51 On the other hand, one can observe
that, in most of the formulations, the antibacterial activities
increased with increasing GO%. However, it is important to
note that the antibacterial activities of all GO NP formulations
were more than four times higher than that of GO bulk, and
thus, modifying and optimizing the SE/SE method in order to
decrease the particle size of GO by more than 10-fold led to a
dramatic enhancement of the antibacterial activities.
CONCLUSIONS
Nanoparticle conjugates of PLGA mixed with GO were success-
fully prepared by the SE/SE method and characterized by SEM
and FTIR. PLGA/GO NPs in the range of 201–319 nm were pre-
pared, and a number of preparation factors, such as solution
settling, filtration through a Buchner funnel, and vortex stirring,
were optimized to create stable and monodisperse NP conju-
gates. The results reveal that filtration through a Buchner funnel
and biological filters (forming polymeric NFs) has a significant
effect on decreasing the average particle sizes, increasing the sta-
bility of the NPs, and enhancing the antibacterial activities
against both gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria. In addi-
tion, the results reveal that all prepared polymeric NPs con-
jugated with GO in this study show stronger antibacterial
activities against both gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria
compared with the original GO. Furthermore, it is obvious that
the formulations prepared at a homogenization time interval of
5 min have better physicochemical properties than those pre-
pared at homogenization time intervals of 10 and 15 min. On
the other hand, it was observed that increasing the homogeniza-
tion time intervals (from 5 to 10 to 15 min) increased the GO%
within the formulations. These results shed more light on the
important factors that need to be considered when preparing
NP conjugates from natural materials and open new avenues in
exploring other extracts with promising antibacterial activities.
