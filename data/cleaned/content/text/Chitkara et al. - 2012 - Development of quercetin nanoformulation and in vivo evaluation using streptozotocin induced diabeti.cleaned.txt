## Materials Methods
Quercetin dihydrate was purchased from Sigma (St. Louis,
MO, USA) and used as received. PLGA (75/25) was synthesized in-house by ring opening polymerization from

DL-lactide and glycolide using stannous-2-ethylhexanoate as
catalyst (0.2% w/w). Both DL-lactide and glycolide were taken
in a round bottom flask (RBF) and distilled in the presence of
toluene and dried under vacuum. Stannous-2-ethylhexanoate
(in toluene) was added to the reaction flask, and toluene
was evaporated under vacuum. The temperature was raised
to 150degC. After 10 min, vacuum was removed, and reaction
was allowed to continue under nitrogen atmosphere for 8 h.
Crude polymer so obtained was then purified twice by dissolving in dichloromethane and precipitating in isopropyl
alcohol. The purified polymer was then dried under vacuum
for 48 h and characterized for molecular weight and LA/GA
ratio by gel permeation chromatography (GPC) and 1H NMR.
GPC was performed using chloroform (HPLC grade) filtered
through 0.22-mm filter and sonicated for 60 min. Chromatography was carried out at 35degC temperature using a
Shimazdu LC-10AD HPLC pump and Shimazdu RID-6A
refractive index detector coupled with a styragel HR4 column
in an isocratic mode with the stated mobile phase at a flow rate
of 1 ml/min. The sample injection volume was 100 ml, and
data were analyzed using "GPC for Class-Vp" software. The
molecular weight distribution obtained was Mw of 94 kDa, Mn
of 75 kDa, and polydispersity index of 1.25. 1H NMR was
used to determine the lactic acid/glycolic acid ratio. A small
amount of the synthesized PLGA was dissolved in deuterated
chloroform (CDCl3) and analyzed using a Bruker (Avane
DPX 300) NMR. Tetramethylsilane signal was taken as the
zero of chemical shift. The spectrum was recorded in the
chemical shift (d) values ranging from 0 to 11 ppm and lactic
acid/glycolic acid ratio was calculated to be 73:27 (in mole
percent). PLGA was used from the same batch for complete
study. All other chemicals were of analytical grade and used as
purchased.

Freeze drying of Qu-NP

Characterization of Qu-NP

Qu-NPs were freeze dried using a VirTis freeze dryer (Wizard 2.0). For this purpose, nanoparticles were suspended in
double distilled water (2 ml) in glass vials, and a solution of
lyoprotective agent was added to keep their concentration at
5% w/v in final suspension. Different lyoprotective agents
such as glucose, sucrose, D-trehalose, lactose, and sorbitol
were screened. Lyophilization cycle encompassed three
steps:  Samples were frozen to -45degC step wise for 7 h
in seven-step process (thermal treatment);  after freezing
step, samples were dried from -45degC to 20degC in 33.5 h in a
ten-step process (primary drying); and  secondary drying
was performed at 25degC for 5 h. The freeze-dried samples
were resuspended in double distilled water and evaluated for
particle size and size distribution, redispersibility and appearance of formed cake.

Particle size distribution and zeta potential

Particle size distribution and zeta potential of nanoparticles
were determined using Zeta sizer (Malvern ZS, UK) by
dynamic light scattering technique. For particle size measurements, nanoparticles were dispersed in double distilled
water, and measurements were carried out in triplicate taking an average of five measurements. Zeta potential, which
is an indicative parameter for the stability of nanoparticles,
was estimated on the basis of electrophoretic mobility of
nanoparticles under an electric field. The measurements
were carried out in triplicate, each being an average of 30
measurements.

In vitro evaluation of the formulation

Drug release study

For efficacy of a drug formulation, it is important that drug
should be released at desired rate to maintain the therapeutic
concentration in the blood stream as well as at the site of
action. In PLGA-based nanoformulation, which is a matrix
type system, drug is released through diffusion and matrix
degradation processes, and thus, it becomes important to
know the drug release behavior under in vitro conditions,
which may be affected by several factors  such as nature
of drug molecule and polymeric matrix, size and shape of the
particles, type of degradation (bulk vs. surface erosion), etc.
Quercetin is a lipophilic drug with a very poor aqueous
solubility (<=10 mg/ml); thus, in order to maintain the sink
conditions during in vitro release studies, Tween-80 (5%
w/v) was added to the release media in which the solubility of
quercetin was found to be 1.3 mg/ml. The release of quercetin
from the Qu-NP (Fig. 3) showed that 20-40% drug was
released in 1 day followed by a very slow release for 42 days
of the study period. To confirm the complete release, mass
balance of drug remaining in the nanoparticles was carried
out; thus, these particles were dissolved in acetonitrile and
analyzed. The residual quercetin in the nanoparticles was
found to be 29%, 26%, and 21%, which confirm that most
of the drug is stable during the release experiments. When
release was compared for different drug loading samples, a
slightly faster release of quercetin from the Qu-NP with 20%
w/w initial drug loading was observed as compared to 10%
and 15% w/w. This could be attributed to the difference in the
proportion of polymer available to entrap quercetin in the
nanoparticles, thereby decreasing the rate of diffusion of quercetin from different Qu-NP. Since 20% w/w drug loading
Qu-NP have lower ratio of polymer to quercetin than 15% and
10% w/w, thus release of quercetin occurred at a faster rate, but
this difference in the release was statistically insignificant.

The objective of this study was to ascertain the functional
stability of quercetin after nanoparticles preparation. Quercetin produces its antioxidant activity by two mechanisms, either
by scavenging the free radicals or by inhibiting enzymes
responsible for the production of free radicals, e.g., xanthine
oxidase, etc. Thus, two different antioxidant assays were conducted, viz. DPPH scavenging activity and xanthine oxidase
inhibition activity.
DPPH is a stable free radical and has been used to determine the free radical scavenging activity of a compound.
Quercetin has free radical scavenging activity due to its ability
to donate the hydrogen ion, which stabilizes the free radicals
and prevents the oxidation of the lipid, protein, and DNA and
reduces the injuries caused by oxidative stress. SC50 value is
the concentration of the antioxidant required to scavenge 50%
of free radicals in the presence of antioxidant, and it was found
to be 10.03 mg/ml for quercetin control. Qu-NPs were analyzed at an equivalent amount of quercetin, and no statistically
significant difference was observed between the quercetin
(control) and Qu-NP (Fig. 4), indicating that the antioxidant

activity of quercetin was preserved after encapsulation in the
nanoparticles.
To access the antisuperoxide formation activity, IC50
value was determined, which is the concentration of the
antioxidant required to inhibit 50% of enzyme activity in
the presence of antioxidant. IC50 value for quercetin was
found to be 10.63 mg/ml. Quercetin in Qu-NP was also able
to retain the antisuperoxide formation activity as that of free
quercetin control as shown in Fig. 4.

## Conclusion
In the present study, quercetin-loaded PLGA nanoparticles
(Qu-NP) were successfully prepared and tested at reduced
dose and lesser frequency for pharmacokinetics as well as
efficacy in rat model. In pharmacokinetic study, it was
observed that Qu-NP showed an increase of 523% in relative oral bioavailability of quercetin to that of quercetin
suspension, indicating the potential to reduce dose using
Qu-NP. Additionally, the plasma levels of quercetin were
also sustained for 6 days in vivo, thus promising a reduced
dosing frequency. In the efficacy studies, decrease in blood
glucose level was observed in diabetic rats treated with
quercetin suspension (150 mg/kg given daily) and Qu-NP
(150 mg/kg given every fifth day) as compared to diabetic
control group. Catalase and SOD levels were also found to
