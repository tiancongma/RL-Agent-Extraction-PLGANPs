Abstract
Aeromonas hydrophila, an opportunistic warm water pathogen, has always been a threat to aquaculture, leading to substan¬≠
tial economic losses. Vaccination of the cultured fish would effectively prevent Aeromoniasis, and recent advancements in
nanotechnology show promise for efficacious vaccines. Oral delivery would be the most practical and convenient method
of vaccine delivery in a grow-out pond. This study studied the immunogenicity and protective efficacy of a nanoparticle-
loaded outer membrane protein A from A. hydrophila in the zebrafish model. The protein was over-expressed, purified,
and encapsulated using poly lactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA)¬Ýnanoparticles via the double emulsion method. The PLGA
nanoparticles loaded with recombinant OmpA (rOmpA) exhibited a size of 295‚Äâ¬±‚Äâ15.1¬Ýnm, an encapsulation efficiency of
72.52%, and a polydispersity index of 0.292‚Äâ¬±‚Äâ0.07. Scanning electron microscopy confirmed the spherical and isolated
nature of the PLGA-rOmpA nanoparticles. The protective efficacy in A. hydrophila-infected zebrafish after oral admin¬≠
istration of the nanovaccine resulted in relative percentage survival of 77.7. Gene expression studies showed significant
upregulation of immune genes in the vaccinated fish. The results demonstrate the usefulness of oral administration of
nanovaccine-loaded rOmpA as a potential vaccine since it induced a robust immune response and conferred adequate
protection against A. hydrophila in zebrafish, Danio rerio.

Introduction
Aquaculture is one of the important food sectors globally
that is expected to address food and nutritional security.
There has been exponential growth in the sector due to the
increased demand for fish coupled with dwindling capture
fisheries (Zhang et al. 2021). According to Food and Agri¬≠
cultural Organization statistics, global aquaculture produc¬≠
tion reached 122.6¬Ý million tonnes in 2020 (FAO 2022).
Intensive practices that involve increased stocking density
induce stress in the cultured species, resulting in disease
outbreaks and consequent economic losses (Maiti et al.
2020). Bacterial diseases are one of the leading causes, and
autochthonous bacteria in the aquatic environment, such as
Aeromonas, Edwardsiella, and Flavobacterium, become
major opportunistic pathogens of fish (Plebanski et al. 2013;
Shaalan et al. 2016) Though antibiotics would be the pre¬≠
ferred choice for treating bacterial diseases, they should be
the least preferred due to the issues of antibiotic resistance
developing and residues in the food fish. Consequently, this
practice contributes to developing and spreading antimi¬≠
crobial resistance in the aquatic environment (Nayak et al.
2023). Clinical signs are shared in many diseases caused by
agents other than bacteria. Most often, antibiotics tend to be
used empirically as it takes more than 48 to 72¬Ýh to culture,
identify and study the antibiogram of the pathogen in ques¬≠
tion. Hence the maxim that ‚Äòprevention is better than cure
‚Äòworks best in aquaculture settings. The most cost-effective
and ethical disease control approach would be vaccinating
the cultured species against target pathogens (Maiti et al.
2020). However, the challenge of an efficient vaccine and
its delivery through a practical mode such as oral adminis¬≠
tration remains challenging (Wang et al. 2015; Zheng et al.
2016; Zhang et al. 2021). Although vaccine development
has evolved significantly from a whole cell, heat-killed to
use single proteins/peptides as antigens. Yet, the issue of
appropriate delivery for maximum protective efficacy of
the vaccines remains a challenge (Smith et al. 2015) It is
now being increasingly recognized that the bacterial outer
membrane proteins are potential vaccine candidates because
of their exposed epitopes on the outer cell surface, which
are highly conserved in nature and immunogenic (V√Ýzquez-
Ju√°rez et al. 2004; Maiti et al. 2009, 2012, 2022; Neema
and Karunasagar 2018). Oral administration of the vac¬≠
cine, though a method of choice, experience failure due
to degradation in the gastrointestinal tract by the diges¬≠
tive enzymes with the consequent loss of their functional
immunogenic potential. Hence, there is an urgent need to
develop a proficient drug delivery system for site-specific
action. A suitable delivery system necessitates activating the
antigen-presenting cells and induction of a strong protective
immune response (Mody et al. 2013) by minimizing antigen
degradation, increasing bioavailability, and presenting the
antigen to target immune cells (Gregory et al. 2013; Zaman
et al. 2013). Polymeric nanoparticles have emerged as effec¬≠
tive drug-delivery vehicles in fish immunization due to their
ability to encapsulate and protect the bioactive molecules
from degradation caused by hydrolysis and enzymatic pro¬≠
cesses (Maiti et al. 2023; Liu et al. 2023). In recent years,
nanoparticle-based vaccines have been developed for use
in aquaculture to enhance drug delivery and immune pro¬≠
tection (Shaalan et al. 2016; Smith et al. 2015). Nanopar¬≠
ticles with the desired size promote endocytosis by the cells,
increasing antigen absorption and presentation (Shaalan et
al. 2016).
In this study, the outer membrane protein A (OmpA) of
Aeromonas hydrophila was cloned and expressed, and the
purified protein was encapsulated in poly lactic-co-glycolic
acid (PLGA) nanoparticles that were characterized and eval¬≠
uated in vitro for their tolerance to the digestive enzymes,
release efficiency and enhancing the immunogenicity of
the antigen. Zebrafish were initially given the encapsulated
vaccine orally through their feed, followed by a subsequent
challenge with pathogenic A. hydrophila to study the level
of protection. The oral delivery of the nano-encapsulated
vaccine afforded protection.
Materials and methods
Bacterial cultures and molecular confirmation
A confirmed pathogenic strain of A. hydrophila (Aero B)
(Nayak et al. 2023) stored in the institute repository in an

nutrient broth and incubated at 30¬∞C for 24¬Ýh, and subcul¬≠

strain of A. hydrophila used in the study was ATCC 49,140.

Vaccination and challenge study
Examination of the health status of fish was based on clini¬≠

unvaccinated; Group 2 was fed with empty NPs without

designated as the low dose NPs (LDNP) group (Dubey et

days, and the fish were fed twice daily. After two weeks of

Discussion
Vaccination is one of the best approaches to prevent dis¬≠
ease and its spread in aquaculture farms. The most practi¬≠
cal method of administration would be by oral route. It is
referred to because it is the least stressful and efficient in
terms of time, cost, and suitability for fish of different sizes
(Silin et al. 2007; Nakajima et al. 2014; Salgado-Miranda
et al. 2013). However, oral vaccines are susceptible to deg¬≠
radation in the enzyme-rich gastrointestinal environment
and may not reach the target sites for effective uptake by
immune cells. Encapsulating the vaccine in an appropriate
nanoparticle would offer protection from degradation in the
stomach and intestinal tract and further enhance the immune
response of the oral vaccine over a prolonged period (Zhang
et al. 2021).
The PLGA nanoparticles generated in this study could
encapsulate the 52.41¬ÝkDa OmpA protein of A. hydrophila.
Following encapsulation, an increase in size (295‚Äâ¬±‚Äâ15.1¬Ýnm)
was observed compared to empty nanoparticles. Several
studies have demonstrated that particles with a size of
<‚Äâ500¬Ýnm can be successfully endocytosed in the body with¬≠

2009). Dubey et al. (2016) reported good protection against
A. hydrophila infection by orally administered OmpW vac¬≠
cine with a particle size of 370‚Äì375¬Ýnm. The PDI has a dis¬≠
tribution range of 0.000 to 0.500, and values‚Äâ>‚Äâ0.5 indicate
particle aggregation (Baumann et al. 2009; Behera et al.
2013). The polydispersity index (PDI) of 0.292‚Äâ¬±‚Äâ0.07 for
the protein-loaded PLGA nanoparticles in this study con¬≠

the PLGA-rOmpA particles as being spherical, regular, and
in the LDNP and HDNP groups in 30 and 50 days, respec¬≠

Conclusions
In conclusion, the developed nano vaccine delivery offers
several advantages, including high antigen loading capacity
and improved biocompatibility. The ability to elicit a dura¬≠
ble immune response and the protection against Aeromonas
species in zebrafish utilizing the PLGA delivery system is
evidence of the commercial potential of an oral vaccine. The
obtained RPS results show promise as it demonstrates the
efficacy of the orally administered rOmpA-PLGA vaccine
against pathogenic A. hydrophila. The vaccine trials can be
extended to the field, and target different fish species, where
Aeromonas epizootic frequently presents a threat. However,
optimization of the vaccine dose, formulation ratio for oral
delivery, and determining the appropriate immunization
dose intervals for field application needs to be standardized
for the individual fish species being cultivated.
