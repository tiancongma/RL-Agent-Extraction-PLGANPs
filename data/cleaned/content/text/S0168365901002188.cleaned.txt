## Materials Methods
Poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA, molar ratio of d,l-lactic to glycolic acid, 75:25, MW=45 kDa), monomethoxy poly(ethylene glycol) (MePEG, MW=5 kDa, purity >95%) and bovine serum albumin (BSA, MW=66 kDa) was purchased from Sigma (St. Louis, MO). Poly(vinyl alcohol) (PVA, MW=16 kDa, 98% hydrolyzed) was obtained from Aldrich (Milwaukee, WI). Dichloromethane (DCM) of HPLC grade was used in this study. All other reagents and solvents were of analytical grade.
2.2. Animals
Sprague-Dawley rats (Grade II, 250+-20 g, , Certificate No. 008) were supplied by Shanghai Experimental Animal Center, Chinese Academy of Sciences. The animals were acclimatized at a temperature of 25+-2degC and a relative humidity of 70+-5% under natural light/dark conditions for 4 days before dosing.
2.3. Synthesis and purification of PEG-PLGA copolymer
PEG-PLGA copolymer was synthesized by ring-opening polymerization of the lactide and glycolide in the presence of MePEG as PEG-PLA copolymer . Briefly, PLGA (10 g) and MePEG (2 g) were dissolved in freshly distilled toluene (150 ml). The solution was placed in a 250-ml three-necked flask equipped with a stirrer, water-cooled condenser, and nitrogen inlet. Stannous 2-ethylhexanoate (20 mg) was added to the solution as a catalyst. The flask was heated in an oil bath at 114degC for 8 h with stirring at 250 rpm. Then, the solvent was evaporated under reduced pressure. The residue was dissolved in 200 ml of DCM, filtered, and then added to vigorously stirred water (1000 ml) at 60degC. After DCM evaporation from the emulsion, a solid was isolated from the aqueous phase by decantation. Finally, the copolymer was purified by GPC.
2.4. Measurement of copolymer
The 1H NMR spectra were recorded in CDCl3 on a Bruker AM 400 MHz spectrometer.
The Fourier transform infrared (FTIR, Nicolet, Magna FTIR-750) spectrum was obtained from a neat film cast from the chloroform copolymer solution between KBr tablets.
GPC was performed in THF with a Waters Associates Model ALC/GPC 244 apparatus. Molecular weight and molecular weight distribution of the copolymer were calculated using polystyrene as the standard.
2.5. Preparation of PEG-PLGA and PLGA nanoparticles
Nanoparticles were prepared by an adjustment of the multiple emulsion (w1/o/w2) method as previously described , ,  to reduce the particle size to the submicron range. Briefly, 0.5 ml BSA solution with 10 mg/ml concentration was emulsified in 5 ml of DCM containing PEG-PLGA (100 mg) or PLGA (100 mg) by homogenization at 5000 rpm in an ice bath for 3x10 s (Bailing, Model DS-200, China). Thereafter, this first emulsion was poured into 50 ml of the PVA aqueous solution (0.4%, w/v) and homogenized at 10 000 rpm in an ice bath for 3x15 s (Bailing Model DS-200, China). The double emulsion was diluted in 150 ml PVA solution (0.1%, w/v) and the DCM was rapidly eliminated by evaporation under reduced pressure. Finally, the nanoparticles were collected by centrifugation at 25 000xg for 25 min (Beckman Model J2-21) and washed twice with water before lyophilization.
2.6. Determination of the BSA entrapment efficiency
The amount of non-entrapped protein in aqueous phase was determined with the Lowry-Peterson protein assay in the supernatant obtained after ultracentrifugation and washing of nanoparticles . This procedure permits analysis of very dilute protein solutions with removal of most interfering substance . The amount of BSA entrapped within nanoparticles was calculated by the difference between the total amount used to prepare nanoparticles and the amount of BSA present in the aqueous phase. Each sample was assayed in triplicate.
2.7. Physicochemical characterization of nanoparticles
The morphological examination of nanoparticles was performed using a transmission electron microscope (TEM, CM12 Philips, The Netherlands) following negative staining with sodium phosphotungstate solution (0.2%, w/v). The particle size and zeta potential of nanoparticles were determined, respectively, by photon correlation spectroscopy and laser doppler anemometry using a zetasizer IV (Malvern Instruments, UK). The results were normalized with respect to a polystyrene standard solution.
2.8. In vitro release experiment
50 mg nanoparticles were suspended in 100 ml of phosphate-buffered saline (PBS, pH 7.4), containing 0.02% (w/v) Tween 80 and incubated at 37degC under gentle magnetic stirring at 100 rpm. At predetermined time intervals, 5 ml samples were withdrawn and centrifuged at 25 000xg for 25 min. The supernatant was assayed for protein release and replaced by 5 ml of fresh release medium. The amount of BSA in the release medium was determined by the Lowry-Peterson protein assay. Each nanoparticle batch was analyzed in triplicate.
2.9. Radioiodination of BSA
BSA was labeled with 125I using the IODO-GEN procedure as follows: 20 mg of BSA in 60 ml of 0.1 M potassium phosphate buffer (pH 7.2) were layered over a freshly prepared film of IODO-GEN (100 mg) and incubated for 10 min at 4degC in the presence of 1 mCi of carrier-free Na125I. The reaction mixture was brought up to 0.5 ml volume with PBS, and the unreacted iodine was removed by gel filtration on a Sephadex G-25 PD10 column equilibrated with PBS. The specific radioactivity of the product was assessed in a Cobra II-Autogamma (Packard Instruments, CT, USA). The 125I-iodinated BSA was used for the study of in vivo biodistribution as described follows.
2.10. In vivo biodistribution of BSA-loaded nanoparticles
In order to obtain the blood clearance curves for PLGA and PEG-PLGA nanoparticles, two groups of rats (n=7) were used in these experiments, group 1 being treated with [125I]BSA-loaded PLGA nanoparticles and group 2 with [125I]BSA-loaded PEG-PLGA nanoparticles. For administration, nanoparticles were suspended in a certain volume of PBS (pH 7.4) to obtain the required concentration of [125I]BSA. Each animal was given intravenously with appropriate preparation containing 25 mg of BSA with a trace of 125I (1 mCi).
After intravenous administration, blood were collected at 0.25, 0.5, 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, 12 and 24 h via caudal vein with heparinized tubes. Plasma was separated, and then 0.1 ml of plasma was mixed with 0.5 ml of trichloroacetic acid, centrifuged at 3000 rpm for 10 min, finally, the radioactivity levels of the residues were measured. The half-life was evaluated by curve fitting by means of the Practical Pharmacokinetic Program-Version 87.
In order to study the tissue distribution, four groups of the rats (n=5) were used as described above. Two groups being treated with [125I]BSA-loaded PLGA nanoparticles and other two groups with [125I]BSA-loaded PEG-PLGA nanoparticles. After intravenous administration, rats were dehematized of the abdominal aorta at 3 and 12 h. Tissues were collected and weighted, and the radioactivity was measured.

## Results
3.1. Measurement and characterization of copolymer
The basic chemical structure of PEG-PLGA copolymer is confirmed by 1H NMR (Fig. 2). One of the striking features is a large peak at 3.65 ppm, corresponding to the methylene groups of the MePEG. Overlapping doublets at 1.55 ppm are attributed to the methyl groups of the d- and l-lactic acid repeat units. The multiplets at 5.2 and 4.8 ppm correspond to the lactic acid CH and the glycolic acid CH2, respectively, with the high complexity of the peaks resulting from different d-lactic, l-lactic, glycolic acid sequences in the polymer backbone.
The FTIR spectrum is consistent with the structure of the expected copolymer (Fig. 3). It shows that absorption band at 3509.9 cm-1 is assigned to terminal hydroxy groups in the copolymer from which PEG homopolymer has been removed. The bands at 3010 and 2955 cm-1 are due to C-H stretch of CH3, and 2885 cm-1 due to C-H stretch of CH2. A strong band at 1762.6 cm-1 is assigned to CO stretch. Absorption at 1186-1089.6 cm-1 is due to C-O stretch.
Molecular weights and molecular weight distribution of the obtained copolymer are determined by means of GPC (Fig. 4). The average molecular weight is 50 800. The unimodal mass distribution excluded the presence of MePEG or PLGA.
3.2. Physicochemical characterization of nanoparticles
In order to investigate the physicochemical characterization of nanoparticles prepared by the double emulsion method. Nanoparticles were observed by transmission election microscope (Fig. 5). From these micrographs, nanoparticles prepared with PLGA or PEG-PLGA containing BSA were spherical in shape and uniform with size about 200 nm. The encapsulation efficiency values achieved for BSA were influenced by the presence of PEG in PLGA chains (Table 1). Compared with PLGA nanoparticles (63.8%), PEG-PLGA nanoparticles showed lower encapsulation efficiency (48.6%). The values of the zeta potential were obviously affected by the presence of PEG chains. Higher negative values were obtained for PLGA nanoparticles (-33.9 mV). A marked decrease in the surface charge for PEG-PLGA nanoparticles was occurred (-16.1 mV).

3.3. In vitro release experiment
The in vitro release profiles of BSA were obtained by representing the percentage of BSA release with respect to the amount of BSA encapsulated (Fig. 6). For two nanoparticles, BSA release both occurred in two phases:  an inintial burst release, a significant amount of protein was release within 12 h, 30.1% for PEG-PLGA nanoparticles and 20.7% for PLGA nanoparticles;  after 12 h, BSA release profiles displayed a sustained release. The amount of cumulated BSA release over 7 days was: 71.4% for PEG-PLGA nanoparticles and 49.8% for PLGA nanoparticles, respectively.
3.4. In vivo biodistribution of BSA-loaded nanoparticles
The blood clearance curves for [125I]BSA-loaded nanoparticles after intravenous injection were shown in Fig. 7. PEG-PLGA nanoparticles showed initial high blood circulating levels compared with PLGA nanoparticles. In fact, [125I]BSA in blood at 1 h after intravenous administration of PEG-PLGA nanoparticles was about 12-fold that observed for the PLGA nanoparticles. [125I]BSA loaded in PLGA nanoparticles was quickly removed from the circulation (circulatory half-life, 13.6 min). On the contrary, [125I]BSA loaded in PEG-PLGA nanoparticles exhibited a markedly delayed blood clearance (circulatory half-life, 270.9 min). It could be seen that the blood-associated [125I]BSA remained higher after 24 h compared with that of PLGA nanoparticles.
The distribution profiles of BSA loaded in PLGA and PEG-PLGA nanoparticles to various tissues after intravenous administration were shown in Fig. 8. After 3 h, [125I]BSA loaded in PLGA nanoparticles was mainly distributed to the liver, spleen, lung and kidney and the level of the blood very low. At 12 h after intravenous injection, [125I]BSA loaded in PLGA nanoparticles was gradually eliminated from all tissues. [125I]BSA loaded in PEG-PLGA nanoparticles was transported from the blood to normal tissues with the differential extent and profile as in PLGA nanoparticles. The plasma levels of [125I]BSA loaded in PEG-PLGA nanoparticles were markedly higher than those in PLGA nanoparticles at same time points, and their transport to the liver and kidney were found to be markedly limited.

## Discussion
In the paper, PEG-coated nanoparticles were prepared with diblock PEG-R copolymers, where R is hydrophobic and biodegradable PLGA. The syntheses of PEG-PLGA copolymer have been reported in elsewhere , , but a very detailed description about its process and determination is few. In our laboratory, PEG-PLGA copolymer was synthesized by ring-opening polymerization of a linear, diblock structure composed of one block of PLGA and MePEG containing hydroxyl functional groups at one end of the chains. The 1H NMR and FTIR spectrum were consistent with the structure of PEG-PLGA copolymer. The molecular weight determined by GPC showed a good correlation with the total amount of MePEG and PLGA.
Stability is one of the most important problems when a biodegradable polymer is used for controlled-release applications of PPD. The destabilized PPD lose biological efficacy and aggregate to increase the possibility of an unwanted immune response. Among a variety of preparing processes, emulsification of an aqueous protein solution in an organic solvent is considered a major risk factor that presents an obstacle to the successful development of emulsion and microsphere-based PPD delivery systems. It is generally agreed that the observed instability from exposure of proteins to heat, shear and interaction of the protein molecule with organic solvent or polymer residuals dissolved in the organic solvent . It has been reported that BSA displayed a strong resistance against denaturing effects of the DMC/water interface. Despite a slight change in the composition of water-soluble species, BSA was almost completely recovered from the aqueous phase after emulsification . In addition, the PEG chains in nanoparticles would not only oriented towards the external aqueous medium but also towards the inner aqueous phase containing PPD. Thus, the protein reservoirs would be theoretically surrounded by a PEG barrier, which would reduce the interaction of the protein with the PLGA matrix and protected protein . However, it is certainty that a little inactive BSA molecules were incorporated into nanoparticles, and the precise proportion of the entrapped BSA molecules retaining the biological activity is presently under investigation in our laboratory.
So far, several techniques have already been developed to prepare nanoparticles loading PPD. In earlier works the protein loaded in nanoparticles were obtained by adsorbing protein at the surface of the particles , . The main limitation of this process is that the protein is only physically bound to the surface of nanoparticles. As a result, desorption of protein from the nanoparticles surface was easily influenced by environment. Thus, it is desirable to incorporating PPD into nanoparticles. For this purpose, the double emulsion technique was considered the most appropriate method. However, influence factors of entrapment efficiency on this technique are very complicated, such as copolymer concentration in organic solution, volume of inner aqueous phase, volume of outer aqueous phase, protein concentration in inner aqueous phase, the first homogenized speed and time, the second homogenized speed and time, PVA concentration, etc. The optimum condition with high entrapment efficiency obtained from the Uniform Design is presently under investigation.
The encapsulation efficiency values achieved for BSA were different between PLGA nanoparticles and PEG-PLGA nanoparticles. The difference could come from the presence of PEG in PLGA chains, but its mechanism is indistinct. Compared with PLGA nanoparticles, PEG-PLGA nanoparticles showed a marked decrease in the surface charge. This could be related to a shift of the hydrodynamic phase of shear to greater distances from the nanoparticles surface. The same observations have been reported for PLGA-PLA:PEG-coated nanoparticles .
For two nanoparticles, BSA release both showed an initial burst release. This fast release could be relation part BSA adsorbed onto the wall of nanoparticles that would be immediately released during the initial stage. After the initial burst, BSA release profiles displayed a sustained fashion. This sustained release could result from diffusion of BSA into the polymer wall and the protein through polymer wall as well as the erosion of the polymers. In this paper, a higher and faster BSA release was observed for PEG-PLGA nanoparticles than those of PLGA during at 12 h. The difference could be relation to the presence of PEG in PLGA chains. There could be more protein molecules close to PEG-PLGA nanoparticles surface including those inserted among PEG chains on the surface of nanoparticles, which also would be immediately released during the initial stage.
Our experimental results showed that PEG-PLGA nanoparticles could extend half-life of BSA to 4.5 h and obviously change the protein biodistribution in rats. This result confirmed the idea of forming particles with a steric PEG barrier that would prevent their rapid uptake by MPS and improve their circulatory half-life. The surface modification of the nanoparticles could have the potential benefit for PPD delivery.
In conclusion, the results obtained in the present study showed that PEG-PLGA could be synthesized by ring-opening polymerization of the lactide and glycolide in the presence of MePEG. 1H NMR and FTIR spectrum were consistent with the structure of PEG-PLGA copolymer. The stealth nanoparticles loading BSA could be prepared by double emulsion technique. The entrapment efficiency was 48.6%, and particle size about 200 nm. BSA release from the stealth nanoparticles showed an initial burst release and then sustained release phase. PEG-PLGA nanoparticles could extend biological half-life of BSA to 4.5 h and obviously change the protein biodistribution in rats. These results showed that PEG-PLGA nanoparticles could be an effective carrier for PPD delivery.
