Stability of insulin during the erosion of poly(lactic acid) and
poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) microspheres

Mohamed Ihab Fetouhb, Achim Go¨pferichc,*

Abstract
In recent years, the acylation of peptides during the erosion of poly(lactic acid) and poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) microspheres
has been described in the literature. To investigate whether insulin is prone to the covalent attachment of lactic or glycolic acid,
insulin-loaded PLA and PLGA microspheres containing 5% bovine insulin were manufactured using a w/o/w multiple emulsion-
solvent evaporation technique. Microspheres were characterized for their insulin encapsulation efficiency and release character-
istics in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) at pH 7.4 and 37 8C. Moreover, the stability of the peptide during 18 days of release was
evaluated using HPLC and HPLC-MS techniques. The results showed that the insulin loading efficiencies of PLA and PLGA
microspheres were 75.18% and 79.63%, respectively. The microspheres were spherical with relatively porous surfaces with an
average diameter of 40 and 53 Am, respectively. Insulin release from the microspheres was characterized by an initial burst, which
was attributed to the amount of protein located on or close to the microsphere surface. The total ion chromatogram (TIC) of
insulin samples extracted after 18 days of erosion in phosphate buffer pH 7.4 at 37 8C revealed that deamidation was the major
mechanism of instability. Surprisingly, no acylation products were found. Control experiments in concentrated lactic acid
solutions confirmed a minimal reactivity of the peptide under these conditions.

1. Introduction
For the past decade, considerable research efforts
have aimed at developing controlled release systems
for protein and peptide drugs from biodegradable
materials such as poly(d,l-lactic acid) (PLA) or
poly(d,l-lactide-co-glycolide) (PLGA) [1]. Entrap-

ment in these polymers has been found to be benefi-

protected from proteolysis, drug release could be
prolonged substantially and therapeutic effects en-
hanced [2]. Despite the success of this strategy, PLA
and PLGA may not provide a microenvironment that
is unequivocally suitable for all protein and peptide
drugs [2]. Since most representatives of this class of
drugs are subject to physical and chemical instability,
it turned out to be a major challenge to design con-
trolled release devices that preserve the biological
activity of proteins and peptides [3]. Consequently,
major research efforts have been undertaken to inves-
tigate the impact of processing techniques as well as
to reveal the physicochemical conditions that prevail
during PLA and PLGA erosion and the effect thereof
on the stability of drugs [4–6]. Valuable information
on polymer degradation [7] mechanisms of protein/
polymer [8] and peptide/polymer [9] interactions as
well as potential sources of irreversible protein inac-
tivation have been identified this way [10]. Elevated
levels of moisture, providing sufficient protein mobil-
ity for reactivity, the acidic microclimate induced by
acidic degradation products, the carboxylic acid end
groups on the polymer and protein adsorption on
polymer surfaces are a few of the innumerable para-
meters that were identified to cause protein instabil-
ities, mainly via unfolding and aggregation [2].
In recent years, we investigated the stability of
peptides during release and have revealed that they
are subject to an acylation reaction during which
lactic and glycolic acids are attached to amine groups
of the N-terminal and lysine side chains [11]. Since
then, the mechanism has been confirmed for a num-
ber of peptides [11,12]. From our first studies it was
obvious that different species of peptides reacted
with different intensities [11]. As numerous cyto-
kines are interesting drug candidates for controlled
release devices, we were interested in the effect of
molecular weight on the process. We, therefore, se-
lected insulin as a high molecular weight peptide.
Insulin-loaded PLA and PLGA microspheres were
manufactured using multiple emulsion techniques
and characterized for morphology, size, insulin load-
ing efficiency and drug release profile. We then
monitored the stability during polymer erosion with
a special emphasis on acylation reactions using
HPLC and HPLC-MS analysis.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Materials
Poly(d,l-lactic acid) with equal amounts of d-
and l-lactic acid (PLA, Mw 17,000, Resomer R
202) and Poly(d,l-lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA,
Mw 14,100, Resomer RG 502) with a lactide/glyco-
lide ratio of 50:50, both end-capped by an ethylester
group, were obtained from Boehringer Ingelheim
(Ingelheim, Germany). Bovine insulin (51 amino
acids, Mw 5734 Da) was kindly supplied by Aventis
(Frankfurt, Germany). Concentrated lactic acid, ana-
lytical grade (approximately 90%), was purchased
from Merck (Darmstadt, Germany). Poly(vinyl alco-
hol) PVA, 98% hydrolyzed (Av. Mw 13,000–23,000)
was obtained from Aldrich Chemical Co. (Mil-
waukee,
USA),
4,4-Dicarboxy-2-2-biquinoline
(Bicinchoninic acid, BCA) from Sigma Chemical
Co. (St. Louis, USA). Acetonitrile was obtained
from Baker (Deventer, The Netherlands). Triflouroa-
cetic acid (TFA) was obtained from Sigma-Aldrich
(Seelze, Germany). All other reagents were of ana-
lytical grade or higher purity. Water used throughout
the investigation was double-distilled and filtered
through a cellulose nitrate filter with pores of 0.2
Am diameter (Sartorius, Go¨ttingen, Germany).
2.2. Stability of insulin in lactic acid solutions
Insulin was dissolved in different lactic acid solu-
tions (1%, 5%, 10% and 20% w/w) to get insulin
concentrations of 170, 210, 110 and 250 Ag/ml, re-
spectively. The resulting solutions (10 ml) were stored
in 15 ml polypropylene tubes (Corning Costar, Bod-
enheim, Germany) at 37 8C for 4 weeks. Samples
were drawn on days 7, 14, 21 and 28, frozen in liquid
nitrogen and stored at 80 8C (HERAfreeze HFU 586
basic from Kendro, Hanau, Germany) until further
analysis. The samples were analyzed using the
HPLC-MS method described below.
2.3. Preparation of insulin-loaded microspheres
Insulin-loaded microspheres were prepared by a
double emulsion-solvent evaporation technique [13].
In brief, the internal aqueous phase (200 Al of 0.1
N HCl) containing 15 mg insulin was added to the

2.4. Investigation of microsphere loading efficiency
10 mg of freeze-dried microspheres was weighed
into a 2 ml micro test tube (Eppendorf, Hamburg,
Germany) and dissolved in 600 Al of dichloro-
methane. 600 Al of a 0.1 N HCl solution were
added and the two phases were vortex-mixed (Hei-
dolph, Schwabach, Germany). The dispersion was
allowed to settle at room temperature for 10 min
before the dichloromethane phase was separated
from the mixture by centrifugation at 2000 rpm for
3 min (G-S-15R, Beckman, Palo, Atto, CA, USA).
The upper aqueous phase containing the extracted
peptide was analyzed using a micro BCA assay and
HPLC analysis.
2.5. HPLC and HPLC-MS analysis
Insulin samples were investigated by HPLC using
a setup that consisted of a degasser (from Knauer,
Berlin, Germany), LC-10-AT pump, FCV-10ATVP
gradient mixer, SIL-10 ADVP autosampler, SPD-10
AV UV detector and SCL-10AVP controller (all from
Schimadzu, Duisburg, Germany). A linear gradient of
15% to 40% solvent B (90% acetonitrile aqueous
solution+0.1% v/v TFA) in solvent A (10% acetoni-
trile aqueous solution+0.1% v/v TFA) over 42 min
was applied as mobile phase at a flow rate of 1.0 ml/
min. 100-Al samples were separated at room temper-
ature using a combination of a C18 reversed phase
pre-column (LC318, 4.6 mm50 mm) and analytical
column (LC318, 4.6 mm250 mm) from Supelco
(Deisenhofen,
Germany).
Chromatograms
were
recorded at 274 nm (UV detector).
For HPLC-MS analysis, samples were analysed
using an Agilent 1100 HPLC system with degasser,
binary pump, autosampler, column oven and diode
array detector (all from Agilent, Waldbronn, Ger-
many), coupled with a TSQ7000 mass spectrometer
(ThermoQuest, San Jose, CA, USA) with API2-
source (capillary temperature: 300 8C, spray voltage:
4 kV). A linear gradient of 20–95% solvent B (ace-
tonitrile+0.1% v/v TFA) in solvent A (double-dis-
tilled water+0.1% v/v TFA) over 30 min served as
a mobile phase at a flow rate of 0.2 ml/min. About
10–20 Al of the samples were separated using a
C18 reversed phase analytical column (Jupiter, 2
mm250 mm) from Phenomenex, Aschaffenburg,
Germany. The XCALIBURR software package (Ther-
moQuest, San JoKe, CA, USA) was used for data
acquisition and analysis.

insulin-loaded microspheres
The microsphere morphology was investigated

The size distribution of insulin-loaded PLA and

were especially useful to characterize the micro-

2.7. Insulin release studies
For the investigation of peptide release from bio-
degradable polymer microspheres, approximately 10
mg of microspheres was weighed into a 2 ml micro
test tube (Eppendorf, Hamburg, Germany) and incu-
bated in 1 ml phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), pH 7.4
(stabilized with 0.02% w/v sodium azide). The sam-
ples were eroded under gentle shaking (frequency 10
min1) at 37 8C in a shaking water bath (Model 1086
from GFL, Burgwerdel, Germany). The buffer was
changed and release samples were taken at regular
time intervals. All samples were analyzed in triplicate
and stored at 80 8C until further analysis.
2.8. Assessment of insulin integrity
The stability of bovine insulin inside polymer
microspheres was determined after 6, 12 and 18
days. Microspheres were harvested from the release
medium by centrifugation and insulin was extracted
using the method described above. The aqueous phase
was
investigated
immediately
using
HPLC
and
HPLC-MS analysis.

4. Discussion
When bovine insulin was extracted from PLA
and PLGA microspheres after 18 days of erosion,
HPLC-MS
analysis
showed
that
a
considerable
amount of protein had undergone structural changes.
Changes in insulin molecular mass indicate that the
main degradation product is desamido insulin. Sur-
prisingly, an acylation with monomers, as has been
reported for many peptides, could not be verified,
despite an acylation in lactic acid solutions.
Brange et al. [21] showed that insulin can decom-
pose via a multitude of different chemical reactions.
It may deamidate along two main reaction pathways
by entirely different mechanisms. In acid media,
deamidation of asparagine in position 21 of the A-
chain catalyzed intramolecularly by the protonated
C-terminal seems to be the prevailing mechanism.
This deamidation of bovine insulin in PLGA micro-
spheres has already been extensively reported in the
literature [22]. It seems to be spurred by the acidic
conditions that prevail during PLA and PLGA deg-

major instability mechanisms were again Asn A21
deamidation and dimer formation [23]. Similar to the
findings in this study Shao et al. [24] reported slow
and incomplete porcine insulin release from PLA and
PLGA microspheres. It was reported that only about
11% of the insulin extracted from microspheres after
release over a period of 4 weeks was intact. The
degradation products observed consisted of about
15% deamidated Asn A21 insulin, about 22% cova-
lent insulin dimer and trace amounts of high molec-

intermediate imide formation occurs at residue Asn
B3, leading to the formation of iso Asp and Asp
derivatives.
While the presence of a deamidation product was
of little surprise to us, we were amazed that no acyl-
ation of insulin occurred during the erosion of PLA or
PLGA micropsheres. Its reaction with oligomers in
lactic acid solution shows, however, that it is in gen-
eral prone to this instability mechanism. Na et al. [12]
as well as Lucke et al. [11] showed that the primary
amines of the N-teminal or in the lysine side chains
are targets for peptide acylation by lactide and glyco-
lide oligomers. Insulin contains three such potential
acylation sites: two N-terminal amino acids (PheB1
and GlyA1) and one lysine side chain (LysB29). Strick-
ley and Anderson [25] showed that in solution at pH
2.5 and 35 8C, insulin degrades by deamidation and
covalent dimerization at the C-terminal AsnA21. This
mechanism is initiated by an intramolecular nucleo-
philic attack of the protonated carboxylic acid of the
C-terminal on a side chain amide, forming a reactive
cyclic anhydride intermediate. It has been described
that this intermediate reacts with water to form desa-
midoA21 insulin. Alternatively, it can bind to the PheB1
N-terminal amine groups of PheB1 and GlyA1 of other
insulin molecules to form desamidoA21-PheB1 or desa-
midoA21-GlyA1, respectively, whereby the latter reac-
tion is slower than the first one. Reactions with the
lysine side chain have not yet been reported.
The selectivity of the reaction with the three
nucleophiles can be explained on the basis of the
pKa
values
of
their
individual
amino
groups
(PheB1b7.0,
GlyA1c8.0
and
LysB29N9.0).
The
amine with the lowest pKa, PheB1, is likely to be the
least protonated one inside the acidic microenviron-
ment of the microparticles. Therefore, it will react
faster than its two dcompetitorsT. This is supported
by the fact that the lysine side chain with its high pKa
is not involved at all in the formation of dimers.
The deamidation mechanism may also explain why
we see no acylation products during microsphere ero-
sion. Both cyclic intermediates of insulin and of PLA
and PLGA oligomers compete for the same nucleo-
philes during the formation of covalent dimers and
during acylation. Under the conditions that prevail
inside the microspheres, the reactive cyclic anhydride
inside insulin most likely has a higher reactivity than
the five-membered ring oligomer intermediate that was
shown to form at the carboxy terminal of PLA and
PLGA oligomers. Therefore, by virtue of kinetic reac-
tion control, the deamidation and dimerization may be
favored. In addition, insulin exists as monomers in low
concentrations only (b0.1 AMc0.6 Ag/ml). At higher
concentrations (b0.01 mM) and low pH values, insulin
dimerizes covalently, as described above, and non-
covalently. The data obtained by Kaplan et al. [26]
indicated that at least the B29 Lys amino group of
insulin is involved in this process and is, therefore, not
available for an acylation reaction. Moreover, in con-
trast to the small peptides, the complex secondary and
tertiary structures of insulin may provide a sterically
hindered environment and thus prevent the attack of
any available free amino groups by the acylating agent.
In the control experiments with lactic acid solu-
tions, the slower reaction of the intermediate seems to
be compensated by the higher oligomer concentration.
Furthermore, insulin cannot escape acylation by ad-
sorption due to the missing polymer surface.
5. Conclusion
In contrast to many small molecular weight pep-
tides, insulin is mainly destabilized by deamidation
rather than by acylation during the erosion of PLA
and PLGA polymers. The different reactivities of the
activated intermediate cyclic oligomers and of cyclic
insulin could account for the phenomenon. Although
it cannot be excluded that other factors such as the
structure of insulin contribute to the prevention of
acylation, one can draw the conclusion that in addition
to the presence of free amine groups, the properties of
a protein decide to a large degree whether it undergoes
acylation or not.
