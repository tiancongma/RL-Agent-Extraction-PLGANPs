A B S T R A C T
While research on mevalonate inhibitors as vaccine adjuvants has made great progress to enhance the effec­
tiveness of the vaccine, co delivery of lovastatin and antigens (OVA) remains an enormous challenge. Here, we
encapsulated lovastatin into PLGA nanoparticles. PLGA loading lovastatin was further emulsified with squalene
to prepare Pickering emulsion. The emulsification conditions of Pickering emulsion were optimized, and the
optimal preparation conditions were obtained. After loading lovastatin and OVA, the size and zeta potential of
LS-PPAS/OVA was 1043.33 nm and –22.07 mv, the adsorption rate of OVA was 63.34 %. The adsorbing of LS-
PLGA nanoparticles on the surface of squalene in Pickering emulsions was demonstrated by Fluorescent confocal
microscopy. After immunization, LS-PPAS enhanced the activation of dendritic cells in lymph nodes, further
study found LS-PPAS not only elicited elevated levels of OVA-specific IgG and its subtypes, but also promoted the
secretion of TNF-α, IFN-γ, and IL-6 in serum as a marker of cellular immunity. Importantly, LS-PPAS showed
sufficient security through monitoring levels of biochemical parameters in serum and pathological observation of
organ following vaccinations. LS-PPAS may act as a promising vaccine carrier to produce strong humoral and
cellular immunity with acceptable safety.
1. Introduction
Vaccines play an important role in preventing and controlling in­
fectious diseases and treating tumors. adjuvants have the capacity to
augment the magnitude, scope, and longevity of the immune response
(Pulendran et al., 2021; APOST´oLICO et al., 2016; Boyle et al., 2007).
Treatment with antigens alone can be subjected to low immunogenicity,
especially subunit vaccines and inactivated vaccines, therefore, an
adjuvant is necessary to boost the immune response to the antigen (Reed
et al., 2013). In the past twenty years, studies on the perception of
microorganisms by the innate immune system have provided enormous
opportunities for understanding the design and development of adju­
vants (Borriello et al., 2022). Alum is currently the widely used adjuvant
in infectious disease vaccination efforts. With the discovery of more
proprietary drug targets and the development of delivery systems, some
new molecular type adjuvants and carriers have also been applied in
vaccine adjuvants (Wang et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2020).
Lovastatin, composed of secondary metabolites produced by fila­
mentous fungi, is a new type of vaccine adjuvant due to the fact that
lovastatin is a competitive inhibitor of HMG-CoA reductase that is a key

recruitment of immune cells in lymph nodes, antigen migration and
maturation of APCs, potently inducing both humoral and cellular

2. Materials and methods
2.1. Reagents and materials
Lovastatin (98 %, C10016511) and squalene were bought from
Shanghai Macklin Biochemical Co., Ltd, PLGA (75:25), MW = 15 kDa)
was ordered by Jinan Daigang Biomaterical Co.,Ltd (Shandong, China).
Pluronic F-68 (F68) was ordered from Yuanye Biomaterical Co.,Ltd
(Shanghai, China). Alum was provided from Thermo Biomaterical Co.,
Ltd (USA). OVA was bought from Sigma- Aldrich (St Louis, USA).
2.2. Preparation of lovastatin PLGA nanoparticles
PLGA nanoparticles loading lovastatin (LS-PLGA) were synthesized
by the modified methods as previous reports (Luo et al., 2016; Zhang
et al., 2020). Briefly, the 5 mg lovastatin and the 50 mg PLGA were
dissolved in 1 mL acetone solution, after complete dissolution, 100 μL of
ultrapure water was added to the mixture while sonicating it at 10 %
power for 1 min. Ultrapure water was added to the PLGA and statin
mixture while sonicating at 10 % power for 1 min. Then 0.7 % wt F68
ultrapure water was added with 10 mL and the mixture was sonicated at
20 % power for 2 min to obtain the emulsion. Then the emulsion was
stirred for 3 h to remove organic reagents. Subsequently, the emulsion
was centrifuged (12,000 rpm for 30 min), washed three times with ul­
trapure water and then lyophilized to obtain lovastatin PLGA nano­
particles (LS-PLGA). The LS-PLGA nanoparticles were stored at 4 ℃.
Then blank nanoparticles (PLGA) without lovastatin were prepared
using the same approach.
The characterization (size, zeta potential) of LS-PLGA were analyzed
by Zeta-sizer Nano Instruments (Malvern, UK). The transmission elec­
tron microscopy (TEM) (Tecnai 12, Philips, Holland) was employed for
observing the morphology of LS-PLGA nanoparticles.
In order to determine the encapsulation efficiency of lovastatin in LS-
PLGA nanoparticles, the supernatant was harvested prior to washing
with ultrapure water and the absorbance number was obtained by
measurement of a UV–Vis (238 nm). The 1 mL LS-PLGA emulsion was
added to a volumetric flask and fixed to 10 mL with methanol. The
emulsion was subjected to centrifugation (12000 rpm for 30 min) and
the supernatant was separated and collected. The free lovastatin was in
the supernatant. The concentration of lovastatin in the uncentrifuged

2.6. Antigen adsorption of LS-PPAS
To create an OVA-absorbing LS-PPAS (LS-PPAS/OVA), the LS-PPAS
and OVA were dissolved and mixed by vortex at low speed for 5 min
at the same volume. Then the solution mixed by shaking at 4 ◦C for 2 h
(LS: 250 μg/mL, OVA: 250 μg/mL). The size, zeta potential, PDI values
were measured.
Antigen loading efficiency of LS-PPAS/OVA was measured by
Bicinchoninic acid (BCA) assay. Firstly, to remove the free OVA antigen,
LS-PPAS/OVA was rinsed with running water for 12 h in a dialysis bag
(50,000 D). Then 1 mL NaCl solution (2 M) was added into 1 mL dia­
lyzed emulsion of LS-PPAS/OVA. After placing for 24 h, the emulsion
was centrifuged (12000 rpm, 30 min) to separate the oil and water
phases. The concentration of OVA antigen in the aqueous phase was
determined by a BCA protein assay kit.
2.7. Release profile of lovastatin and OVA in vitro
For in vitro drug and antigen release profiles of LS-PPAS, OVA loaded
LS-PPAS was transferred to a dialysis bag (50000 D) and placed in PBS
buffer (pH = 7.4) with shaking (37 ◦C, 100 rpm). The 1 mL sample in the
dialysis bag was replaced by 1 mL fresh PBS buffer during the pre­
determined periods (3, 5, 10, 12, 24 h, and 1, 3, 5, 7, 14, 21 days). 1 mL
of NaCl salt solution (2 M) was added to the removed emulsion and left
for 24 h. Following centrifugation at 12000 rpm for 30 min, the con­
centrations of lovastatin and OVA in the aqueous phase were quantified.
2.8. Preparation of bone marrow-derived dendritic cells
Briefly, the tibias and fibulas were isolated from BALB/c mice (6
weeks, female) under aseptic conditions. The bone marrow lumen was
then rinsed with PBS, the rinsed fluid was collected through a 70 μm cell
sieve, treated with the red blood cell lysis for 2–3 min. Bone marrow-
derived dendritic cells (BMDCs) were counted (5 × 105/mL) and inoc­
ulated in culture dishes. BMDCs were cultured in RMPI media with 10 %
fetal bovine serum, 3 % penicillin–streptomycin, GM-CSF (40 ng/mL),
and IL-4 (40 ng/mL) stimulating factors. The media was replaced with
half fresh media on days 2, 4, and 6. On day 7, the suspended cells were
collected, which serve as immature marrow-derived dendritic cells for
subsequent experiments.
2.9. Cytotoxicity assay in vitro
Firstly, 100 μL of BMDCs were added to 96-well plates. Then LS,
PPAS, and LS-PPAS were cultured for 24 h (0 ~ 250 μg/mL). The effect
of different concentrations of drugs on cell activity was determined by
MTT method (Liu et al., 2015).

through CLSM.
2.12. Detection of the level of OVA cross-presentation
DCs were cocultured with OVA, LS/OVA, PPAS/OVA or LS-PPAS/

Alum adjuvant group was served as a positive control group while PBS

blood samples were collected and the serum was separated and stored.
All animal experiments were carried out in accordance with IACUC
guidelines of Nanjing Agricultural University.

3. Results and discussion
3.1. Preparation of Pickering emulsion stabilized by LS-PLGA
nanoparticles
Pickering emulsion are formed by an oil phase, a particle stabilizer,
and a water phase. In order to have a stable lovastatin PLGA-stabilized
Pickering emulsion (LS-PPAS) with optimal condition, the oil volume
fraction (Φ), the concentration of PLGA, ultrasonic power and time were
investigated.
To detect the effect of different oil volume fraction on the stability of
LS-PPAS, the emulsions were prepared through sonication using various
oil volume fraction (Φ = 0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9). No significant
change in the particle size of the emulsions was observed at Φ values of
0.1, 0.2 and 0.3. When the Φ value was 0.5, the particle size and PDI
value of the emulsion increased. After centrifugation, more oil released
in upper layer indicates less stable emulsion (Kalashnikova et al., 2013;
Qi et al., 2014; Bao et al., 2019). As the increase of Φ values of the oil
volume from the upper layer was increased. Emulsions were not ob­
tained at the Φ value of 0.7 and 0.9. The emulsion prepared by the
condition of Φ = 0.1, no oil release in the upper layer, suggesting that it
possessed the greatest stability (Fig. 1A and Fig. S1). Therefore, the Φ
value of 0.1 was chosen for subsequent experiments.
At Φ value of 0.1, emulsions stabilized by various PLGA concentra­
tion (1, 3, 5, 10, 15, 20 mg/mL were prepared as shown in Fig. 1B and
Fig. S2. Obviously, the emulsion showed a big size about 2166 nm, a
high PDI value about 0.348, and more oil release from the emulsion at 1
mg/mL. The size was gradually decreased at PLGA concentration above
1 mg/mL, which may due to increasing number of particles used to
stabilize the emulsion and the presence of a large amount pf nano­
particles at the oil–water interface. Above 1 mg/mL PDI values were less
than 0.3, indicating relatively stable emulsion were prepared. In
addition, above 3 mg/mL, no release oil was measured from the emul­
sion. Therefore, the PLGA concentration of 5 mg/mL was chosen to
make the emulsion. Because the sufficiently particle concentration in the
emulsion could provide high particle coverage of droplets, limiting the
extent of film drainage between droplets, and preventing the aggrega­
tion of particles (Binks et al., 2005).
At Φ value of 0.1 and PLGA concentration of 5 mg/mL, various the
ultrasonic powers (5 %, 10 %, 30 %, 40 %, 50 %) was chosen to prepare
the emulsion as shown in Fig. 1C and Fig. S3. When the ultrasonic power
was greater than 10 %, the particle size of the emulsion showed no
significant changes about 1000 nm and no oil was released from the
emulsion. The PDI value of the emulsion were always less than 0.3 at
different ultrasonic powers. Thus, the ultrasonic powers of 30 % was
chosen to make the emulsion.
When the Φ value, PLGA concentration and ultrasonic powers was
set at 0.1, 5 mg/mL and 30 % respectively, the different ultrasonic times
(0.5, 1, 2, 3 min) was used to obtained the emulsion as illustrated in
Fig. 1D and Fig. S4. At 0.5 min, the emulsion has a very small particle
size and a large volume of oil is released at the same time. This may be
due to the ultrasonic time was too short and the emulsion failed to
emulsify caused by small particle size. At these sonication times, the PDI
values of the emulsions were consistently less than 0.3. The ultrasonic
time of 2 min was used to prepared the emulsion. Finally, the condition
of 0.1 oil volume fraction, 5 mg/mL PLGA, 30 % ultrasonic powers, 2
min ultrasonic time were chosen for preparing Pickering emulsion sta­
bilized by Lovastatin PLGA nanoparticles (LS-PLGA).
LS-PLGA nanoparticles were synthesized, yielding nanoparticles
with a consistent size of 253.56 ± 2.33 nm, PDI values of 0.122 ± 0.052,
and a zeta potential of −21.53 ± 0.17 mV. The lovastatin encapsulation
efficiency in LS-PLGA nanoparticles was determined to be 80.21 ± 0.22
% (Table 1).
3.2. Characterization of LS-PPAS
Pickering emulsions, bolstered by particles, exhibit a hierarchical,
adaptable, and dynamic quality, establishing them as a potent platform
for vaccine delivery (Xia et al., 2018). These particles are adsorbed
within an intermediate oil core, providing a substantial specific surface
area that aids in the recognition of target molecules and receptors by
antigen-delivering cells. Concurrently, the emulsion droplets display
deformable characteristics, enabling them to adhere to cell membrane
surfaces, thus enhancing their contact area and facilitating antigen
cross-presentation (Staats and Burkhart, 2018). Accordingly, the char­
acterization of Pickering emulsion often has a significant impact on the
activity of vaccine adjuvants. The typical LS-PPAS loaded with lova­
statin had an average diameter of 1096.30 nm, Zeta potential of −20.80
mV and PDI of 0.161.

In order to load OVA on LS-PPAS, the LS-PPAS and OVA were mixed
through a simple one-step approach with vortex and shaking. As shown
in Table 1, LS-PPAS/OVA (1043.33 nm) showed a similar size to LS-
PPAS (1096.30 nm), which indicated that the adsorption of OVA did
not affect the particle size of LS-PPAS. The zeta potential of LS-PPAS/
OVA and PPAS/OVA was around –22.07 mV and −20.97 mV respec­
tively, while LS-PPAS were about −20.80 mV. High zeta potential often
leads to a strong effect between particles, helping to maintain the sta­
bility of emulsion. In addition, the PDI of all particles was less than 0.3,
indicating that these particles showed uniform characteristics.

3.3. The stability and release profile of LS-PPAS
The stability of vaccine has a significant impact on the effect of im­
mune. The unique structure of Pickering emulsion often shows superior
stability. In order to test the stability of LS-PPAS, particle size, zeta
potential and PDI were tested at different time points. As shown in
Fig. 3A, after 30 days of storage at 4℃, the particle size and zeta po­
tential of LS-PPAS did not change significantly, and the PDI was lower
than 0.3, which showed that it was still stable after 30 days of storage.
However, after 60 days of storage at 4℃, the particle size and PDI have
significantly increased compared with the original emulsion, but there is
no obvious change by observing the appearance of LS-PPAS. The release
of encapsulated lovastatin within LS-PPAS plays a pivotal role in
enabling its metabolic regulatory efficacy. To investigate the drug
release profiles of lovastatin in a pH 7.4 PBS solution, we observed a
sustained-release pattern with LS-PPAS. Over the course of 7 days, the
cumulative release reached approximately 70 %. This sustained release
profile is of paramount significance for lovastatin’s adjuvant activity.
Pickering emulsion is synthesized by PLGA and squalene emulsifi­
cation, and the granular structure on its surface has the ability to adsorb
antigens, so the antigen release profiles of the OVA in PBS (pH 7.4) was
investigated. The result showed LS-PPAS/OVA exhibited a sustained-
release pattern and the cumulative release percentage reached approx­
imately 80 % at 14 days.
3.4. Antigen uptake by BMDCs
Dendritic cells (DCs) hold a pivotal position as antigen-presenting
cells (APCs) in the immune system, assuming a critical role in the
orchestration of immune responses. DCs serve as the instigators of im­
mune reactions by efficiently internalizing and processing antigens,
subsequently presenting these antigens to T cells (Macri et al., 2023).
Consequently, the process of antigen uptake by APCs stands as a
linchpin, influencing the initiation and duration of immune responses.
Firstly, the security of LS-PPAS was analyzed on the DCs, as shown in
Fig. 4A, LS-PPAS did not have a significant effect on cell viability of DCs
compared to PPAS, indicating that the addition of lovastatin did not
alter the safety of PPAS. Previous studies had found that lovastatin could
affect the retention time of antigen in DCs, and Pickering emulsion could
increase cellular uptake of antigen by interacting with DCs. Upon
treatment of DCs for 12 h, flow cytometry analysis revealed LS-PPAS
showed high level of FITC+DCs, likely because lovastatin and Picker­
ing emulsion play a dual role in promoting antigen uptake. Further,
confocal microscopy revealed the FITC fluorescence signal of LS-PPAS
inside the DCs was higher compared to LS and PPAS. Consistently, LS-
PPAS can enhance intracellular uptake of antigens effectively, which
will enhance DCs’ ability to process antigens and further enhance im­
mune effects.
3.5. LS-PPAS boosted antigen cross-presentation
Lysosomal escape of antigen is an important pathway for antigen
cross-presentation, which is significant in promoting the immune
response. There is substantial evidence that LS has the potential to
prolong antigen presentation to dendritic cells (Xia et al., 2018; Yang
et al., 2022), and that PLGA also exhibited endosomal escape effects
(Silva et al., 2016; Xia et al., 2018). After co-culturing DCs with OVA,
LS/OVA, PPAS/OVA or LS-PPAS/OVA for 12 h, the intracellular FITC-
OVA antigen transport of DCs was observed by CLSM. Our results
showed that the OVA antigen in the control group was highly co-
localized with the lysosome, whereas the OVA antigen in the LS, PPAS
and LS-PPAS group was partially not co-localized with the lysosome
(Fig. 5), which was a phenomenon of “lysosomal escape”. This phe­
nomenon was more significant in the LS-PPAS group than other controls.
The transfer of antigen to the cytoplasm via lysosomal escape helps to
activate the antigen processing and presentation of the MHCI pathway,
triggering a robust immune response. We examined the expression of the
dendritic cell surface molecule MHCI by flow cytometry. After 24 h of
stimulation with OVA, the expression level of MHCI molecules in the LS
group was 1.92 times higher than that in the OVA group, and in addi­
tion, the expression level of MHCI molecules in the LS-PPAS group was
significantly higher than that in the LS group and other controls. These
compelling findings strongly suggest that LS-PPAS has the ability to
boost antigen cross-presentation and trigger a robust immune response.
3.6. Activation of DCs in lymph nodes
After the vaccine was injected into the body, antigen was uptake by
DCs in the tissue and transported to the lymph nodes. T and B cells is
activated in lymph nodes by interacting with DCs to generate humoral
and cellular immunity. Considering that LS-PPAS can improve antigen
uptake of DCs and the correlation between antigen processing and DCs
maturation. The effect of LS-PPAS on DCs maturation was measured and
analyzed in lymph node. In this study, we utilized flow cytometry to
evaluate the expression of surface molecules on dendritic cells (specif­
ically, CD40 and CD86) within lymph nodes. This assessment was con­
ducted 48 h post-vaccination to gauge the activation and maturation
status of dendritic cells. As shown in Fig. 6, compared to OVA group, LS
and PPAS produced higher proportion of CD40+CD11c+
and
CD86+CD11c+. After loading LS into PPAS, LS-PPAS significantly pro­
moted the expression of CD40 and CD86 compared to LS and PPAS
group. These results showed that new Pickering emulsion offered a
potentially more effective way to promote the maturation and activation
of DCs in lymph nodes after LS loaded into PPAS, indicating LS-PPAS
had a great potential as a vaccine adjuvant.
3.7. LS-PPAS induced enhanced humoral immune responses
To systemically evaluate the enhancement of the adaptive immunity,
6-week female ICR mice were inoculated with PBS, OVA, Alum, LS,
PPAS, and LS-PPAS by intramuscular injection, 14 days later for another
administration (Fig. 7A). LS-PPAS induced significantly higher levels of
OVA-specific IgG, showing the robust capability to elicit humoral im­
mune responses. The OD450 of lgG antibodies from LS-PPAS-adjuvanted
mice reached 1.16 at 35 days after immunization, which were 1.43 and
1.40 folds higher than that of mice immunized with LS and PPAS,
respectively. In addition, the level of IgG1 showed the similar trend,
with higher level in LS-PPAS than that of LS and PPAS immunized mice,
which was considered as relevant indicators of Th2 response. The level
of IgG2a showed a similar trend and LS-PPAS was higher than LS and
PPAS group at 21, 28, 35, and 42 days after immunization. As an indi­
cator of Th1 immunity, IgG2a plays an important role in the cellular
immunity of vaccines. On the whole, we found that LS-PPAS was more
potent at generating OVA-specific IgG, IgG1 and IgG2a, which declares
that LS-PPAS triggers a systemicTh1/Th2 immune response.
These findings are exciting as they demonstrate that it is an excellent
strategy to load statin through Pickering emulsions, which has con­
structed a new type of vaccine adjuvant. This will allow for more
effective in the construction of such vaccine adjuvant than statin adju­
vants alone.
3.8. Cytokines secretion
To assess cytokine concentrations, we meticulously determined the
levels of Th1 cytokines (TNF-α and IFN-γ) and Th2 cytokines (IL-6) in the
serum of mice on day 35 post-primary vaccination. As illustrated in
Fig. 8, the LS-PPSA group exhibited a substantial increase in the secre­
tion of TNF-α and IFN-γ (p < 0.05), aligning with previous observations
of IgG2a levels. Additionally, the LS-PPSA group displayed a significant
rise in IL-6 levels (p < 0.05).
Cytokines are important signaling molecules that regulate the dif­
ferentiation and function of T cells. IFN-γ can enhance the activity of

macrophages and promote cytotoxic immune responses (Conlon et al.,

3.9. Biosafety of LS-PPAS in vivo
A promising vaccine adjuvant candidate should possess a high safety
profile. Herein, the systemic safety of LS-PPSA was investigated. In this
study, we preliminarily evaluated safety properties of LS-PPSA by
monitoring levels of various serum biochemical parameters and patho­
logical observation of organ on day 42 after first immunization. In
general, LS-PPSA vaccinated mice were well-tolerated and these pa­
rameters remained stable after vaccination. First, the serum of mice was
collected and measured for the following biochemical indicators on day
42 after first immunization: glutamic oxaloacetic transaminase (AST),
glutamic pyruvic transaminase (ALT), alkaline phosphatase (ALP),
lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) and urea nitrogen (BUN). As depicted in
Fig. 9 A and B, the levels of AST, ALT, ALP, LDH, and BUN in LS-PPSA
group exhibited no significant compared other groups and fell within
the normal range. Further, by observing and analyzing H&E staining
image including heart, liver, spleen, lung, and kidney, there was no
obvious pathological change in these organs. Moreover, Given the use of
LS in conditions such as coronary artery disease and hypercholester­
olemia, we evaluated whether LS/PPAS would lead to elevated serum
cholesterol levels and transient liver injury. Serum was collected from
mice 12 h after the administration and analyzed by blood biochemistry
for serum total cholesterol (TC) as well as markers of hepatic function.
Our results showed that total cholesterol in serum was not significantly
reduced in the LS/PPAS group of mice compared with the control group,
and all liver function-related indices were within the physiological range
(Figure S5). Collectively, our data indicated a high-level safety property
of LS-PPSA.
4. Conclusions
In this study, we engineered a Pickering emulsion stabilized by
lovastatin nanoparticles. The optimized formulation parameters for LS-
PPAS were meticulously established: a 0.1 oil volume fraction, a PLGA
concentration of 5 mg/ml, and ultrasonication at 30 % power for 2 min.
LS-PPAS, when loaded with lovastatin, exhibited an average diameter of
1043.33 nm, a zeta potential of –22.07 mV, a PDI of 0.175, and a regular
spherical shape with distinct surface protrusions. LS-PPAS demonstrated
exceptional stability and efficiently accommodated OVA antigen
loading. Furthermore, LS-PPAS demonstrated a remarkable capacity to
induce dendritic cell (DC) maturation and activation within the lymph
nodes. Most notably, when employed as an adjuvant in immunization,
LS-PPAS elicited a robust and antigen-specific immune response, char­
acterized by heightened antibody levels and augmented secretion of pro-
inflammatory cytokines. Additionally, comprehensive safety assess­
ments in mice underscored the excellent safety profile of LS-PPAS.
In summary, this investigation unveils novel applications for lova­
statin and Pickering emulsion. LS-PPAS emerges as a promising vaccine
carrier with the potential to serve as an effective delivery system for
enhancing immune responses. These findings present exciting prospects
for the field of vaccine development.
